# Maude — Funnel, Ads and Masterclass Strategy Call

Coaching call with [[maude]] on 2026-07-15, about 76 minutes. The agenda was how to read the paid-ads numbers the agency is reporting, what to fix in the masterclass funnel, and how to reposition the Rise masterclass copy and the sales calls.

## High-signal summary

- Maude's real goal for the numbers was understanding, not reporting. She said she was putting the agency's numbers into the checklist without knowing what they meant, and she wanted to walk into agency calls knowing what to advocate for.
- The July masterclass produced about six participants and Maude expected two or three of them to become clients.
- Alyssa's core diagnosis of the ad reporting: the agency optimizes front-end metrics (clicks, video views, reach, cost per purchase) while the business needs back-end metrics (cost per booked call, cost per client). The agency was not tracking calls booked at all, so no ad set could be attributed to actual revenue.
- Facebook was reporting the week as down 33 percent on purchases, but gross revenue for that same week was actually up, because half of buyers were taking the order bump. Seven customers produced ten orders. This is the concrete reason not to trust the platform's own verdict.
- The bottleneck is the cart conversion rate on the first offer page, currently around 4 percent. Target is 15 percent minimum, ideally 20, with a stretch goal of 30.
- Spend is roughly 250 dollars a week, about 15 dollars a day per creative, with a total monthly investment near 3000 dollars once the retainer and ad budget are counted. Maude flagged real financial pressure behind this.
- Only two or three creatives were being tested. Alyssa's counter: images are cheap to swap, so test five or more image styles rather than waiting on new video.
- Price is moving from 7 dollars to a split test of 27 versus 37 on the masterclass page. Alyssa modelled it live and the funnel goes from underwater to roughly 140 dollars positive at the higher price with the bump and upsell in place.
- Maude is going through a demanding personal period and described her energy and creative capacity as lower than usual right now. Plan her workload accordingly.

## Decisions made

- Split test the masterclass page at 27 dollars against 37 dollars, same page duplicated, price the only variable. Watch the URL: if ThriveCart's A/B test issues a different URL, the ads have to be updated, so keep the URL stable if possible.
- Rebuild the upsell, downsell and bump pages. The template Alyssa sent did not render correctly and even the buttons point to the wrong place.
- Ask the ads team to test more image creatives, not only video, and to give three or more options per round.
- Ask the ads team to track calls booked and to attribute them per ad set using UTM parameters.
- Keep the pre-masterclass call as it is, but treat it internally as a sales call rather than a soft personalization call. Do not require people to have watched the masterclass first.
- Do not run the old recorded July masterclass as a prerequisite. Most buyers never show up live, and gating the call behind a replay adds friction for no gain.
- Do not name "the Power Couple Method" inside the Rise masterclass. Use the words power couple and magnetic on the page as through-line language, and save the method name for the paid program.
- Structure the masterclass email flow as two emails before the event and three after, with a 48 to 72 hour booking window, stripped of anything date-specific so the sequence is reusable for August, Christmas, Valentine's Day and summer.
- Seasonal, date-tied emails can go to the whole list; the tight post-masterclass urgency emails stay scoped to attendees inside that 48 hour window.
- Promotional windows worth planning around: September and October as the strongest, late January into early February for a Valentine's angle, plus Black Friday, Cyber Monday and the gap between Christmas and New Year for low-ticket impulse offers. Repeat last year's Pleasure Portal play in that gap.

## Copy and positioning direction

Alyssa's rewrite of the Rise masterclass promise, based on what Maude's clients actually say rather than the softness-and-desire framing that was on the page:

1. He takes initiative.
2. Every day of the relationship feels like being in love on vacation.
3. The relationship multiplies your life rather than draining it.

Maude's own articulation of why those three stack, worth keeping: when he takes initiative, she stops carrying the mental load, which frees space for creative passion; shared passion raises emotional energy; that energy goes into her goals. Alyssa liked the stacking and told her to write it that way because it will stick in someone's mind.

Alyssa also named a recurring editing habit for Maude to self-check: she lists multiple near-synonyms ("vacation, romance, fun, love, flirt") and the pile-up dilutes the point. The fix is to group everything into three ideas and pick one phrase per idea.

On the tone of the promise: Maude's buyer is not asking to soften and receive, she is asking to feel fired up again. Language like creative fuel returning fits better than language about softening.

## The tracking model Alyssa taught

The forecast sheet works backwards from close rate. Book rate, show rate and close rate produce the number of calls needed per month for a revenue goal. Alyssa's placeholder industry figures were roughly 70 percent show rate and a 20 percent buyer rate, to be replaced with Maude's real numbers as they arrive. Monthly, Maude fills in total spend, page views, leads, calls booked, calls shown, and closes, which yields cost per lead, cost per call and cost per customer.

The sequencing Alyssa gave: first prove the low-ticket offer pays for the ads, then find out which leads actually turn into high-ticket clients, because otherwise the work is optimizing ads for a list that never buys.
