Alyssa Content Relay: Product Vision + Success Brief
One-sentence product vision
Build a local weekly content production app that turns my existing ideas, emails, Obsidian notes, BOH curriculum, best-performing content, content themes, and real CTA map into source-grounded content batches that sound like me, sell my world, and require very little weekly thinking from me.
The real goal
The goal is not to generate more content.
The goal is to make my best thinking easier to retrieve, shape, repurpose, and turn into strategic assets without making me re-explain my business, my audience, my offers, my CTAs, my taste, or my standards every single time.
This app should become the local content relay team that takes the things I already have:
ideas
emails
voice notes
Obsidian notes
BOH modules
launch arguments
best-performing posts
weekly themes
content calendar rails
CTA keywords
freebies/products/offers
rejected examples
gold standard examples
And turns them into aligned content outputs:
educational posts
offer posts
“for the girls” posts
email-to-carousel transformations
captions
first comments
CTA matches
BOH-module-based teaching
designer-ready copy handoff
The app should make content production feel less like inventing from scratch and more like pulling from an already-stocked pantry.
The strategic purpose of my content
My content exists to turn my best thinking into repeatable belief shifts that make experienced service providers and creatives trust Back of House as the obvious next layer of support.
Every strong content asset should do at least one of these jobs:
Make the right people feel found.
Name a pattern they are living inside but may not have clearly articulated.
Teach one useful, nuanced thing.
Create a gap between what they are doing now and the system they actually need.
Move them toward the correct existing CTA, freebie, product, or offer.
Build the world of Back of House: email-first, archive-first, systems as relief, slow marketing, Pinterest as discovery, and the belief that their best thinking should become assets instead of expiring in the feed.
What would make this a success
The app is successful if it becomes a repeatable weekly machine I actually use.
Success looks like:
I paste in one idea, email, note, or BOH module and get back a usable mini-batch.
The app creates more than one asset from a single idea, usually an edu post, an offer post, and a for-the-girls post.
It uses my real monthly themes, weekly post slots, CTAs, products, freebies, and BOH modules instead of inventing them.
It searches my Obsidian second brain and pulls relevant source knowledge before generating.
It uses my best-performing posts and emails as pattern references.
It creates content that feels specific to my business, not generic marketing advice.
It can repurpose an email into a carousel without flattening the point.
It can take a BOH module and teach one small public-facing piece in a nuanced way.
It creates a desire gap toward an existing freebie, product, or offer.
It gives me content that feels 70 to 90 percent usable, not “technically fine but spiritually beige.”
It reduces the number of decisions I have to make.
It exports copy in a format that can go to Notion, Canva copy fields, or a designer handoff.
It improves over time as I add approved examples, rejected examples, performance data, and better source files.
It runs locally on my computer after Fable 5 builds it.
The clearest success signal:
I can run it once per week and feel like it found the actual strategic shape of my ideas without me having to wrestle every output into being.
What would make this a failure
The app fails if it becomes another generic AI content machine.
Failure looks like:
It invents CTAs, keywords, offers, freebies, or themes.
It ignores my Notion/source-truth data.
It generates random content ideas instead of working inside my existing weekly content system.
It makes beginner-level content for an experienced audience.
It turns BOH into a rescue offer for broken businesses.
It makes everything about posting more, content tips, or generic productivity.
It uses fake examples or weak invented examples.
It overuses frameworks without taste.
It creates “viral hook” slop instead of right-person hooks.
It sounds like a generic social media strategist.
It loses the original spark of the idea.
It teaches either too much or nothing useful.
It creates pretty concepts with no persuasive mechanism.
It gives me outputs I immediately want to rewrite from scratch.
It requires huge prompts or lots of context every week.
It needs Fable 5 or a cloud frontier model every time it runs.
It feels harder to use than just asking ChatGPT manually.
The most dangerous failure mode:
The app appears productive because it creates a lot of content, but the content does not carry my taste, my strategy, my source knowledge, or my actual sales logic.
Core operating belief
Creative generation should happen inside source-truth rails.
The app should always load the known inputs first:
monthly themes
weekly structure
post types
CTA map
offer map
BOH module index
best-performing content
approved examples
rejected examples
voice rules
annoyance rubric
Obsidian context
The model should not be asked to remember these things from vibes. It should retrieve them locally.
What we learned from researching existing tools and systems
1. Skills should be modular, not mega-prompts
Anthropic’s Skills model is useful because skills are packaged as folders of instructions, scripts, and resources that are loaded dynamically for specialized tasks. This confirms that the app should use small reusable skill files instead of one giant prompt swamp.
For this app, that means separate skills for:
source truth loading
idea intake
theme routing
BOH module matching
concept bake-off
hook selection
persuasion arc building
CTA matching
annoyance detection
voice checking
final QA
export formatting
The skill structure matters because I need repeatable behavior, not improvisational AI jazz.
2. Social content systems work best as a relay, not one writer bot
The BlackTwist social media skills repo shows a useful content-specific breakdown: separate skills for post writing, thread writing, carousel writing, caption writing, repurposing, hook writing, performance analysis, and pattern analysis.
That confirms the app should not have one “write content” skill.
It should have a relay where each stage does one job:
source truth → context → concept → score → draft → QA → export
3. Carousel and caption writing need platform-specific structure
The researched social media skills separate carousel writing from caption writing because those are different jobs. Carousel copy needs a slide-by-slide argument arc. Caption copy needs hook, context, meaning, and CTA.
For my app, this means:
slide copy and caption copy should be separate outputs
the carousel needs a concept arc before slide copy
the caption should not merely repeat the slides
the first comment should be its own output
CTA language must come from the source-truth CTA map
4. The highest-value part is the grading loop
The strongest content workflows do not just generate. They grade, revise, and regrade.
For me, the grading loop matters more than the first draft because my usual negative feedback is very consistent:
too obvious
too basic
too simplified
too generic
not persuasive enough
lost the original point
examples are bad
CTA is made up
sounds like everyone else
does not respect an experienced audience
This is why the app needs an Annoyance Detector before drafting and a Mind-Blown Gate after drafting.
5. Hooks need to be right-person hooks, not viral bait
The research surfaced many hook-writing systems and social media skills, but the key lesson is that a hook library is only useful if it is filtered through brand, audience sophistication, and post purpose.
For my app, hook quality should be scored by:
specificity
curiosity
voice match
audience sophistication
relation to the teaching point
relation to the CTA
whether it feels like me
whether it avoids generic “viral hook” language
The app should not chase broad virality. It should create hooks that make the right people feel like the post was written from inside their business brain.
6. Best-performing content should become source knowledge
The app should not write from theory only.
It should use my own proven content as training-style reference material:
best-performing posts
best-performing emails
best-performing hooks
posts that got replies
posts that drove clicks
posts that made people say “this is exactly it”
launch emails that moved people
examples I personally approved
Each best-performing asset should become a compact pattern card:
hook pattern
angle
structure
CTA
why it worked
reusable phrases
content type
related offer
This keeps the app grounded in my actual audience, not generic best practices.
7. Obsidian is not optional
My second brain should be part of the product.
The app needs to search my Obsidian vault before generating because many of my best ideas already exist in old notes, chats, transcripts, launch docs, and fragments.
The app should not pass the entire vault into the model. It should create compact context cards from notes:
title
path
summary
strong phrases
related offers
related themes
related BOH modules
tags
relevance reason
Then it should pull only the most relevant cards for each run.
This is how the app stays low-token and source-grounded.
8. Local-first matters
The app should run on my computer every week without needing Fable 5 again.
Fable 5 is the builder, not the weekly engine.
Local-first matters because:
the source knowledge can live on my machine
weekly runs should be cheaper
the workflow should be repeatable
the app should not need a giant cloud-model context dump every time
deterministic lookup should handle CTAs, themes, weeks, and BOH modules
local retrieval should handle Obsidian and example matching
Ollama is relevant because it provides a local API for running and interacting with models programmatically, served locally by default at localhost:11434.
The local model does not need to be a genius if the app is structured well. The source truth, examples, rubrics, and QA gates should carry as much of the intelligence as possible.
9. Third-party skills are inspiration, not plug-and-play truth
The research found useful public skill patterns, but the product should not blindly install a pile of third-party skills.
A recent study on SKILL.md files found that real-world skill files often contain quality issues, and other research warns that skill metadata and instructions can create supply-chain risks.
For my app, this means:
study public skills
borrow patterns
rewrite them for my source truth
keep only the skills I actually need
avoid skill bloat
treat third-party skills as untrusted until reviewed
prioritize custom skills built around my actual workflow

This should feel like an assembly line, not a brainstorm.
The important creative logic
One idea should be able to create more than one post.
For example, one idea can become:
an edu post
an offer post
a for-the-girls post
Each post should use the same source idea but perform a different strategic job.
The app should not assume one idea equals one asset.
The app should think:
What strategic lives can this idea have inside this week’s existing content plan?

Then it should generate concept options, score them, and only draft the strongest ones.
The concept bake-off matters
Before drafting, the app should create concept options across the required post types.
Each concept should include:
angle
hook direction
teaching point
BOH module source
persuasion goal
gap created
matched CTA option
why it fits the week
risk flags
Then the Annoyance Detector should score the concepts before the app wastes time drafting weak ones.
This is crucial because the best output will come from choosing the strongest concept before writing, not from polishing a weak concept after the fact.
The BOH module matcher is a core feature
The app should be able to take an idea or email and ask:
Which Back of House module, method, belief, or concept does this naturally connect to?

Then it should use that module to teach one small public-facing thing.
This keeps my content from becoming generic POV content.
It turns my public content into a tasting menu of my actual method.
The app should also identify the paid gap:
What does this post help someone understand they need from the full system?

The CTA matcher is lookup-only
The app must never invent CTAs.
It should look up the correct CTA from my source map.
If it cannot find a confident match, it should flag the post for manual CTA selection.
Acceptable behavior:
No confident CTA match found. Needs manual CTA selection.

Unacceptable behavior:
Comment SYSTEMS to get my free guide.

unless SYSTEMS is an actual keyword in the CTA map.
The output should be designer-ready, not designed
I do not need the app to design the post.
I need it to prepare the copy and handoff.
Final output should include:
post type
monthly theme
week

concept
hook
slide copy
caption
first comment

freebie/product/offer
designer note
QA score
status
Possible status:
ready for review

needs stronger hook
needs manual edit

My strongest constraints
The app must preserve these constraints:
Do not use em dashes.
Do not use the “it’s not this, it’s that” structure.
Do not invent CTAs.
Do not invent products.
Do not invent freebies.
Do not invent monthly themes.
Do not invent weekly post types.
Do not make BOH sound like a rescue mission for broken businesses.
Do not write for beginners.
Do not overexplain basic marketing.
Do not make generic “post less” content.
Do not chase trends without translating them into my POV.
Do not create huge token-heavy prompts.
Do not pass giant source files into the model every time.
Do not make the app dependent on Fable 5 for weekly use.
The core product decision
This should become a local source-grounded content system, not a cloud AI assistant.
Fable 5 should be used to build the app, structure the skills, create the source file schemas, and help produce the first working version.
After that, the app should run locally each week using:
local source files
local Obsidian retrieval
deterministic lookup tables
compact context cards
local model calls
scoring rubrics
approved/rejected examples
export files
What the app should learn over time
The app should improve as I add:
approved outputs
rejected outputs
better gold standard examples
performance data
new CTAs
new weekly themes
new BOH module notes
new Obsidian notes
new launch language
It should treat my approvals and rejections as product data.
Rejected content is especially important because it teaches the app what “technically fine but not Alyssa” looks like.
The simplest success version
The MVP is successful if it can do this:
I paste in one idea or email.
The app loads my current content rails.
It searches my Obsidian notes and best-performing content.
It matches a BOH module.
It creates edu, offer, and for-the-girls concepts.
It scores those concepts.
It drafts the strongest versions.
It uses real CTA keywords.
It exports clean designer-ready copy.

That is the first win.
Everything else can come later.
