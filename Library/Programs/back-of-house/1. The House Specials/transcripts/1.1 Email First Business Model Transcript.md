# An Email First Business Model

← [[../INDEX|1. The House Specials]]

> **What this covers:** The psychology of buying through email, the repetition effect, action training, and the three rules for building an email-first offer suite. This is the foundational "why email works differently" video.

---

[00:00:00] Let's talk about the psychological environment of buying from emails because when people are in their inbox, they're acting totally differently than they would be on social, than they are at a webinar, than they are on a sales call. And so we've got to be really clear on what that is and make sure that we lean into why people are most likely to purchase from email.

## The Email Mindset

[00:00:16] The first thing is the email mindset. So when you're on social you are in entertainment mode, you're in scrolling mode. People when they're in their inbox are in checking mode. They are in more of this like quieter, less distracted mood, but they are also in an action-taking mood. Because you're checking your email, you want to just do a check and then get out of there. So one of the ways that we want to make sure that we're optimizing for this is by getting to the point as quick as possible.

[00:01:05] The other mindset side of things that I always try to remember and bring back to the focus of any email is that they have made an intentional choice to be here. They opted in, they subscribed, they clicked, they opened this email. And so they're acting on really intentional behavior right now. And that's amazing for conversions and for their attention.

[00:01:37] Making sure that we're always bringing back to the front of their mind — why you deserve a spot in their inbox, who are you, how did they get here, what did they opt in for. Just making sure that you have that clarity and that through line in your messaging through all of your content is going to be so important.

## The Repetition Effect

[00:02:05] We all know the classic marketing rule that says you need seven to 47 touch points before someone's willing to take action. And I have a feeling that it's way higher than that now.

[00:02:24] One thing that I want you to know is that you don't need to match social media's level of exposure because email accelerates that factor. Because of what I mentioned before about their inbox being really precious real estate to them, the more often they see your name there, the more trust they feel with you. It's a completely different relationship.

[00:03:41] What we want to do and how we want to actually leverage this: we want to, as often as possible and as casually as possible, bring up our offer as much as we can in our emails. And have that really clear through line of messaging so that people are clear what our offer is and they start to have this feeling of "I'll get that eventually" because they keep choosing to continue to read about it.

## Action Training

[00:04:19] The last thing is my favorite thing — action training. Creating habits with your subscriber. When they see your name in their inbox, they want to open it, click it, take action on it, enjoy it. And the more that you actually send them emails that they enjoy opening, enjoy reading, and enjoy clicking — giving them cool things to click on, whether it's polls or great resources or old videos or blog content — the more we can habit-form with them that they love to open and click your emails.

[00:04:55] When it comes time to actually make sales, they already have the habit of opening stuff. They're just going to add to cart. We want people to be clicking often and we want people to be clicking early.

## The Three Rules for an Email-First Offer Suite

[00:05:23] I have three rules for a really good offer suite. Right now we're specifically talking about your core offer, your signature dish.

**Rule #1: The offer should be able to sell without you over-explaining it.**

[00:05:54] Your offer needs to be so crystal clear that someone could go to your sales page and know exactly what it is you do, who it's for, why they need it, what they're going to get, what the specific outcomes are. All of that has to be so clear on your sales page, but also in every other part of your marketing. If I didn't know you, I've never met you, I don't know your personality — would I know exactly what you do, who it's for, and why I should buy that thing over the other things I'm currently researching?

**Rule #2: The offer should always be available.**

[00:08:34] An email-first offer is ready when your audience is ready. This is truly evergreen. Rather than relying on urgency or limited time offers or door-closing type offers, instead we want to focus on having multiple entry points to your offer. Buyers get to show up to the cart when they're ready to purchase. My favorite way is having some kind of rolling enrollment. Although we'll have sales or promotions, people can join at any point in time. This takes a lot of pressure off you.

**Rule #3: It should be easy to reoffer.**

[00:10:00] There should be multiple times and multiple different reasons that somebody should join every time you make the offer. The first offer gives them the best price you're ever going to give them. If they don't take that, next offer is a little bit higher but maybe you add in a bonus. They might then see a different offer with a 72-hour window after a webinar replay in your sequence. All of this is automated — going to that one person after they've been through your funnel.

[00:13:01] This creates integrity with your word and with your offer — they know "okay, this is really only available for 72 hours. Either I buy it now or I don't." That allows you to evolve your business model over time: maybe promotions once per quarter, then twice per year, then one live launch once per year. It creates real excitement without you needing to spearhead that excitement.

## Common Pitfalls When Moving to Email-First

[00:14:07] Two things to avoid:

1. **Making DMs or comments a required step to close sales.** If someone has to DM you to get the details before they'll buy, your offer isn't clear enough for email-first.

2. **Over-complicating your offer suite with lots of small offers.** When people are moving through your sequence, they might see all of these little things that don't add up to the transformation in your actual signature dish. On social that's fine — you make little sales until the big promotion. In an email-first model, it tends to create distraction. Keep your offer suite a very clear journey.

[00:15:13] Go do a little audit of your core offer and your offer suite in general. Make sure it's ready to be email-first, evergreen-first, and avoids those pitfalls. Then decide on your reoffer strategy.
