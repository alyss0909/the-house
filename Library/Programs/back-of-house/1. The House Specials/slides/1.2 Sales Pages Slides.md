## The Sales Page Framework

Your sales page is your virtual salesperson. A well-executed one:
- Overcomes objections without you
- Adds a level of professionalism and legitimacy
- Leads people to take action and checkout
- Clearly lays out the features and benefits
- A bad one can tank a good offer

> Your philosophy has to be so disruptive and unique that it makes people stop mid-scroll and say, "Wait, what?" in a sea of boring offers. If your philosophy and method feel generic or vague, nothing else on the page will land. This is your "sticky idea" moment, like bacon and butter in a world of oatmeal.

**The three-part framework:**
1. The Story
2. The Details
3. The Close

This is a simple framework to start with — it's open to interpretation and reorganization to tell your offer's story. Each section should be thoughtfully considered because they're an opportunity to illustrate the nightmare, dream, and bridge from different angles.

## Part One: The Story

Sections in this part:
- The Sexy Title
- The Descriptive Sentence
- The Lemme Guess

---

### Writing a Hot Title

One sentence description — in case they don't read anything else.

**Formula:** The [name of offer] is a [what it is] to help you [result]

The title and one-liner work together. If they don't feel seen, called out, and a little uncomfortable in the first three lines, they're already gone. If they don't feel the gap, they won't feel the urgency.

*Cross-ref: One Sentence + Big Benefit sections in The Cozy Business Plan*

---

### The Lemme Guess

> "Let me guess — you feel like this."

This shows your reader that you understand their problem. More importantly, you're going to get inside their head and even have specific "words" they say so it's clear you really know where they're at and what they're thinking.

Goal: "Omg you're talking to me."

Call out their nightmares in a way that feels so relatable, they think, "Oof, that's me." If they're not nodding along, the emotional hook isn't strong enough.

*Cross-ref: Fourth core question — Email First Ecosystem; Buyer Persona — The Cozy Business Plan*

**The Symptom of the Symptom** — don't just name the symptom; go one level deeper:

- *Sales Struggle* — Symptom: Not enough clients, inconsistent revenue. Symptom of the Symptom: Cancelling kids' sports camp, avoiding emails, skipping date nights, saying no to a dream trip again.
- *Overwhelm in Marketing* — Symptom: Can't keep up with content. Symptom of the Symptom: Missing bedtime, working through weekends, snapping at your partner, wondering if you even like your business anymore.
- *Self-Sabotage* — Symptom: Stuck in procrastination. Symptom of the Symptom: Watching friends achieve while feeling left behind, afraid you'll never actually change, feeling like you're wasting potential.

---

### The But You Wish

This is to make it clear that you understand what they want — what they will be, do, and have as a result of your offer. What's the specific result they want? How can you paint a picture of this?

> A great question: "Who is your dream client jealous of?" Who has what they want?

Paint a dream so vivid and emotionally charged that they can't help but imagine themselves living it. This is about showing the emotional relief they crave.

*Cross-ref: First core question — Email First Ecosystem*

---

### Well, That's Exactly Why I Created This

This is the missing link between their current frustration and what they really desire. Show them you've created an offer that will take them from point A to point B.

> A good sales page introduces the offer. A great one makes them realize they've already said yes before they even saw it — the pitch should feel like relief, not persuasion.

This is the Bridge Reveal — the moment you hand them the solution like you're slipping them the secret code. It should feel bold, disruptive, and like an obvious bridge from nightmare to dream. If it's soft or vague, it dies here.

**The Bacon and Butter Rule:** Your offer must stand out like bacon and butter in a world of oatmeal.

---

### The About You That's Really About Them

This is not your story. Short and sweet. This is your cred — why you are the right person to help them get the outcome they want.

- 1 part personality, 1 part credibility
- Goal: "Omg she's amazing, I have got to buy from her"
- People are skeptical, so give yourself the cred you deserve

Great time to add logos etc. Does your About Me section subtly show why your perspective makes this offer essential? If it just lists facts, you're missing a chance to build trust. Are you making it about them?

> If you can name the problem better than they can, they'll believe you know how to solve it — even if you say nothing else.

## Part Two: The Details

Sections in this part:
- The What
- The How
- The Deliverables
- The Call to Action
- The Bonuses

---

### The What

Quick high-level breakdown of what the program is — how many weeks, how many modules, what it's designed to do, and what outcome it will get them.

*Example: An 8-week health coaching program to take you from burnt out to full of energy. Holding your hand through 4 essential phases that will heal adrenal fatigue using freezer meal plans, no-cardio workouts and a customized supplement protocol.*

---

### The How

Everything included in your offer. Once you buy, here's what's gonna happen. Breakdown all the phases or modules, workbooks, coaching sessions, months, etc.

This is THE most important section — really sell here. Think about the cover of a magazine.

> I'm going to show you how melatonin will help you sleep better >> the one ingredient that will transform your night into a restorative spa for your cells.

**So That You Finally Get to Hawaii** — always come back to the outcome your ideal client cares about. Transform copy from features to destination:

- Done-for-you grocery list → Done-for-you grocery list *so that* you can get in and out of the grocery store in 15 minutes

> Sell the chocolate smoothie, give them the spinach. Sell them what they want, but make sure you're hinting at the unique depth they'll actually get.

The titles need to be spicy and the bullets need to feel like a movie trailer for the transformation they've been dying for. No one cares about the flight; they care about the view from the beach.

*Cross-ref: Third core question — Email First Ecosystem*

---

### The Deliverables

Make this visually appealing — it's for the skimmers. What will they GET?

> Tangible is Buyable. If they can't picture themselves holding it, using it, or benefiting from it immediately, it's not a deliverable. It's noise.

---

### The Calls to Action

Ask for the sale. Tell them to take action. Add a bit of real scarcity or urgency.

- Cart close, price increase, or disappearing bonus
- Essential even for evergreen or quarterly offers
- A great CTA includes: urgency + price + button
- Use first-person CTA language: "I'm ready to _____" / "I want the course"

**Buy Now vs. Book a Call:** Under $997 → buy now button. Over $499 → book a call.

---

### The Bonuses

Bonuses should feel like they're getting the best part for free, not like they're being handed extra chores. It's about making the process easier, faster, more fun. If they don't immediately think, "Oh, I need that" — it's not a bonus worth including.

## Part Three: The Close

Sections in this part:
- The Right Place
- The Outcomes
- The FAQ
- The Testimonials

---

### You're in the Right Place If

This section helps your reader decide yes or no. Overcome objections by letting them know they're in the right spot. Who is the perfect fit for this offer?

Avoid vague fit criteria like "willing to do the work." Be specific:
> "You know that a high-converting sales funnel is your next big thing but every time you try to DIY you get caught up in shiny object syndrome and techy drama."

---

### The Outcomes

By the end of this program you will… What will they be, do, and have after completing the program — not just what they'll know.

> People don't buy outcomes. They buy shortcuts.

Make a quick checklist, straight to the point. If they see something they want here, they'll take action.

Are you painting a clear picture of what they'll *have* — not just what they'll *know*? Learning is great, but owning a result is better.

---

### The FAQ

Strategic section — continue selling here. This is where people are making decisions.

Ask yourself: What is one of the biggest hesitations your dream client might be having right now? Do NOT skip this section.

> This is where we crush every doubt like it's the final boss in a video game. If you're not directly addressing their deepest fears — price, timing, effort — you're leaving conversions on the table. This section should whisper "I knew you'd ask, and here's why you don't need to worry."

Handling brand new / no testimonials yet: beta vs. pop culture references, recycling character testimonials or testimonials from related offers.

---

### Testimonials / Social Proof

Use them in multiple places throughout the sales page where it makes sense. Shorten and bold the key line. These are your essay's supporting evidence — come with receipts.

**Testimonial Template (Mad Libs Style):**

> Before working with [Your Name/Your Offer], I was struggling with [specific challenge or problem].
>
> I felt [emotion linked to the struggle] and was worried about [nightmare].
>
> But after [using your service/working with you], I [specific result] and now I [emotion or outcome].
>
> The best part was [unique experience or standout moment].
>
> I'd recommend [Your Offer] to anyone who [similar nightmare or dream outcome].

## Putting It All Together

Once you've brainstormed all these sections, start a fresh doc and turn it into paragraphs and more "sales page" format. Go back and inject personality and conversational copy. Read it back to yourself — is it a compelling story? Would it help you make a decision if you were "on the fence"?

**When in doubt, cut it out.**

**The Editing Process** — write in rounds:
1. **First draft** — free flow mess, just get something on the page
2. **First focus edit** — clarity, message potency, and disruptive moments
3. **Fun edit** — does this sound like me? Like my dream client?
4. **Persuasion edit** — have I really overcome their doubts, objections, fears? Am I answering before they ask?

(You can also run edits for humor, tone, grammar — up to you.)

**Gut-check questions:**
- Could someone close their laptop and instantly pitch this to a friend in one sentence? If not, it's too complex.
- Is it genuinely disruptive? If it feels like it could blend into the noise of social media, it's not ready yet.

**Poll your audience and other communities when you need more copy or inspiration.**
