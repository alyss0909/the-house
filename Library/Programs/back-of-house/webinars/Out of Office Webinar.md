## Why We're Here — The Antisocial Era

The goal of this class is to set up a system for you to spend more time offline — to actually be out of office — by training a team to keep working for you while you're gone. That team is your back of house.

The reason this class exists is that we're leaning into a more antisocial era. Last year we almost completely ghosted Instagram — didn't post anything for months — and we still booked clients. That's not necessarily the prescription, but it *is* possible, and it shouldn't be the terrifying thing it feels like right now.

Most of us didn't sign up to be influencers. Most service providers, coaches, and course creators signed up because of something they loved doing. But somewhere along the way our entire sales strategy ended up depending on how much we show up online — and that's a problem.

The vision board moment: when Alyssa really shifted into this model, the vision she had was time freedom — the ability to sit by a candle-lit window, write ideas in a notebook, try new things, actually serve clients deeply. Instead, the reality was being stuck in the weeds. Constant pressure to post. Research the algorithm. Switch to carousels. Rinse, repeat.

> "I would be willing to bet that you did not sign up to be an influencer."

The promise of this hour: a marketing system that feels good and nourishing, that can be set up once and trusted to keep running. Something that actually gives back the time you were promised when you started your business.

## What's Actually Happening to Organic Marketing

The truth that most of us are afraid to say out loud: organic marketing the way we've known it is dying. Even if you have decent reach right now, new research suggests there's a 90% chance that reach goes down this year.

Here are the actual numbers:

- **0.1%** of people who see a post on Instagram click the link in bio. That's 10 clicks for 10,000 views — and most of us aren't getting 10,000 views regularly.
- **80% decline** in overall Instagram reach over the last 5 years — from 10–15% reach in 2020 down to 2–3% now.
- **60%** of Google searches now end without a click, because AI summaries serve up the answer without sending anyone to your website.
- **19.4 hours** — the average lifespan of a social media post. Not 48 hours. Less than a day.

AI search summaries are literally scraping our work and serving up answers without sending people to our sites. Google told us this directly. These platforms are using our content to make our own visibility problem worse.

> "It feels like a ruse, guys."

Creators are being forced to make more content for fewer impressions and less reach than ever. And every piece of content we make makes those platforms more valuable, not us. The more we post, the more people have a reason to stay on their apps — and the more money the billionaire platforms make.

Even when a post goes semi-viral and gets good outbound clicks, we still have to turn around within 19.4 hours and try to replicate that. Post once a day became twice a day, then one reel plus five stories, then repurposing into shorts, carousels, threads. Even when there was a system — even when repurposing was dialed in — it was sucking the life out of every good idea.

> "I'm not selling them to the manosphere."

Somewhere along the way, "done is better than perfect" became the whole strategy. And that's just not sustainable.

## How We Got Here

Most businesses are running entirely on front-of-house energy. This is literally how Back of House was born — the realization that being under constant pressure to put out more was the core problem.

Here's the thing: your best ideas don't come when you're planning your next carousel. They come when you're offline. Walking, showering, after a client call. But because your marketing depends on you manually showing up, you're always in reactive mode instead of building something that lasts.

Funnels are great — they work well — but a funnel is only as good as the volume of leads you pour into it. And where do most people get those leads? Back to social media. And ads are getting harder to justify: prices keep rising, people are ignoring them more, and these platforms will happily burn your budget while people scroll past.

The Portugal story: about a year ago, sitting in Portugal with her daughter, Alyssa hit a wall. She'd never felt that way in eight years of running a business — almost sick thinking about work. She loved work, was fully an entrepreneur through and through — and yet there was this dread.

The business was high maintenance in the wrong way. She'd automated a lot, hired team members, added new software. But instead of freeing her up for the creative parts, she'd accidentally built a job managing people and testing funnels. She'd hired people to do the things she actually liked.

> "I had built my own nightmare of a job, honestly."

So she burned it all down. Turned off socials, ads, funnels. Passive products that were working fine — off. Starting from scratch, simpler. And something kind of crazy happened.

The pumpkin farmer analogy: there's a guy who sells pumpkins near where Alyssa lives. No sign. Not on Google Maps. Doesn't even have a website. His pumpkins sell out every single year.

> "There is another way."

## The Solution — Email + Pinterest

Back of House is not a new invention. It's not trend forecasting. What Alyssa did was look back — at her own business, and at the research — and ask: *what has always worked?*

She was literally in Google Scholar. There are research studies on email marketing, digital marketing, the creator economy. And the question she brought to all of it was: when we look at the graph, what has never shown that massive change? What still works today, even as every algorithm changes?

When you zoom out, you've probably seen this pattern too: every 3–5 years a new platform explodes. Facebook pages. Live videos. Instagram Reels. Twitter. Shorts. Threads. LinkedIn thought leadership. TikTok. Now AI-generated content. Every time, everyone scrambles.

But two things have never changed:

**Email** has been the #1 highest ROI channel for 20 years. It's well-documented. It doesn't matter what's trending — email has always converted highest. It used to convert way higher than everything else; now it still converts about 80% higher than other platforms.

**Search-based content** — Pinterest, Google SEO, YouTube — has always outlasted social. An Instagram story you post this morning might be dead by tonight. Old Pinterest pins are still driving traffic years later. Old blog posts still bring leads. Search engines send you traffic for years from the same piece of content.

> "We're going to look back at this and be like — did we fully black out for a little bit there?"

Old pins were getting more traffic than Instagram stories posted that same morning. So the question stopped being *what is working right now* and became *what has always worked* — and how do we build an entire back of house trained on only those things.

## The Pinterest Strategy

Pinterest isn't just a cute place to hang out. The people on it are dream clients. **55% of Pinterest users are shopping** — they're there to solve problems, not just scroll. These are intent-based users.

Think of it like thrifting with a list versus thrifting blind. When you go in knowing what you're looking for, you find it. Pinterest users are like that — they show up looking for something specific. That intent makes the content convert at a much higher rate than social.

Pinterest is the best parts of search and social combined: fun to scroll through, but also fully keyword-optimized. And it gets better with age.

**Content lifespan:**
- Instagram post: ~19.4 hours average lifespan
- Pinterest pins: still driving traffic 2+ years later
- **41% of pins with new saves are over 2 years old** — Pinterest actually serves your older content *more* as it accumulates engagement

The algorithm uses every single part of your profile — your bio, your board names and descriptions, your pin titles and overlay text, even where your pin links to — to understand what you're about and get you in front of the right people. Put your keywords everywhere: profile bio, board descriptions, pin titles, overlay text.

Finding your keywords is simple: put what you do into the Pinterest search bar. Look at the dropdown suggestions, the related searches, the little bubbles that pop up. Those are your keywords. Add at least one keyword about *who* your audience is.

**Bread and butter content:** The BOH recommendation is to start with just four strategic, forever pieces of content. These are the posts that answer your audience's biggest questions — the things people are searching for *before* they work with you. You take your time with them, make them really good (blog posts, podcasts, videos), optimize them with your keywords, and then add an opt-in so that the Pinterest traffic converting on that content flows directly onto your email list.

From those four posts, you then create a continuous stream of *fresh pins* — new images pointing back to the same four pieces of content, plus your freebie and offers. Pinterest doesn't care if the content is 10 years old; they just want fresh pins.

**The batch:** Alyssa built a custom script that generates 6 months of pins in 6 seconds from your four posts. **1,400 pins, batched in an afternoon, scheduled for 6 months.** Seven pins per day, every day, pointing people to your content — without ever touching it again after setup.

> "We need to stop normalizing content calendars."

Once your pins are scheduled, that's the system. You don't have to create new content. You just need the four cornerstone posts done right once, and the Pinterest machine runs itself.

## The Email Strategy

Email has changed — but not in the way most people think.

Yes, open rates are down. People click less. Unsubscribes are up because ESPs (email service providers) are making it easy to unsubscribe. But people are *not* spending less time in their inboxes. In fact, people are spending *more* time in their inboxes now than they were five years ago — they're just getting better at ignoring emails that aren't worth their time.

**The numbers:**
- **88%** of people check their email every single day
- About **half** of people are actively looking to buy from their inboxes
- **78%** of marketers say email is their highest ROI channel

ESPs don't just care about spam anymore. They want proof that your subscribers are actually into your emails. Their job is to keep people in their inbox — which means they reward emails that get engagement and penalize the ones people ignore.

The trends in email have changed wildly since 2016. But the approach that works hasn't changed much at all — because what works in email hasn't really changed since the Mad Men era. Three things:

1. **Curiosity** — get people curious
2. **Attention** — hold it
3. **Engagement** — keep them engaged

Don Draper was doing this. If you know those frameworks, the trends don't matter. You're building on something that's always worked.

**Welcome sequence ("Wanna Hear the Specials?"):** This sequence has to be so good it makes people stick around. It can't sound like the email sequence from your dentist. Everyone's become a savvy enough buyer that we can feel a generic sequence from a mile away. The goal is to take leads from not knowing you at all to being genuinely hungry — in just a few emails.

**Weekly simmer emails:** This is where you write little love letters to your community. You show up consistently — weekly, biweekly, or monthly — to keep people warm and top of mind. This is also how you build the kind of deep connection that competes with the parasocial relationships people have with social creators. People buy from people they feel they know.

**"Not your grandad's email marketing"** — right-time offers, segmentation, P.S.'s that sell, sign-off specials. Finding ways to make your audience feel like you're talking personally to them, even when you're writing to hundreds or thousands. Every time you send a simmer email, you get the opportunity to slide in a right-time offer — a low-key, very chill call to action that turns readers into buyers without a full promo.

The core shift: most of us don't have a lead problem. We have a conversion problem. We've been told it's a numbers game — just get more leads. But audiences can convert at 5%, 10%, even 30% when you actually have a conversion system. The goal isn't more leads up top; it's a better experience for the people you already have.

## The Offer Suite — Double Yes Method

Before the traffic and email systems can work, your offer has to be built to sell through email. Most of us, in the last five years, have redesigned our businesses to sell on social — through stories, DMs, voice notes, urgency posts. That has to change.

**Three rules for an email-first offer:**
1. It has to be able to sell without over-explaining
2. It should always be available — ready when your audience is ready
3. It should be easy to re-offer — so the same offer can surface again and again in regular emails without needing a whole new campaign

**The Double Yes Method** — this is where gatekeeping comes back in.

**Step one (First Yes): Your freebie.** This isn't just any freebie. The only people who should *want* your freebie are the people who are perfect for your offer. It's a pre-qualifier. It filters out the wrong leads before they ever get on your list.

> "Bring back gatekeeping."

**Step two (Second Yes): Your tripwire / low-ticket offer.** This is the second bouncer. A low-ticket offer ($11, for example) placed right after the freebie opt-in. People who buy from you once are **70% more likely to purchase from you again.** And it segments your hottest leads — by the time someone has taken both the first and second yes, you know they're the right person.

The result: by the time someone lands on your sales page or gets on the phone for a discovery call, they're already pre-qualified. You're not pitching to a cold room. You're talking to people who have already said yes twice.

This isn't new — freebie, tripwire, welcome sequence. Those aren't new because *they still work.* What changes is how you deliver them. The structure itself is the thing that's always worked, which is why it's still the foundation.

> "You are the front of house girly. That's where you belong. Let your back of house handle the rest."

## The Sales Strategy

Your email system is already nurturing leads automatically — they're getting warm, raising their hand, sometimes even booking calls on their own. The traffic funnel is bringing in new people continuously. At this point your business is genuinely low maintenance.

The only active things you need to do: batch 6 months of pins once every 6 months, and send a regular email (maybe an hour a week, unless you're Alyssa and write very long emails).

But for cash injections, the sales strategy has two parts working together:

**Active invites:** Promos. "Back of House is open this week." "I'm hosting a webinar, come join." These create quick revenue boosts. You don't have to do them constantly — once per quarter or twice a year is enough when your evergreen system is running underneath.

**Automated invites:** The right-time offers already living inside your email sequences. They go out continuously, without you having to think about them.

The one-two punch: active promos + evergreen sequences running simultaneously. Active sales from the promo, plus a regular trickle from the automated side. When your list is small or not yet conditioned to right-time offers, you need those active promos more. Over time, the balance shifts.

Best promo emails don't die after one send. They get added to the evergreen sequence. Those are your best sales emails — you keep them in rotation, and they keep working.

When your marketing is handled in the background, you get to be more creative in the foreground. Take more risks. Try weird things. Alyssa launched a "midnight menu" before this webinar — gave away pieces of BOH in a low-key, fun way — and ended up with more webinar registrants than a straight two-week promo would have produced.

## Low Maintenance Rules

An audit checklist for your whole system — whether or not you follow the exact BOH model:

**Create once, let it work forever.** The majority of your content should be something you can make once and let run. Take your time with it. Make it something you're proud of.

**Set up once.** Continuously check whether what you're doing requires daily upkeep to stay relevant. If it does, that's a liability. Does it stop working the moment you stop feeding it?

**Focus on impact over volume.** Go all in on evergreen content. YouTube videos, blog posts, podcast episodes, private podcasts — things that continue to work for you years later. When you get on the phone with people and they say "I heard you on this podcast and knew I had to book a call" — those are your evergreen pieces. Leverage those.

**Automate thoughtfully.** Not just automate for the sake of it. Automate in ways that still sound like you — as much as possible — so you can reclaim your time without feeling like you've sold out.

> "You don't need to do what I did. You don't need to burn everything down."

Most of us just need support and a clearer system. The things worth craving: a slower, softer pace. Something intentionally low maintenance. Something with breathing room built in — for creativity, for rest, for long lunches, for deep work. And for the good things outside the business too.

## The BOH System — Big Picture

The complete flywheel — from traffic to sale, on repeat:

1. **Pinterest → Bread & Butter Content** — Your 4 forever posts, optimized with keywords, continuously surfaced via fresh pins (7/day, batched 6 months at a time)
2. **Bread & Butter Content → First Yes (Freebie)** — Your content leads directly to an opt-in; only the right people want it
3. **First Yes → Second Yes (Tripwire)** — Low-ticket offer immediately after opt-in; pre-qualifies buyers; builds passive income; 70% more likely to buy again
4. **Welcome Sequence** — Takes new subscribers from strangers to hungry; strategically moves them toward your signature dish
5. **Weekly Simmer Emails** — Ongoing love letters; keeps community warm; right-time offers appear naturally; segmentation happens quietly in the background
6. **Right-Time Offers** — Surface in simmer emails whenever your story or content naturally tees up your offer; low-key CTA; no full promo required
7. **Active Promos** — Quarterly cash injections; best promo emails get added to evergreen sequences and keep working
8. **Back to the top** — New Pinterest traffic enters the top of the flywheel continuously; the loop runs without you

The funnel is inverted. Instead of spending all your energy at the top chasing new leads, you spend your energy on the people already in your community — nurturing them, serving them well, giving them what they need at the right time. Better clients. Better results. Better referrals. And the leads at the top keep coming in, because the system is already running.

> "That's a system, guys. That's it."
