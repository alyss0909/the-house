## Who This Is For

This class exists for a specific type of person. The creative who has their best ideas in the shower or on a long walk and wants the time and space to bring those things to life. The person who knows they need to be marketing but kind of hates marketing. The pen-and-paper person. The person with a million ideas and no time to execute them.

But mostly, it's for the person who is absolutely sick of never being done with their marketing. The feeling of — I did the dishes yesterday and they're already back. The laundry. The dinner. The relentless, never-crossable-off nature of modern content creation. Every day the algorithm resets. Every day you start from scratch. If that's exhausting you, this is made for you.

The promise: just one strategic page can be leveraged on Pinterest into an asset that's discoverable multiple times per day — something waiting for your dream client, sending daily leads to your email list, for literally years.

## The Viral Dream — and Its Real Holes

Most of us, whether we're saying it out loud or not, are hoping something hits. We're all out here hoping for that golden ticket — a post that takes off, explosive reach, new leads, credibility built overnight. And the reasons make sense.

One viral post can deliver up to 8x more organic reach than a regular post. 63% of people find new brands through viral content. When something's doing well, platforms immediately reward it with more exposure. In 2025, 58% of people discover new brands on social — and a viral moment can spike profile visits and follower growth by 300–1000% in the first 24 hours.

So it's real. The perks are real. And we've all had at least a moment of micro-virality where something does better than everything else and it's exciting and validating — and it gets us thinking: *if this happened consistently, would my calendar just be full?*

But the reality paints a different picture. When something goes viral, we don't control who it reaches. We've all seen the person who goes viral for accidentally ordering a tiny purse from Alibaba when they're actually a financial consultant. The exposure happens, and then it's gone. The algorithm favored the piece you threw up to keep up, not the thing you're proud of. And the click-through rates on organic social are, frankly, abysmal.

Research backs this up: viral spikes almost never create sustained growth. The spike reverses immediately after. 90% of people who create viral content never manage to replicate it consistently. Unless you're Mr. Beast, this might genuinely be a delusion we've all bought into.

Not to mention the real costs: the mental load of daily content decisions, the pressure to re-explain yourself in new ways every morning, the reach that's volatile and resets constantly. All while our actual businesses wait.

The truth is most service providers don't actually want virality. They want clients. They'd be happy to be offline doing the work — they're just still posting because they need the next client.

## Feed-Based vs. Index-Based Visibility

After almost ten years of online business and working behind the scenes with hundreds of service providers, the thing that finally clicked was recognizing two completely different types of visibility.

**Feed-based visibility** — your reach is distributed in real time, it gets in front of people, and then it expires just as fast. This is Instagram, TikTok, threads. The algorithm wants fresh content every single day because their users want fresh content. So we're incentivized to create constantly, and every single day that reach spikes and then drops. We start from scratch. Again.

**Index-based visibility** — your work is stored and resurfaced to the right people over time. This is Pinterest, Google, YouTube. Content is indexed, not just fed. It accumulates. It compounds.

One resets. One compounds.

The metaphor that makes this real: feed-based is like hunting, or the casino. Every morning you wake up, go to the slot machine, and see what happens. Some days you win. Most days you don't. You need to show up every single day or the machine stops. Index-based is farming. Or better — it's a library. You write a book, you give it to the library, and people can check it out over and over again. You don't have to be there. The book keeps doing the work.

Once this distinction really landed, the decision to stop feeding the things that reset and focus entirely on creating assets — things that compound — was obvious. That's when project slow marketing was born, with three rules: it can't require daily content, it needs to keep working months or years after setup, and it has to have a higher conversion rate than most social platforms.

## The Slowly Viral Bird's Eye View

The full system in one sentence: Pinterest drives traffic to your best evergreen content, which pre-qualifies visitors and sends them to your email list, where you consistently sell your signature offer.

Most of us are already doing something like this in concept. The shift is changing the traffic source to Pinterest — and that one change makes the whole thing compound instead of reset.

The marketing gold standard most of us actually want (even if we're framing it as "going viral") is three things:
- **Exposure that compounds** — getting in front of the right people, not just any people, more every day
- **Engagement with real ideas** — your best work getting attention, not throwaway content made to keep up
- **Predictable sales** — people coming in every day without you living on social media

The slowly viral system delivers all three. It just has a slower arc. And that slower arc is the whole point — because time is on your side on Pinterest. The work you do today keeps working for you day in and day out.

## The 7×10×4×5 Formula

This is the formula taught inside Back of House. The name is not catchy, but the math is.

**Step one: One evergreen page.**
Pick one page on your website you'd love to get viral-level traffic to. This is your bread and butter content — a blog post, podcast, YouTube video, or any page that answers your audience's biggest questions. The thing that, when someone finds it, makes them think: *she gets exactly what I'm dealing with.* Optimize this page for Pinterest search with keywords — in the title, the headings, the meta description, the image alt text.

**Step two: One opt-in on that page.**
Embed an opt-in on the page — a PDF, template, quiz, coupon, pop-up, whatever makes sense. This is the gatekeeper. It pre-qualifies people before they get on your list. If you're sending a lot of compounding traffic, you want that traffic to actually convert — so the opt-in needs to be genuinely relevant to the right person for your offer.

**Step three: Two keyword-optimized titles.**
Give the page an alternative title. You're not changing the page — you're just creating a second way to describe it, using different keywords. Example: *The Secret Branding Formula Wellness Coaches Swear By* and *How to Design a Nutrition Brand That Clients Trust Instantly.* Same page. Two titles.

**Step four: Five pin designs.**
Create five branded, easy-to-read pin templates in Canva. Don't skip the research step here — search your niche on Pinterest first and see what image styles actually perform. Different niches have different visual norms (recipe posts have a look, wellness tends toward infographics, design work shows mockups). Pick five templates, save them.

**Step five: Four copy variations.**
For each title, write four variations of the pin text. This means four different headline + description combinations. You can use AI to generate these from your page copy and keywords — the point isn't perfection, it's variation. Pinterest reads the metadata, so more variations = more ways for the algorithm to understand and surface your content.

Put it together:
- 2 titles × 5 pin designs = **10 unique images**
- 10 images × 4 copy variations = **40 unique pin combinations**
- 40 combinations × 5 boards = **200 unique pins in Pinterest's eyes**

That's the formula. One page. 200 open doors.

## The Math — One Page to 73K Visitors

Post one variation per day. Don't post the same URL to the same board twice in a row — rotate across your five boards so Pinterest reads each post as fresh content going somewhere new. Fresh pins don't have to be new content; a new image or a new description going to a new board counts as fresh.

200 pins, one per day = 200 days of scheduled content from one page. After that, those pins stay live and continue to surface in search results. 38% of viral pins are over two years old — they don't expire, they accumulate.

Now the math with conservative numbers:
- If each pin gets just **one click per day** (very conservative — some get zero, some get hundreds)
- By day 200, with all pins live: **200 clicks per day**
- Per month: **~6,000 visitors**
- Per year: **~73,000 visitors** to that one page

That's the slowly viral arc. Month one, you've shared 30 pins and have 30 open doors. The traffic is small. By month six, the doors are all open and the compounding kicks in. No spike that reverses — just a slow build that doesn't stop.

For those who've had something go viral: it gave you a spike. This gives you the same 73,000 people over time — but they're the right people, they found you through search intent, and they're still arriving two years later.

> "That's really like the idea of slowly viral."

When a pin is clearly a dud — zero clicks, low impressions — that's data. Don't replicate that design or that title in your next batch. The formula gets sharper every cycle.

## Scaling to 500K — Seven URLs

Everything above was for one page.

Do the same process for seven strategic evergreen pages on your site. These should cover different points in your audience's buyer journey — some for people brand new to the problem, some for people already researching solutions, some for people closer to booking. Different content pillars. Different entry points into your world.

Seven URLs × 200 pins each = **1,400 unique pins**
1,400 pins × one click per day = **~500,000 visitors per year**

Most of us already have seven pages sitting on our websites getting very few visitors. The formula doesn't require new content — it requires making the content you already have into 200 entry points each.

This is what Pinterest does that nothing else does: it turns your body of work into an asset that keeps compounding instead of expiring. The work you do once keeps paying you back.

## Pinterest + Email — The Full System

Pinterest has one job: send traffic. Everything after that happens in the back of house.

What makes this system convert so well is the combination of two things that, separately, are each the highest-performing in their category:

**Pinterest = highest buyer intent.** Most people come to Pinterest midway through a buying decision. They're already looking for solutions, not just scrolling for entertainment. This is fundamentally different from Instagram or TikTok where the intent is leisure. Pinterest users are like someone who walks into a store with a list.

**Email = highest conversion rate.** 78% of digital marketers say email is their highest ROI channel. A hundred email subscribers will outperform a thousand social followers almost every time. Email is where trust gets built and sales actually happen — not because of the size of your list, but because you have their attention in a private space and you can reach them again and again.

Put them together: Pinterest brings the right people (high intent) → bread and butter content answers their questions and pre-qualifies them → opt-in filters for the right ones → email sequence does the trust-building and selling.

The pre-qualification step is important. Your opt-in should be designed so that only the person who would actually want your offer wants the freebie. That filters the list before they're on it — and makes every email you send convert better because you're not talking to a general audience, you're talking to people already halfway there.

Once they're on the list: welcome sequence to move them from stranger to warm → regular simmer emails to keep them warm → right-time offers woven in naturally → active promos for cash injections when you want them.

Rather than a big viral spike that you can't sell to, you get a consistent-looking sales curve — sales every week from a list that grows every day without you having to think about it.

## Why Most People Don't Do It

None of this is secret information. The stats about Instagram's declining reach are public. The data on email conversion rates is documented. Pinterest's buyer intent is well-researched. So why isn't everyone doing it?

Because what's being described here is an infrastructure change.

This isn't a new posting strategy or a new content type — it changes what you actually do every day. And most entrepreneurs are already so busy they have no time to build the things that would make them less busy. We're too deep in cooking every meal from scratch every day to step back and build the kitchen that could cook for us.

Setting this system up requires time and thought: writing bread and butter content worth sending people to, building a keyword ecosystem, setting up an email sequence, designing an opt-in that actually pre-qualifies. It requires doing it *thoughtfully* — because if you build this infrastructure around keywords your audience isn't searching for, or around a sales page that doesn't convert, you've just built the same broken game on a new platform.

The work is real. But it's front-loaded. After the infrastructure exists, the daily marketing requirement goes to near zero — a batch session every six months, a weekly email, and the rest is the system running itself.

> "If you wanna get out of the kitchen, you have to take the time to build the back of house."

## What Slowly Viral Actually Gives You

When the system is working, you get all the things the viral dream promised — without the reset.

**Exposure that compounds.** Not a spike followed by silence, but a slow build of the right people finding your best work every single day, for years.

**Engagement with what actually matters.** Pinterest rewards content that answers questions — which means you're incentivized to make your four cornerstone pieces really good, not to churn out content to feed a beast.

**Predictable lead flow.** A consistent trickle of new subscribers every day. Not a flood after a viral moment that you can't convert, but a steady stream you have a whole system ready for.

**Mental freedom.** This is the part that's hardest to quantify but most worth naming. When your next client doesn't depend on your next post, social media becomes optional. The creative pressure lifts. Ironically — and this has been true — your best social content tends to come after you stop needing it to perform. When you're posting by choice instead of necessity, you take more creative risks. You try things. You have more fun.

The slowly viral process is investing vs. day-trading. Dollar-cost averaging vs. trying to time the market. Most people who try to time the market lose. Most people who invest consistently and let it compound win — and have way less stress along the way.

> "The work you do today keeps working for you day in and day out."
