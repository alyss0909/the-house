## Why Pinterest

Pinterest has been my number one organic traffic source for years and years. I originally started leveraging it because I had absolutely no money to spend on ads.

For so many of us, organic marketing seems like the only viable option right now — choosing between spending all your time and ideas on social, or spending cash on ads for a funnel you haven't validated. Pinterest doesn't make you choose.

Although the way we leverage it has changed, the underpinnings have always stayed the same: **Pinterest really allows you to create a library that continues to drive traffic for months and years on the same piece of content.** That evergreen nature is what brings me back time and time again.

Pinterest is a beast — billions of pins saved every single week. People aren't passively scrolling. They're actively searching for ideas, inspiration, and solutions to problems they already know they have.

**The numbers:** Tailwind analysis shows 38% of viral pins were over two years old, and 41% of viral pins that got new saves were over two years old. Pinterest doesn't just keep content alive — it still has a chance to go viral years later.

---

## How Pinterest Actually Works

Pinterest is the dream combo of Google's high-intent evergreen search and Instagram's enjoyable social discovery — without the bad parts of either.

Google has been taking your website information to build AI summaries, so people never have to visit your site even when they're getting your content. Pinterest still drives actual clicks.

**Our goal:** Take the bread-and-butter content you've already created — your offers, lead magnets, evergreen blog posts — optimize them for Pinterest's platform, and watch them get indexed and continue getting traffic indefinitely.

**How the algorithm reads your content:**

1. **Your profile** (name, bio, website, boards) → tells Pinterest who you are and who you serve
2. **Board categories** → tells Pinterest what category you fall into; example: if you're a nutritionist with boards about keto + kid-approved meals, Pinterest knows you serve parents who care about keto
3. **Pin image text overlay** → Pinterest reads the actual text on your image, not just the metadata
4. **The image itself** → Pinterest analyzes what's visually happening in the image (a bowl of lettuce = someone making salads; a garden bed of lettuce = someone growing their own food)
5. **All of the above combined** → plus where other people are pinning your content, and what board they're pinning it to

That's why being thoughtful with keywords and where we're using them matters so much. When you understand this, you get a much longer lifespan out of all your pins without needing to create new content.

## Account Setup

If you've never used Pinterest before, or your account is super old and you want a fresh start, that's fine. If you want to convert your existing account, that's fine too.

**Required steps:**
1. **Convert to a Business account** — go to YouTube, search "how to convert to a Pinterest business account," follow the most recent video
2. **Claim your website** — search "how to claim my [Wix/Squarespace/Showit/etc.] website on Pinterest" — this changes by platform, follow the most recent tutorial
3. **Turn on profile visibility** — in Settings > Search Privacy; if you don't do this, your pins will be private

These three things are literally the most important part. Everything else builds on them.

## Keyword Research: The Simple Approach

Don't get in the weeds with SEO. Think of keywords like this: they're just the things that tell Pinterest what your content is about, what your offers are about, and what type of person is likely to be interested in them.

After years of trying the most complicated, most expensive, and most detailed SEO research — the thing that has the biggest return on your time is truly the simplest. The Pinterest algorithm is really smart and uses keywords in a very nuanced way.

Don't say to me, "I don't think my person searches on Pinterest." Everybody searches at some point. And even if they don't search the exact terms you're thinking of, every search a user makes informs Pinterest about the types of problems they have. Every pin they save teaches Pinterest about what you do.

---

## Three Buckets of Keywords

**Problem-aware:** They know they have a problem but don't know how to solve it yet
> *"How do I be more productive?" / "How do I grow my email list?" / "How do I start growing my list with Pinterest?"*

**Solution-aware:** They know what type of solution exists and want to know more
> *"How to time block" / "Best Squarespace templates" / "Best notion templates for business owners"*

**Brand-aware:** They already know what solution and even what brand they want (put less emphasis here — doesn't always work for every niche)
> *"How to time block in Notion" / "When to apply Tretinoin" / "Review of The Ordinary retinol"*

**Skincare educator example:**
- Problem-aware: "skincare routine for anti-aging"
- Solution-aware: "best retinol aids for wrinkles" / "how to layer retinol with the rest of my skincare routine"
- Brand-aware: "when to apply Tretinoin"

Notice how each bucket uses different keywords. That's why using a mix of both seed and long-tail works.

---

## Seed Keywords vs. Long-Tail Phrases

**Seed keyword:** Core word that summarizes what you do — *productivity, notion, anti-aging*. Millions of people search this. Very competitive.

**Long-tail phrase:** A more specific phrase that includes your seed keyword
- Seed: productivity → Long-tail: "how to be more productive working from home"
- Seed: notion → Long-tail: "best notion templates for business owners"
- Seed: anti-aging → Long-tail: "best serums for wrinkles" / "anti-aging retinol"

Using a mix helps Pinterest understand: within productivity, are you for students or business owners? Are you productivity + notion, and is it for the 75 Hard group or the 75 Soft group? Pinterest starts to understand people on a very nuanced level.

**Rule of thumb per pillar:** Choose 2 seed keywords + a cluster of long-tail phrases for each.

## How to Find Your Keywords

**1. Pinterest search bar (most effective)**
Type a broad phrase related to what you do. Write down every autocomplete suggestion. Press enter, note every "more ideas like this" bubble — these are the most searched long-tail phrases for that seed keyword.

> *Typed "interior design" → autocompletes appeared → bubbles included: vintage, green, minimalist. Mix them: "minimalist interior design" / "80s interior design aesthetic" / "Scandinavian kitchen design mood board"*

**2. Related searches (scroll your feed)**
After you search, scroll and look for "related searches" bubbles — these show naturally appearing in the feed. These are a direct window into the mind of your Pinterest searcher. Note the natural language people are using.

> *Typed "notion template for business" → related: "business hub notion template" / "notion template dashboard" / "free notion templates" / "notion homepage ideas"*

"Notion homepage ideas" — I would never have thought of that as a keyword. Now it's one of mine.

**3. Pinterest Trends tool**
Use to understand when things are trending. Pins take time to index — if you want people to see your content in January, pin that content well before January. Use trends to see when searches peak for your topics throughout the year. (Notion template ideas peaked in September — start pinning that content well before September.)

**4. Pinterest Ads Manager (for search volume)**
Create a campaign → don't actually turn on an ad → use the keyword targeting section to type in your keywords. You get actual monthly search volume data — something you can't get anywhere else. This is a major reason to have a business account. Use this to find the sweet spot between high-search and less-saturated keywords.

> *"Interior design" → 5M monthly searches (too saturated). "Minimalist interior design" → sweet spot. "Minimalist living room" → 10K–100K searches, less competitive.*

**5. Competitor boards**
Search your keywords, see what's coming up on top. What are the top performing pins? What visuals, fonts, and keywords are they using? What content angles are already covered?

Look at competitors' board names and descriptions — what keywords are they probably using? What are their most popular boards?

> *Searched "Notion template business" → Julia Chick came up. She has "aesthetic notion templates" board + "free notion templates" board. Now I know "aesthetic" is a keyword I should use, and I should probably have a "free notion templates" board.*

**6. ChatGPT (for analysis, not keyword generation)**
Don't ask ChatGPT what keywords you should use — it doesn't have search volume or keyword data and will make things up. Use it for:
- Analyzing a batch of competitor board descriptions: "What keywords are they likely ranking for?"
- Taking a blog post and asking: "What keyword buckets does this content fall into, and what keywords should I look into for Pinterest?"

Then go verify those suggestions in the actual Pinterest search bar.

## Organizing Your Keywords

Once you have a big messy list of keywords, decide on:
- 2 seed keywords per content pillar
- A cluster of long-tail phrases for each seed

**Example (email marketing pillar):**
- Seed 1: email marketing → long-tails: email marketing strategy, how to grow your email list, automated email sequences
- Seed 2: email marketing design → long-tails: how to design your email to get more clicks, email template design, email marketing inspo

**Example (productivity pillar):**
- Seed 1: productivity → long-tails: how to be more productive working from home, how to stop procrastinating, why time blocking is the secret for ADHD entrepreneurs
- Seed 2: notion template → long-tails: how to create a to-do list in Notion, best notion planners for your iPad, how to be more productive with time management using Notion

Do the same for your 4 signature topics + your freebie, tripwire, and offer.

## Seven Places to Put Keywords

Once you know your keywords, we maximize visibility by putting them in all of these places:

1. **Profile name** (65 characters)
2. **Bio** (500 characters)
3. **Board titles**
4. **Board descriptions** (500 characters)
5. **Pin titles**
6. **Pin descriptions**
7. **Image overlay text**

**Bonus places:**
- Alt text on images
- File names

All of these are used by Pinterest to either categorize your content or create context around your keyword. We want Pinterest to know who you are, who you serve, how you serve them, and exactly what that person is probably struggling with now.

---

## Optimizing Your Profile Name and Bio

**Profile name (65 characters):**
Most people just put their name. But you can fit your name + a couple of identifying keywords. Example: *Alyssa Coleman | Productivity for Entrepreneurs*

People whose actual profile name says "Notion" in it come up in the search bar — Pinterest indexes it.

Make it about what the account does, not your job title. "Atlanta interior designer | home decor ideas" vs. just "Atlanta Interior Designer."

**Bio (500 characters):**
Pack in as many keywords as you can in natural language. Pinterest favors accounts that use natural language — don't just list keywords, and don't use hashtags.

Try to use all 500 characters. Get in at least 5 long-tail keywords.

**Formula:** *Helping [target audience] achieve [goal] | [keywords] | [personality/vibe] | [CTA]*

Include:
- Who you serve
- How you solve their problems
- At least 5 long-tail keywords naturally
- Something about your personality
- A soft call to action

## Boards: Setup and Descriptions

**Five boards:**
- 4 boards, one for each content pillar
- 1 board for your freebie, tripwire, and offer (choose keywords that encompass all your offers)

**Board design rule:** Make sure any of your four blog posts could technically work on any of your other boards. The way the system works: you want to pin all your content to all your boards — because every time you post a pin to a new board, it's seen as a fresh pin. So your boards need to be specific enough to align with your pillar, but broad enough that an email marketing post could make sense on a productivity board.

**Choosing your keywords per board:**
Look at your keyword spreadsheet, choose the strongest seed + long-tail keywords for each pillar, and use those to choose board titles and descriptions.

**Board description formula:**
1. Hook — gets them interested
2. Solve the search intent — "You're going to be so much more productive with these specific templates made to stop procrastination and get more done as a work-from-home entrepreneur"
3. What's inside this board — pack in the keywords here: "Inside you'll find tips about [X, Y, Z]"
4. What it solves — benefits: "You'll stop procrastinating, you'll be less overwhelmed, you'll streamline your workflows"
5. CTA — "Click follow this board if you want [benefit]"

Use all 500 characters. Aim for 5 long-tail keywords per description. The board description template:
> *Need [solution or idea]? This board is full of [keywords and phrases] to help you [goal]. From [content type] to [content type], you'll find all the inspiration here. [CTA]*

**Board covers:** Make them cute, branded, and searchable. A good board cover often gets more pin saves and engagement than a lot of the actual pins. Overlay text with your keyword on it. Pinterest reads that overlay text too.

---

## A Note on Group Boards

Group boards used to be my secret sauce in 2018 — how I grew most of my email list. The Pinterest algorithm has changed, and now Pinterest favors fresh pins and actually suppresses over-repinned group boards.

Some clients still get results from group boards, but they have to be really selective:
- Join boards where participants are actively engaged
- Look for pins that regularly get 10+ saves each as a minimum
- Contribute only content that truly matches the board's focus
- Or: create your own super curated group board with 3–4 aligned, trusted people who share an audience

If I'm working with a new client, I just skip group boards. If you still have some you love, assess them with these criteria.

## Optimizing Your Seven URLs

Pinterest doesn't just look at your pin — it actually goes to the link you're sending people to and uses that information for context. This is often missed.

While you're in keyword mode, go back to your 4 cornerstone blog posts + your freebie opt-in page, tripwire page, and sales page.

**Blog posts:**
- Add vertical (pinnable) images
- Put keywords in the alt text of images; save image files with keywords in the file name
- Optimize blog post titles for search (solution-focused, under 60 chars)
- Use H2s with supporting keywords throughout
- Optimize meta description with primary keyword at the beginning
- Customize the URL slug: `yourdomain.com/email-funnel-tips`

**Opt-in and tripwire pages:**
- Keyword in the title of the page
- Keyword-rich headline
- Keyword in the URL slug
- Optimized meta description
- Vertical, pinnable mockup image with keyword in file name and alt text

**Sales page:**
- Clear, keyword-rich headline with benefit keywords and audience keywords
- Meta title: offer type + niche keywords (e.g., "notion template course for business coaches")
- Same URL and meta description optimization as above

*This section is somewhat optional — if you're in the weeds, skip it and come back after you've done the account setup. But once you know your keywords, this becomes a super quick job.*

## Setup Recap

1. Create your business account and claim your website
2. Find your go-to keywords (search bar → autocomplete → related searches → trends → ad account search volumes → competitor audit)
3. Optimize your account for those keywords (name, bio, board titles, board descriptions, board covers)
4. Optimize all seven of your URLs for those keywords
5. You've now set up the most insane foundation for batching and scheduling
