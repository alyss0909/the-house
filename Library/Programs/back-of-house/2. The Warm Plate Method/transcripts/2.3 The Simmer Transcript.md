## Why the Simmer Exists

Now that people have gone through your freebie, maybe taken you up on your tripwire, gone through your sequence — they're in your community. And if they aren't working with you, they're members of your little corner of the internet.

It's so important to really foster that community and keep them hot, warm, and simmering.

This is the anchor of an email-first business model. We have to do this in order to keep our community warm. We really want to be providing actual true nourishment and takeaways and value that gets them to trust you, but also gets them to see you as an authority — and that allows you to make sales between promotions.

This is why it's so important to have that always-open revolving door business model. You don't have to put so much pressure on your promotional periods. You don't have to rely as much on social because you know your emails are taken care of.

The only non-automated thing we do every single week — and it's also the most important. Everything else in your back of house should run itself. This is the thing you actually show up for, and when you do, your audience reciprocates by paying attention, trusting you, seeing you as the authority — and making purchases.

If the only thing you have to do to keep your business warm is send one email a week, that's a pretty low-maintenance, high-return setup.

---

## Frequency

I'm calling these weekly simmer emails and I do recommend at least one a week. But what's more important is choosing a sustainable rhythm that actually works for you and that your audience can count on — weekly, every other week, monthly. Having some kind of consistency is what matters.

Right now, weekly or twice a week is the norm. Some big names in email marketing are now recommending every single day. What I prefer is to send one more thought-out email and one a little more chill email — but one a week is honestly more than enough.

**Why consistency matters:**
- Stay present in their inbox (it's full)
- Stay top of mind between launches
- Get them to know your philosophy in their bones — because you've come at it from so many different angles, from different stories, by sharing different takeaways related to the same philosophy

---

## What Simmer Emails Are — and Aren't

**These are not:**
- Sales emails at any point
- A place to explain your philosophy and method in detail
- Something to be perfectionist about

**These are:**
- A cozy little corner of the internet — treat it that way
- A little love letter every week: what does my audience need to hear from me today?
- A reminder to your community that you're still here and this thing is still right for them
- Freedom from graphics, hashtags, worrying about what's trending

I would rather you send one crappy email than no email that week. Get it out the door.

## The Four Ingredients of a Great Simmer Strategy

1. **Flavor** — What is your signature style? What do people know your emails for?
2. **Story** — The most low-lift way to consistently send emails
3. **Value** — Always add value, keep them coming back for more
4. **Invite** — The actual soft sales, in practice

---

## Step 1: Find Your Flavor

Before you write anything, you need to know what your emails are going to taste like.

Somebody's newsletter that you absolutely love to open every single week has its own signature flavor profile. Really figuring out the tone, cadence, and point of view of your emails. How does your audience feel when they actually open them?

**Questions to answer for your flavor profile:**
- After someone reads your email, how do they feel? Like they just had a deep conversation? Silly?
- What do they actually leave with? Mindset shift? Practical, actionable item? A story?
- What do they look like? Super skimmable? Messy? Short and drafted on the stair machine?
- What's your writing style? Ranty? Deeply researched essays? Quick ideas?
- What's your workflow? When are you writing it? Draft it in your calendar same as your audience expects it.

When your flavor is truly dialed in, your emails become easier to write. You have a go-to process. Ideas that come to you throughout the week — you see where they fit. People start craving your email and recommending it because it has a distinct tone.

## Newsletter Naming and Concepts

I used to hate the word newsletter, but newsletters are having a comeback. Branded names for your emails really do instantly have a little credibility to them.

**Formula:** community + gap + vibe

**Examples:**
- *Nest Notes* — Easy, dreamy ideas to make your space feel like home (interior designer)
- *Room Service* — Interior ideas delivered straight to your mental mood board
- *Draft Punk* — Helping writers turn their messy ideas into something publishable
- *The Sketchpad* — Real diaries from a millennial manifesting art and making bank
- *The Second Brain Club* — Actual Notion tips for people with big dreams and messy tabs

Decide on the concept first, then come up with the name. If you're getting hung up on this, keep moving and come back to it later. It's not required — but it's worth doing.

## Step 2: Tell a Story

The easiest way to consistently get out these simmer emails is to just tell stories. We're hardwired to take in stories, learn from stories, and remember them better. Stories let your people in. You get to teach without preaching every single week.

It's one of the most sustainable ways to create week after week with very little effort from you. Even if your life is boring, you have stories. The best stories are actually the most boring ones. The ones that we think are the most boring always end up being the most relatable, and people reply to them.

The best emails start like: *"This is random, but..."*

These are the things you text your friend. The voice memos you contemplated deleting. The little things you picked up on while in line at the grocery store. Something you overheard, something your kids said, something from a podcast or what you're reading.

Getting good at noticing where these stories are hidden is a big part of this job. To write emails people actually want to read — ones that feel like they came from you — you just have to figure out how to find these little moments and then attach meaning to them.

---

## Where to Find Stories

**You've changed:** Something you shifted lately, intentionally or not. A new habit, something you stopped saying, a mindset shift. Shows how you evolve and walk your talk.
> *Example: I signed up for pottery lessons to remind myself what it's like to suck at something and not try to monetize it.*

**The ick:** What's annoying you right now? Great for poking fun at what's broken in your industry or what you disagree with. Good for positioning you as a certain type of person.
> *Example: It's always the same kind of person who tells me how I should run my business. / Please retire the word "value." We get it. You're helpful.*

**Borrowed stories:** Not all stories have to be about you. Client stories, something a friend said, a TikTok you saved and sent to everyone, a Kardashian headline. These prove your point with examples people might already know.
> *Example: The Kardashians taught me more about self-promotion than I'd like to admit.*

**Starter prompts to fill your story stash:**
- The weirdest thing I saw this week was...
- I once made a business decision based on how hungry I was...
- I accidentally did X in front of a client...
- The most unhinged DM/email/question I ever received was...
- I knew I had to change this when it made me cry on a Tuesday
- This made me rage-clean my entire kitchen

If it feels a little bit unhinged, if it feels like you might get an unsubscribe from sending it — then it's probably perfect.

---

## From Story to Point

Once you've shared the story, make your point seamlessly. Don't make it feel like "and the moral of the story is..." — you want this to pivot naturally as part of the story.

Ask yourself: why am I telling them this? If I zoom out, how can I make this relatable for everyone? How does the lesson of this story apply to my niche?

You can also work backwards: decide what point you want to make (related to your philosophy), then find a story that tees it up.

The Sarah Jessica Parker moment: "...and it got me thinking about how..."

**Story arc recap:**
1. What happened
2. What's the idea or lesson that came out of it
3. One piece of value you can actually give people from this

---

## Step 3: Value Drop

After the story and point, every email needs to end with a takeaway. The story gets them to feel something, but the value drop grounds that feeling with something they can actually use or remember.

This can be as simple as:
- A quick tip
- A mindset shift
- A template you want to share
- A little checklist
- A pep talk / nudge in the right direction

This is the section that creates the habit of expecting something actually useful from you.

---

## Step 4: The Right-Time Offer

Here's the thing: there's a small portion of your audience that is ready to buy your offer right now. They don't want to wait for your next promo. They might not even want to join during a launch because they don't want to go to the webinar.

They just read your story, they're compelled, and they want to buy now.

The right-time offer provides you with this really natural opportunity to invite them one step deeper. Meeting your community where they are and *when* they are ready — instead of getting them to match up with your promo schedule.

**After the value drop, add a short sentence:**
> *"That's exactly why I created [offer]. Right now, anyone who joins gets access to [specific thing] totally for free before we start selling it to the rest of our audience. If you want the details, just comment 'simmer' and I'll send everything over to you."*

What's incredible about right-time offers:
- Almost no work from you — you don't have to make anything new, write a sales email, or add sales bullets
- Almost no work from your audience — they don't have to read all your emails, check out a sales page, or make any decisions. All they have to do is reply with one word.
- Finds the people who are ready now, while sending the newsletter you were already going to send

**Don't put this in every single email.** Sign-off specials can go in every email. A right-time offer with a magic word — sprinkle those in.

---

## Building Your Right-Time Offer

**The pivot:** Lead into your right-time offer from the story and value drop. Example:

> *One of my clients recently did that three-step hidden asset vault, and she had this mini course that had been sitting in her Notion for a year. It was beautiful when we opened it up, but there were absolutely zero sales. What we did is we added one segmented welcome email and gave it a little soft launch to her list — and before she even created her onboarding assets, she had five enrollments before the weekend. This is exactly the type of thing we're testing in this round of [offer]. It's messy, it's in progress, but it's high-reward work. If you want in — reply **info** and I'll send you the scoop.*

**Five steps in every right-time offer:**
1. **Clarify the offer** — I'm walking a small group through beta versions of their quiet funnel setup this month
2. **Tie it back to the story** — Her mini course had been sitting in Notion for a year, just like the random workbook I just found on my desktop
3. **Highlight one specific feature** — We're testing CTAs, refreshing lead magnets, building a repeatable rhythm
4. **Give a reason to join now** — Beta access, limited spots, collective energy
5. **Give them the magic word** — Reply **info** and I'll send you everything

**Rule:** No logistics in the right-time offer email. Save that for after they reply. This is not that. They reply a specific word, and you follow up with details, a loom, a Google Doc, a checkout link — whatever the next step is.

## Feature Highlights (7 Ideas)

1. **Cool template or swipe file** — something tangible inside your program
2. **New thing** — a new resource you just added; lets you be timely
3. **The quickie** — a mini transformation they could get right now ("I'll help you refine your offer angle, write a scrappy sales page, and launch it in a single weekend")
4. **The setup** — a specific thing you help them set up
5. **Tangible deliverable** — "You'll walk away with three branded templates, a mini color system, and..."
6. **The experiment** — what you actually test, workshop, or create together with clients
7. **AB test** — show what you test or compare for them

---

## Reasons to Join Now (12 Ideas)

1. Special price / coupon if you join before [date]
2. Secret bonus for joining this week
3. Limited spots (works especially for 1:1 elements)
4. Seasonal energy — "Get your family in routine before back to school season" / "Have your promo ready before Black Friday" / "Tax season is coming..."
5. Upcoming event — guest speaker, live session; "Join before Tuesday to get your [thing] reviewed"
6. Existing bonus being highlighted for a limited time
7. Collective energy — "Everyone inside right now is working on [X] together"
8. Beta / first access — "This is the round where we're testing..."
9. Price will increase
10. Disappearing bonus
11. Limited live spots filling up
12. Seasonal or cultural moment — New Year, halfway through the year, Q4 push

---

## The Magic Word and Follow-Up

Keep your magic word very specific — so you know which right-time offers are in your sequence vs. which are live.

**When they reply:**
- Keep it casual, natural, human — don't try to automate this
- "Hey, I love that you're curious. Here's a bit of a rundown about the offer. You get [X]. We have [X] seats left and I'm doing [X] next week. Here's the link to find out more..."
- Send a loom, a simple Google Doc, your checkout link — the more personal, the better
- Don't overwhelm them with information — give them a reason to join now and ask if they have any questions

**Follow-up 1–2 days later if no response:**
> "Have you had a chance to check this out?"
> "Let me know what questions you have." *(Not "do you have questions?" — what questions do you have?)*
> "Hey, I just wanted to remind you that coupon is expiring at midnight..."
> "I've got one bonus left — I just wanted to check in because I know you were interested..."

Keep it super human and casual.

## Putting It All Together: The Anatomy

**Graphic designer example:**
1. **Story:** I tried to organize all of my client's files by color palette and it was a disaster
2. **Point:** Over-organizing can kill creativity; chaos is sometimes what makes the best designs
3. **Value drop:** Three-step file sorting system that's loose but actually keeps the chaos away
4. **Right-time offer (optional):** "I teach this inside my branding intensive. If you want the sorting system, reply 'files' and I'll send you the details."

**Nutritionist example:**
1. **Story:** A client texted me in a panic about a snack emergency at a birthday party — surrounded by cake and candy
2. **Point:** Willpower doesn't win in high-pressure moments; preparation does
3. **Value drop:** Here's how I build snack safety nets into client routines
4. **Right-time offer (optional):** "This is something we build into your daily routine inside Nourish. If you want to see how it works, reply 'flow' and I'll send you a behind-the-scenes look."

---

**After writing, do a quick pass:**
- Did I actually hook them in the first line? Can I make it more hoish?
- Could I add a segmentation moment (sweet segment)?
- Can I think of ways to add more clicks throughout?
- Am I using a PS?
- Did I add all email elements from my checklist?

Then schedule it. You're done.

---

## Action Steps

1. **Flavor profile** — fill it all in; know how you're going to email people and do it consistently
2. **Story stash** — go through every single prompt and brain dump all your past stories into your story vault. If you have the idea AND a hint of what point you want to make with it, future you will thank you enormously.
3. **Right-time offers** — write out your feature highlights, write out your reasons to join now
4. **Simmer planner** — start mapping email ideas, link up subject lines, decide on CTAs

**Most important:** Nail down when you're going to be writing your emails, when you're going to be sending them, and what the style of that newsletter is. This consistency is what fuels your entire back of house.
