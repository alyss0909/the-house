## Why Email Has — and Hasn't — Changed

Email has changed — but not in the way everyone thinks. Yes, open rates are down. Yes, it's harder to get people to click. But people aren't spending less time in their inboxes. In the last five years, on average, people are spending *more* time in their inboxes, checking them more times per day.

People aren't ignoring their inboxes — they're just getting better at ignoring emails that aren't worth their time. Service providers like Gmail and Outlook are making changes to reflect that. They want proof that people are actually into what you're sending them. Their job is to keep people in their inboxes for as long as possible. And that's ours too.

88% of people are checking their inbox daily. Nearly half are actively looking to buy. 78% of marketers report email is their highest overall converting channel.

What a lot of bro-marketers are doing is doubling down — emailing every single day, adding random code to keep emails out of the promotions tab. That's not going to help. Gmail and readers are only asking one question: **is this actually interesting and does it belong in my inbox?**

## Three Core Goals: Curiosity, Attention, Engagement

The stuff that actually works hasn't changed since the Mad Men era. These are marketing and copywriting strategies from books written 50 years ago. They keep working because they're not based on what buyers want *right now* — they're based on what humans always respond to.

**You need people to be:**
1. **Curious** — if they don't open, nothing else matters
2. **Interested** — once they open, we need them to keep reading
3. **Engaged** — clicks are the love language of email marketing

When these frameworks become second nature, you can ask yourself: "okay, this is the trend right now — how do I apply that to one of these frameworks?" And you don't have to change your strategy at all. That's low maintenance. That's what all of Back of House is built on.

## Anatomy of a Near-Perfect Email

If I could only have one perfect email, it would follow this framework:

1. **Subject line** — double curiosity (see below)
2. **Preview text** — teases the audience, adds a third layer of curiosity
3. **Hook** — first sentence creates an open loop or emotional pull
4. **Meat and potatoes** — the main content; includes multiple CTAs, segmentation, philosophy breadcrumbs, visual elements, CTA button
5. **PS** — for people who skimmed to the bottom; reiterates the CTA
6. **Sign-off specials** — your always-on offer links
7. **Short bio** — never assume they remember who you are

## Subject Lines: Double Curiosity

The biggest mistake I see: people use subject lines as a summary of what's inside the email. This is not a table of contents. This is the cover of a magazine that really gets someone to open it to see what's inside.

**Wrong:** "5 email tips for coaches" (I already know what's in there)
**Right:** "The secret $3 ingredient that doctors say has J-Lo looking 10 years younger" (Now I need to know)

If there was also subtext: "and why you've probably walked past it every single time you went to the pharmacy" — that's double curiosity.

**Double curiosity framework:** Make them curious about *what* + curious about *why* or *how*. When you follow this, you will write good subject lines.

**Quick wins:**
- "Download Inside" or "Gift for [Name]" → higher open rate
- Emojis can work but can be overdone — think: does this still feel like a message from a friend?
- AB test purposefully — one variable at a time
- Look at your copy croissant vault for subject lines that caught your eye; write in that style

**Subject line styles (from swipe vault):**
- The Spy / Spirally
- The Open Loop
- Super relatable
- Hot take / pokes at them
- The Rags to Riches (something great was happening and then I lost everything)
- Data slap (people love numbers)
- Impending doom (if you don't do this...)

## Preview Text

This is a continuation of the subject line — how you get triple curiosity. Keep it short and sweet because it gets cut off. Remember they likely won't remember you, so don't assume they know you're a nutritionist or that your thing is about PCOS.

Preview text tips:
- Short — less gets cut off
- Bring back who you are and what they opted into
- This is where you can get weird and test different things
- Never skip it — it's a major missed opportunity

Examples that work:
- Short and sweet, shocking ("This chick is shocking, not sarcasm")
- Continuation of the conversation ("You thought I was gone for good, didn't you?")
- Personal style ("Can I call you this week?")

## The Hook

They've opened the email. Now you have about three seconds before they click out. You have to hook them in that first sentence.

If the subject line is opening the door, the hook is freshly baked cookies they can smell — compelling them to go inside. They cannot help but start to scroll and want to click.

**Rules:**
- Keep the first part short and sweet — don't hit them with a novel right off the bat
- If you open a loop in the hook or subject line, make sure you come back to close it in the email (often in the PS)
- One thing that works: open the loop in the subject line, bring it back in the PS

**Hook templates from the vault:**
- **Open loop energy:** "Great branding won't save you from a broken client experience" → Template: *This nice thing won't save you from the reality of this ugly truth*
- **Weird connection:** "What ghosting my first therapist taught me about onboarding dream clients" — breaks the brain, makes you curious
- "What waitressing taught me about running a business" — weird connections always work

## Meat and Potatoes

This is where you deliver value, give aha moments, and create that relationship with your community.

**Format rules:**
- Start with the hook as your taster line
- No textbooks — short paragraphs
- Length varies; shorter tends to perform better but match your tone
- Know your flavor profile — what do people come to know your emails for? A mindset shift? A practical tip? A story? When people know what to expect, it removes the barrier.
- Always have a good **headline stack** — if someone just skimmed your email, would they understand what it was about just by reading the bold headers? This is your most important check before sending.
- Simple design has higher deliverability — too many images and buttons looks like spam
- In every single email, come back to your philosophy

**Visual elements that work:** GIFs, memes, screenshots, a handwritten note photo from you — don't overdo it, but "pretty emails get more opens."

## Click Tricks and Clickbaits

An open and read is nice. **Clicks actually pay the bills.**

These are small layout tricks or link tagging that can gently get someone out of skim mode and into click mode. But more importantly — clicks in your sequence are habit forming. You are training people that when they see your name in their inbox, they open it and they click it, and on the other side of the click is something good. That proof of concept will change the trajectory of your email marketing.

> **Bait and gift, not bait and switch.** Whatever happens after they click should be surprising and delighting.

**The CTA Trio:** Every email needs a call to action mentioned at least three times:
1. One above the fold (with hyperlinked juicy text, not "click here")
2. One somewhere in the body (often as a button)
3. One towards the end (often in the PS)

**Hyperlink tip:** Instead of hyperlinking "click here," hyperlink the most interesting or memorable part of your sentence.
- Weak: "I used this exact planner. Click here to see it."
- Strong: Hyperlink the phrase **"this exact planner"** directly
- When someone is skimming, those hyperlinks become a highlighter over the parts you want them to see

**Click bait vault styles:**
- Juicy hyperlinks (above)
- Interest-based redirects to other content you've made
- Surprise and delight gifts (no opt-in required)
- Segmentation clicks (sweet segments — see below)

## Sweet Segmentation

Everyone says you should be segmenting more. But complicated tagging and branching automations? We've tested them and the conversion rate didn't go up much. Not worth how much work we were doing.

**Sweet segments** are tiny personalized moments designed to make automated emails feel more human. Rules for any sweet segment:

1. **Low maintenance** — set it up once, it's done
2. **Feels human** — something you'd text a friend; fun, playful
3. **Sparks joy** — creates a LOL moment, surprise, or real connection
4. **Purposeful** — helpful for them and useful for you

**Sweet segment examples:**

**Lifecycle segment** (put early in the sequence):
> Where are you at in your journey?
> — I have no email list and want to grow one
> — I have a list but I'm not consistent at sending
> — I have a big list but it doesn't convert anymore
Based on what they click, send a stage-specific resource. They get something tailored; you know who's on your list.

**On-the-fence segment** (during a promo):
> Button in the email: "I'm on the fence"
Triggers a prerecorded video from you — behind-the-scenes of the course, coaching them through the hesitation. Feels personal without being one-to-one.

**Vibe picker** (in an early email):
> Pick your vibe: lo-fi deep work / hustle mode / cozy focus
Based on what they pick, send them your go-to Spotify playlist for that mood. Burned-the-CD-for-you energy. Surprise and delight.

**Fitness coach example:** "What's going on with you this week?"
— Gaining momentum → she hypes them up
— Not feeling it → "I know this feeling, but I believe in you, what's ONE small step you can take right now? Just get your workout pants on."
People reply to these emails like crazy.

**Looked-but-didn't-book:** If someone clicks to book a call but doesn't schedule, follow up 24 hours later: "I saw that you looked but didn't book, and I wanted to share a little bit about what these calls are about..."

**Low-ticket in-sequence offer:** Offer something for $1–$9 with a coupon in your welcome sequence. The sooner you can make a sale, the better. Gets that second "yes" even if they didn't take the tripwire. Creates a high-value picture of you in their mind.

## The PS

Never, ever skip a PS. Most people will skim straight to the bottom. The PS is one of the most powerful sale-making parts of your email.

**PS frameworks (from vault):**

- **Attention grabber + outcome tease:** "PS I almost didn't share this story because it's so embarrassing and if my mom found out she would disown me, but I had to because it could save you thousands of dollars."
- **The wild card** — unexpected twist that makes them want to click
- **Plot twist** — reframes something from earlier in the email
- **Last call** — "PS only two spots left if you want 70% off..."
- **Identity PS** — "PS you're not here to blend in. The accelerator was built for people who lead with boldness, not beige." (Ties into their identity)
- **Cred PS** — establishes authority one more time
- **CTA + identity + urgency combo:** "PS do you know that this course comes with [thing], and I handpick [it] because extraordinary doesn't come from copying someone else's homework. Go book your one-on-one call before all the spots run out."

When you understand these frameworks, you don't need the examples — you can write any PS from any framework. But the vault has hundreds.

## Sign-Off Specials

At all times, always have your sign-off specials in your emails. You've already made yours in the pre-work — now update it to include your offer, freebie, and current price points.

Put your sign-off special right there, save it, and copy-paste it into every single email. Or save it as a snippet if you're using Kit (or whatever your platform calls it). Sign-off specials are *extra* — they are not your main CTA. Never use your sign-off specials as your only call to action.

## Deliverability Basics

If you're sending good emails with some value and only to people who asked to get them, you're going to be okay. A few things that are actually useful:

**Get whitelisted:** Put in one of your first emails — "Hey, go ahead and move me into your primary tab." Drag them over into primary in their inbox.

**Avoid spam signals:** Too many links, too many images. A sneaky one: lots of Bitly/redirect links. Providers find that sketchy.

**Clean your list:** Bounces will ruin your reputation. If people aren't opening your emails or emails have wrong domains → red flag. Have a re-engagement campaign and clean your list regularly.

**Most important:** Opened, clicked, and replied-to emails get delivered and prioritized. That's what you're optimizing for.

## Upgrading Your Delivery Email

Your delivery email is your highest open rate email by far — they just opted in, they want this thing. It is a really big opportunity. Don't just say "here's your freebie, look out for more."

**Delivery email goals:**
- Deliver the thing
- Let them know what to expect
- Get into the primary inbox (ask them to move you)
- Try to get a reply
- Surprise and delight

**Half-Baked example (near-perfect delivery email):**
- Remind them who you are (logo, brand)
- Tell them what to expect (we send emails Monday–Friday every single day)
- Deliver the freebie link
- Add surprise: "Your 5 free ideas AND a surprise" → a low-ticket tripwire for the pitch deck database
- Whitelisting ask: "Step one: move us to primary. Step two: click the button below and it will automatically populate a pre-filled email."
- The pre-filled email said: "Hey, I'm human I swear. Can I please get those pitch decks that raised billions?"
- Immediate automation reply: "You're a legend. Here's those pitch decks. Our only ask — if you strike it big, you owe us a beer."
- End with an authority-building action plan: here's how to make the most of what you just downloaded

The most important things they wanted the reader to do were above the fold.

---

## Frequency

Frequency is dependent on your community and how they engage with their inbox. No one-size-fits-all answer. My recommendation if you have no idea:

- Start more frequently at first — delivery email immediately, oops email one day later, then every day for the first couple, then every other day, then every three days
- Don't make a super long sequence — if someone downloads your freebie and you're getting into prescription emails two weeks later, they've totally forgotten who you are
- Don't worry about burning out your list — truly not a concern in your welcome sequence
- Get feedback from your audience using polls; find out how often they like receiving emails
