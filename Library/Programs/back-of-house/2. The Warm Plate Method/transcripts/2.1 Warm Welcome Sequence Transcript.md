## Introduction

You've done most of the hard work at this point. You've answered so many questions that clarify what your offer is, who it's for, how disruptive it is, and really audited and prepared your business model to be ready for an email first strategy. It's about time we actually get into some of the emails.

---

## Why Most Welcome Sequences Fail

Most welcome sequences suck. Most people frame this as something that's really about them and their business — even really about their offers — when every part of this model is about your buyer.

What I've noticed as I've audited thousands of emails: the way people talk at events or in person is so starkly different from the way they write in their emails and on their sales pages. It creates this really strange disconnect between what they actually believe, who they are, how they speak to people, and what their business is projecting onto their dream buyers.

One of the quickest ways to get yourself in the right headspace for writing these sequences is to think about how you talk to people when you're in the room with them. The whole point of what I've created in this sequence is not to teach you anything other than how to take what you naturally do in normal everyday conversation and apply that to an actual email sequence.

A well-executed sequence truly becomes your best salesperson — cloning yourself so you are out there doing the work and converting people at all times of day.

---

## The Documentary Metaphor

This creates a documentary moment — you know, when you're scrolling through Netflix, click on a random documentary called like *The Happiness Project*, and by the end of 90 minutes they've convinced you that the key to true lasting happiness is actually minimalism. You ordered organizers, got garbage bags ready, and you're pitching 90% of your stuff. You came in wanting to feel happier and you've left with a completely different viewpoint on happiness.

That's the goal of this email sequence. We want to make a huge impact at this point. The impact sequence is designed to give your audience that aha moment over and over again.

If you get this wrong, people will not end up on your sales page. They're not going to get there if you don't get this part right.

Partway through that documentary, people don't only realize that the real problem is not their happiness — the real problem is overwhelm. Once they realize the real problem is overwhelm, they begin automatically looking for a solution. And that's why this part is so key.

If the documentary started with minimalism right away, you might turn it off because you don't care about minimalism. It's not until you truly have that shift — until they make them believe the diagnosis that is your philosophy — that they're ready to invest in your solution.

---

## Three-Part Structure: Symptoms, Diagnosis, Prescription

One part documentary and one part doctor. The perfect sequence has three main parts:

1. **Symptoms** — Really dig into what they're experiencing right now
2. **Diagnosis** — Get them to believe the diagnosis
3. **Prescription** — Here's what I think you should do

In the beginning of the documentary: here's what you're experiencing. By the middle: here's the real problem. It's not that you're unhappy because you're depressed. The real problem is that you're so overwhelmed that you never want to spend any time in your house. And you're constantly trying to escape. Part three is the prescription.

If I went to the doctor and they immediately heard my symptoms and said, "okay, you have a headache, here take this" — I would be like, hang on. But if they start asking: does it hurt over here? Does it get worse when you're working? Does it hurt when I do this? And you're like, "oh my God, yeah, I had no idea that's related" — then I'm invested. I'm like, this person gets it. And when they say "here's what we're going to do," I'm writing it down.

**Recommended minimum:** 2 symptoms emails, 2 diagnosis emails, 2 prescription emails. Start with 6. If beefing it up, add more to diagnosis — that's where you can really put a couple extra emails.

Think about what your audience needs, how often they're likely to open emails, how quick they are to make decisions.

## Writing the Symptoms Emails

At this point your goal is to paint the picture of their problem. Highlight the symptoms they're already experiencing, and introduce some they might not have considered yet.

Like when you're Googling your symptoms and you start to see: "do you have headaches and are you peeing a lot?" You're like, what? I thought it was just because I was getting older. And then they say, "in that case, you might be pregnant." Or: "do you have headaches and a ringing in your ears? In that case, you might want to look into this." We want to introduce those odd connections between things going on in their life and start to really draw their attention to them while solving their problems.

**On awareness levels:** Take into consideration where your reader might be on the scale of awareness. Think about what your freebie solved and whether they're aware of the problems related to your real problem, or are they just experiencing the pain, or are they so aware of the pain and the real problem and the solution that they just don't know which product they want? Try to hit on all these stages in most of your emails because most people do not just open email one, then two, then three in order. People are jumping around.

**On sharing your story:** Share your story, but remember — just like the sales page, the about you is really not about you at all. This is not your resume. This is something short and sweet woven in as you're sharing parts of the story that are relevant to them. Only share the parts of your story that are relevant to the conversation you're having. Be slowly interjecting your personality, your credibility — little hints at how you experienced this throughout each email.

One part personality and one part credibility. If your dream client doesn't see themselves in sentence one, they're not going to read sentence two.

---

## Writing the Diagnosis Emails — The Most Important Part

This is the most important section of all of these emails. Take your time here. **They will not buy if they don't believe the diagnosis.**

We have to make sure they believe the diagnosis. Give them aha moments and get them to see your solution as the only solution. Spend a long time here — make sure each email is actually giving them quick wins, because the more quick wins you can give them, the more likely they are to open the next emails.

Think of this like the osteopath. You go in with knee pain, and they do a couple of adjustments. They get you to try to touch your toes again, and this time it's effortless. Now you're like, what? I'm listening. What do I need to do? Because clearly you know what you're doing.

**Every single email should follow a four-part process:**

1. Create a belief shift
2. Diagnose and get them to buy into the diagnosis
3. Give them a win while teasing the solution — show a little bit of that bridge, paint the picture of what outcomes are when they follow that solution
4. Every single email has to follow these four steps

## A Real Diagnosis Email Example

Here's one of my top performing emails — maybe my top performing email of all my sequences ever.

**Subject line:** How to stop being so damn busy
*(Calls out a symptom, suggests they'll learn how to solve it)*

**Opening:** Tell me if this rings a bell. When your friends ask how you've been, you pretty much always answer, "oh my God, I've been so busy."

And that's not a lie. You've been spending all your time on this passion project. Your business is your baby. There's this nagging feeling you're spending all this time at your desk but not actually getting anywhere.

**Personal share:** I was so busy all day long. I was working nights, working weekends, I ate lunch at my desk. And the ate lunch at my desk thing is something I know from discovery calls — I ask people about their day, what time they take lunch, these probing questions about their calendar. And almost always people say, "what's lunch?"

The cold hard truth is I wasn't being productive at all. *(Belief shift — it's not that they're busy, it's that they're not being productive.)*

As soon as I implemented some very strategic business moves, I suddenly became a lot less busy. *(Teasing the solution.)*

**Dream painting:** I suddenly had a lot more time to work on the passion project. Or to actually take that road trip with my boyfriend — you better believe we took the scenic route. I used to share about one specific day I made it to yoga at 5pm and I laid on my mat and I just thought, I must have forgotten something. Because there's no way I got away from my desk and made it to actual yoga at 5pm.

**Steps:** If you're ready to stop being so damn busy and finally implement something you can rely on, here's what I want you to do. *(Bullet point steps — quick win that gives them a practical takeaway.)*

**Redirect:** Plus, if you want to take this one step further, I actually have a whole YouTube video on this...

**PS:** PS if you want to finally make it to Sunday brunch with your girlfriends, click here to watch this video on the one thing you have to do to stop working weekends.

---

**Why this works:**
- Shifting their belief — it's not just the life of an entrepreneur to be busy, it's the life of an unproductive entrepreneur
- Getting them clear on the root cause of their problem
- Making it truly valuable by giving steps that create a quick win
- Gets them to envision what their life could be like
- Sharing parts of my story paired with a vulnerable story to tease the solution
- Future pacing — getting them to paint the picture of their future

**For the diagnosis emails:** Go back to the email-first ecosystem section and lean into the old way / new way. You want old way / new way to bleed into a lot of your diagnosis emails.

Your job truly in these emails is not to convince them of your way, your process, your offer — but to remind them of who they are and what they want.

---

## Writing the Prescription Emails

If you've done your job well, people have bought in. The real problem is not unhappiness, the real problem is overwhelm. Now you get to say: here's what we're going to do. Here's how we're going to solve this problem.

Lean hard into your philosophy. Do not go into the nitty gritty details of your offer without getting them to buy in on your philosophy. These emails are a spotlight for your signature dish. Assume they've never gone and will never go to your sales page — what do you think they need to know?

At this point, you are overtly saying it is time for you to book a call. You are calling them to action. Use your best copy from your sales page — it's fine to repeat yourself. Use the things that are really powerful and disruptive as much as possible, because at this point:
- Your first email is designed to make them feel **seen**
- Your last ones should make them feel **certain**

You want them to feel certain they're in the right place, certain this is the right time, certain you are the right person.

---

## Final Tips

- This is a stock nurture sequence — it has nothing to do with your freebie. In a dream world, 30 freebies all lead to this sequence. Keep your freebie out of it as much as possible.
- Every single email has to have a clear call to action — just one, mentioned or hinted at multiple times throughout. Three ways for them to click on the CTA. Always add a PS.
- Don't write novelish stuff. Imagine someone reading on their phone. Short paragraphs, bold areas, different lines. Really leverage conversational copy.
- Once you've written the bulk of your emails, do a tone edit. Ask yourself: does this sound like me for real? How would I actually talk? What would this actually be like in a real conversation?
