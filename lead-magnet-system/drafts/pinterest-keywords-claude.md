### How to stop guessing at keywords and start using the search bar (and a little AI) to get your pins actually found, and actually kept found.

---

Okay so real talk. Most people treat Pinterest like a chill, low-key version of Instagram. Cute pictures, nice fonts, maybe a recipe or two. And that's exactly why their pins go nowhere.

Here's the actual argument for why you should care right now, not the vague "it's a search engine" version. A single pin keeps driving traffic for 3-6 months after you post it. Compare that to Instagram or TikTok, where a post is basically dead within days. You're not choosing between platforms here, you're choosing between a system that compounds and one that resets every morning. 619 million people are on Pinterest monthly, running 80 billion searches. And 85% of weekly Pinterest users buy something directly from a brand pin they've seen. That's not a maybe-someday audience. That's a buying audience, mid-search, handing you the exact words they're using to look for what you sell.

So the entire game is keywords, not vibes. People are typing actual problems into that search bar ("how to grow my email list," "notion templates for business owners," "pinterest marketing for entrepreneurs") and Pinterest is deciding, based on your keywords, whether your pin gets to be the answer.

Here's the part that gets skipped: you don't need to become an SEO nerd to win this. You need real keywords, found the free, slightly annoying, very effective way, and then you need help structuring them into titles and descriptions that don't sound like a robot wrote them. That second part is where Claude comes in. It drafts around keywords you've already found and verified. It's a structuring tool, not a research tool, and that difference is the one this whole guide is built on.

## 1. Start With the Search Bar, Not Your Brain

Open Pinterest. Type in one broad term related to what you do: "productivity," "notion template," "email marketing," whatever your thing is. Don't hit enter yet.

Watch the autocomplete suggestions drop down. Those aren't Pinterest being helpful. Those are real, actual searches real, actual people have typed before you. Write down every single one.

Then hit enter and look at the colored bubbles under "more ideas like this." Same deal, these are Pinterest handing you its search data for free, no tool, no login, no $97 course required.

**Do this for 5-10 broad seed terms** across whatever you teach or sell. Not one term. Five to ten. You're building a real list, not confirming a hunch.

## 2. What the Free Method Gets You, and What the Paid Tools Add

The search bar method above gets you about 90% of the way there. For free. That's genuinely most of the work. But it's worth knowing what exists past it, so you're choosing to skip a tool because you don't need it, not because you didn't know it existed.

- **PinClicks.** Pinterest-only. Its whole thing is rank tracking, meaning it actually shows you whether the keywords you picked are working over time, not just at the moment you picked them.
- **Keysearch (~$17/mo).** The budget option. Does Google and Pinterest keyword research in one place, which matters if Pinterest isn't your only channel.
- **Tailwind.** Free tier gives you 5 posts a month, paid runs $14.99-$49.99/mo. Strong on scheduling. Its keyword research exists but it's shallow, don't go here for the research, go here if you already have keywords and need the batching.
- **Pingroupie.** A completely different angle. It doesn't tell you what people search, it shows you what's already working for similar accounts and boards. Reverse-engineering instead of forward research.

None of these are required reading. I don't sell any of them, and I'm not about to pretend you need a subscription to do what the search bar already does for free. But if you're scaling past a handful of pillars, PinClicks or Keysearch earn their keep.

## 3. Check Pinterest Trends Before You Assume Anything's Evergreen

Pinterest Trends is the free, built-in tool most people don't know exists (or forget exists, same thing). It shows you when interest in a term actually peaks.

This matters because pins take time to get indexed, and seasonal content needs real lead time. 45% of users plan seasonal purchases and content 60+ days out, and the working rule is 45-90 days of lead time before the actual peak. If "notion template ideas" peaks every September, you want that pin live in June or July, not August.

**The underused move here: compare terms.** Pinterest Trends lets you put up to 4 related terms side by side on one graph, each indexed 0-100 against its own peak. Instead of guessing between two close options, you just look.

Say you're deciding between "notion templates" and "notion for business owners" for a systems-adjacent pillar. Pull both into Compare. One of them might spike hard every January and flatten out the rest of the year. The other might run a lower, steadier line all year long. Now you know which one to build your evergreen content around, and which one to time for a January push. You're not choosing blind anymore.

## 4. Long-Tail Beats Generic, Every Time

"Kitchen" is a keyword nobody wins. Millions of people search it, which means millions of pins are fighting over it, which means yours is a whisper in a stadium.

"Modern farmhouse kitchen ideas" is a keyword you can actually rank for. It's specific enough to match real intent: someone who types that already knows what they want, and they're closer to acting on it than someone who typed "kitchen."

This is the whole logic: specificity beats volume-chasing. You're not trying to be everything to everyone. You're trying to be the exact answer to the exact question someone typed at 11pm while they should've been sleeping.

## 5. Fresh Pins Aren't Just New Text on the Same Image

This is the part that trips people up the most, so let's be precise about it. "Fresh" doesn't mean you slapped a new title on last month's graphic. Fresh means a genuinely new image, and fresh pins are responsible for over 90% of website traffic on the platform. Reposting your best pin with a different headline does not count, no matter how tempting the shortcut is.

Pinterest ranks everything on four signals: quality, engagement, relevance, freshness. Of those, saves are the single most-weighted engagement signal, outbound clicks come second. So you're not optimizing for likes or comments here, you're optimizing to be the pin someone saves for later.

Batching, posting rhythm, and what to actually put on the pin visually are a whole separate system (that's what my Pin Planner freebie covers). This guide is about the keywords, not the calendar.

## 6. Know Where Your Keywords Actually Carry Weight (and How Long They Can Run)

Not every spot on Pinterest matters equally, and this is the part people get backwards. The hierarchy:

1. **Pin titles.** The single most heavily-weighted signal. Hard limit is 100 characters, but 40-60 characters is the actual sweet spot for full visibility in-feed before anything truncates. This is where your best keyword goes, full stop.
2. **Board titles.** Second priority. Your boards are telling Pinterest what category you live in.
3. **Descriptions.** Only the first 50-60 characters show in-feed before truncation, so front-load your keyword there. A normal description runs 100-200 characters, but pins that actually go viral average 220-232 characters, so don't shrink it down out of fear. Longer is fine if it's earning saves.

If you only have the energy to optimize one thing today, optimize your pin titles. Everything else is icing.

## 7. Here's Where Claude Comes In (and Where It Doesn't)

This is the part I need you to actually hear: **Claude does not find your keywords for you.** It has no idea what real people are searching. It will happily make something up that sounds plausible and is completely unverified, and you will never know the difference until your pins go nowhere.

So the order matters. You do the research first, in the actual Pinterest search bar and Trends, with your own eyes. THEN you bring Claude in, after you've got a list of real, verified keywords, and you use it to draft the structure around them.

Here's the thing though. You don't want to be rewriting a giant prompt every single time you sit down to write a pin. You want to set the rules once, and then just work. That's what a Claude Project is for.

**Set this up once, and don't retype anything.** Grab the [Pinterest Pin Assistant Project Knowledge file](https://app.notion.com/p/398c8b43e56581cb8fc6d10763f4615a), go to Claude, open a Project, and under Add Content, upload it. That's the whole setup. One upload, reused forever.

What's actually in that file, so you know what you're uploading: the assistant's role (structure real keywords into pin copy, never invent them), the same trigger words below, format reminders on character counts, and one worked calibration example so Claude has a real reference point before you even ask it anything.

**Day to day, the whole workflow is:** open the Project, paste in a row from your tracker, and type one word.

- **"title"** → 5 pin title options, 40-60 characters each, keyword as close to the front as possible, 100 character hard max
- **"description"** → one pin description, keyword front-loaded in the first 50-60 characters, 150-220 characters total, natural language, one soft call to action
- **"batch"** → treats your paste as one piece of content, gives you 5 distinct titles + descriptions from it, each with a different image concept, no repeats
- **"alt text"** → alt text under 125 characters describing the actual image plus the keyword, not stuffed in

**Your first prompt, right after setup:** paste in the "notion templates for content creators" row from your tracker (it's one of the pre-loaded starter rows) and just type "title." That's the whole first test. No blank cursor, no wondering what to try first.

No re-explaining the character limits, no re-pasting the rules, no hunting for the prompt you used last time. You uploaded the guardrails once, and now you're just feeding it keywords.

Claude is genuinely good at this part: respecting character counts, making the keyword feel natural instead of stuffed, drafting several title variations so you're not staring at a blank cursor. It's just not the part where the real research happens. The research already happened, sitting in your tracker. Claude is the person setting the table, not the one who grew the food.
