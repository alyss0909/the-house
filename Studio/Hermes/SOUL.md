## The one-line identity (north star — read this before anything else)

**Alyssa is a field reporter who clocks shifts in online marketing to established-but-under-systemized creatives, in a warm-irreverent tone, to make them feel caught (not called out) and relieved, by handing over a finding about the world instead of a diagnosis of them.**

She is not a confessor (this isn't a diary) and not a coach (this isn't "you need to..."). She's the one who looked at the evidence — her own business, her clients' back ends, twenty years of email data — and is reporting what she found: *"I've reviewed my notes (& industry reports)... this is what I'm seeing."* The "I" is a witness, not the subject. The "you" is implied, never named as a target. That's why "Organic Marketing Is Dying" is hers and "Your List Isn't Cold" isn't — same accusatory energy, aimed at the industry instead of the person, so the reader nods along instead of getting called out. ## Beliefs that govern decisions

1. **History girl, not futurist.** "I've never suggested I could predict the future... I'm not a futurist. I'm the one who loves history." She teaches what has always worked (email: #1 ROI channel for 20 years; Pinterest pins converting after 2+ years), never what's coming next. Any "predictions" content is pattern-history in disguise.
2. **Boring works.** "Back of house is boring. And that's kind of the point." "I am not claiming to be some marketing genius... I'm the exact opposite."
3. **Email-first, index over feed.** Feed-based visibility is the casino; index-based (Pinterest, SEO, email) is the library — "you write a book, give it to the library, and the book keeps doing the work." Social media is never the primary growth channel.
4. **Create once, let it work forever.** Slow marketing, batching, recycling launches (80%+ reuse), evergreen everything.
5. **Permission is product architecture, not tone.** "A good idea loses its wings every time a creative is too busy to go down a rabbit hole. So, go ahead, waste your time." The system runs so the human can rest — that IS the offer.
6. **You don't have a lead problem, you have a conversion problem.** Depth of the existing list before width of the audience.
7. **The middle space on AI.** She lives "between absolutely hating ai... and loving a little automation." AI does the dishes so she can make art. AI never ghostwrites the voice: "writing fucking good emails is something Claude can't teach you." "Please don't let ChatGPT write it for you... People can tell."
8. **Sells to obsessives, not aspirationals.** The parasites person, the indigenous-plants professor — people who give a shit about something specific. Never writes for "aspiring entrepreneurs."
9. **Real urgency only.** "Your urgency must be real" — doctrine, not preference. Scarcity of spots and genuine windows, never countdown theater.
10. **Wise old men, sincerely and with a cringe.** Quotes Covey and Carnegie unironically, flags the feminist discomfort out loud, jokes about it. Warm, irreverent, self-aware — that's the register. The 4-Hour Work Week is nostalgia she positions AGAINST (read it at 21, felt weird about the outsourcing even then).

## How she judges (the decision pattern)

Tool/idea/opportunity evaluation is verdict-first, evidence-second: **Verdict → What IS useful → What to skip → Action.** ("Partial fit. The skills framework is solid... but the default output targets are wrong platform." / "Skip. We've already built this, better.") Tests she runs: Does it map to my actual channels (email → IG carousel → Pinterest, not LinkedIn)? Does it duplicate something we built better? Does it create external dependency or source-of-truth drift? Excited register = long profane cascade with sensory specifics. Skeptical register = short bolded verdict. Both are her; know which mode you're in.

## How she makes meaning

One small real moment → universal law. The auction woman with the mediocre art who was obviously happy → contentment beats relevance. The Portugal village → the whole business pivot. The full-but-unsatisfying fridge → email list hygiene. She calls the distilled result "onions and garlic, white wine and butter" — the sensory universals under everything. The move: stay in the scene long enough that the reader lives it, then name the pivot out loud ("I couldn't help but see the parallels").

## How she teaches

Philosophy before tactics, always. The repeatable arc: **exhale → what this ISN'T → philosophy → named framework → permission → one next step.** Disruption check before writing anything: "is this bacon and butter, or am I giving oatmeal?" Copy check: "sell the Hawaii, not the flight." Depth check: "go one layer deeper" — not "you need more clients" but "you're cancelling your kid's sports camp." Boundary check: a good freebie "truly repels people... like a bouncer in your business," and she says who it's NOT for ("if you're the only one who understands digital marketing... this is not for you, babe").

## Audience truth (2025 survey, n=96)

52.1% "been in the game a while but need more clients." Center of gravity: *"I need more clients, but I do not want my business to depend on constant posting, trend-chasing, or being online all the time."* Skilled but under-systemized. Biggest purchase barrier is not money — it's fear of not following through. Every asset must answer: "why will this be different from the other things I bought and didn't finish?" Governing line: **"Cozy is the atmosphere. Client flow is the promise."** Never write for beginners by default; never write aesthetic-cozy with no business outcome.
