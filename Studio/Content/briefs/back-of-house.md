# Back of House — flagship program, $3,000

## What this is
Her flagship program (say the thing: a set-it-up-once Pinterest + email course/program, $3,000) with lifetime access, weekly live calls, personal reviews, and a done-for-you vault of templates — the "automated sales & marketing department" build.

## What it sells
A business that sells without her posting: an email-first, evergreen-first system where Pinterest drives the traffic and email does the converting, set up once. Marketing on the back burner, by design.

## Buying beliefs (what someone must believe before the CTA makes sense)
1. Their business model — not their effort — is the problem: it runs on front-of-house energy and depends on things that disappear.
2. The highest-converting channels have always been email and search; trends are noise on top of that.
3. Their weird, niche obsession is the marketable thing — they don't need to become a marketer, they need a system that handles the marketing.

## Approved argument lanes (one lane per deck, never mashed — a $3,000 flagship warms belief, it does not close)

### Lane A — Your business is running on front of house energy
Point: the business is too needy — every sale, post, and touchpoint relies on them, nothing compounds, and marketing has become a second full-time job; flip to email first & evergreen first and everything shifts.
Villain: the high-maintenance business model (posts that die in 24 hours, feeding a machine that's never full).
Promise: a digital Back of House quietly working in the background, bringing in leads and closing sales while you're on Do Not Disturb.
Best frameworks: Old Way / New Way (high-maintenance vs low-maintenance model — her own checklist comparison), House Report.
Use when: the reader's pain is burnout, no breathing room, "my marketing is a second job."
Do not mix with: Lane B or C. One deck, one villain — this one is the model, not the trend history and not the identity.
Verbatim source lines: "👀 Your business has become way too needy." · "It relies on you for everything. Every sale, every post, every client touchpoint. There's no breathing room, no built-in momentum." · "🕳️ Your model depends on things that disappear." · "Posts that stop working after 24 hours. Leads that ghost if you don't respond immediately. Income that dries up when you're between launches. Nothing compounds, and you're tired of constantly starting from scratch." · "🥵 Your marketing feels like a second full-time job." · "your business is running entirely on front of house energy." · "But when you flip the model, when you go EMAIL FIRST & EVERGREEN FIRST everything shifts." · "You don't need MORE (content, leads, time), you need a better system!"

### Lane B — What has ALWAYS worked (the history girl argument)
Point: every 3–5 years a "new" marketing trend emerges and dies, but email and search have never stopped being the highest-converting channels — she didn't predict the future, she studied the patterns and built a system on what never changes.
Villain: trend-chasing — building the business on "what's working right now."
Promise: a system built on 20+ years of what always works, immune to the next algorithm change.
Best frameworks: House Report (the market-history receipts ARE the report), Hot Take.
Use when: the reader is skeptical of gurus and hacks, or rattled by another algorithm change.
Do not mix with: Lane A or C. This deck argues from history, never from the reader's burnout.
Verbatim source lines: "the question isn't: What's working right now? The question is: What has ALWAYS worked?" · "Email has been the #1 highest ROI channel for over 20 years." · "the highest-converting platforms have always been email and search." · "I didn't invent some secret viral hack." · "I looked back at what always worked in online business." · "And I built a system around it." · "🔍 People search when they have a problem" · "💌 ppl buy from people they trust" · "🥺 ppl forget you if they never hear from you" · "those truths haven't changed in decades" · "Marketing Mirepoix." · "social platforms reward volume not value (and definitely not conversions)."

### Lane C — We don't need another marketing expert (the weird girl argument)
Point: the internet is turning the most interesting people in the room into marketers — but their weird obsessions and niche POVs are the marketable thing; the system handles lead gen and converting so they get to stay the expert.
Villain: the pressure to become a content creator / marketing expert instead of being the best at their actual thing.
Promise: be obsessed with your thing; the Back of House handles the rest — "You bring your weird obsession, I'll bring the back of house."
Best frameworks: For The Girls (the mango / color-season / meltdown-whisperer roll call IS the format), Hot Take.
Use when: the reader identifies as a craftsperson or niche expert, not a marketer.
Do not mix with: Lane A or B. This deck is identity-first; it never leads with the model critique or the history receipts.
