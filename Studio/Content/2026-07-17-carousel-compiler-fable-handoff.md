# Carousel Compiler Fable Handoff

## Why This Exists

Alyssa's carousel system had too much knowledge and not enough forced writing behavior.

The house already had strong material:

- real carousel examples in [[Library/Examples/Carousels/INDEX]]
- full slide breakdown in [[Studio/Analysis/carousel-slide-by-slide]]
- winning flow synthesis in [[Studio/Analysis/carousel-winning-flow-report]]
- voice and method files across [[Studio/Voice/formats/carousels]], [[Studio/Content/METHOD]], and Hermes craft files

The problem was not missing knowledge. The problem was that agents were loading too much, flattening it, and then writing from labels.

The named failure:

> A writer said "How To (Metaphor Plan)" and then skipped the actual copy steps.

That is the disease. It looks organized, but it does not force persuasion, source use, or Alyssa-style execution.

This session rebuilt carousel production as a compiler:

1. Start from one required carousel outline.
2. Lock one concept.
3. Choose one framework card.
4. Require a source receipt.
5. Send two full drafts to Alyssa.
6. Feed her reactions back into the system.

The goal is daily carousels that sound like Alyssa, use her real source material, teach or move someone for real, earn the CTA, and do not require loading the whole house every time.

## The Core Decision

Production starts at [[Studio/Content/carousel-dispatch]].

Not the winning flow report.
Not the full slide-by-slide analysis.
Not METHOD.
Not hook-forensics.
Not a pile of Hermes files.

Normal production load:

**Orchestrator loads**

1. [[Studio/Content/carousel-dispatch]]
2. one offer brief, angle brief, or FTG mood brief
3. [[Studio/Content/taste-ledger]] only if the offer or angle has live corrections

**Each writer loads**

1. [[Studio/Content/writer-kit]]
2. the same brief
3. one framework card

The framework cards are compiled source packets. They carry the working parts of the winning flow report and slide-by-slide analysis so writers go deep into one lane instead of wide across five reports.

## The New Required Order

Every carousel starts here:

```md
Cover hook:
Slide 2 open loop:
Old way / problem:
Proof or validation:
Teaching:
Turn:
CTA:
```

This is the first gate.

No route.
No framework.
No hook polish.
No subagent.
No draft.

If those seven jobs are vague, the post is not ready.

The production order is now:

1. **Carousel outline:** fill the seven spine jobs in plain language.
2. **Concept lock:** one argument, one villain, one promise, one teaching, one offer bridge.
3. **Framework choice:** How To, Old Way / New Way, Hot Take, or For The Girls.
4. **Source receipt:** name the brief, source lines, framework card, real carousel precedent, and Alyssa-specific texture.
5. **Writer subagents:** two full drafts from narrow context.
6. **Alyssa review:** she chooses between finished posts, not vague directions.
7. **Taste compounding:** her correction lands in [[Studio/Content/taste-ledger]] or the relevant card.

## Why Carousel Outline First Matters

Concept lock alone did not solve the problem.

An agent can say:

> This post argues gourmet email marketing is out.

And still write a deck that accidentally argues:

> Your six-channel content calendar is exhausting.

Both can be true. Both can be Alyssa. They are still two different posts.

The carousel outline forces the writer to prove the deck has a persuasive path before it gets clever:

- what stops the scroll
- what creates the second swipe
- what problem gets built
- what proves or validates the problem
- what useful thing the reader gets
- what turn makes the point land
- what CTA is earned

This should prevent "good idea, bad carousel."

## The Four Active Writing Cards

Production now uses four cards:

1. [[Studio/Analysis/Frameworks/carousels/how-to]]
2. [[Studio/Analysis/Frameworks/carousels/old-way-new-way]]
3. [[Studio/Analysis/Frameworks/carousels/hot-take]]
4. [[Studio/Analysis/Frameworks/carousels/for-the-girls]]

House Report is no longer a default writing framework. It stays in [[Studio/Analysis/carousel-winning-flow-report]] as a source pattern and market-pressure mode.

The cards are not taxonomy. They are writer packets.

Each card should give the writer:

- when to use the framework
- framework outline
- carousel outline
- pressure and proof logic
- hook options
- Alyssa Move Library
- source patterns
- write-toward lines
- do-not-write lines
- content-role cautions
- failure modes
- deep source links only for debugging

## What Each Framework Is For

### How To

Use when the reader wants the result, but the method feels too big, random, precious, platform-dependent, or hard to repeat.

Main job: make the method feel usable enough that the offer becomes the easiest container.

Danger: the agent writes "teaches the full method" instead of naming the actual teaching parts.

The card forces:

- outcome promise
- false premise or hidden opportunity
- simpler operating order
- 3 to 4 teaching parts
- memory device
- easier-than-expected turn
- CTA bridge

### Old Way / New Way

Use when the reader needs permission to leave something they have been treating as responsible, obvious, or required.

Main job: make the old way feel too expensive to keep choosing, then give the calmer or more durable model.

The card preserves the copywriting moves Alyssa liked from earlier drafts:

- name the old belief the reader already suspects is failing
- steelman why it used to make sense
- show why it is breaking now
- name the cost of staying
- validate the reader so she does not feel stupid
- ask the better question
- teach the calmer model
- CTA to the exit door

Critical Alyssa move:

The old way should become undesirable without blaming the reader. The cost is pressure, volume, exhaustion, disappearing content, bad-fit attention, high maintenance. Not "you failed."

### Hot Take

Use when the reader will not believe the claim unless the post proves it first.

Main job: state the thing the reader half-suspects, stack receipts until the old belief collapses, then teach the rebuild.

The card forces:

- scary or disruptive claim
- lived validation
- receipt stack
- escape route to kill
- villain reveal
- rebuild in proof-backed parts
- CTA to the bigger fix

Danger: outrage with thin proof. If there are not receipts, do not use Hot Take.

### For The Girls

This was the biggest late-session correction.

FTG is no longer an exception or side door.

It follows the same dispatch path:

1. carousel outline first
2. concept lock second
3. framework outline third
4. source receipt fourth
5. draft last

The difference is style, not process.

FTG fills the same spine differently:

- cover hook: declaration, permission, identity line, seasonal/love-letter frame
- slide 2 open loop: recognition line
- old way/problem: guilt or pressure
- proof or validation: tiny concrete emotional detail
- teaching: permission lines and one principle, not tactics
- turn: reclassify the "unproductive" thing as part of the work
- CTA: soft close, question, sign-off, or brand seed

This solves the old split where FTG was described as "no structure." That was wrong. FTG has structure. It just does not teach tactics or sell hard.

## The Writer Kit

[[Studio/Content/writer-kit]] is the only rules file every carousel writer loads.

It now says, at the top:

> First instruction: fill the carousel outline. No framework, hook, concept flourish, or draft happens before the outline exists.

It also carries:

- the seven carousel spine jobs
- the copy-paste law
- Alyssa's sentence-level signature
- framework-specializes-the-spine rule
- required receipts before drafting
- hook rules
- no imagery rule
- three final gates
- FTG style handling

Key law:

On offer decks, teach the WHAT and let the offer show the HOW.

Meaning:

- the teaching slides give the generic method a stranger could do by hand
- the product is the shortcut or container
- do not make the product mechanics the whole value

This exists because product decks were turning into product descriptions instead of useful posts.

## The Two-Draft Rule

Alyssa does not want one "best" version selected by a confused agent.

The system should put two complete options on her desk.

Version B cannot be only a new hook.

It must differ by at least one:

- framework
- buying belief
- pressure
- content role
- angle

Each version must include:

```md
Carousel outline
Framework outline
Source receipt
5 hooks
Full carousel copy
Caption
CTA
Source lines used
```

The strategic reason: taste is Alyssa's asset. Volume is the machine's asset. The team should bring her two real things worth reacting to, not make her author from fragments.

## What Got Fixed

### Problem 1: Too much context, low-quality output

Old pattern:

The writer loaded METHOD, carousel-winning-flow, slide-by-slide, hook-forensics, Hermes, examples, offer files, and whatever else felt relevant.

Failure:

The writer summarized the summary and wrote a cleaned-up fake Alyssa voice.

Fix:

Dispatch plus writer-kit plus one brief plus one framework card.

The writer goes deeper into the chosen framework, not wider across the house.

### Problem 2: Framework labels replacing copywriting

Old pattern:

The agent wrote "How To (Metaphor Plan)" and treated that as strategy.

Failure:

No actual persuasive sequence.

Fix:

Each card requires a filled framework outline. "Teaches the full method" is explicitly a fail.

### Problem 3: Hooks chosen too early

Old pattern:

The cover became the concept, then the body chased it.

Failure:

Cover and body argued different things.

Fix:

Hook comes after carousel outline, concept lock, and framework outline. The hook compresses the argument. It does not invent the argument.

### Problem 4: Two villains in one deck

Old pattern:

The 5 Minute Newsletter post mashed:

- gourmet email marketing is out
- your six-channel content calendar is too much

Failure:

Two villains, no coherent deck.

Fix:

Concept lock names:

- this post argues
- this post is not about
- villain
- promise
- metaphor or bit
- teaching
- offer bridge

If there are two villains, kill the concept.

### Problem 5: FTG treated as shapeless

Old pattern:

FTG was an "exception" that routed away from the spine.

Failure:

Agents could either over-structure it into a lesson or under-structure it into vibes.

Fix:

Same path, different style. FTG now has a full framework card with outline, spine mapping, pressure/proof, hooks, move library, cautions, and fail modes.

### Problem 6: Agents writing from analysis instead of Alyssa

Old pattern:

The system gave agents analysis language like "belief move" or "reframe."

Failure:

The draft sounded like a content strategist explaining Alyssa, not Alyssa writing.

Fix:

Cards include "How Alyssa does it," "Write toward," and "Do not write" lines grounded in real examples.

Example:

Write toward:

> the old move asks for more than it gives back now

Do not write:

> if you keep doing this, you'll stay overwhelmed and inconsistent

That distinction matters.

## What Fable Should Pressure Test

Pressure test this as a production workflow, not as a pretty knowledge system.

### 1. Can a cold writer use the narrow load?

Give a writer only:

- [[Studio/Content/carousel-dispatch]]
- [[Studio/Content/writer-kit]]
- one offer or angle brief
- one framework card

Can they produce a carousel that has a real argument and sounds like Alyssa?

If not, where do they fail?

### 2. Does outline-first actually stop concept mush?

Try to break it with a messy prompt like:

> write a post about The 5 Minute Newsletter with "gourmet email is out" and also how content calendars are exhausting

The correct system should split this into two possible posts, not mash them.

### 3. Are framework outlines specific enough?

A bad output will still say:

- "teaches the method"
- "shows the reader how"
- "walks through the framework"
- "builds belief"

Those are labels.

The system should force actual beats:

- what boring moment starts the email
- what makes it feel like a story
- what creates the natural sell
- what the reader does tonight

Fable should identify where labels can still sneak through.

### 4. Does FTG survive the same path?

This is the delicate part.

FTG now uses the same production path, but the style card must protect it from becoming:

- tips
- therapy poster copy
- productivity advice
- beige affirmation
- a hidden product pitch

Fable should test whether the seven-part carousel outline makes FTG too logical or whether the card keeps it vibey and specific.

### 5. Does the source receipt need more teeth?

Current source receipt names:

- offer brief
- offer line the writer must preserve
- framework card
- real carousel precedent inside the card
- Alyssa-specific texture/detail

Possible missing gate:

Require 3 to 5 exact source lines from the offer brief or real example before writing.

Fable should decide if this prevents paraphrase slop without bloating tokens.

### 6. Are the cards too long or just right?

The cards are richer than summaries on purpose. Token-aware does not mean thin. It means narrow and high-context.

Fable should test:

- does one card give enough to write?
- does it cost too many tokens?
- could any section move to debug-only without hurting output?

### 7. Is there a compounding loop after Alyssa reacts?

This only works if every real correction has a home.

Possible destinations:

- [[Studio/Content/taste-ledger]] for offer, angle, or line-level taste
- a framework card if the correction changes how that framework should be written
- [[Studio/Content/writer-kit]] if the correction affects every carousel
- [[Studio/Team/TEAM-BRAIN]] only if it is cross-cutting team behavior

If Alyssa says the same thing twice, the system failed to metabolize it.

## Success Criteria

The workflow is successful when daily carousel production can:

- start from a small load
- preserve Alyssa's real source material
- force one clear argument
- build a real persuasive sequence
- make the old way or pressure specific
- teach or move someone before asking
- create two full drafts worth reacting to
- sound like Alyssa, not an analysis of Alyssa
- avoid re-skinning old winners
- improve after each Alyssa correction

The highest standard:

> A stranger should get a useful idea or a real feeling from the post even if they never click the CTA.

If the post only sells, it failed.

If the post only teaches but does not create desire for the next step, it also failed.
