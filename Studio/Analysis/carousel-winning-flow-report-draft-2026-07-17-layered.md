# Carousel Winning Flow Report

Goal: give the content team clear buyer-psychology carousel flows, backed by Alyssa's real examples, so an angle plus an offer becomes a persuasive post instead of a generic summary.

This file is forward-facing. The examples prove the pattern. The instructions tell the next writer what job each slide must do for a new angle, content pillar, and offer.

Old draft preserved here: [[Studio/Analysis/carousel-winning-flow-report-draft-2026-07-17]].

## The Clean Model

There are only two master persuasion arcs.

Everything else is a hook type, execution variant, visual lane, or offer context.

The team got lost because those things were sitting at the same level. They are not the same level.

## How The Layers Link

One carousel is built from four layers.

`master arc + hook type + optional execution variant + offer belief`

The master arc is the parent. It controls the persuasion order.

The hook type controls slide 1 only.

The execution variant is the child. It changes how the middle proves, teaches, or pivots.

The offer belief controls what the post is trying to make the reader believe before the CTA.

Use the layers in this order:

1. Choose the master arc.
2. Choose the hook type for the cover.
3. Choose one execution variant only if it helps the offer or angle.
4. Pull the offer belief from the real offer source.
5. Write the deck.

Do not stack two sequences. If a variant is chosen, it specializes the master arc. It does not add a second flow on top.

Conflict rule:

- Master arc wins for belief order.
- Variant wins for how the middle teaches.
- Offer source wins for facts, product meaning, and CTA.
- Real carousel folders win over every summary.

If the master arc and variant look like they overlap, use this translation:

| Layer | Controls | Does not control |
|---|---|---|
| Master arc | The order of belief change | The exact metaphor, proof style, or product details |
| Hook type | The cover's job | The full post structure |
| Execution variant | How the middle performs the parent arc | Whether problem comes before solution |
| Offer belief | The old belief, new belief, and CTA meaning | The slide order by itself |

Example: `Trend Report` plus `Receipts And Rebuild` means the deck still has to name the shift, confirm the problem, teach the implication, and earn the ask through analysis. The variant only makes the proof section heavier: stack receipts, kill the easy excuse, name who benefits from the broken system, then rebuild.

Example: `Personal Pivot` plus `Proof-First Plan` means the deck still has to name a false belief, agitate the cost, validate the reader, pivot, and CTA. The variant only changes the proof style: start with a weird comparison, prove it has a business reason, then teach a plan.

Simple rule: the master arc decides the psychological order. The variant decides the teaching texture.

Do not write from both numbered lists at once. Write the deck from the master arc, then use the variant to sharpen slides 2 through the CTA.

## Master Arc 1: Trend Report

Source examples: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-15-slow-marketing-trends-pt-1]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-20-whats-actually-shifting-in-2026-pt-2]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-29-smart-founders-going-low-maintenance]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-03-organic-marketing-is-dying]]

Forward sequence:

1. Name the shift, threat, or pattern the reader can already feel.
2. Establish why Alyssa can call it.
3. Confirm the problem before teaching the fix.
4. Show what the old move will cost next.
5. Teach the implication, not just the fact.
6. Give the better path.
7. Make the offer feel like the first useful step after the analysis.

Old belief it attacks: the old plan is still the safest plan.

New belief it installs: the market moved, so the smarter move is to build around what lasts now.

Required middle:

- At least 2 proof or teaching slides.
- At least 1 implication slide that says what the proof means for the reader.
- At least 1 alternative slide that makes the next move feel lighter, smarter, or more durable.

CTA rule: the ask comes after real analysis. The offer cannot appear as the answer before the reader believes the problem.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-15-slow-marketing-trends-pt-1]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-20-whats-actually-shifting-in-2026-pt-2]], and [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-29-smart-founders-going-low-maintenance]] make the reader feel early to a shift. [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-03-organic-marketing-is-dying]] uses the same arc with heavier receipts and a bigger offer.

## Master Arc 2: Personal Pivot

Source examples: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-24-swapped-instagram-for-pinterest]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-19-pinterest-sugar-mama]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-22-wasting-time-for-the-girls]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-06-pinterest-marketing-for-beginners]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-10-get-clients-from-pinterest]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-01-80-days-until-summer-90d-clock-in]]

Forward sequence:

1. Hook with identity, a personal switch, a weird claim, or a specific promise.
2. Name the false premise or old belief.
3. Agitate the cost of that belief.
4. Validate the reader's experience so they do not feel blamed.
5. Make the pivot.
6. Teach the better model in 2 to 4 concrete parts.
7. CTA to the resource, product, class, or offer that helps them make the pivot.

Old belief it attacks: the thing they have been trying is the responsible thing to keep doing.

New belief it installs: the calmer, stranger, more specific move is actually the more strategic move.

Required middle:

- One emotional validation slide in the middle third.
- One moment that protects the reader from feeling stupid for choosing the old way.
- One pivot line that makes the better way feel obvious.

CTA rule: the ask should feel like the next step after the pivot, not a product interrupt.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-24-swapped-instagram-for-pinterest]] protects the reader from the old visibility belief before offering a different model. [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-10-get-clients-from-pinterest]] makes the new method feel simple enough to try. [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-01-80-days-until-summer-90d-clock-in]] turns time pressure into a focused work shift.

## Hook Types

Hook type is not the full flow. It is the cover job.

| Hook type | Forward job | Source example |
|---|---|---|
| Series Title | Make the post feel like part of a larger body of thinking | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-15-slow-marketing-trends-pt-1]] |
| Identity Swap | Show the reader a decision that changes the game | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-24-swapped-instagram-for-pinterest]] |
| Contrarian Claim | Say the thing the reader suspects but has not said plainly | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-03-organic-marketing-is-dying]] |
| Provocative Reframe | Use a strange comparison that forces the reader to resolve it | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-19-pinterest-sugar-mama]] |
| Identity Flattery | Name the smart version of the reader before teaching them | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-29-smart-founders-going-low-maintenance]] |
| Permission Slip | Release the reader from a pressure they secretly resent | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-22-wasting-time-for-the-girls]] |
| Aspirational Utility | Promise a useful tool that feels desirable, not remedial | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-06-pinterest-marketing-for-beginners]] |
| Specific Promise | Promise a concrete result with enough specificity to demand proof | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-10-get-clients-from-pinterest]] |

Hook rule: every cover must do one of three jobs.

1. Name an identity the reader wants.
2. Make a claim they suspect is true but nobody is saying.
3. Promise a specific transformation.

## Execution Variants

Execution variants are not separate post types. They are ways to run one of the two master arcs.

The numbered sequence inside a variant is a filled-in version of the parent arc. It is not a second sequence to stack on top of the master arc.

### Market Shift Report

Master arc: Trend Report

What changes from parent arc: the deck stays close to the clean Trend Report structure.

Input signal: the angle is mostly about a market shift, trend, or new condition.

Forward sequence:

1. Name the shift the reader can feel.
2. Prove Alyssa is close enough to call it.
3. Name the old move losing power.
4. Show the cost of staying with it.
5. Teach 2 specific shifts with implications.
6. Give one lower-maintenance next move.
7. CTA to the tool or freebie that helps them take that move.

Old belief it attacks: the thing that worked last year is still the safe plan.

New belief it installs: the market moved, so the next move should be built for the new conditions.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-15-slow-marketing-trends-pt-1]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-20-whats-actually-shifting-in-2026-pt-2]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-29-smart-founders-going-low-maintenance]]

### Receipts And Rebuild

Master arc: Trend Report

What changes from parent arc: the proof section gets heavier before the better path appears.

Input signal: the reader will not believe the claim unless the deck brings receipts.

Forward sequence:

1. State the scary claim plainly.
2. Validate what the reader is already experiencing.
3. Stack proof until the old belief has nowhere to hide.
4. Kill the obvious escape route.
5. Name who benefits if the reader keeps obeying the broken system.
6. Teach the rebuild in 3 proof-backed parts.
7. CTA to the bigger offer because the small fix would not be enough.

Old belief it attacks: this is just another annoying update or normal business problem.

New belief it installs: the system is built to keep extracting effort, so the smart move is to build a back end that keeps working without feeding it every day.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-03-organic-marketing-is-dying]]

### Broken Channel Pivot

Master arc: Personal Pivot

What changes from parent arc: the old behavior gets steelmanned before it gets replaced.

Input signal: the post needs to make the current behavior feel too costly to keep doing.

Forward sequence:

1. Name the old belief the reader already suspects is failing.
2. Steelman why that belief used to make sense.
3. Show why it is breaking now.
4. Name the cost they will keep paying if they stay there.
5. Ask the better question.
6. Teach the calmer model in 2 or 3 parts.
7. CTA to the offer that helps them leave the old pattern.

Old belief it attacks: more of the old channel behavior will eventually pay off.

New belief it installs: the better model is not more effort in the same place. It is a different system.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-24-swapped-instagram-for-pinterest]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-20-whats-actually-shifting-in-2026-pt-2]]

### Product Walkthrough

Master arc: Personal Pivot

What changes from parent arc: the product appears early because the product itself teaches the method.

Input signal: the offer can prove the idea by showing what is inside.

Forward sequence:

1. Name the concrete result the reader wants.
2. State the philosophy behind the product.
3. Show the product early as the small solution.
4. Turn every feature into an objection-killer.
5. Teach through the product.
6. Drop the deeper diagnosis late.
7. CTA again with the product positioned as the easiest first system.

Old belief it attacks: I need to understand the whole strategy before I can use the tool.

New belief it installs: the tool is the easiest first version of the strategy.

Product-early rule: the product can appear early only when the product is doing the teaching. If it is only being sold, it is too early.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-06-pinterest-marketing-for-beginners]]

### Recipe System

Master arc: Personal Pivot

What changes from parent arc: the middle becomes a named step system.

Input signal: the offer or idea needs a simple operating order.

Forward sequence:

1. Make a specific outcome promise.
2. Name the hidden opportunity most people are underusing.
3. Teach the method as a named metaphor system.
4. Give 4 steps, each with a job and a concrete instruction.
5. Make the steps feel easier than the reader expected.
6. Ask if they want the full system.
7. CTA to the product that packages the method.

Old belief it attacks: this result is complicated, random, or platform-dependent.

New belief it installs: this result follows a recipe when the pieces are in the right order.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-10-get-clients-from-pinterest]]

### Proof-First Plan

Master arc: Personal Pivot

What changes from parent arc: the proof arrives right after the weird hook so the comparison does not stay cute.

Input signal: the cover uses an unexpected comparison and the deck has to prove it fast.

Forward sequence:

1. Use a weird visual joke or unexpected comparison to stop the scroll.
2. Immediately prove the joke has a real business reason behind it.
3. Name the opportunity the reader has been underestimating.
4. Give a 4-step plan from scratch.
5. Add examples inside the steps so it feels usable.
6. Move from plan to class, product, or resource invite.
7. End with a soft brand close so the CTA does not feel like a hard stop.

Old belief it attacks: this thing is only for the surface-level use everyone associates with it.

New belief it installs: this thing can produce a serious business outcome when attached to the right system.

How to apply beyond Pinterest: swap the underestimated thing and the serious outcome. For a 5 Minute Newsletter angle, the old belief might be that good emails require gourmet effort. The new belief might be that a repeatable email structure gets the useful email out faster and more often.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-19-pinterest-sugar-mama]]

### Clock-In Plan

Master arc: Personal Pivot

What changes from parent arc: the pivot is organized around a time window.

Input signal: the angle is about a season, quarter, sprint, or unfinished work.

Forward sequence:

1. Name the time window.
2. Explain why that window creates momentum.
3. Show the payoff if they use the window well.
4. Give 3 operating rules for the season.
5. Break the season into jobs.
6. Make unfinished work feel like assets, not failure.
7. CTA to the dashboard, planner, or container that helps them clock in and finish.

Old belief it attacks: unfinished ideas mean I am behind.

New belief it installs: unfinished ideas are assets if I clock in for one focused shift and close the right loops.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-01-80-days-until-summer-90d-clock-in]]

### Permission Litany

Master arc: Personal Pivot, with FTG exception

What changes from parent arc: the middle releases pressure instead of teaching tactics.

Input signal: the post is meant to make the reader feel permitted, not instructed.

Forward sequence:

1. Name the guilt or pressure.
2. Give the reader a more generous interpretation of that behavior.
3. Repeat short permission lines in the same visual world.
4. Include one line that turns the whole post into a principle.
5. Pause on the brand.
6. End with a question, sign-off, or soft invitation.

Old belief it attacks: taking time, changing your mind, or moving slowly means you are lazy.

New belief it installs: the slower choice can be the higher-standard choice.

FTG rule: no tactical teaching body, no hard CTA, no forced offer.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-22-wasting-time-for-the-girls]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-14-valentines-for-the-girls]]

## Carousel Anatomy

### Cover Page

Word range: 4 to 10 words in the real winners. Aim for 7 or fewer unless the time anchor or specific promise earns the extra words.

Forward job: create a reason to swipe.

Must do one: identity, suspected truth, specific transformation, time window, visual joke, permission.

Never does: vague topic label, generic tips, question-mark cover, tidy reframe that resolves itself.

### Slide 2

Headline: usually 4 to 10 words.

Body: 0 to 55 words depending on the lane.

Forward job: give the reason to keep reading before the teaching starts.

Can use: credibility, stakes, false belief, hidden opportunity, proof that the claim has receipts.

Never does: generic teaching before the reader knows why it matters.

### Problem And Stakes

Usually 1 to 3 slides after the cover.

Forward job: make the old way feel specific, costly, or annoying before the fix appears.

Leaves them with: that is the thing I have been feeling.

### Middle Slides

Usually 4 to 6 teaching or proof slides.

Headline: usually 3 to 7 words.

Body: 25 to 65 words when teaching.

Forward job: teach one named move per slide, with enough detail to use.

Each slide needs: a claim, a detail, and why it matters.

Never does: thin label plus filler sentence.

Always leaves them with: one useful thing a non-buyer could act on.

### The Turn

Usually lands around slide 5 to 8.

Forward job: change the explanation.

Can be: bait-and-switch, system diagnosis, villain diagnosis, better question, personal decision.

Never does: rely on a pretty background switch to carry the point.

### CTA

Usually final 1 to 2 slides.

Forward job: give the next step that solves the exact problem the deck just made obvious.

The CTA is a thread, not only a final slide.

Thread shape:

1. Early seed if the reader is already ready.
2. Mid-deck signal that the method has a container.
3. Final comment-word or offer reveal.

Never does: ask for a product that the post did not earn.

## Non-Negotiables

- Choose one master arc first.
- Choose one hook type second.
- Choose one execution variant only after the arc is clear.
- Problem confirmation comes before solution.
- Emotional validation belongs in the middle.
- Villain assignment protects the reader from blame.
- Product can show early only when the product teaches.
- The CTA must solve the problem the deck made obvious.
- Teaching slides need density: real numbers, examples, screenshots, steps, coined terms.
- The old way should feel less desirable by the end of the post.
