## When to Use This File

Reach for this file (not just `writing-style.md`) when you need:

- **Writing or updating Alyssa's bio** for a guest post, podcast pitch, collaborator intro, newsletter introduction, or press feature — this file gives you the architecture of how she presents herself, not just her voice.
- **Any first-person personal brand copy** where the central subject is Alyssa herself: who she is, why she does this work, what makes her different.
- **Writing an "about me" section** for a landing page, opt-in, or freebie — especially where trust-building happens before any offer is named.
- **Understanding how she earns trust before she sells** — the about page is the trust architecture; the sales page is where that trust is spent. Read this first if you're writing the warm-up.
- **Studying her origin story** so you can reference it accurately in emails, social copy, podcast intros, or pitch decks without distorting the arc or adding invented details.
- **Writing any intro sequence or welcome email** where she's introducing herself to someone new — the about page is the master template for that context.
- **Understanding how she thinks about herself vs. how she presents herself** — she self-edits strategically. This file maps what she includes, what she omits, and why each decision is made.

Do NOT use this file as a replacement for `writing-style.md`. That file covers the full voice system. This file covers how that voice operates specifically in first-person, self-revealing contexts. Use both when writing as Alyssa about Alyssa.

## 1. What the About Page Is Actually Selling (and What It's Not)

The about page is not selling Back of House. It is not selling a program, a price point, or a feature list. There is no testimonial on this page. There is no module breakdown. There is no urgency mechanism.

**What it is selling: the right to be trusted.**

More precisely: by the time someone reaches the CTA at the bottom of the about page, she has sold one thing — the feeling that she is the kind of person who earns access to your business problems. The CTA ("SET UP YOUR BACK OF HOUSE") only works because the reader has already decided: "This person gets it. She's been where I am. I want what she found."

**The feeling she's engineering by the end of the page:**

A reader who finishes this page should feel relief mixed with recognition — "she describes my situation better than I could, she already solved it, and she built a thing to help me do the same." Not excitement. Not urgency. Relief. The page does not create desire; it meets desire that already exists and gives it a name.

**The CTA reflects this.** "SET UP YOUR BACK OF HOUSE" and "TURN THOSE NOTEBOOK DREAMS INTO YOUR REALITY" are not "buy now" CTAs. They're permission slips. The page has given you 800 words of earned trust; the CTA says "okay, here's what to do with it." The final line — "So, go ahead, waste your time" — is itself a kind of CTA. It doesn't link anywhere. It gives you permission.

**How "I found her interesting" becomes "I trust her enough to buy from her":**

The conversion mechanism is specificity + vulnerability + competence, in that order. She opens by being specific about your experience (you). She becomes vulnerable about her own failure (her). She demonstrates competence through the shape of her solution — not by listing credentials, but by showing how the hyperfixation worked and what it found. By the time she names email + Pinterest, you trust it because you watched her earn it.

**The unspoken contract:** She shares something real (the meltdown, the dread, the "I was kinda miserable" admission) and in doing so, invites you into a relationship based on honesty rather than authority. You don't have to believe her credentials. You believe her because she told you the part she didn't have to tell you.

**What she deliberately keeps OFF this page:**

- No testimonials (she has them on the sales page — she saves them for where they do work)
- No certifications, degrees, or course credentials
- No press mentions or speaking gigs
- No client roster or case study names
- No before/after metrics for client results
- No feature list for Back of House

Every omission is a choice. Testimonials would shift the page from "you trust her story" to "you trust her results." That's a sales page job. Here, the story is the proof.

## 2. The Opening Line as a Mirror

> "Dreaming of late-night creative sessions by candlelight, but instead you're burning the candle at both ends just trying to keep your business running?"

**Why it opens with the reader's experience, not Alyssa's introduction:**

She doesn't say "Hi, I'm Alyssa." She doesn't say "Welcome to my about page." She says *you* — before she's even said her own name in the copy. This is structural empathy: she puts the reader's experience before her own existence on her own page. The implicit message is: you are why this page exists, not me.

**The candlelight imagery and the pivot:**

"Dreaming of late-night creative sessions by candlelight" is aspirational — it's soft, deliberate, romantic. It names what the reader actually wants: not just success, but a version of work that feels meaningful and chosen. Then the pivot: "burning the candle at both ends" takes the same image and applies it destructively. She's not comparing two different things. She's showing you the same object — a candle — used two completely different ways. The aspiration (candlelight sessions) and the exhaustion (burning at both ends) are the same energy being consumed differently. This is economy of metaphor; one image does two jobs.

**"Dude, we must be soulmates." — the relationship-builder:**

This line closes the gap immediately. "Soulmates" is not a business word. It's an intimacy word. It says: I'm not going to be formal with you. We're already in this together. The speed of that pivot — from a question about your exhaustion to declaring soulmate status — is what makes the about page feel like a conversation with someone who already knows you.

**Why "dude" specifically:**

"Dude" signals a very specific personality register: casual, unimpressed by formality, direct. It implies a friendship where you skip the pleasantries. Replace it with "Wow" and it sounds like a service provider being enthusiastic. Replace it with "OMG" and you lose the dryness that is central to her voice. Remove it entirely and the line reads as just a claim ("we must be soulmates") without the intimacy of a real conversational opener. "Dude" is the word that makes the line feel said, not written.

## 3. The Personal Brand Story Architecture

The about page is a narrative in seven movements. Here's the full arc:

### The admission

> "Before I became a soft CEO, running my business felt, well, *hard* (lol)."

**What the softened self-deprecation accomplishes:** The "(lol)" at the end does something specific — it signals that she can look back at this with humor now, which proves she survived it. The admission isn't dramatic; it's matter-of-fact. "Well, *hard*" with the italic and the hedge ("well") says: "I know this sounds like an understatement." She opens with failure, but she opens with it lightly enough that you don't feel sorry for her. You just feel seen.

### The catalyst

> "I knew something had to change when, after spending a peaceful week in a small fishing village in Portugal, I, for the first time ever, *actually dreaded going back to work.*"

**Why a specific place:** "Portugal" is not decorative. A vague "I went on vacation and realized something" is forgettable. A "small fishing village in Portugal" is visual. You can see it. The specificity earns the emotional specificity that follows — "for the first time ever, *actually* dreaded going back to work" is allowed to land as significant because the setting is real and particular. Specific place → specific feeling → specific decision.

### The "things you should know about me" list

> 1. I got my dream job right outta university, and promptly left 3 months later to ~~squander my potential~~ work for myself.
> 2. Although my early 20s had *a series of brief stints* in other industries (environmental policy, nutrition, yoga, waiting tables to pay off student loans).. **I've always been an entrepreneur at heart.**
> 3. My idea of a good time is crossing something off my to-do list in black pen (a checkmark could never).
> 4. I have 5848 tabs open (on my computer and just as many in my head) at all times…
> 5. I'm known to get lost on my way home from the antique market bc i *forgot* my phone at home.

**Why these five things, in this order:**

Item 1 establishes that she's always been unconventional — she left the dream job almost immediately, which frames everything that follows as the result of an intentional temperament, not a career failure. Item 2 shows the wandering, which humanizes her without undermining her expertise — the wandering is evidence of genuine curiosity. Item 3 is the first personality reveal that has nothing to do with work ethic or credentials: it's about the *experience* of crossing off a list (in pen, specifically, because a checkmark "could never"). This is her staking a very specific aesthetic/personality claim. Item 4 is the tabs — the ADHD-adjacent overwhelm that she shares with her exact audience. Item 5 is the antique market, which is the most mundane, most specific, most human thing on the list. By the end of five items, you know more about who she is in daily life than most business bios reveal in ten paragraphs.

**The strikethrough joke:**
> "~~squander my potential~~ work for myself"

She names the self-critical read on her own decision before you can apply it. Crossing it out says: "I've already thought this about myself. I'm over it." It's also funny because she includes the mean thought at all. The joke is in her willingness to say the unflattering thing.

**Why the tabs and antique market items land harder than aspirational ones:**

Because nobody includes these on their bio. She could have said "I'm passionate about helping my clients succeed" or "I care deeply about the creative community." Instead she says she forgets her phone at the antique market. This is memorable precisely because it's unremarkable as a business credential. It makes her feel like a real person rather than a personal brand construct.

**The black pen line:**
> "My idea of a good time is crossing something off my to-do list in black pen (a checkmark could never)."

This one line tells you: she has aesthetic opinions. She is precise. She cares about physical specificity in small things. And she has a sense of humor about it. Her audience (creative service providers, also precise, also opinionated about small things) reads this and thinks: "same."

### The "most important thing"

> "BUT MAYBE THE SINGLE MOST IMPORTANT THING YOU SHOULD KNOW ABOUT ME IS THIS: I love my work, and I'm quite literally obsessed with my clients."

**Why this is a credibility statement, not just a nice thing to say:**

What follows this declaration is evidence: she describes going down research rabbit holes "for fun," sending "k here's the plan" voice notes with a full strategy she came up with in the shower, and her "party trick" of getting creative people to reveal secret business ideas. These aren't promises — they're behavioral patterns. The declaration leads the evidence; the evidence makes the declaration credible. The sequence matters: she doesn't say "I'm obsessed with my clients because I do X" — she says it, then shows it.

### The scene-setting section

> "LET ME SET THE SCENE FOR YOU:"

**How she switches modes:** The ALL CAPS header is a mode-change signal. Everything before it was list-and-personality. Everything after is narrative. "Let me set the scene for you" is explicit about the shift — she's asking permission to tell you a story. The "for you" is not incidental: it positions the story as a service, not a confession.

### The mini-meltdown

> "On paper, everything looked solid. My business was doing well (actually better than it had ever been), I'd just come back from a biz funded maternity leave, saved 10 hours each week and cut my work week down to 3 days. Everything was '*working*'."

**How her own failure/confusion bridges to the reader's pain:** By first establishing that everything looked correct — the metrics, the hours, the systems — she prevents the reader from dismissing her experience as "well, she just didn't work hard enough." She worked the system correctly and still found it hollow. This matters because her audience is, similarly, doing the things they're supposed to be doing (posting, being consistent, systematizing) and still feeling behind. Her failure is a mirror for theirs.

> "Au contrair. I was, well, for a lack of a better term, kinda miserable."

The deliberately slightly-wrong French ("Au contrair" instead of "Au contraire") functions as a tiny signal of playfulness — she's using a fancy word incorrectly and knows it. "For a lack of a better term" is performative hedging; "kinda miserable" is actually a precise word. The hedge makes the honesty land softer.

> "👍This girl 👍"

The emoji on both sides of "This girl" is one of the most distinctive moments on the page. It's self-mockery with a visual shrug. She's pointing at herself as the punch line without narrating it. Words couldn't land this as cleanly.

### The hyperfixation pivot

> "So I burned it all down, and did what I do best: Hyperfixated on the problem, until I was inspired again."

**How this frames her ADHD/creative nature as a feature:** The word "hyperfixated" is doing careful work. She's naming an ADHD/neurodivergent pattern by name, but she's naming it as her problem-solving mode — "what I do best." This is significant for her creative, often neurodivergent audience: it reframes the thing they've probably been told is a liability (obsessive focus, going deep on one thing, losing the thread of everything else) as the mechanism by which she found her answer. You don't have to fix this about yourself. It's how you solve the hard problems.

### The solution reveal

> "AT THE END OF THE RABBIT HOLE: Pinterest and Email / I've been using them for years… But now, they're the ONLY things I'm focusing on."

**Why she buries the solution this deep:** Because if she led with it, you wouldn't trust it. "Email and Pinterest" buried 600 words in — after you've watched her fail, rebuild, research, and emerge — lands as a conclusion. The same information at the top of the page would read as a pitch. At this point in the story, it reads as a finding.

The ellipsis before "But now" is doing work: it signals genuine reflection, as though she's catching herself in the middle of the thought. "I've been using them for years" contains the admission that she knew and wasn't fully committed. The "ONLY things" in ALL CAPS is the commitment statement — this is where she draws the line.

### The close

> "THE MORAL OF THIS STORY? / We went down that rabbit hole so you can go down yours (without EVER getting behind on sales and marketing.)"
>
> "A good idea loses its wings every time a creative is too busy to go down a rabbit hole / So, go ahead, waste your time."

**Why a fable-style ending works for a business page:** It releases the pressure. A sales page ends with urgency. An about page ends with permission. The "moral of this story" framing says: this is not a pitch, it's a lesson I learned that I'm now passing to you. "Go ahead, waste your time" is the distillation of everything she's been building: your creative diversions are not the problem. The system she built means you can pursue them without guilt.

## 4. How She Presents Her Expertise Without Credential-Dumping

She does not say "I'm an expert in email marketing." She does not list certifications, degrees, or client names. Instead:

**The specific language she uses:**

> "after almost 10 years running an online business, being behind the scenes in hundreds of service pros, & digital marketing research from the last 20 years they're the most low-maintenance, sustainable tools for growth I've found."

"Almost 10 years" — time-in-practice as credential. "Behind the scenes in hundreds of service pros" — breadth of exposure. "Digital marketing research from the last 20 years" — depth of study. Three different credibility vectors in one sentence, none of which are a certification.

**The "I data-mined historical marketing trends so you don't have to" reframe:**

> "(Actually, that's not true. I *did* look back, but only to data mine historical marketing trends so you don't have to)"

This line converts credentials into service. She didn't look at marketing history to impress you — she did it so you don't have to. The "(Actually, that's not true)" opener is a self-correction that reads as spontaneous, as though she caught herself in a slightly overstated claim and corrected it in real time. This authenticity reinforces the credibility more than the data point itself.

**How the Portugal story functions as proof of depth:**

The story only works as credibility evidence if you understand what she's claiming: that she built every conventional system, achieved conventional success, and still found it inadequate — and then built something better. She couldn't have built something better without having deeply understood what "better" needed to solve. The failure is the proof of depth.

**The hyperfixation identity as trust-builder:**

> "Hyperfixated on the problem, until I was inspired again."

Her creative audience understands hyperfixation as a legitimate research methodology. When she names it as her mode, she signals: "I solved this the way you solve things — by going so deep that you can't help but find the answer." This resonates with her audience in a way that "I did extensive research" never could.

## 5. Self-Deprecating Humor as Relationship Architecture

The about page has the highest humor density of any of her copy. This is not accidental — the about page needs to close the most distance (stranger to trusted person) in the fewest words, and humor is the fastest way to signal that she's a real person.

**"~~squander my potential~~ work for myself":**

She names her harshest self-criticism and crosses it out, but she names it first. You see the crossed-out version. The joke is in her willingness to include it. The strikethrough says "I thought this about myself too, and then I crossed it out." The reader who has had the same thought about their own unconventional choices feels understood, not judged.

**"(lol)" after "running my business felt, well, *hard*":**

The "(lol)" appended to a genuine admission is a very specific move — it signals she can look back on the hard thing with enough distance to laugh, which proves she survived and grew. Without "(lol)" the line is just an admission. With it, it becomes a wink at the reader: "can you relate?"

**"Au contrair. I was, well, for a lack of a better term, kinda miserable.":**

The deliberately slightly-wrong French is a kind of anti-sophistication move — she's using a formal phrase incorrectly and with a "for a lack of a better term" hedge, as if "kinda miserable" is the best approximation she could find. This is comic understatement: "kinda miserable" is clearly not the whole picture, and she knows you know that.

**"*(Small European towns really put things into perspective, don't they?)*":**

The parenthetical italics place this line in the mode of a dry aside. The "don't they?" is a rhetorical invitation — she's not waiting for your answer. The understatement is the joke: she had a significant professional crisis, and she reduces it to a quip about European charm. This prevents the page from becoming a therapy session.

**"👍This girl 👍":**

Emoji on both sides. The double thumbs-up is usually celebratory; here it's applied to the revelation that she ended up managing the very system she built to free herself. The irony of a celebratory emoji at an embarrassing moment is the joke. Words would over-explain this. The emoji is precise.

**How the humor prevents the page from feeling self-pitying:**

She never lingers. Each admission of struggle is immediately followed by a wink (the "(lol)"), an understatement (the "kinda miserable"), a self-aware aside (the Small European Towns line), or a mock-celebration (the 👍). These aren't defensive deflections — they demonstrate that she's processed the difficulty and is narrating from the other side of it. Humor as proof of resolution.

## 6. The Empathy Bridge — Where She Crosses From Her Story to Yours

The empathy bridge is not a single sentence. It's a structural move that happens across three moments:

**The first bridge — the opener itself:**

> "Dreaming of late-night creative sessions by candlelight, but instead you're burning the candle at both ends just trying to keep your business running?"

The page opens in your experience, not hers. So in a sense, the empathy bridge is already established before she's said a word about herself. She returns to the reader's world at the end, but the page begins there.

**The second bridge — the rhetorical question after the meltdown:**

> "Does my mini meltdown coming back from vacay make a little more sense now?"

This is the exact line where the pivot happens. "Now" implies: I've just given you context you didn't have before. The question is directed at the reader, not at the narrative — it breaks the fourth wall. It says: "I know you've been reading my story wondering how it connects to yours. Here's the connection." The question is doing the work of an explicit explanation ("this applies to you because...") without sounding like a pitch.

**The third bridge — the "we went down" close:**

> "We went down that rabbit hole so you can go down yours (without EVER getting behind on sales and marketing.)"

"We" is doing the inclusion work. She's moved from "I" (the story) to "we" (the shared experience of having done the work together) to "you" (what you get out of it). The "EVER" in caps is the emphasis she gives to the promise: not "without getting too far behind," not "without sacrificing too much" — without EVER getting behind. The permission is absolute.

**How she makes her experience feel universal without claiming it is:**

She never says "every creative feels this way" or "I know you've experienced this." She just describes her own experience in enough detail that the reader makes the connection themselves. The specificity (Portugal, the fishing village, the tabs, the pen) is what makes it feel real and therefore transferable. Generic experience descriptions are easy to dismiss. Specific ones demand a response: "wait, is she describing me?"

## 7. The "Things to Know About Me" List — Pattern Extraction

### Length: why five?

Five is enough to establish a full personality picture without exhausting the reader's attention. Three would feel like a bullet-pointed bio. Seven starts to feel like a resume. Five lands in the zone where each item has weight and the list doesn't overstay its welcome.

### What makes each item land

Every item is specific, self-aware, and contains at least one detail that couldn't have been invented. "5848 tabs" is too precise to be generic. "Black pen (a checkmark could never)" contains an aesthetic opinion. "bc i *forgot* my phone at home" (lowercase, italic on forgot) reads like a real text message. The items feel lived-in, not crafted.

### The professional/personal mix

Items 1 and 2 are professional-adjacent (career arc, entrepreneurial identity). Items 3, 4, and 5 are personal (habits, cognitive style, daily life). The mix is roughly 40% professional, 60% personal. This ratio is deliberate: on an about page, more personal than professional is the right balance because the page is earning trust, not listing qualifications.

### The escalation

Item 1 is the boldest professional admission (left the dream job, strikethrough joke). Item 5 is the quietest personal detail (forgets her phone at the antique market). The list moves from "here's the biggest thing about my career arc" to "here's the smallest, most specific thing about who I am on a Tuesday." The ending is quieter than the beginning, which means the final image is intimate. She closes on the antique market, not the career achievement.

### What goes IN vs. what stays OUT

**In:** personality details, habits, cognitive style, mundane specifics, aesthetic preferences, a career arc told as a personality story.

**Out:** awards, speaking engagements, press mentions, client metrics, certifications, program names, revenue claims.

The rule: if it would appear on a LinkedIn profile or press bio, it stays out. If it would appear in a conversation between friends who are getting to know each other, it goes in.

### Writing a version of this list for other contexts

For a guest post bio, podcast intro, or email welcome sequence, the same pattern applies:
- 4–6 items maximum
- At least 2 must be personal/mundane (not business achievements)
- At least 1 must contain a specific number or sensory detail
- At least 1 must have a small joke or self-aware observation built in
- Start with something that signals unconventionality; end with something that feels intimate
- Do not include certifications, press mentions, or client roster items in this style of list

## 8. What This Page Teaches About First-Person Copy in General

### The ratio of vulnerability to authority "I" statements

On this page, for every "I" statement that asserts authority ("almost 10 years," "hundreds of service pros," "digital marketing research from the last 20 years"), there are roughly two "I" statements that reveal vulnerability or personality ("I was, well, for a lack of a better term, kinda miserable," "I forgot my phone at home," "I go down research rabbit holes for fun"). The ratio matters: too much authority and the page reads as a credentials dump. Too much vulnerability and she loses the credibility she needs for the close. The about page is weighted 2:1 toward vulnerability, which is only sustainable because the vulnerability is specific enough to be credible.

### How to tell a business story without making it a LinkedIn brag

The Portugal story could easily be a LinkedIn post: "I achieved X, Y, Z, then realized I needed more meaning, so I pivoted and built [product]. Here are 5 lessons I learned." Instead: she tells it in scenes, lets herself be the confused protagonist for most of the arc, and buries the product reveal until after you've fully earned it. The business outcome (Back of House) is the last sentence of a long story, not the first. On LinkedIn, the insight is the headline. In Alyssa's about page, the insight earns its place by being the end of a genuine journey.

### The difference between a personal brand story that converts and one that informs

A story that informs: tells you what happened, what she did, what the outcome was. Logical. Forgettable.

A story that converts: makes you feel something at each stage. You feel the aspiration in the opener (candlelight), the recognition in the admission (running my business was hard), the specific irony of the meltdown (built the system, became the manager of the system), and the relief of the resolution (burned it all down, found the thing that actually works). Each stage has an emotional payload, not just a narrative function.

**Her technique:** she always names the emotional note explicitly — "for the first time ever, *actually* dreaded," "kinda miserable," "hell bent," "inspired again." She doesn't let you figure out how to feel. She names it and trusts you to recognize whether you've felt it too.

### How she keeps the reader in the story even when the page is about her

Three mechanisms:

1. **Second-person openers and bridges.** The page opens on "you" and returns to it at key moments. She never lets the "I" run more than three paragraphs without a "you," "we," or a direct question that involves the reader.

2. **She describes her experience in your language.** "Throwing money at expensive software," "spending most of my time in meetings" — these are the reader's experiences, mapped onto her story. She's describing herself using the reader's vocabulary.

3. **The questions.** "You think I'd be thrilled, right?" / "Does my mini meltdown coming back from vacay make a little more sense now?" — rhetorical but functional. They pause the narrative and turn to the reader. Even in a long story about Alyssa, these question breaks remind you: you're being talked to, not talked at.

## 9. The Transition from Personal to Professional — The "Meet the System" Close

### The exact language of the pivot

> "MEET: THE SYSTEM OF ALL SYSTEMS"
> "You've been craving more sales without spending more time online? *Well, I've cooked up just the thing*."

The ALL CAPS header signals the mode shift from narrative to offer. But she doesn't just say "here's what I sell." She asks a question about YOUR craving — not her solution. "You've been craving more sales without spending more time online?" returns the page to the reader's desire before introducing the product. The food metaphor ("cooked up just the thing") ties the offer to the story's aesthetic world.

### Why the pivot works

By this point in the page, the reader has: recognized themselves in the opener, learned about Alyssa's personality (5 items), watched her fail at success, watched her burn it down and rebuild, and arrived at the conclusion she found. The emotional state is one of complete trust. She's been vulnerable, specific, funny, and smart. The pivot to the offer lands as a gift — "here's what I found, and here's how you can use it" — not a sales move.

The reader is also, at this point, relieved. The page has named their problem (burning the candle at both ends, dreading work) and shown it has a solution. The CTA is just the bridge to the solution.

### How the CTA is earned

> "SET UP YOUR BACK OF HOUSE"

This CTA works because "your Back of House" implies: you already have one, or you should. It doesn't say "buy Back of House" or "sign up for Back of House." It says "set up YOUR Back of House" — as if it's already yours and you just need to complete the setup. The possessive does trust work.

The secondary CTA — "TURN THOSE NOTEBOOK DREAMS INTO YOUR REALITY" — is a callback to her brand language around the notebook, which appears in the home page copy ("ideas ~~scribbled in your notebook~~ now haunting your good night's sleep"). It's specific enough that it lands as recognition, not generic promise.

### The final "moral of this story" frame

The fable ending is earned because the whole page has been a story. "THE MORAL OF THIS STORY?" is the reader's cue that the narrative is resolved. Fables don't end with pitches; they end with wisdom. She closes with wisdom ("a good idea loses its wings every time a creative is too busy to go down a rabbit hole") before the final permission ("so, go ahead, waste your time"). The CTA that follows is structurally the epilogue, not the climax.

## 10. Dos for About Page / First-Person Copy

**Start with the reader's experience before naming yourself.** The about page opens with "Dreaming of..." directed at the reader. Don't open with "Hi, I'm Alyssa." Open with what the reader is feeling.

**Name the aspiration before the frustration.** "Dreaming of late-night creative sessions by candlelight" comes before "burning the candle at both ends." She leads with what the reader wants, then contrasts with what's happening. Lead aspiration first.

**Use specific, non-impressive personal details in bio lists.** "bc i *forgot* my phone at home" and "crossing something off my to-do list in black pen (a checkmark could never)" — these land harder than professional credentials because nobody includes them. Include the weird, specific, real detail.

**Self-deprecate by naming the harsh read and crossing it out.** The strikethrough ("~~squander my potential~~ work for myself") says: I thought this too, and I've moved past it. Your audience has probably thought the same thing. Cross it out with them.

**Use "(lol)" and "(obvi)" sparingly and only as parenthetical asides.** These work because they're throwaway — they don't headline anything. Never use "lol" to open a sentence or paragraph.

**Let the solution arrive late.** She names email and Pinterest more than halfway through the page. In first-person copy where trust-building is the goal, the solution should arrive as a conclusion, not a premise.

**Show your problem-solving mode, not just your solution.** "Hyperfixated on the problem, until I was inspired again" is as credible as any testimonial because it shows how she thinks, not just what she found.

**Write the "things to know" list with at least 60% personal content.** Career arc, awards, certifications — keep these minimal. Daily habits, aesthetic opinions, cognitive style — lead with these.

**Close with permission, not urgency.** Her page ends with "go ahead, waste your time" — explicit permission to do the thing the reader has been afraid to do. This is the emotional release that makes the CTA feel like relief rather than pressure.

**Use rhetorical questions to bring the reader back into the story.** "Does my mini meltdown coming back from vacay make a little more sense now?" and "You think I'd be thrilled, right?" — these are moment breaks that remind the reader they're being addressed. Use one every 3–4 paragraphs in a long first-person piece.

**Use "we" when you want the reader to feel included in a shared journey.** "We went down that rabbit hole so you can go down yours" shifts from Alyssa's story to a collective experience. "We" earns more than "I did this, now you can too."

**Let humor live in the parenthetical and the aside, not in the headline.** The funniest lines on the page are parenthetical — "(Small European towns really put things into perspective, don't they?)" — or structural (the strikethrough). The humor is buried, not announced.

## 11. DO NOT EVERs for About Page / First-Person Copy

**Do not open with your name or credentials.** "Hi, I'm Alyssa, and I'm a slow marketing strategist with 10 years of experience..." — this is a press bio, not a personal brand page. It starts with her; the real page starts with you.

**Do not list certifications, awards, or press mentions on the about page.** These belong on a sales page or a media kit. On the about page they read as insecure credential-dumping. Example of what NOT to write: "As a certified digital marketing strategist featured in Forbes and Marketing Weekly..."

**Do not use generic empathy openers.** "Are you feeling overwhelmed by the demands of running your business?" is generic. The real opener is specific ("burning the candle at both ends just trying to keep your business running") and uses imagery the reader can feel. Do not write the generic version.

**Do not stay in the first-person story for more than three paragraphs without involving the reader.** The page is about Alyssa, but if the reader disappears from it for too long, they stop caring. No long "here's what I did" blocks without a rhetorical question, a "you" statement, or a direct bridge.

**Do not telegraph your humor.** "And this might be funny, but..." kills the joke before it lands. Her humor lives inside the structure of a sentence, not announced before it. Do not write "I know, I know — sounds crazy, right?"

**Do not use hustle language, even to contrast it.** She never says "I used to hustle hard until I realized..." The word "hustle" signals the wrong world. She says "running my business felt, well, hard" — not "I was grinding 24/7."

**Do not name the offer before earning the trust.** "I created Back of House, a program that..." in the first paragraph is too early. The reader needs to decide they like and trust her before she names what she sells. Example of what NOT to write: "I'm Alyssa, creator of Back of House, the email + Pinterest marketing system for service providers."

**Do not describe her as an "expert" in any first-person context.** She is never "an expert in email marketing" in her own copy. She uses time, experience, and research to imply expertise without claiming the title. Do not write "as an email marketing expert" or "as a Pinterest expert."

**Do not write in passive voice.** "It was realized that the business had become unsustainable" — this would not appear anywhere in her copy. She is always the agent: "I realised," "I burned it all down," "I hyperfixated." The first-person perspective is active, always.

**Do not use motivational poster language to close.** Her close is specific and permission-giving ("go ahead, waste your time"). Do not close with "You've got this," "Your success is waiting," or "The best version of your business starts today." These are generic inspiration and she never uses them.

**Do not list client achievements or testimonials on the about page.** The about page has zero testimonials. This is by design. Including them here shifts the page from "trust my story" to "trust my results" — which is a sales page move and arrives too early.

**Do not use exclamation points to create enthusiasm.** She uses them extremely sparingly. "Dude, we must be soulmates." ends with a period, not an exclamation point. Enthusiasm in her copy comes from specificity and structure, not punctuation.

## 12. The About Page vs. The Sales Page — What Changes When

### The same Portugal story, two different edits

**What's the same:**
- Portugal, fishing village, the peaceful week
- Came back dreading work
- "On paper, everything looked solid"
- Built systems, saved hours
- Ended up managing the system instead of enjoying the creative work
- Burned it down → hyperfixation → email + Pinterest

**What changes:**

**On the about page:** Personal, confessional, chronological. She includes the full "things to know about me" list before the story. The humor is higher. The "kinda miserable" admission is front and center. The story ends with permission ("go ahead, waste your time"). The reader she's writing for is someone who found her page and wants to understand who she is.

**On the sales page:** Edited for stakes. She introduces the "Certified Solutions Person" framing ("As a Certified Solutions Person, I just knew every time a new algorithm update or platform popped up, I'd have to come up with a new solution to 'feed the machine'") which sharpens the irony — the person whose job is to systematize became enslaved by her own system. The pace is faster. She cuts the personality list. The ending is a proposition, not a permission: "What if we just slashed the menu, so to speak?" The reader she's writing for has already decided they're interested in the offer; they're evaluating whether to trust her specifically.

**About page version tone:** "Here's what happened to me — do you recognize yourself in this?"

**Sales page version tone:** "Here's what I learned — here's why it applies to your exact situation — here's what I built because of it."

### What this teaches about editing your origin story for context

**The about page edit:** maximize emotional truth and personality. Include the mundane details. Let the humor run. Make the failure feel real and specifically yours. The credibility comes from honesty.

**The sales page edit:** maximize stakes and applicability. Add the frame that makes the irony clear ("Certified Solutions Person"). Tighten the arc. Close with the proposition. The credibility comes from the solution she built, not just the vulnerability of her failure.

**The rule:** same footage, different cut. Decide what's doing work for your reader in this context. For trust-building, cut toward personality. For conversion, cut toward stakes and resolution.
