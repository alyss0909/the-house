# For The Girls

Use this card to write one For The Girls carousel. It follows the same [[Studio/Content/carousel-dispatch]] path as How To, Old Way / New Way, and Hot Take.

Same path. Different style.

Do not treat For The Girls as a side process. The writer still fills the carousel outline first, locks the concept, fills this framework outline, names the source receipt, then writes.

## When To Use This

Choose For The Girls when the post is for recognition, relief, identity, permission, self-trust, and brand-world affinity more than tactical teaching.

The job: make the reader feel named, relieved, and more loyal to the world Alyssa is building.

This is not a normal educational carousel with the teaching removed. It still has structure. The teaching is a principle or permission line, not a tactic.

## Framework Outline

Fill this before drafting.

1. **Felt pressure:** the guilt, pressure, or quiet resentment the reader already has.
2. **Permission line:** the simple line she wishes someone would say.
3. **Identity being protected:** who this is for, stated as a type or first-person self.
4. **Concrete little detail:** app, laptop, notes app, shell, domain, inbox, rabbit hole, archive, summer, slushie, phone, Pinterest, or another specific object.
5. **Reclassification:** the "unproductive" thing becomes part of the work.
6. **Principle line:** one line that makes the post more than a vibe.
7. **Soft close:** question, sign-off, brand seed, or waitlist mention only if natural.

Fail condition: if the outline sounds like tips, steps, a lesson, or a content strategy argument, it is not For The Girls.

## Carousel Outline

Map the framework onto the same carousel spine.

1. **Cover hook:** short declaration, permission line, identity address, or seasonal/love-letter frame.
2. **Slide 2 open loop:** second recognition line that makes the reader keep swiping because she feels seen.
3. **Old way/problem:** name the guilt or pressure without turning it into blame.
4. **Proof or validation:** validate the feeling through a tiny concrete detail or first-person truth.
5. **Teaching middle:** permission lines and one principle, not tactical steps.
6. **Turn:** reclassify the "wasted," slow, offline, unfinished, or soft thing as part of the work.
7. **CTA:** soft close, question, sign-off, or brand seed. No hard ask.

## Pressure And Proof

Pressure choice:

- **Inner pressure:** the default. The reader feels guilty for moving slowly, changing her mind, not posting, not finishing, wandering off, or wanting a softer business.
- **Cultural pressure:** use when the post responds to a seasonal mood, trend, burnout pattern, or industry energy, but deliver it through FTG lines rather than a report.

Proof types:

- **Recognition proof:** the line is so specific the reader thinks "me."
- **Object proof:** a concrete little detail makes the feeling real.
- **Permission proof:** the post says the thing she wanted permission to believe.
- **Principle proof:** one line gives the post a business spine without becoming a lesson.

## Hook Options

Use one of these shapes:

- Permission Slip.
- Identity Address.
- First-Person Declaration.
- Seasonal Mood.
- Love Letter.

No curiosity-gap covers. No question covers. No promise covers. No "how to."

## Alyssa Move Library

### Name The Guilt

What this does: makes the reader feel recognized before anything gets reframed.

How Alyssa does it:

- Uses a guilt word plainly: wasting time, closing the app, taking my time, changing my mind.
- Makes the feeling specific without overexplaining it.
- Lets the line be short enough to screenshot.

Source pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-22-wasting-time-for-the-girls]] starts with "Wasting Time Is Part of My Process." The whole post grows from one guilt word becoming permission.

Write toward: "the thing you feel guilty for might be part of the work."

Do not write: "you are allowed to honor your creative process."

### Reclassify The Unproductive Thing

What this does: turns relief into a point of view.

How Alyssa does it:

- Makes slowness, wandering, saving, resting, or changing her mind sound intelligent.
- Uses "counts as," "is part of," "helps," or a clean first-person declaration.
- Keeps the business point underneath the line instead of explaining it on top.

Source pattern: [[Studio/Hermes/craft/for-the-girls-posts]] names the core FTG move: reclassify something "unproductive" as part of the work.

Write toward: "leaving it alone helps."

Do not write: "rest is actually a powerful productivity strategy."

### Protect The Identity

What this does: gives the reader a self she wants to repost.

How Alyssa does it:

- Names a type, not a demographic.
- Makes the reader feel more interesting, deliberate, creative, and hard to rush.
- Uses first-person lines when the post is intimate, and "TO:" lines when it is love-letter shaped.

Source pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-14-valentines-for-the-girls]] addresses reader identities directly: founder, visionary, artist, contemplative CEO.

Write toward: "for the version of me who got excited before she got strategic."

Do not write: "for entrepreneurs who want a sustainable content strategy."

### Add One Principle Line

What this does: keeps the post from becoming pure mood.

How Alyssa does it:

- Drops one line that could stand alone as a saved maxim.
- Makes slow, soft, deliberate, offline, or unfinished feel strategic.
- Does not expand into a lesson.

Source pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-22-wasting-time-for-the-girls]] uses "Thoughtful decisions age better" as the principle line.

Write toward: "thoughtful decisions age better."

Do not write: "when you slow down, you make better decisions for your business."

### Close Softly

What this does: lets the post end without breaking the spell.

How Alyssa does it:

- Ends with a question, sign-off, waitlist seed, or brand-world line.
- Keeps the CTA in the world of the post.
- Never turns the permission into a pitch.

Source pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-22-wasting-time-for-the-girls]] ends with a waitlist seed and "What are your January reminders?" [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-14-valentines-for-the-girls]] ends with "Love, your secret admirer."

Write toward: "what are your january reminders?"

Do not write: "comment WAITLIST and I'll send you the link."

## Content-Role Cautions

- **Freebie:** caption only if it feels natural. Do not make the slides carry a comment-word ask.
- **Product/template:** usually no. If the product needs to be explained, use another framework.
- **Webinar/class:** only as a soft caption bridge or seasonal mention.
- **Paid offer:** no hard sell in the slides.
- **Waitlist:** allowed as a soft world-seed if it reads like brand, not pitch.
- **Affinity:** this is the main job. End with recognition, a question, a sign-off, or a soft invitation.

## Failure Modes

- Concept named as a lesson or clever mechanism instead of a simple, felt line.
- Curriculum vocabulary anywhere near the copy ("belief move," "reframe," "the formula"). It kills the intimacy instantly here.
- Re-skin of a published winner: the Jan 22 wasting-time premise with a new noun is a re-skin.
- Quirky-conceit cover instead of a line the reader would send to a friend.
- Sneaking in a teaching framework or steps. Teaching turns permission into homework.
- Pure mood with no principle line. One line has to make the post more than a vibe, or it saves nothing.
- Hard CTA, comment word, or product explanation in the slides.
- Applying normal carousel curiosity-gap hook logic.
