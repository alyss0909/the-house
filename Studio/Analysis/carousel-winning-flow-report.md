## The Working Brief

Every carousel brief should name five things.

`offer + buying belief + framework + content role + hook`

Offer: what we are selling, seeding, or inviting them into.

Buying belief: what the reader needs to believe before the CTA makes sense.

Framework: the kind of argument the carousel is making.

Content role: what the post has to do for the offer path.

Hook: the cover line that makes the argument worth swiping into.

Hook comes last. The cover should be the sharpest expression of the argument, not a clever line the post has to chase.

A framework label is not a framework.

Bad:

`How To (Metaphor Plan). Teaches the full method so the reader can do the thing tonight.`

That is a drawer label. It lets the writer skip the actual thinking.

Before drafting, the writer must produce a framework receipt:

1. Framework and version.
2. Old belief the post attacks.
3. New belief the post installs.
4. Filled forward sequence: every numbered step from the chosen framework rewritten for this exact offer, angle, and reader.
5. Slide-job map: cover, slide 2, problem, proof/validation, teaching slides, turn, CTA.

If the receipt says "teaches the full method," "walks through the framework," or "shows them how" without naming the actual beats, stop. The writer has not used the framework yet.

Content role keeps the CTA from drifting.

- Freebie: teach enough to make the comment-word feel useful, not needy.
- Product or template: product can show early only if the product is part of the teaching.
- Webinar or class: the post should create appetite for the full method, not try to teach the whole class.
- Paid offer: build the belief harder before asking. No hard sell before the reader believes the problem.
- Waitlist: seed the world and the desire. The CTA can stay softer.
- Affinity or brand trust: do not force a sale. End with recognition, question, sign-off, or soft invitation.

## The Clean Model

The top posts mostly share one spine.

`cover hook -> second hook -> old way/problem -> proof or validation -> teaching -> turn -> CTA`

The difference is the kind of argument the idea needs.

The five frameworks below are the source taxonomy. Production has been simplified: normal offer/education posts use How To, Old Way / New Way, or Hot Take. For The Girls is a separate style lane. House Report stays here as a source pattern and market-pressure mode, not a default production framework.

Framework decision rules:

1. If the idea is about what is changing and what it means, choose House Report.
2. If the idea is about leaving an old belief, behavior, or channel, choose Old Way / New Way.
3. If the idea teaches a method, choose How To and pick a version.
4. If the idea needs receipts before anyone will believe the rebuild, choose Hot Take.
5. If the idea is identity, permission, or brand trust with no tactical teaching, choose For The Girls and load its dedicated writing style.

## The Carousel Spine

Every strong carousel does these jobs in this order.

1. Cover hook: compress the argument into one swipe-worthy claim, identity, promise, or provocation.
2. Second hook: give the reader a reason to keep going. Use credibility, stakes, a false belief, a hidden opportunity, product philosophy, or one receipt.
3. Old way/problem: make the current path specific. Name what they have been doing, believing, tolerating, or avoiding.
4. Proof or validation: prove the problem is real, or validate the emotion so the reader does not feel blamed.
5. Teaching: give the useful thing. This can be shifts, steps, receipts, product proof, operating rules, or permission lines.
6. Turn: change the explanation. Use a better question, system diagnosis, villain reveal, personal decision, time-window payoff, or "that's not a system" moment.
7. CTA: offer the next step that solves the exact problem the carousel just made obvious.

Write the post from the spine, then choose the framework below to decide what each job should sound like.

The spine is the default operating order, not a rigid outline. Frameworks can bend it:

- Product Plan can show the product early because the product teaches.
- For The Girls routes out of the normal carousel spine into its own writing style because recognition is the job.
- Hot Take expands proof before teaching because the reader needs receipts before the rebuild.
- House Report may spend longer on implication because the reader needs to understand what the shift means before acting.

Proof is not always data. Use the proof type that matches the framework.

- Data proof: stats, reports, platform numbers, benchmark comparisons.
- Lived proof: what Alyssa is seeing in her own business, clients, audience, or inbox.
- Product proof: screenshots, feature views, dashboards, templates, workflows.
- Example proof: one concrete before/after, niche example, keyword example, or scenario.
- Testimonial proof: student/client result, message, screenshot, or outcome.
- Metaphor proof: prop, visual world, or named metaphor that makes the concept easier to remember.
- Validation proof: naming the feeling so precisely the reader trusts the diagnosis.

Pressure is not a briefing field. It is how you write slides 2 to 4.

Market pressure: something changed outside the reader. Build the first half with shift, proof, cost, implication, better path.

Inner pressure: the old premise is making the reader blame themselves. Build the first half with false premise, cost, validation, permission, better model.

## The Five Frameworks

### 1. House Report

Meaning: trends, market shifts, patterns Alyssa is noticing, things changing in the house of online business.

Examples: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-15-slow-marketing-trends-pt-1]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-20-whats-actually-shifting-in-2026-pt-2]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-29-smart-founders-going-low-maintenance]]

Use this when: the idea is stronger as analysis than as advice. The reader needs to believe the market moved before the CTA feels useful.

First-half pressure: market pressure. Open with what changed outside the reader, then translate that shift into a smarter move.

Best hook styles: Series Title, Identity Flattery, Contrarian Claim.

Forward sequence:

1. Name the shift the reader can already feel but has not fully sorted yet.
2. Establish why Alyssa can call the shift.
3. Name the old move that is getting weaker.
4. Show what staying with the old move will cost next.
5. Teach 2 specific shifts, each with proof and a "what this means for your next move" implication.
6. Give one lower-maintenance alternative so the smarter move feels lighter, not just more work.
7. CTA to the tool or freebie that helps them take that first action.

Old belief it attacks: the thing that worked last year is still the safe plan.

New belief it installs: the market has moved, and Alyssa noticed before the reader had language for it.

CTA rule: the ask comes after real analysis. The offer cannot appear as the answer before the reader believes the shift.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-15-slow-marketing-trends-pt-1]] says "things are changing," then uses AI authenticity and tiny-list behavior to make the shift feel real. [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-29-smart-founders-going-low-maintenance]] says "Smart Founders" are going low maintenance, then proves search and Pinterest are becoming friendlier to slower assets.

### 2. Old Way / New Way

Meaning: the thing the reader has been doing made sense, but it is costing them now. The post makes the old way feel too expensive, then gives the better model.

Examples: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-24-swapped-instagram-for-pinterest]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-20-whats-actually-shifting-in-2026-pt-2]]

Use this when: the reader needs permission to leave something they have been treating as responsible, obvious, or required.

First-half pressure: market pressure if the old way broke because conditions changed. Inner pressure if the old way is making the reader blame themselves.

Best hook styles: Identity Swap, Contrarian Claim, Specific Promise.

Forward sequence:

1. Name the old belief the reader already suspects is failing.
2. Steelman why that belief used to make sense.
3. Show why it is breaking now.
4. Name the cost they will keep paying if they stay there: pressure, volume, exhaustion, disappearing content, bad-fit attention.
5. Validate why they chose the old way so they do not feel stupid.
6. Ask the better question that makes the old belief feel incomplete.
7. Teach the calmer model in 2 or 3 parts.
8. CTA to the offer that helps them leave the old pattern.

Old belief it attacks: more of the old behavior will eventually pay off.

New belief it installs: the better model is not more effort in the same place. It is a different system.

CTA rule: the ask should feel like the next step after relief, not a product interrupt.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-24-swapped-instagram-for-pinterest]] protects the reader from feeling stupid for using the old way, then makes the old way feel too expensive to keep choosing. [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-20-whats-actually-shifting-in-2026-pt-2]] does the same thing inside a House Report.

### 3. How To

Meaning: teach the plan, system, recipe, product, seasonal method, or operating order.

Examples: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-06-pinterest-marketing-for-beginners]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-10-get-clients-from-pinterest]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-19-pinterest-sugar-mama]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-01-80-days-until-summer-90d-clock-in]]

Use this when: the reader needs a usable operating order. The post should make the method feel concrete enough that the offer becomes the easiest container.

How To always needs a version. Choose Metaphor Plan, Product Plan, Proof-First Plan, or Clock-In Plan before writing. If none of those fit, the idea probably belongs in Old Way / New Way or Hot Take instead.

Choosing the version is not enough. A How To brief must fill the sequence below before a draft starts. "How To (Metaphor Plan)" is only the door. The work is the filled framework outline and carousel outline.

First-half pressure: market pressure if the plan matters because something changed. Inner pressure if the plan matters because the old process felt too hard, precious, scattered, or confusing.

Best hook styles: Specific Promise, Aspirational Utility, Provocative Reframe.

Forward sequence:

1. Make a specific outcome promise.
2. Name the hidden opportunity most people are underusing, or the false premise making the result feel hard.
3. Give the reader a simpler operating order.
4. Teach the method in 3 to 4 parts, each with a job and a concrete instruction.
5. Add the memory device that makes the method stick: metaphor, numbered steps, product screenshots, seasonal container, or proof examples.
6. Make the steps feel easier than the reader expected.
7. CTA to the product, class, dashboard, or resource that packages the method.

How To receipt:

1. Outcome promise: write the exact result this post gives the reader.
2. Hidden opportunity or false premise: name what they have been missing or making too hard.
3. Simpler operating order: state the new order in one plain sentence.
4. Teaching parts: name each part, its job, and one concrete instruction.
5. Memory device: name the metaphor, product surface, proof pattern, or seasonal container.
6. Easier-than-expected beat: show why this is lighter than the old way.
7. CTA bridge: name why the offer is the easiest container for the method.

For example, if the offer is Bingeable and the version is Metaphor Plan, do not write "teaches the full 5 Part Simmer." Fill the parts: what boring moment starts the simmer, what makes it feel like a story, what keeps the reader opening, what creates the natural sell, and what the offer packages so they can repeat it.

Old belief it attacks: this result is complicated, random, platform-dependent, or too much work to repeat.

New belief it installs: this result follows a system when the pieces sit in the right order.

How To versions:

- Metaphor Plan: teach the steps through a named metaphor world. The metaphor makes the method easier to remember. Proof: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-10-get-clients-from-pinterest]]
- Product Plan: teach through the product. Every feature has to become a reader outcome. Proof: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-06-pinterest-marketing-for-beginners]]
- Proof-First Plan: prove the weird hook or underestimated opportunity first, then teach the plan. Proof goes right after the joke so the comparison does not stay cute. Proof: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-19-pinterest-sugar-mama]]
- Clock-In Plan: use time, seasonality, open loops, and future-self payoff as the container. The date becomes a focused shift. Proof: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-01-80-days-until-summer-90d-clock-in]]

CTA rule: the ask packages the method. It should feel like the full system after the post gave away the usable first version.

Proof pattern: the How To posts all give a useful plan before the CTA. The style changes, but the job is the same: make the method feel concrete enough that the offer becomes the easiest container.

### 4. Hot Take

Meaning: disruptive, contrarian, receipt-heavy, or spicy. The post makes a claim the reader half-suspects, then proves it hard enough that the old belief collapses.

Example: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-03-organic-marketing-is-dying]]

Use this when: the claim needs receipts before the reader will trust the rebuild.

First-half pressure: mostly market pressure, with one inner-pressure relief beat so the reader does not feel blamed.

Best hook styles: Contrarian Claim, Provocative Reframe, Identity Flattery.

House Report vs Hot Take: House Report says "here is what is changing and what it means." Hot Take says "the thing you believe is broken, and here are the receipts." [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-15-slow-marketing-trends-pt-1]] is a House Report because it teaches implications from trends. [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-03-organic-marketing-is-dying]] is a Hot Take because it prosecutes the old belief before rebuilding.

Forward sequence:

1. State the scary or disruptive thing plainly.
2. Validate what the reader is already experiencing.
3. Stack proof until the old belief has nowhere to hide.
4. Kill the obvious escape route.
5. Name who benefits if the reader keeps obeying the broken system.
6. Pivot into what Alyssa would build instead.
7. Teach the rebuild in 3 proof-backed parts so the reader is not left with only outrage.
8. CTA to the bigger offer because the small fix would not be enough.

Old belief it attacks: this is just another annoying update or normal business problem.

New belief it installs: the system is built to keep extracting effort, so the smart move is to build a back end that keeps working without feeding it every day.

CTA rule: the ask has to solve the rebuild, not the outrage. This framework only works when the offer can carry a bigger strategic fix.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-03-organic-marketing-is-dying]] shows how to move from alarm to proof to villain to rebuild. Future decks should use this framework only when the offer can carry a bigger strategic fix, like [[Library/Offers/back-of-house/MAP|Back of House]].

### 5. For The Girls

Meaning: a dedicated writing lane for identity, permission, brand world, self-trust, "I needed someone to say this."

Required loads before writing: [[Hermes/craft/for-the-girls-posts]] for the method, then one full example from [[Library/Examples/Carousels/for-the-girls-slide-copy]] in slide order.

Examples: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-22-wasting-time-for-the-girls]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-14-valentines-for-the-girls]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-07-march-affirmations]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-10-guest-cheque-quote]]

Use this when: the post is for recognition, relief, and brand trust more than teaching. This is not a normal educational carousel with the teaching removed. It has its own line style.

First-half pressure: inner pressure. The reader needs permission, not a plan.

Best hook styles: first-person declaration, permission slip, love-letter address, affirmation, or quote card. Do not use normal comment-CTA hook logic here.

Forward sequence:

1. Start from the FTG style skill, not the general carousel spine.
2. Name the guilt, pressure, or "unproductive" thing the reader secretly resents.
3. Reclassify it as part of the work, part of the process, or part of a better business.
4. Write short permission lines that feel like saved screenshots, private notebook lines, or tiny verdicts.
5. Add one concrete detail so the post does not turn into beige affirmation copy: app, laptop, notes app, shell, domain, inbox, rabbit hole, archive, summer, slushie, phone, Pinterest.
6. Include one principle line that makes the post more than a mood.
7. End with a question, sign-off, brand seed, or soft invitation.

Old belief it attacks: taking time, changing your mind, or moving slowly means you are lazy.

New belief it installs: the slower choice can be the higher-standard choice.

FTG rule: no tactical teaching body, no hard CTA, no forced offer, and no normal carousel-hook logic. Load the FTG writing skill.

CTA rule: end with a question, sign-off, brand seed, or soft invitation. Do not turn the permission into a pitch.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/for-the-girls-slide-copy]] shows the style directly: first-person declarations, guilt-to-permission reframes, identity addresses, soft CTA at most, and slow/soft/deliberate as the value.

## Hook Styles

Hook is last. Pick it after the buying belief and framework are clear.

| Hook style | Forward job | Source example |
|---|---|---|
| Series Title | Make the post feel like part of a larger body of thinking | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-15-slow-marketing-trends-pt-1]] |
| Identity Swap | Show the reader a decision that changes the game | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-24-swapped-instagram-for-pinterest]] |
| Contrarian Claim | Say the thing the reader suspects but has not said plainly | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-03-organic-marketing-is-dying]] |
| Provocative Reframe | Use a strange comparison that forces the reader to resolve it | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-19-pinterest-sugar-mama]] |
| Identity Flattery | Name the smart version of the reader before teaching them | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-29-smart-founders-going-low-maintenance]] |
| Permission Slip | Release the reader from a pressure they secretly resent | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-22-wasting-time-for-the-girls]] |
| Aspirational Utility | Promise a useful tool that feels desirable, not remedial | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-06-pinterest-marketing-for-beginners]] |
| Specific Promise | Promise a concrete result with enough specificity to demand proof | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-10-get-clients-from-pinterest]] |

Hook rule: every cover must do one of three jobs.

1. Name an identity the reader wants.
2. Make a claim they suspect is true but nobody is saying.
3. Promise a specific transformation.

## Carousel Anatomy

### Cover Page

Word range: 4 to 10 words in the real winners. Aim for 7 or fewer unless the time anchor or specific promise earns the extra words.

Forward job: create a reason to swipe.

Must do one: identity, suspected truth, specific transformation, time window, visual joke, permission.

Never does: vague topic label, generic tips, question-mark cover, tidy reframe that resolves itself.

### Slide 2

Headline: usually 4 to 10 words.

Body: 0 to 55 words depending on the lane.

Forward job: give the reason to keep reading before the teaching starts.

Can use: credibility, stakes, false belief, hidden opportunity, product philosophy, proof that the claim has receipts.

Never does: generic teaching before the reader knows why it matters.

### Problem And Stakes

Usually 1 to 3 slides after the cover.

Forward job: make the old way feel specific, costly, or annoying before the fix appears.

Leaves them with: that is the thing I have been feeling.

### Middle Slides

Usually 4 to 6 teaching or proof slides.

Headline: usually 3 to 7 words.

Body: 25 to 65 words when teaching.

Forward job: teach one named move per slide, with enough detail to use.

Each slide needs: a claim, a detail, and why it matters.

Never does: thin label plus filler sentence.

Always leaves them with: one useful thing a non-buyer could act on.

### The Turn

Usually lands around slide 5 to 8.

Forward job: change the explanation.

Can be: bait-and-switch, system diagnosis, villain diagnosis, better question, personal decision, time-window payoff.

Never does: rely on a pretty background switch to carry the point.

### CTA

Usually final 1 to 2 slides.

Forward job: give the next step that solves the exact problem the deck just made obvious.

The CTA is a thread, not only a final slide.

Thread shape:

1. Early seed if the reader is already ready.
2. Mid-deck signal that the method has a container.
3. Final comment-word or offer reveal.

Never does: ask for a product that the post did not earn.

## Non-Negotiables

- The brief names offer, buying belief, framework, content role, and hook.
- Pick one framework, then fill its receipt. A label alone is a fail.
- Decide whether slides 2 to 4 are driven by market pressure or inner pressure.
- Problem confirmation comes before solution.
- Emotional validation belongs in the middle when the pressure is inner.
- Implication belongs in the middle when the pressure is market.
- Villain assignment protects the reader from blame.
- Product can show early only when the product teaches.
- The CTA must solve the problem the deck made obvious.
- Teaching slides need density: real numbers, examples, screenshots, steps, coined terms.
- The old way should feel less desirable by the end of the post.
