## 1. Her POV signature — the actual stance, with receipts

### The grammatical fact first

Look at where "I," "you," and no-pronoun sit across her real covers:

| Cover | Grammatical person |
|---|---|
| "Organic Marketing Is Dying" | No pronoun — flat claim about the world |
| "Wasting Time Is Part of My Process." | "My" — but the sentence is a **general law disguised as a personal one** |
| "Why I Swapped Instagram For Pinterest (and never looked back)" | "I" — but the payoff is a decision the reader is meant to consider making too |
| "Why Smart Founders Are Going Low Maintenance in 2026" | Third-person identity label — no "I," no "you" |
| "The Pinterest Marketing Plan for Beginners" | No pronoun — audience named by category |
| "What's Actually Shifting in 2026 (And What To Do Instead)" | No pronoun — reporter voice |
| "How To Make Pinterest Your Sugar ~~Daddy~~ Mama" | "Your" — but aimed at an object (Pinterest), not the reader's identity |

Direct "you/your" almost never appears on a cover as a personal indictment. When "you" does show up, it's a CTA-slide device ("BEEN GHOSTING YOUR EMAIL SUBSCRIBERS?"), not a cover device.

### What this actually is

She is not writing confession ("about me") and she is not writing advice-column diagnosis ("about them"). She's doing something a level up from both: **she reports a shift she noticed, using herself as the instrument that noticed it, then hands you the observation as a fact about the world you both live in.** The "I" is a witness, not a subject. The "you" is implied by the fact you're reading it, never named as a target.

This is why "Your List Isn't Cold. It's Just Full." died — it points the sentence directly AT the reader, diagnosing them. Compare "Organic Marketing Is Dying" — same accusatory energy, but aimed at **the industry**, not the person. The reader gets to nod along instead of getting called out. That's the difference between "not about them" (no direct indictment) and "not about me" (not a diary entry either).

**Verbatim evidence for the witness-not-subject stance:**
1. "I've reviewed my notes (& industry reports) from last year and this is what I'm seeing in my own business and BTS with clients who make their money online but don't want to live online." — she positions herself as someone who looked at evidence, not someone confessing a feeling.
2. "Not to be dramatic but things are changing 👀" — reports a shift, doesn't own it as personal drama.
3. "Marketing Nerd Reporting for Duty" — literally names herself as a reporter, not a diarist.
4. "I stopped posting every day and booked more clients than ever." — the "I" supplies the proof-of-concept; the sentence's actual claim ("stopping posting books more clients") is general, not personal.
5. "Slow marketing books more clients than posting every day. I can prove it." — note the order: the general claim comes FIRST, the "I" arrives only as the evidence-bearer.
6. "The clients who pay the most never found me on Instagram." — third-person subject ("the clients who pay the most"), her own experience is the data point, not the headline.

**One-sentence rule for makers: she is the field reporter, not the confessor and not the coach. The hook states a finding about the world (marketing, business, the algorithm, the industry) and she happens to be the one who clocked it.** If a draft reads like a diary entry, it's too "about me." If it reads like direct advice/diagnosis of the reader ("your list," "you need to," "stop doing X"), it's too "about them." The fix in both directions is the same move: route the claim through an observation about the world, with her as the noticer.

## 2. Her real hook frameworks (named, with verbatim examples)

### A. The Reporter's Finding
**What it is:** She frames herself as having reviewed evidence and is now reporting a shift. Credibility-first, not confession-first.
**Verbatim:** "What's Actually Shifting in 2026 (And What To Do Instead)" · "Marketing Nerd Reporting for Duty" · "I've reviewed my notes (& industry reports)... this is what I'm seeing"
**When it fits:** Trend/prediction content, start-of-quarter or start-of-year posts, anything positioning her as ahead of the curve.
**Topic it carries:** Marketing predictions, industry shifts, "what's working now."

### B. The Industry Verdict
**What it is:** A flat, declarative claim about the state of the world — no pronoun, no hedge, period (never a question mark).
**Verbatim:** "Organic Marketing Is Dying" · "2026 Slow Marketing Trends" · "Your Business Should Be More BORING" (per grid intake) · "Slowly Viral." (per grid intake)
**When it fits:** Cold-audience follow-drivers. Best performer for follows (491) in the ranked dataset.
**Topic it carries:** Marketing philosophy, contrarian business takes.

### C. The Permission Slip
**What it is:** Names a taboo the reader already does and feels guilty about, then legitimizes it as fact, not question. Ends with a period.
**Verbatim:** "Wasting Time Is Part of My Process." · "Procrastavoidance" (per grid intake) · "small fixations have led me to good places" (per grid intake)
**When it fits:** For The Girls / brand-world content, zero-CTA share-bait.
**Topic it carries:** Rest, non-hustle, "productive procrastination," soft-CEO identity.

### D. The Confession-With-Conviction
**What it is:** "Why I [dramatic personal decision]" + a confident kicker using "and," never "but" (the kicker is a consequence, not a concession).
**Verbatim:** "Why I Swapped Instagram For Pinterest (and never looked back)" · "Why I burned down a perfectly good business (on purpose)" · "Why I Delete Subscribers On Purpose" (REJECTED — see anti-patterns)
**When it fits:** Personal-decision story arcs, highest comment driver in the ranked set.
**Topic it carries:** Platform switches, business-model pivots, things she quit doing.
**Caution:** this is her single most over-mined skeleton. Do not reuse `(on purpose)` or `(and never looked back)` as a bolt-on parenthetical again — flagged explicitly by her as an "AI tell" the moment it's a swapped-noun reheat.

### E. The Coined Phenomenon
**What it is:** Names a concept as if it already exists in the world, rather than describing it. A proper-noun frame: "the [noun] [effect/problem/incident/math]."
**Verbatim:** "the tiny list effect" (her own coinage for "tiny lists outperform big ones") · "Stoner Math" · "the potato chip problem" · "the beige email incident" · "socks & sandal marketing" · "your Business needs a BBL" (per grid intake) · "SEO Becomes a Girl's Girl"
**When it fits:** Subject lines and Soft Sunday front phrases especially — but the naming move works on covers too.
**Topic it carries:** Marketing mechanics she wants to make sticky and repeatable in conversation.

### F. The Audience Self-Select
**What it is:** Names the reader by category or skill level, not by pronoun. "The [named framework] for [colloquial audience]."
**Verbatim:** "The Pinterest Marketing Plan for Beginners" · "Why Smart Founders Are Going Low Maintenance in 2026" · "for the girls" · "FOR CEOS REFUSING TO RUSH"
**When it fits:** Bottom-of-funnel, qualified-audience posts.
**Topic it carries:** Tactical how-tos, identity-flattery posts.

### G. The Visual Joke / Correction
**What it is:** An eyebrow-raising phrase with a strikethrough or self-correction, screenshot-worthy before any body copy is read.
**Verbatim:** "How To Make Pinterest Your Sugar ~~Daddy~~ Mama"
**When it fits:** Once per content cycle max, cold-audience share campaigns. #1 all-time for shares (3,038).
**Topic it carries:** Anything where the platform/tool itself can be personified.

### H. The Withheld Cliffhanger (3-punch)
**What it is:** Fear + validation, then observable symptom, then a rage-bait withheld cause. "But" is the hinge.
**Verbatim:** "Organic Marketing Is Dying" → "You're not imagining it." → "Reach is way down." → "But the real reason might piss you off."
**When it fits:** Cold-audience follow-driving covers paired with body copy, not standalone.
**Topic it carries:** Algorithm changes, industry disruption.

### I. The Unexpected Command, Casual Mouth
**What it is:** An imperative that sounds like sabotage or transgression, delivered in spoken register (lowercase, "quick," "babe," a stray comma).
**Verbatim:** `🙅‍♀️ DON'T "grow your list" do this instead…` · `oopsie, I've been gatekeeping?` · "delete your leads, quick" (her own suggested fix, not yet posted verbatim)
**When it fits:** Subject lines primarily; can carry to covers if the register stays casual, not clean.
**Topic it carries:** List-building, lead gen, anti-hustle marketing advice.

## 4. What makes them land — the specific voice moves

- **Extended vowels for intensity, never exclamation points:** "BUUUT," "EEEVERYTHING," "VERRRRRY"
- **Casual register words:** "gonna," "wanna," "girlies," "Sorry but," lowercase mid-line
- **"Actually" as an insider-truth trust device** — implies a wrong answer is circulating and this is the correction
- **Year anchors and maximizers as the specificity tool, not hard stats.** Numbers like "2026," "80 Days," "all your clients" belong on covers. Hard stats ("90%," "1600x longer") live on interior evidence slides only.
- **Period, not question mark, on paradox/claim covers.** A period reads as settled fact; a question mark is reserved for CTA/engagement slides only.
- **The eyebrow/label line does one job: names a series with a stake, or qualifies an audience.** Never a filing slug ("email things, pt. 1" — rejected for exactly this).
- **The "wrongness" test:** the line contains something slightly off — a contradiction, a command that sounds like sabotage, punctuation in the wrong register. If nothing is off, it's too clean, and too clean reads as AI.
- **Object-world specificity in the visual world of the cover** (per grid intake): toast, garlic, martini glass, shoe, pepper, receipt, plate, phone. The prop argues with the copy — it's not decoration.

## 5. ANTI-patterns — what is NOT her (makers stop doing these)

**1. Essay-title voice ("The Case For...")**
- Barf: *"The Case For Being The Girl Who Posts Less"*
- Hers: *"Wasting Time Is Part of My Process."* — a flat claim, not an argumentative thesis title. She never frames a hook as a position paper. If a draft could be a blog post's H1 on Medium, it's not hers.

**2. "It's not X, it's Y" reframe-on-a-cover**
- Barf: *"Your List Isn't Cold. It's Just Full."*
- Hers: NEVER uses this shape on a cover. Confirmed across the full corpus — the reframe shape only appears on interior implication/closing slides (e.g. "It's Not —Just— The Em Dash," slide 5, deep in a deck after stakes are built). On a cover it's a diagnosis with nothing left to resolve — no reason to swipe.

**3. "Breaks the belief that..." scaffolding**
- Barf: *"Breaking the belief that busy = successful"*
- Hers: never names the mechanic out loud. She states the belief as something someone else thinks ("Most people think to get more sales you just need more eyeballs") and lets the demolition happen in body copy, never in the cover's own grammar.

**4. Generic guru "How to X" listicles without a maximizer or time anchor**
- Barf: *"5 Ways To Grow Your Email List"*
- Hers: "How To Get All Your Clients From Pinterest This Year" — always over-promises with a maximizer ("all," "every") and a finite time anchor. A bare numbered-list hook with no exaggeration and no deadline is not hers.

**5. Recycled winning skeletons with swapped nouns**
- Barf: *"Why I Delete Subscribers On Purpose"* (= "Why I burned down a perfectly good business (on purpose)" with nouns swapped)
- Rule: never reuse a construction that appears verbatim in a logged top performer. `(on purpose)` and `(and never looked back)` are burned as tacked-on parentheticals — they now read as her formula, i.e. as AI.

**6. Direct second-person indictment on the cover**
- Barf: *"You're Doing Content Wrong"* / *"Your List Isn't Cold"*
- Hers: "you/your" stays off covers as a personal accusation. It shows up only on CTA slides ("BEEN GHOSTING YOUR EMAIL SUBSCRIBERS?") or aimed at an object, not the reader's identity ("Your Sugar Mama" = Pinterest, not the reader).

**7. Rhetorical questions with obvious answers, or questions on paradox/claim covers**
- Barf: *"Are You Tired of Posting Every Day?"*
- Hers: covers end in a period or no punctuation. Question marks are reserved for CTA/engagement slides ("wanna learn how?", "What Are Your January Reminders?").

**8. Crutch/banned vocabulary reheats**
- Watch for: "unlock," "elevate," "game-changer," "level up," any "it's not about X, it's about Y" symmetry anywhere in a hook (cover, subject line, or header) — this shape is banned across ALL surfaces per ANTI-AI.md, not just covers.

**9. Filing-slug eyebrows**
- Barf: *"email things, pt. 1"*
- Hers: eyebrows either name a series WITH a stake ("ONLINE BUSINESS PREDICTIONS," "Part One/Two/Three" — FOMO for the rest) or qualify the audience ("FOR ONLINE SERVICE PROVIDERS, COACHES & CREATIVES"). A bare content-calendar label is a tell she called out herself: *"literally not even a social media hook."*

## 6. Quick gate — run every draft cover through this before it ships

1. Is she the **witness/reporter**, not the confessor or the coach? (Section 1)
2. Does it match ONE of the 9 named structures (A–I), not an invented shape?
3. Does it carry a real payoff — fix, threat, confession, series stake, or joke? No payoff = not a hook.
4. Period or no punctuation — never a question mark, unless it's explicitly a CTA/engagement slide?
5. Is there something *slightly wrong* in it (the wrongness test)? If it reads clean and symmetrical, kill it.
6. Cross-check against Section 5 — is it accidentally an anti-pattern wearing a costume?

If any answer is no, it doesn't ship as-is.
