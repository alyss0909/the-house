## When to Use This File

Reach for this file when:

- **Writing any email subject line** — Simmers, Soft Sundays, sales emails, welcome sequences, or promos
- **Writing preview text** — the preview is half the open rate; this file treats it as a first-class deliverable, not an afterthought
- **Auditing a subject line batch** — checking if drafts sound like Alyssa or like generic email marketing
- **Training any team member on email** — subject lines are the most imitated and most easily botched element

> Do NOT use this file alone for Soft Sunday subject lines — also read `Studio/Analysis/writing-style.md` Section 7 (humor) and Section 6 (permission voice) for the full emotional register those emails require.

## The Voice: Raccoon in a Cashmere Sweater

This is Alyssa's own phrase for her subject line voice. It is the north star descriptor for anyone writing subject lines in her name.

What it means: the subject lines have a scrappy, slightly feral, chaotic energy — wrapped in a sophisticated brand container. They feel accidental, handwritten, dashed off mid-thought. The casualness is calibrated. The texture is deliberately rough; the instincts underneath are sharp.

> **The formula: Short + casual + slightly emotionally loaded + a tiny bit wrong**

"A tiny bit wrong" is the key. The best subject lines have something slightly off — an unusual word, a contradiction, a phrase that shouldn't work but does. That wrongness creates the "wait, what does that mean?" pull that drives the open.

**The raccoon-in-cashmere filter:** Would a person who knows Alyssa personally recognize this as something she'd actually send? If yes, it's on-brand. If it sounds like it came from a swipe file or was A/B tested into blandness, it's too polished.

## The Big Picture: Opens vs. Clicks

**Your audience opens for Alyssa the person. They click for Alyssa the useful strategist.**

These are two different modes requiring two different kinds of subject lines. The best emails need both: a subject that sounds like you're sliding into their inbox with a half-finished thought, and a body that quickly turns that thought into a plan, fix, tool, replay, template, or offer.

The worst-case subject line is neither personal enough to feel human nor specific enough to feel useful.

The best-case subject line is one of two things:
> "Wait, what did she just say?"
> "Wait, I need that."

Those are different magic. Don't try to do both in the same subject line every time — you'll flatten both.

**The single most important data point in 351 broadcasts:**

The subject line that made the most people *click* was not the most personal, poetic, or branded. It was:

`🙅‍♀️ DON'T "grow your list" do this instead…` — **5.42% click rate, 1,209 total clicks**

When you want action, the strongest move is not softer. It's **sharper**.

The winning click formula: **call out the wrong default + promise the better system.** That is the entire Back of House philosophy in one subject line.

## Decision Framework: The Four Subject Line Jobs

Pick the subject line formula based on what the *email* needs to do — not what sounds most interesting to write. Identify the job first. Then pick the pattern.

### Job 1: Relationship / Trust
*Optimize for opens.*

The email is a story, a confession, a worldview piece, a Soft Sunday. There is no hard CTA — the goal is to be read and felt.

**Use:** DM voice · `Why I ___` · confession · identity shift · contradiction · Soft Sunday wrapper · `re:` language

**Example:** `Why I stopped trying to make content "perform"`

### Job 2: Resource / Training
*Optimize for clicks.*

The email contains something usable — a plan, template, checklist, class, replay, fix. The click is the point.

**Use:** High-value nouns (plan, master plan, checklist, fix) · `I FIXED your ___` · `DON'T ___, do this instead` · `[inside]` with a concrete noun

**Example:** `DON'T write another newsletter until you try this`

### Job 3: Sales / Promo
*Optimize for action.*

The email has an offer, a deadline, a coupon, or an open cart.

**Use:** Personal update frame + exact dollar amount · urgency bracket (`[Last Chance]`) · limited access framing (`for 5 of you`) · reason-why · `I saw you looking`

**Example:** `personal update & a very specific coupon`

### Job 4: Event / Replay
*Optimize for clarity.*

The subscriber is already primed — they signed up, they want the link. Cleverness can hurt here. Clarity wins.

**Use:** `re: tmrw's [thing]` · `📌 Class link {{ subscriber.first_name }}!` · `[MISSED IT?] Replay for ya` · `happening NOW`

**Example:** `📌 class link {{ subscriber.first_name }}!`

**The rule:** Identify the job before writing the line. A relationship email with a click-driving subject feels off. A resource email with a soft `re:` opener loses clicks because readers don't know there's something to grab.

## Quick Reference: Ingredients by Job

**Opens (relationship / trust):**
`re:` · `tmrw` · `meeting` · `Why I` · confession · identity shift · trend prediction · personal story · "what I learned" · "this isn't about X (but it kinda is)" · Soft Sunday wrapper · meme format (Me: / Also me:)

**Clicks (resource / training):**
`DON'T` · `do this instead` · `class link` · `replay` · `invite` · `plan` · `master plan` · `FIXED` · `coupon` · specific dollar value · `last chance` · `inside` (with a weighted noun) · named method (Slowly Viral / Anti-Social / Back of House)

**Action (sales / promo):**
exact dollar amount · `personal update` frame · reason why (something actually changed) · deadline with specificity · `for [small number] of you` · `I saw you looking` · `I changed my mind about [offer]`

**Link (event / replay):**
`re: tmrw's [thing]` · `class link {{ subscriber.first_name }}` · `starting now` · `link inside` · `replay expires tonight` · `[MISSED IT?]`

## The 11 Patterns

---

### Pattern 1: The DM Voice

**What it is:** Subject lines that sound like someone texting mid-thought — low polish, high intimacy.

**Examples:**
- `thought of you`
- `you coming?`
- `k embarrassing story from my 20s`
- `sry but.. You need to hear this [like NOW]`
- `oopsie, I've been gatekeeping?`

**Why it works:** The low-polish phrasing creates intimacy. It feels accidental, which makes it feel real. "oopsie" is not a word someone uses if they're trying to sound like an email marketer. "k embarrassing story from my 20s" sounds like iMessage, not Mailchimp. The informality is doing persuasion work.

**Performance note:** `oopsie, I've been gatekeeping?` appeared in the top click-rate list (1.67%). This is the only pattern that consistently wins on both opens and clicks — the intimacy pulls the open, the curiosity pulls the click.

**The rule:** Ask: "Would I write this exact sentence in a text to a close friend?" If yes, it's probably right. If it sounds like it belongs in a subject line swipe file, it's too polished.

---

### Pattern 2: The "re:" Fake Reply

**What it is:** Subject lines formatted to look like a reply in an existing email thread. The reader processes "re:" before the subject line registers — and instinctively assumes this is a response to something they sent.

**Top performers:**
- `re: tmrw's meeting` — **57.35% open rate** — #1 opener across all 351 broadcasts
- `re: tmrw's Soft CEO meetup` — 56.5% open rate — #3 opener
- `re: tmrw's planning appointment [🥖 Soft Ceo Session]` — 55.11% — #8 opener

**Why it works:** "re:" signals correspondence, not marketing. The open happens before the brain evaluates whether this email is worth reading. It bypasses the "is this worth opening?" gate entirely.

**The formula:** `re: tmrw's [event name or vague meeting noun]`

"tmrw's" does specific work — creates urgency without saying "URGENT" and anchors the email to a real calendar moment.

**⚠ Unsubscribe watch:** These emails ran above-average unsubscribe rates (~0.39–0.41%). Inbox-native language can create "wait, I didn't sign up for this" friction if the email body doesn't immediately honor the premise. Fix: the first line of the body should acknowledge the setup. Don't open with a pitch.

> `re: tmrw's meeting` → *"Ok, technically we don't have a meeting on the calendar — but I wanted to reach out before tomorrow's planning session with something I think you'll want..."*
> `re: tmrw's Soft CEO meetup` → *"Your Soft CEO session is tomorrow. Here's your tiny pre-meeting note..."*

**When to use:** Event day-before and day-of reminders only. These open rates are partly earned by subscriber intent (they signed up because they want the event) — don't try to engineer them on cold nurture emails.

**Template bank:**
- `re: tmrw's [event name]`
- `re: your [month] plan`
- `re: the thing we talked about`
- `re: your soft ceo reset`
- `tmrw: your [specific outcome]`
- `you coming tmrw?`
- `details for tmrw, {{ subscriber.first_name }}`
- `we're starting, link inside`
- `class starts in 1 hour`

---

### Pattern 3: The Soft Sunday Wrapper

**What it is:** A recurring brand container that signals: this is the cozy, personal, non-pitch email.

**The format:** `[front phrase] |🍦Soft Sunday`

**Performance note:** Soft Sunday appears in 7 of the top 20 open-rate performers. The wrapper is not overhead — it's brand equity. Subscribers who have trained themselves to open these bring their own lift. But the phrase *before* the wrapper still has to pull its weight. The wrapper closes the "what is this?" loop. The front phrase is still responsible for the hook.

| Weak front phrase | Strong front phrase |
|---|---|
| `content ideas \|🍦Soft Sunday` | `Me: I need ideas. Also me: forgets 87 I already had \|🍦Soft Sunday` |
| `social media update \|🍦Soft Sunday` | `Why I'm leaving social media \|🍦Soft Sunday` |
| `Black Friday planning \|🍦Soft Sunday` | `No BFri offer? No problem, Fix Inside \|🍦Soft Sunday` |

**The rule:** Keep the wrapper. But let the phrase before it stay weirder, shorter, and less helpful. The front phrase should aim for 2–5 words, emotionally loaded, slightly unfinished. The wrapper signals "cozy, personal, not a pitch" — the front phrase doesn't need to do that job too.

**Template bank for front phrases:**
- `Why I [counterintuitive decision]`
- `I failed`
- `I'm leaving [thing]`
- `Me: [X]. Also me: [Y]`
- `This isn't about [topic] (but it kinda is)`
- `[Weird two-word phrase]` — e.g. `Stoner Math`, `socks & sandal marketing`
- `End of Month CEO reflection`
- `What I learned from [specific experiment]`
- `What's shifting in [year]? Here's what I'm betting on`
- `Being [identity] & thriving.`
- `Hate [thing]? You'll love this.`

### Pattern 4: "Why I ___" Narrative Open

**What it is:** A first-person declaration that opens a story without completing it.

**Top performers:**
- `Why I burned down a perfectly good business (on purpose)` — **56.08% open rate AND 1.7% click rate** — appears in BOTH the top openers and top click-rate lists. Extremely rare. This is the gold standard.
- `🐌 Why i'm leaving social media |🍦Soft Sunday` — 56.59% open rate, #2 overall opener

**Why it works:** "Why I ___" is a story premise, not a headline. It creates an immediate protagonist (Alyssa), an implied conflict, and a resolution the reader doesn't have yet. The parenthetical `(on purpose)` layers in Pattern 6 (contradiction) — it makes what sounds like a crisis into something intentional, which is disorienting in the best way.

**What makes a strategic confession vs. an emotional one:** Alyssa's audience responds to confessions where the decision *looks wrong from the outside but has calculated logic underneath.* Not "I was scared" — but "I made a business decision that looked weird, and here's the deeper logic." That's catnip for smart people who are tired of being talked down to.

**The rule:** Works best when the action in the subject line sounds like a mistake.

**Template bank:**
- `Why I ___ed my perfectly good [thing]`
- `Why I stopped ___ing (and what happened instead)`
- `Why I ___ on purpose`
- `Why I stopped doing [normal business thing]`
- `Why I rebuilt [thing that was technically working]`
- `Why I'm leaving [platform / strategy / identity]`
- `Why I stopped trying to [expected goal]`
- `Why I changed my mind about [popular tactic]`
- `Why I'm making [counterintuitive decision]`
- `Why I'm no longer measuring [vanity metric]`

### Pattern 5: Confession + Personal Update

**What it is:** Subject lines that open with a personal admission or frame an update as news rather than marketing.

**Top performers:**
- `I failed |🍦Soft Sunday` — 44.95% open rate, **341 total clicks** — strong for a cozy Sunday email
- `oopsie, I've been gatekeeping?` — 1.67% click rate, top click-rate list
- `Personal Update & a $12,212 coupon 🫢🤯` — 1.75% click rate, top click-rate list

**The two modes:**
- **Story-led:** `I failed` — promises a lesson, not a product. Use for Soft Sunday and nurture.
- **Update-led:** `Personal Update & a $X coupon` — the update frame earns the promotional payload. The specific number adds the click pull.

**The rule:** Lead with the confession before the offer. The admission earns the attention; the offer is the reward for staying. Don't flip the order.

**"Personal Update" — don't waste it:** Reserve for emails where something actually changed.

Good uses:
- You changed the offer or added a bonus
- You opened a pay plan or made a one-time exception
- You made a decision after DMs
- You're retiring or changing something
- You want to explain *why* a sale exists
- There's behind-the-scenes context the reader would want

Don't use it for routine promos where nothing is actually updating.

**Template bank:**
- `personal update & a weird little coupon`
- `personal update, {{ subscriber.first_name }}`
- `a small update and a big coupon`
- `I changed my mind about [offer]`
- `ok, tiny plot twist`
- `this changed the plan a little`
- `for 5 of you` *(very limited access framing)*
- `I saw you looking — here's [discount]`

### Pattern 6: Contradiction + Odd Phrasing

**What it is:** Subject lines where something is slightly off — a contradiction, a weird word, a phrase that shouldn't scan but does.

**Examples from the dataset:**
- `try this DIRTY marketing plan? 🌶️` — "dirty" in a slow/cozy marketing brand
- `ur (not)NEW march plan inside` — the paradox makes you want to resolve it
- `Stoner Math` — two words that create a category that shouldn't exist
- `socks & sandal marketing` — a fashion crime applied to business
- `oopsie, I've been gatekeeping?` — the question mark on a confession creates funny guilt
- `when your audience ≄ clients` — a math symbol doing what a word can't
- `This isn't about email (but it kinda is)` — explicit self-contradiction

**Conceptually crooked ≠ vague curiosity.** This is the key distinction between Alyssa's contradiction style and generic curiosity-gap copy. Vague curiosity gives the reader nothing to hold onto. Conceptually crooked gives them enough to *feel* the tension — but not enough to resolve it without opening.

| Vague curiosity | Conceptually crooked |
|---|---|
| `You need to see this` | `This isn't about email (but it kinda is)` |
| `I changed my mind` | `Why I burned down a perfectly good business (on purpose)` |
| `Something interesting happened` | `ur (not)NEW march plan inside` |
| `You won't believe this` | `Hate marketing? You'll love this.` |

The crooked version gives the reader a specific tension to sit with. They know the category. They just can't resolve the contradiction without opening.

**The rule:** The subject line opens the door. The preview text stands in the doorway. The weird phrase is the hook — preview text adds context without explaining the joke.

### Pattern 7: The Anti-Advice Reframe

**What it is:** Subject lines that call out a common piece of marketing advice, reject it, and promise a better system inside.

**The #1 click subject line in the entire 351-broadcast dataset:**

`🙅‍♀️ DON'T "grow your list" do this instead…`
- 16,088 recipients
- 34.41% open rate *(unremarkable — not a top opener)*
- **5.42% click rate** *(more than double the #2 click rate)*
- **1,209 total clicks** *(most total clicks in the dataset)*

The open rate is not special. The click rate is extraordinary.

**Why it works — three things simultaneously:**
1. Attacks advice the audience already half-suspects is wrong
2. Creates a replacement loop — "do this instead" implies a better answer exists
3. Primes the click before the open — readers arrive knowing exactly what category they're in: "I may be doing this wrong, and she has a fix"

**Why it matters for the brand:** The anti-advice format is the Back of House worldview in a subject line. BOH exists because mainstream marketing advice is often wrong for creative entrepreneurs. Every `DON'T "X" do this instead` subject line is a micro-proof-of-concept for the brand's entire thesis.

**Use it with restraint.** It's powerful because it's not overused. If every email is "DON'T do X," the pattern gets dulled.

**Template bank:**
- `DON'T "grow your audience" do this instead…`
- `DON'T write more content. Fix this first.`
- `DON'T launch again until you have this`
- `DON'T make another freebie yet`
- `DON'T "nurture your list" like this`
- `DON'T post more. Reuse this instead.`
- `STOP trying to get more leads`
- `STOP marketing like this`
- `The list-growth advice I'd ignore`
- `The marketing advice making your buyers colder`
- `The [tactic] advice I'd ignore right now`

### Pattern 8: "I Fixed Your ___"

**What it is:** Subject lines where Alyssa has already done the work — identified the problem, built the fix, and packaged it for the reader.

**Top performer:**
`🪄 I FIXED your email list (the plan inside) | 🍦Soft Sundays`
— 52.36% open rate · **1.38% click rate · 358 total clicks** — strong for a Soft Sunday nurture email

**Why it works:** Positions Alyssa as proactive, specific, and already doing the work *for* them. Matches a deep audience psychology: her readers often want someone to tell them what to do, organize the mess, and hand them the steps. "I FIXED" does exactly that framing. It's also slightly cheeky — "I FIXED YOUR email list" implies she's been thinking about their specific problem, which is very concierge-brand-consistent.

**Note:** The all-caps "FIXED" is doing work — it's slightly dramatic, slightly funny. Keep the caps for impact.

**Template bank:**
- `I FIXED your welcome sequence`
- `I FIXED your promo plan`
- `I FIXED your Pinterest strategy`
- `I FIXED your email list`
- `I FIXED your "I don't know what to send" problem`
- `I FIXED your content calendar`
- `I FIXED your Q4 plan`
- `I FIXED your [specific problem]`
- `I FIXED the [common mess]`

### Pattern 9: High-Value Click Nouns

**What it is:** Certain nouns reliably drive clicks because they signal a usable, concrete deliverable — not just a thought or a story.

**The insight:** Alyssa's audience wants ideas. They *click* for plans.

Story-forward subjects win opens. What moves people to click is the promise of something they can *use* — and certain nouns have trained the audience to recognize "this email has a thing to grab."

**Confirmed high-click nouns from the dataset:**
`plan` · `master plan` · `checklist` · `to-do list` · `fix` / `fixed` · `replay` · `class link` · `template` · `swipe` · `coupon` (with specificity) · `invite` · `steps`

**Examples from top performers:**
- `your 2026 MASTER PLAN inside💸` — 1.69% click rate
- `Your 2026 business plan inside 📈` — 1.41% click rate
- `ur (not)NEW march plan inside` — 1.46% click rate, 496 total clicks
- `Your Pre-Plan Checklist for 2025` — 457 total clicks
- `Your Q4 pre-sale to-do list` — 408 total clicks

**The weight rule:** "Inside" is not a noun with weight. "Plan", "checklist", "master plan" are.

| Low-weight | High-weight |
|---|---|
| `details inside` | `your Q4 to-do list inside` |
| `coupon inside` | `a weird $12,212 coupon` |
| `free thing inside` | `your 2026 master plan inside` |
| `plan inside` | `your [month] reset plan inside` |

**How to use:** Build backward from the noun. What is the most concrete, usable thing in this email? Name that thing. The subject line's job is to make the noun feel like something they specifically need.

**Template bank:**
- `your [month] plan inside`
- `your [season] pre-plan checklist`
- `the plan for [specific annoying problem]`
- `the [offer] to-do list`
- `your low-maintenance [goal] plan`
- `no [thing]? no problem, fix inside`
- `the [time period] plan I'd use now`
- `[problem] checklist inside`
- `your [result] master plan`

### Pattern 10: The INSIDE Pattern

**What it is:** A bracketed or trailing descriptor that names what's in the email.

**Examples:** `[PLAN INSIDE]` · `[replay inside]` · `[INVITE inside]` · `[$500 INSIDE]` · `free pinterest class inside`

**When it works:** When what's inside is genuinely desirable and not implied by the subject line. `[$500 INSIDE]` works because $500 is clear, concrete value. `[replay inside]` works when the subscriber wanted the replay.

**When it weakens:** When paired with a subject that already over-explains. "Inside" is redundant when the subject line already told you what's there.

| Weak | Strong |
|---|---|
| `[PLAN INSIDE] clock out by summer` | `ur (not)NEW march plan inside` |
| `telling you what to do [list inside]` | `thought of you` |

**The rule:** Use `[inside]` to close the loop *after* you've opened curiosity — not to reassure, and not when the subject already explained the contents. Works best when the noun in front of it has weight (see Pattern 9).

### Pattern 11: Personalization as Concierge Energy

**What it is:** Subject lines that feel hand-picked for the individual reader.

**Examples:**
- `{{ subscriber.first_name }}'s Custom Pinterest plan`
- `📌 Class link {{ subscriber.first_name }}!`
- `thought of you`
- `you coming?`

**Why it works for this brand:** Back of House is built on a concierge/restaurant metaphor — "I made this for you, the chef prepared this for your table." Personalization is brand-consistent, not just a conversion tactic.

**Soft personalization (no merge tag required):**
These feel personal without using a first name:
- `thought of you`
- `saved this for you`
- `this felt like yours`
- `made you a tiny plan`
- `put this on your little tray`
- `your next move, probably`

**Direction:** The implied personalization (no merge tag) is underexplored and has high potential in this brand register. Test it.

## Preview Text

**Current state:** Many emails have blank preview text — the biggest missed leverage point in the whole operation. Treating preview text as a second subject line (rather than a description) could lift open rates without changing anything else.

**The best examples from the dataset:**

| Subject | Preview | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| `Stoner Math` | `girl math's chill cousin (kinda weird, kinda deep bro)` | Adds context without explaining; "bro" is funny and wrong in the best way |
| `you coming?` | `ends in 2 hrs ⏰` | Subject is soft; preview hardens it with pure urgency |
| `thought of you` | `Hey {{ subscriber.first_name }} ..` | The trailing ellipsis creates a lean-in effect |
| `k embarrassing story from my 20s` | `the time i went viral (for the wrong thing 🙈)` | Subject opens the door; preview adds a specific, unexpected detail |
| `oopsie, I've been gatekeeping?` | `lemme fix that lol!` | Consistent register; "lemme fix that" creates forward momentum |

**The three jobs preview text can do:**

**A. Add context without explaining**
- Good: `girl math's chill cousin (kinda weird, kinda deep bro)`
- Bad: `new workshop! (save ur seat)` — brochure-clean

**B. Create urgency**
- Good: `ends in 2 hrs ⏰`
- Bad: `your COZY biz plan inside` — summarizes instead of pressuring

**C. Make it personal**
- Good: `Hey {{ subscriber.first_name }} ..`
- Bad: `write cool newsletters (quick)` — generic

**The rule:** If the subject line is a locked door, preview text is you putting your ear against it and hearing something. Not enough to know everything — just enough to need to know more.

## By Email Type

### Soft Sunday

**What it is:** Weekly nurture email. Personal, cozy, not a pitch.

**Dominant formula:** `[weird/emotional phrase, 2–5 words] |🍦Soft Sunday`

**Front phrase rules:**
- The wrapper already signals "cozy, personal" — the front phrase doesn't need to do that job too
- Keep it weird, short, slightly unfinished
- The wrapper closes the "what is this?" loop; the front phrase creates the hook

**Open rate benchmark from data:** Top Soft Sundays cluster 53–57%.

**What works in the front phrase:**
- Confession or personal moment: `I failed`, `I'm Leaving`
- Absurdist phrase: `Stoner Math`, `socks & sandal marketing`
- Narrative hook: `Why I burned down a perfectly good business (on purpose)`, `Why i'm leaving social media`
- Meme format: `Me: I need ideas. Also me: forgets 87 I already had`
- Contradiction: `This isn't about email (but it kinda is)`

**Default formula:**
- **Subject:** Weird phrase, 2–6 words — no explanation, slightly unfinished
- **Preview:** Specific little clue — funny, intimate, or emotionally loaded
- No full explanation until they open

**Template bank for front phrases:**
- `Why I [counterintuitive decision]`
- `I failed`
- `I'm leaving [thing]`
- `Me: [X]. Also me: [Y]`
- `This isn't about [topic] (but it kinda is)`
- `[Weird two-word phrase]`
- `End of Month CEO reflection`
- `What I learned from [specific experiment]`
- `What's shifting in [year]? Here's what I'm betting on`
- `Being [identity] & thriving.`

### Webinar / Event Reminder

**What it is:** Day-before or day-of email for a live event — webinar, cohort session, planning call.

**Default formula:** `re: tmrw's [event name]`

**Confirmed variants from the data:**
- `re: tmrw's meeting` — 57.35% (best single opener in dataset)
- `re: tmrw's Soft CEO meetup` — 56.5%
- `re: tmrw's planning appointment [🥖 Soft Ceo Session]` — 55.11%
- `Soft Ceo Session happening NOW! ☁️` — 53.93% (day-of live notification)
- `tomorrow: stable April vibes` — 53.93% (softer vibes-led day-before variant)

**When to use which:**
- Day before → `re: tmrw's [thing]`
- Same day / live → `[Event] happening NOW! ☁️`
- Cozy event, brand-register day-before → `tomorrow: [vibe]`

**Note:** These open rates are partly earned by subscriber intent. Don't try to engineer them on cold nurture emails.

**Template bank:**
- `re: tmrw's [event name]`
- `re: your [session type]`
- `tomorrow: [vibe or outcome]`
- `[Event name] happening NOW! ☁️`
- `your link for today`
- `details for tmrw, {{ subscriber.first_name }}`
- `class starts in 1 hour`
- `we're starting, link inside`

### Launch / Sale Email

**What it is:** Promotional email with a direct offer — sale, coupon, cart open, new product.

**The money + drama + specificity rule:** The strongest sale subjects lead with a story or an unusually specific number — not the discount itself.

- `Personal Update & a $12,212 coupon 🫢🤯` — the odd number creates "wait, what happened?" energy. A round $12,000 would be flatter.
- Why the odd number works: it implies a *reason* for that exact amount. Something priced to match a specific value or story feels real, not manufactured.

**What drives clicks on launch emails:**
- Dollar specificity: `Personal Update & a $12,212 coupon 🫢🤯` — 1.75% click rate
- Urgency bracket: `[🎥Last Chance] Replay: Slowly Viral 📌` — 1.97% click rate
- Invite frame: `[INVITE inside] Slowly Viral 💌 free pinterest class inside` — 2.25% click rate
- Confession hybrid: `oopsie, I've been gatekeeping?` — 1.67% click rate

**Template bank:**
- `Personal Update & a $[odd specific number] coupon 🫢🤯`
- `I saw you looking — here's [discount]`
- `for [small number] of you, [access]`
- `the weird $[amount] coupon`
- `ok, tiny plot twist [offer reason]`
- `a low-maintenance [year] is calling`
- `I changed my mind about [offer]`
- `this changed the plan a little`

### Replay Email

**What it is:** Post-webinar email delivering the replay link.

**Formula:** Bracket the format + name the replay + add urgency. The audience is already warm and wants the link — give them a clear signal it's inside.

**Confirmed performers:**
- `[🎥Last Chance] Replay: Slowly Viral 📌` — 1.97% click rate
- `[MISSED IT?] Replay for ya for a limited time 💻` — 1.41% click rate
- `[replay inside] Slowly Viral + $1k off BOH` — 18.54% click rate (836 recipients, very warm segment)

**Template bank:**
- `[🎥Last Chance] Replay: [event name]`
- `[MISSED IT?] Replay for ya`
- `replay is live, but not for long`
- `[replay inside] [event name]`
- `last chance to watch this`
- `replay expires tonight`

## The Sweet Spot Formula

> **Short + casual + slightly emotionally loaded + a tiny bit wrong**

Subject lines with the most pull feel like they were written while holding coffee, avoiding a task, and laughing at your own thought. The ones that feel "email marketing optimized" are less interesting.

**Subject + preview pairings as direction:**

| Subject | Preview |
|---|---|
| `the potato chip problem` | `why your content is making everyone snacky but broke` |
| `tiny plan, big consequences` | `this is where the money starts behaving` |
| `the beige email incident` | `I regret to inform you the robot got involved` |

**What these have in common:**
- Subject: weird noun/phrase combo that creates a specific-but-undefined image
- Preview: one sentence that reframes without explaining — adds emotional stakes or a specific consequence

## Patterns to Avoid

**1. Over-explaining** — names the format AND the promise in one line
- Weak: `[PLAN INSIDE] clock out by summer |🍦Soft Sunday` — tells you the format, outcome, and container
- Better: `clock out by summer` or `I made you a summer plan` — one idea, not three

**2. Generic "inside" without a weighted noun**
- Weak: `details inside` · `coupon inside` · `invite inside`
- Better: `your Q4 pre-sale to-do list` · `a weird $12,212 coupon` · `you coming tmrw?`
- "Inside" has no power without a concrete noun in front of it. See Pattern 9.

**3. Overlong subjects on launch or sales emails**
Long subjects can work for resource emails (they read like a mini-promise). For launches, shorter and more specific usually wins.
- Long: `Your Exact Fall To-Do List for a Chill Holiday & Fiery Q1 |🍦 Soft Sunday` — skimmable as information, not as curiosity
- Shorter: `your Q4 reset plan inside` — cleaner tension

**4. Urgency without a story or reason**
- Weak: `LAST CHANCE` · `SALE ENDS TONIGHT`
- Better: `I saw you looking — here's 60% off`
- Better: `personal message, {{ subscriber.first_name }}...`
- Better: `a low-maintenance 2025 is calling`
- Urgency works when there's a human behind it.

**5. "Sale Alert" without a twist**
`SALE ALERT [product name]` functions on timing, but it's the least interesting version. Wrap the sale in a narrative reason or specificity and it will outperform.

**6. Preview text that summarizes instead of extends**
- Weak: `your COZY biz plan inside` — describes the email
- Weak: `new workshop! (save ur seat)` — brochure energy
- Better: treat preview as a second subject line doing a *different* job from the subject

**7. Anything that sounds A/B-tested into blandness**
Apply the raccoon-in-cashmere filter: would someone who knows Alyssa personally recognize this as something she'd send? If not, it's too polished.
