# Carousel Winning Flow Report

Goal: give the content team simple, memorable carousel shapes backed by Alyssa's real examples, so an offer plus an idea becomes a persuasive post instead of a generic summary.

Use this file forward. The examples prove the pattern. The instructions tell the next writer what each slide has to do for a new offer, content pillar, and buying belief.

Old drafts preserved here: [[Studio/Analysis/carousel-winning-flow-report-draft-2026-07-17]], [[Studio/Analysis/carousel-winning-flow-report-draft-2026-07-17-layered]], and [[Studio/Analysis/carousel-winning-flow-report-draft-2026-07-17-pressure-framework]].

## The Working Brief

Every carousel brief should name four things.

`offer + buying belief + framework + hook`

Offer: what we are selling, seeding, or inviting them into.

Buying belief: what the reader needs to believe before the CTA makes sense.

Framework: the kind of argument the carousel is making.

Hook: the cover line that makes the argument worth swiping into.

Hook comes last. The cover should be the sharpest expression of the argument, not a clever line the post has to chase.

## The Clean Model

The top posts mostly share one spine.

`cover hook -> second hook -> old way/problem -> proof or validation -> teaching -> turn -> CTA`

The difference is the kind of argument the idea needs.

## The Pressure Check

Do not brief pressure as another category unless it helps.

Use it as a thinking check before choosing the hook.

Market pressure asks: what changed outside the reader?

Inner pressure asks: what old premise is making the reader blame themselves?

Most posts lean one way, but the writer does not need to announce it. The pressure just tells the first few slides what kind of problem to build.

Market pressure usually adds: shift, proof, cost, implication, better path.

Inner pressure usually adds: false premise, cost, validation, permission, better model.

## The Five Frameworks

### 1. House Report

Meaning: trends, market shifts, patterns Alyssa is noticing, things changing in the house of online business.

Examples: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-15-slow-marketing-trends-pt-1]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-20-whats-actually-shifting-in-2026-pt-2]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-29-smart-founders-going-low-maintenance]]

Pressure: usually market pressure.

Forward sequence:

1. Name the shift the reader can already feel but has not fully sorted yet.
2. Establish why Alyssa can call the shift.
3. Confirm the problem before teaching the fix.
4. Name the old move that is getting weaker.
5. Show what the old move will cost next.
6. Teach the implication, not just the fact.
7. Give one small action that lowers the reader's workload now.
8. CTA to the tool or freebie that helps them take that first action.

Required middle:

- 2 proof or teaching slides.
- 1 implication slide for the reader's next move.
- 1 lower-maintenance alternative.

Old belief it attacks: the thing that worked last year is still the safe plan.

New belief it installs: the market has moved, and Alyssa noticed before the reader had language for it.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-15-slow-marketing-trends-pt-1]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-20-whats-actually-shifting-in-2026-pt-2]], and [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-29-smart-founders-going-low-maintenance]] all make the reader feel early to a shift, then hand them a lower-maintenance next move.

### 2. Old Way / New Way

Meaning: the thing the reader has been doing made sense, but it is costing them now. The post makes the old way feel too expensive, then gives the better model.

Examples: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-24-swapped-instagram-for-pinterest]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-20-whats-actually-shifting-in-2026-pt-2]]

Pressure: market pressure when the old way broke because conditions changed. Inner pressure when the old way is making the reader feel like the problem.

Forward sequence:

1. Name the false premise or old belief the reader already suspects is failing.
2. Steelman why that belief used to make sense.
3. Show why it is breaking now.
4. Agitate the cost of that belief.
5. Validate the reader's experience so they do not feel blamed.
6. Ask the better question that makes the old belief feel incomplete.
7. Teach the calmer model in 2 or 3 parts.
8. CTA to the offer that helps them leave the old pattern.

Required middle:

- 1 slide that protects the reader from feeling stupid for choosing the old way.
- 1 cost slide that makes staying feel expensive.
- 1 better-question or pivot slide before the fix.

Old belief it attacks: more of the old behavior will eventually pay off.

New belief it installs: the better model is not more effort in the same place. It is a different system.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-24-swapped-instagram-for-pinterest]] protects the reader from feeling stupid for using the old way, then makes the old way feel too expensive to keep choosing. [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-20-whats-actually-shifting-in-2026-pt-2]] does the same thing inside a House Report.

### 3. How To

Meaning: teach the plan, system, recipe, product, seasonal method, or operating order.

Examples: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-06-pinterest-marketing-for-beginners]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-10-get-clients-from-pinterest]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-19-pinterest-sugar-mama]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-01-80-days-until-summer-90d-clock-in]]

Pressure: market pressure if the plan matters because something changed. Inner pressure if the plan matters because the old process felt too hard, precious, scattered, or confusing.

Forward sequence:

1. Make a specific outcome promise.
2. Name the hidden opportunity, false premise, or missing piece most people overlook.
3. Show why the old way makes the result feel harder than it should.
4. Give the reader a simpler operating order.
5. Teach the method in 3 to 4 parts.
6. Add proof, examples, screenshots, or product views so it feels usable.
7. Make the method feel easier than expected.
8. CTA to the product, class, dashboard, or resource that packages the method.

Required middle:

- 3 to 4 teachable parts.
- Each part needs a job and a concrete instruction.
- The middle needs a memory device: metaphor, numbered steps, product screenshots, seasonal container, or proof examples.

Old belief it attacks: this result is complicated, random, platform-dependent, or too much work to repeat.

New belief it installs: this result follows a system when the pieces sit in the right order.

How To versions:

- Metaphor Plan: teach the steps through a named metaphor world. Proof: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-10-get-clients-from-pinterest]]
- Product Plan: teach through the product. The product can appear early only when it is doing the teaching. Proof: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-06-pinterest-marketing-for-beginners]]
- Proof-First Plan: prove the weird hook or underestimated opportunity first, then teach the plan. Proof: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-19-pinterest-sugar-mama]]
- Clock-In Plan: use time, seasonality, open loops, and future-self payoff as the container. Proof: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-01-80-days-until-summer-90d-clock-in]]

Proof pattern: the How To posts all give a useful plan before the CTA. The style changes, but the job is the same: make the method feel concrete enough that the offer becomes the easiest container.

### 4. Hot Take

Meaning: disruptive, contrarian, receipt-heavy, or spicy. The post makes a claim the reader half-suspects, then proves it hard enough that the old belief collapses.

Example: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-03-organic-marketing-is-dying]]

Pressure: usually market pressure, with an inner-pressure relief beat so the reader does not feel blamed.

Forward sequence:

1. State the scary or disruptive thing plainly.
2. Validate what the reader is already experiencing.
3. Stack proof until the old belief has nowhere to hide.
4. Kill the obvious escape route.
5. Name who benefits if the reader keeps obeying the broken system.
6. Pivot into what Alyssa would build instead.
7. Teach the rebuild in 3 proof-backed parts so the reader is not left with only outrage.
8. CTA to the bigger offer because the small fix would not be enough.

Required middle:

- At least 3 receipts before the rebuild.
- One villain or system diagnosis that protects the reader from blame.
- 3 proof-backed rebuild parts.

Old belief it attacks: this is just another annoying update or normal business problem.

New belief it installs: the system is built to keep extracting effort, so the smart move is to build a back end that keeps working without feeding it every day.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-03-organic-marketing-is-dying]] shows how to move from alarm to proof to villain to rebuild. Future decks should use this shape only when the offer can carry a bigger strategic fix, like [[Library/Offers/back-of-house/MAP|Back of House]].

### 5. For The Girls

Meaning: identity, permission, brand world, self-trust, "I needed someone to say this."

Examples: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-22-wasting-time-for-the-girls]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-14-valentines-for-the-girls]]

Pressure: inner pressure.

Forward sequence:

1. Name the guilt or pressure.
2. Give the reader a more generous interpretation of that behavior.
3. Repeat short permission lines in the same visual world.
4. Include one line that turns the whole post into a principle.
5. Pause on the brand.
6. End with a question, sign-off, or soft invitation.

Required middle:

- Short permission lines, not tactical teaching.
- One principle line that gives the post a spine.
- No forced hard sell.

Old belief it attacks: taking time, changing your mind, or moving slowly means you are lazy.

New belief it installs: the slower choice can be the higher-standard choice.

FTG rule: no tactical teaching body, no hard CTA, no forced offer.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-22-wasting-time-for-the-girls]] and [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-14-valentines-for-the-girls]] show how to make the reader feel named without teaching a framework.

## Hook Styles

Hook is last. Pick it after the buying belief and framework are clear.

| Hook style | Forward job | Source example |
|---|---|---|
| Series Title | Make the post feel like part of a larger body of thinking | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-15-slow-marketing-trends-pt-1]] |
| Identity Swap | Show the reader a decision that changes the game | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-24-swapped-instagram-for-pinterest]] |
| Contrarian Claim | Say the thing the reader suspects but has not said plainly | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-03-organic-marketing-is-dying]] |
| Provocative Reframe | Use a strange comparison that forces the reader to resolve it | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-19-pinterest-sugar-mama]] |
| Identity Flattery | Name the smart version of the reader before teaching them | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-29-smart-founders-going-low-maintenance]] |
| Permission Slip | Release the reader from a pressure they secretly resent | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-22-wasting-time-for-the-girls]] |
| Aspirational Utility | Promise a useful tool that feels desirable, not remedial | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-06-pinterest-marketing-for-beginners]] |
| Specific Promise | Promise a concrete result with enough specificity to demand proof | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-10-get-clients-from-pinterest]] |

Hook rule: every cover must do one of three jobs.

1. Name an identity the reader wants.
2. Make a claim they suspect is true but nobody is saying.
3. Promise a specific transformation.

## Carousel Anatomy

### Cover Page

Word range: 4 to 10 words in the real winners. Aim for 7 or fewer unless the time anchor or specific promise earns the extra words.

Forward job: create a reason to swipe.

Must do one: identity, suspected truth, specific transformation, time window, visual joke, permission.

Never does: vague topic label, generic tips, question-mark cover, tidy reframe that resolves itself.

### Slide 2

Headline: usually 4 to 10 words.

Body: 0 to 55 words depending on the lane.

Forward job: give the reason to keep reading before the teaching starts.

Can use: credibility, stakes, false belief, hidden opportunity, product philosophy, proof that the claim has receipts.

Never does: generic teaching before the reader knows why it matters.

### Problem And Stakes

Usually 1 to 3 slides after the cover.

Forward job: make the old way feel specific, costly, or annoying before the fix appears.

Leaves them with: that is the thing I have been feeling.

### Middle Slides

Usually 4 to 6 teaching or proof slides.

Headline: usually 3 to 7 words.

Body: 25 to 65 words when teaching.

Forward job: teach one named move per slide, with enough detail to use.

Each slide needs: a claim, a detail, and why it matters.

Never does: thin label plus filler sentence.

Always leaves them with: one useful thing a non-buyer could act on.

### The Turn

Usually lands around slide 5 to 8.

Forward job: change the explanation.

Can be: bait-and-switch, system diagnosis, villain diagnosis, better question, personal decision, time-window payoff.

Never does: rely on a pretty background switch to carry the point.

### CTA

Usually final 1 to 2 slides.

Forward job: give the next step that solves the exact problem the deck just made obvious.

The CTA is a thread, not only a final slide.

Thread shape:

1. Early seed if the reader is already ready.
2. Mid-deck signal that the method has a container.
3. Final comment-word or offer reveal.

Never does: ask for a product that the post did not earn.

## Non-Negotiables

- The brief names offer, buying belief, framework, and hook.
- Pick one framework.
- Do the pressure check before writing the first few slides.
- Problem confirmation comes before solution.
- Emotional validation belongs in the middle when the pressure is inner.
- Implication belongs in the middle when the pressure is market.
- Villain assignment protects the reader from blame.
- Product can show early only when the product teaches.
- The CTA must solve the problem the deck made obvious.
- Teaching slides need density: real numbers, examples, screenshots, steps, coined terms.
- The old way should feel less desirable by the end of the post.
