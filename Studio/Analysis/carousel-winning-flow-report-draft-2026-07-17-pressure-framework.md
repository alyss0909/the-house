# Carousel Winning Flow Report

Goal: give the content team clear buyer-psychology carousel frameworks, backed by Alyssa's real examples, so an offer plus an angle becomes a persuasive post instead of a generic summary.

Use this file forward. The examples prove the pattern. The instructions tell the next writer what each slide has to do for a new offer, content pillar, and buying belief.

Old drafts preserved here: [[Studio/Analysis/carousel-winning-flow-report-draft-2026-07-17]] and [[Studio/Analysis/carousel-winning-flow-report-draft-2026-07-17-layered]].

## The Working Brief

Every carousel brief should name five things.

`offer + buying belief + framework + pressure + hook style`

Offer: what we are selling, seeding, or inviting them into.

Buying belief: what the reader needs to believe before the CTA makes sense.

Framework: the full carousel recipe.

Pressure: why the post feels persuasive now.

Hook style: how the cover earns the first swipe.

Example:

Offer: Bingeable Newsletters

Buying belief: I do not need gourmet email effort. I need a repeatable structure people want to keep opening.

Framework: Recipe System

Pressure: Inner Pressure

Hook style: Provocative Reframe

Cover idea: "sry julia child emails are out"

## The Clean Model

Framework is the writing recipe.

Pressure is the reason the recipe feels persuasive.

Hook style is the cover job.

Buying belief is the thing the post has to make true before the CTA.

Do not ask a writer to combine two full sequences. Pick one framework, then use pressure to shape the problem build and the middle.

The two old high-level analyses are not writer-facing flows anymore. Their best work lives here as pressure types.

## Pressure Types

Before writing, ask: what pressure makes this post persuasive?

### Market Pressure

Persuasive force: the old move is less safe because conditions changed.

Choose this pressure when the post needs to make the reader feel early to a shift, late to a risk, or ready for a smarter next move.

Forward sequence:

1. Name the shift, threat, or pattern the reader can already feel.
2. Establish why Alyssa can call it.
3. Confirm the problem before teaching the fix.
4. Show what the old move will cost next.
5. Teach the implication, not just the fact.
6. Give the better path.
7. Make the offer feel like the first useful step after the analysis.

Old belief it attacks: the old plan is still the safest plan.

New belief it installs: the market moved, so the smarter move is to build around what lasts now.

Required middle:

- At least 2 proof or teaching slides.
- At least 1 implication slide that says what the proof means for the reader.
- At least 1 alternative slide that makes the next move feel lighter, smarter, or more durable.

CTA rule: the ask comes after real analysis. The offer cannot appear as the answer before the reader believes the problem.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-15-slow-marketing-trends-pt-1]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-20-whats-actually-shifting-in-2026-pt-2]], and [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-29-smart-founders-going-low-maintenance]] make the reader feel early to a shift. [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-03-organic-marketing-is-dying]] uses the same pressure with heavier receipts and a bigger offer.

### Inner Pressure

Persuasive force: the old premise is making the reader blame themselves.

Choose this pressure when the post needs to make the reader feel relieved, called out without being shamed, or ready to stop performing the hard version.

Forward sequence:

1. Hook with identity, a personal switch, a weird claim, or a specific promise.
2. Name the false premise or old belief.
3. Agitate the cost of that belief.
4. Validate the reader's experience so they do not feel blamed.
5. Make the pivot.
6. Teach the better model in 2 to 4 concrete parts.
7. CTA to the resource, product, class, or offer that helps them make the pivot.

Old belief it attacks: the thing they have been trying is the responsible thing to keep doing.

New belief it installs: the calmer, stranger, more specific move is actually the more strategic move.

Required middle:

- One emotional validation slide in the middle third.
- One moment that protects the reader from feeling stupid for choosing the old way.
- One pivot line that makes the better way feel obvious.

CTA rule: the ask should feel like the next step after the pivot, not a product interrupt.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-24-swapped-instagram-for-pinterest]] protects the reader from the old visibility belief before offering a different model. [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-10-get-clients-from-pinterest]] makes the new method feel simple enough to try. [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-01-80-days-until-summer-90d-clock-in]] turns time pressure into a focused work shift.

## How Pressure Folds Into Framework

The framework owns the slide recipe.

Pressure changes what the problem build says.

Market Pressure asks: what changed outside the reader?

Inner Pressure asks: what false premise is making the reader blame themselves?

For any framework:

- Market Pressure adds shift, proof, cost, implication, then alternative.
- Inner Pressure adds false premise, cost, validation, pivot, then better model.

Do not write both pressure builds. Pick the one that makes the buying belief feel most necessary.

## Hook Styles

Hook style is not the full flow. It is the cover job.

| Hook style | Forward job | Source example |
|---|---|---|
| Series Title | Make the post feel like part of a larger body of thinking | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-15-slow-marketing-trends-pt-1]] |
| Identity Swap | Show the reader a decision that changes the game | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-24-swapped-instagram-for-pinterest]] |
| Contrarian Claim | Say the thing the reader suspects but has not said plainly | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-03-organic-marketing-is-dying]] |
| Provocative Reframe | Use a strange comparison that forces the reader to resolve it | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-19-pinterest-sugar-mama]] |
| Identity Flattery | Name the smart version of the reader before teaching them | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-29-smart-founders-going-low-maintenance]] |
| Permission Slip | Release the reader from a pressure they secretly resent | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-22-wasting-time-for-the-girls]] |
| Aspirational Utility | Promise a useful tool that feels desirable, not remedial | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-06-pinterest-marketing-for-beginners]] |
| Specific Promise | Promise a concrete result with enough specificity to demand proof | [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-10-get-clients-from-pinterest]] |

Hook rule: every cover must do one of three jobs.

1. Name an identity the reader wants.
2. Make a claim they suspect is true but nobody is saying.
3. Promise a specific transformation.

## Frameworks

Frameworks are the actual carousel recipes. The team should choose one framework per post.

### 1. Market Shift Report

Examples: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-15-slow-marketing-trends-pt-1]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-20-whats-actually-shifting-in-2026-pt-2]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-29-smart-founders-going-low-maintenance]]

Pressure options:

- Market Pressure is the normal pressure.
- Inner Pressure only belongs here if the "shift" is a mindset shift. If the post is mostly about relief from an old premise, use Broken Channel Pivot instead.

Forward sequence:

1. Name the shift the reader can already feel but has not fully sorted yet.
2. Establish why Alyssa is qualified to call the shift.
3. Name the old move that is getting weaker.
4. Show what staying with the old move will cost next.
5. Teach 2 specific shifts, each with a "what this means for your next move" slide.
6. Give one small action that lowers the reader's workload now.
7. CTA to the tool or freebie that helps them take that first action.

Required middle:

- 2 proof or teaching slides.
- 1 implication slide for the reader's next move.
- 1 lower-maintenance alternative.

Old belief it attacks: the thing that worked last year is still the safe plan.

New belief it installs: the market has moved, and Alyssa noticed before the reader had language for it.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-15-slow-marketing-trends-pt-1]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-20-whats-actually-shifting-in-2026-pt-2]], and [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-29-smart-founders-going-low-maintenance]] all make the reader feel early to a shift, then hand them a lower-maintenance next move.

### 2. Broken Channel Pivot

Examples: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-20-whats-actually-shifting-in-2026-pt-2]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-24-swapped-instagram-for-pinterest]]

Pressure options:

- Market Pressure version: the channel or behavior worked before, but the conditions changed.
- Inner Pressure version: the reader keeps blaming their effort, consistency, or talent when the real issue is the premise.

Forward sequence:

1. Name the old belief the reader already suspects is failing.
2. Steelman why that belief used to make sense.
3. Show why it is breaking now.
4. Name the cost they will keep paying if they stay there: pressure, volume, exhaustion, disappearing content, bad-fit attention.
5. Ask the better question that makes the old belief feel incomplete.
6. Teach the calmer model in 2 or 3 parts.
7. CTA to the offer that helps them leave the old pattern.

Required middle:

- 1 slide that protects the reader from feeling stupid for choosing the old way.
- 1 cost slide that makes staying feel expensive.
- 1 better-question or pivot slide before the fix.

Old belief it attacks: more of the old behavior will eventually pay off.

New belief it installs: the better model is not more effort in the same place. It is a different system.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-24-swapped-instagram-for-pinterest]] and [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-20-whats-actually-shifting-in-2026-pt-2]] both protect the reader from feeling stupid for using the old way, then make the old way feel too expensive to keep choosing.

### 3. Product Walkthrough

Example: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-06-pinterest-marketing-for-beginners]]

Pressure options:

- Market Pressure version: the product is the easiest first step after a shift.
- Inner Pressure version: the product helps the reader stop overcomplicating the job.

Forward sequence:

1. Name the concrete result the reader wants.
2. State the philosophy behind the product.
3. Show the product early as the small solution.
4. Turn every feature into an objection-killer.
5. Teach through the product: what to plan, what to use, what to reuse, what to stop overcomplicating.
6. Drop the deeper diagnosis late: the real problem is not the missing asset, it is the missing system.
7. CTA again with the product positioned as the easiest first system.

Required middle:

- Every feature has to become a reader outcome.
- The product has to teach the method, not just appear in the deck.
- The deeper diagnosis should arrive after the reader already sees the product's usefulness.

Old belief it attacks: I need to understand the whole strategy before I can use the tool.

New belief it installs: the tool is the easiest first version of the strategy.

Product-early rule: the product can appear early only when the product is doing the teaching. If it is only being sold, it is too early.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-06-pinterest-marketing-for-beginners]] sells early without feeling thin because the product is also the teaching surface. Copy the job, not the exact structure.

### 4. Recipe System

Example: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-10-get-clients-from-pinterest]]

Pressure options:

- Market Pressure version: the old process is getting heavier because conditions changed, so the reader needs a simpler operating order.
- Inner Pressure version: the result feels hard because the reader is using a messy or precious process.

Forward sequence:

1. Make a specific outcome promise.
2. Name the hidden opportunity most people are underusing.
3. Teach the method as a named metaphor system.
4. Give 4 steps, each with a job and a concrete instruction.
5. Make the steps feel easier than the reader expected.
6. Ask if they want the full system.
7. CTA to the product that packages the method.

Required middle:

- One named metaphor or organizing world.
- 3 to 4 steps with a job and concrete instruction.
- One moment that makes the method feel easier than expected.

Old belief it attacks: this result is complicated, random, or dependent on luck.

New belief it installs: this result follows a recipe when the pieces are in the right order.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-10-get-clients-from-pinterest]] proves a metaphor can carry the teaching order. Future decks need their own metaphor world that makes the method easier to remember.

Example application:

Offer: Bingeable Newsletters

Buying belief: I do not need gourmet email effort. I need a repeatable structure people want to keep opening.

Framework: Recipe System

Pressure: Inner Pressure

Hook style: Provocative Reframe

What changes in the middle: name the precious-email premise, validate why they thought good emails had to be impressive, then teach the repeatable show recipe.

### 5. Proof-First Plan

Example: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-19-pinterest-sugar-mama]]

Pressure options:

- Market Pressure version: prove the opportunity exists before teaching the plan.
- Inner Pressure version: prove the strange comparison is not embarrassing or unserious before asking the reader to trust the plan.

Forward sequence:

1. Use a weird visual joke or unexpected comparison to stop the scroll.
2. Immediately prove the joke has a real business reason behind it.
3. Name the opportunity the reader has been underestimating.
4. Give a 4-step plan from scratch.
5. Add examples inside the steps so it feels usable.
6. Move from plan to class, product, or resource invite.
7. End with a soft brand close so the CTA does not feel like a hard stop.

Required middle:

- Proof immediately after the joke.
- 4-step plan from scratch.
- Examples inside the steps so the plan feels usable.

Old belief it attacks: this thing is only for the surface-level use everyone associates with it.

New belief it installs: this thing can produce a serious business outcome when attached to the right system.

How to apply beyond Pinterest: swap the underestimated thing and the serious outcome. For a 5 Minute Newsletter angle, the old belief might be that good emails require gourmet effort. The new belief might be that a repeatable email structure gets the useful email out faster and more often.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-19-pinterest-sugar-mama]] turns a joke into a plan by proving the opportunity before teaching steps.

### 6. Receipts And Rebuild

Example: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-03-organic-marketing-is-dying]]

Pressure options:

- Market Pressure version: the system changed or revealed itself, so the old move is not safe.
- Inner Pressure version: receipts prove the reader is not failing, the premise or system is broken.

Forward sequence:

1. State the scary thing plainly.
2. Validate what the reader is already experiencing.
3. Stack proof until the old belief has nowhere to hide.
4. Kill the obvious escape route.
5. Name who benefits if the reader keeps obeying the broken system.
6. Pivot into what Alyssa would build instead.
7. Teach the rebuild in 3 proof-backed parts.
8. CTA to the bigger offer because the small fix would not be enough.

Required middle:

- At least 3 receipts before the rebuild.
- One villain or system diagnosis that protects the reader from blame.
- 3 proof-backed rebuild parts.

Old belief it attacks: this is just another annoying update or normal business problem.

New belief it installs: the system is built to keep extracting effort, so the smart move is to build a back end that keeps working without feeding it every day.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-03-organic-marketing-is-dying]] shows how to move from alarm to proof to villain to rebuild. Future decks should use the same persuasion job only when the offer can carry a bigger strategic fix, like [[Library/Offers/back-of-house/MAP|Back of House]].

### 7. Clock-In Plan

Example: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-01-80-days-until-summer-90d-clock-in]]

Pressure options:

- Market Pressure version: a seasonal window is opening or closing, so the reader needs to act while the timing is useful.
- Inner Pressure version: the reader is not behind, they need to close loops inside a focused container.

Forward sequence:

1. Name the time window.
2. Explain why that window creates momentum.
3. Show the payoff if they use the window well.
4. Give 3 operating rules for the season.
5. Break the season into jobs.
6. Make unfinished work feel like assets, not failure.
7. CTA to the dashboard, planner, or container that helps them clock in and finish.

Required middle:

- One reason the time window matters now.
- 3 operating rules for the season.
- One slide that turns unfinished work into usable assets.

Old belief it attacks: unfinished ideas mean I am behind.

New belief it installs: unfinished ideas are assets if I clock in for one focused shift and close the right loops.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-03-01-80-days-until-summer-90d-clock-in]] shows how a date becomes a work container. Future productivity decks should turn time into a focused shift, then attach the offer to finishing.

### 8. Permission Litany

Examples: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-22-wasting-time-for-the-girls]], [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-14-valentines-for-the-girls]]

Pressure options:

- Inner Pressure is the normal pressure.
- Market Pressure only belongs here if the pressure is a cultural or business norm the reader needs permission to leave.

Forward sequence:

1. Name the guilt or pressure.
2. Give the reader a more generous interpretation of that behavior.
3. Repeat short permission lines in the same visual world.
4. Include one line that turns the whole post into a principle.
5. Pause on the brand.
6. End with a question, sign-off, or soft invitation.

Required middle:

- Short permission lines, not tactical teaching.
- One principle line that gives the post a spine.
- No forced hard sell.

Old belief it attacks: taking time, changing your mind, or moving slowly means you are lazy.

New belief it installs: the slower choice can be the higher-standard choice.

FTG rule: no tactical teaching body, no hard CTA, no forced offer.

Proof pattern: [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-01-22-wasting-time-for-the-girls]] and [[Library/Examples/Carousels/2026-02-14-valentines-for-the-girls]] show how to make the reader feel named without teaching a framework.

## Carousel Anatomy

### Cover Page

Word range: 4 to 10 words in the real winners. Aim for 7 or fewer unless the time anchor or specific promise earns the extra words.

Forward job: create a reason to swipe.

Must do one: identity, suspected truth, specific transformation, time window, visual joke, permission.

Never does: vague topic label, generic tips, question-mark cover, tidy reframe that resolves itself.

### Slide 2

Headline: usually 4 to 10 words.

Body: 0 to 55 words depending on the lane.

Forward job: give the reason to keep reading before the teaching starts.

Can use: credibility, stakes, false belief, hidden opportunity, proof that the claim has receipts.

Never does: generic teaching before the reader knows why it matters.

### Problem And Stakes

Usually 1 to 3 slides after the cover.

Forward job: make the old way feel specific, costly, or annoying before the fix appears.

Leaves them with: that is the thing I have been feeling.

### Middle Slides

Usually 4 to 6 teaching or proof slides.

Headline: usually 3 to 7 words.

Body: 25 to 65 words when teaching.

Forward job: teach one named move per slide, with enough detail to use.

Each slide needs: a claim, a detail, and why it matters.

Never does: thin label plus filler sentence.

Always leaves them with: one useful thing a non-buyer could act on.

### The Turn

Usually lands around slide 5 to 8.

Forward job: change the explanation.

Can be: bait-and-switch, system diagnosis, villain diagnosis, better question, personal decision.

Never does: rely on a pretty background switch to carry the point.

### CTA

Usually final 1 to 2 slides.

Forward job: give the next step that solves the exact problem the deck just made obvious.

The CTA is a thread, not only a final slide.

Thread shape:

1. Early seed if the reader is already ready.
2. Mid-deck signal that the method has a container.
3. Final comment-word or offer reveal.

Never does: ask for a product that the post did not earn.

## Non-Negotiables

- The brief names offer, buying belief, framework, pressure, and hook style.
- Pick one framework.
- Pick one pressure.
- Problem confirmation comes before solution.
- Emotional validation belongs in the middle when using Inner Pressure.
- Implication belongs in the middle when using Market Pressure.
- Villain assignment protects the reader from blame.
- Product can show early only when the product teaches.
- The CTA must solve the problem the deck made obvious.
- Teaching slides need density: real numbers, examples, screenshots, steps, coined terms.
- The old way should feel less desirable by the end of the post.
