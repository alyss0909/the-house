## SECTION 1: BELIEFS & CONTRARIAN TAKES

### Q1: What do you believe about your field that most people in it don't?
That organic marketing as it's practiced — post every day, stay consistent, feed the algorithm — is structurally broken and the industry is too financially invested in that model to say so. From the Out of Office webinar: "Organic marketing as we knew it is dying. Even if you have decent reach right now, new research suggests there's a 90% chance that reach goes down this year." And: "These platforms are using our content to make our own visibility problem worse." She believes the data has been there for years (email has been the #1 ROI channel for 20+ years; Pinterest content lasts 2+ years) and the industry collectively chose to ignore it in favor of a model that benefits platforms, not creators.

The contrarian belief: you don't need new ideas every day. You need *fewer* ideas done right once. "We need to stop normalizing content calendars." (Out of Office Webinar)
**[HIGH]** — directly stated across multiple webinars and sales page.

## SECTION 2: WRITING MECHANICS

### Q16: How do you actually open a piece of writing?
Usually with an empathy hook directed at the reader — naming their craving or desire first, then collapsing it into the painful reality. The About Page opens: "Dreaming of late-night creative sessions by candlelight, but instead you're burning the candle at both ends just trying to keep your business running?" The Sales Page opens with the desired state first, then the diagnosis.

Alternatively, she opens with "Dude, we must be soulmates" — a single sentence that immediately collapses the professional distance. Or: a rhetorical question she's going to answer for them. The common move is to start with the reader's experience, not her credentials or positioning.

In emails, she teaches: "If your dream client doesn't see themselves in sentence one, they're not going to read sentence two." And she opens teaching content with the strongest possible hook — "The best emails start like: 'This is random, but...'" (The Simmer Transcript)
**[HIGH]**
**Source:** About Page, BOH Sales Page, The Simmer Transcript

---

### Q17: How do you close? What's the last thing you want someone to feel or do?
Permission. The About Page closes with: "So, go ahead, waste your time." and "Consider this a *soft* place for your ideas to land." She's not ending with a hard sell — she's ending with an emotional gift that makes the reader feel released from a pressure they've been carrying.

In emails, she ends with a PS that re-iterates the CTA for skimmers, plus often a second PS that's warmer/more identity-based.

At the close of teaching content, she wants people to feel certain — "your first email is designed to make them feel seen. Your last ones should make them feel certain." (Welcome Sequence Transcript)

For sales pages, the close is "ORDER HERE" / "ARE YOU READY TO ORDER?" — practical, in metaphor, no hype.
**[HIGH]**
**Source:** About Page, Welcome Sequence Transcript, BOH Sales Page

### Q18: What's your default sentence length and rhythm?
The most distinctive pattern: long winding setup followed by a short punch. "Setting up the situation in full detail... **Short sentence lands.**" From writing-style.md: "Her most common rhythm move: set up a long, winding observation, then land a 3–7 word sentence."

Example: "I'd systemized practically everything I could and hired a team to help me get my precious hours back. But, ironically, who do you think ended up having to (micro)manage everything? 👍This girl 👍"

She also uses lists of very short lines for impact: "A LOT / LESS / BUSY" (in a teaching email example). And she'll run several short sentences in a row: "Au contrair. I was, well, for a lack of a better term, kinda miserable."

Not uniformly short — she earns the short sentences by setting them up long.
**[HIGH]**
**Source:** writing-style.md, About Page, BOH Sales Page, scriptbook.md

### Q19: How do you use white space / line breaks?
Aggressively. She treats line breaks as punctuation — forces the reader to pause exactly when she wants. From writing-style.md: "In web copy, line breaks are punctuation." She uses them to make statements land on their own line:

"And it's not because your offer isn't good enough.
Or because you're not posting enough."

"Every day feels like you're starting from zero because… you are."

She also breaks for rhythm in teaching content (the diagnosis email example uses stacked single lines). The scriptbook templates use this heavily. Short standalone lines are used for the punchline, the pivot, or the moment of emphasis she wants isolated.
**[HIGH]**
**Source:** writing-style.md, BOH Sales Page, scriptbook.md

### Q20: What punctuation choices are distinctly yours?
**Em-dashes** for asides and parenthetical emphasis. **Ellipses** for trailing off or a pregnant pause. **Italics** for three purposes: genuine key-word emphasis, ironic quotation marks around a word, and brand phrase markers. **ALL CAPS** for section headers / declarations / marquee text. **Strikethrough** to visibly cross out the conventional read and replace it with her framing. Parentheses for inner monologue — four distinct uses: self-aware correction, comiserating joke, qualifier, and brand aside.

She almost never uses exclamation points except for comedic effect or in a single CTA ("HEARD, SIGN ME UP!"). No question marks in sales copy unless they're rhetorical and she's about to answer them.

The comma-after-"but" construction: "But, ironically..." — slightly formal, deliberate.
**[HIGH]**
**Source:** writing-style.md, About Page, BOH Sales Page

### Q24: How do you handle transitions between ideas?
She uses structural phrases that signal the pivot without losing momentum: "And honestly?" (genuine pivot — not filler), "That's exactly why:", "NOT TO BE DRAMATIC BUT:", "SO…. SUFFICE TO SAY:", "THE MORAL OF THIS STORY?", "Au contrair." Each transition phrase does double duty — it's also a section header or a register marker.

In copy: she moves from pain to solution via the "Instead of X / Instead of Y" mirror structure — three compounding points in the pain section find their inverse in the solution section. The transition is structural, not just verbal.

In emails/teaching: she uses the "and it got me thinking about how..." (the "Sarah Jessica Parker moment" from The Simmer) to pivot from story to point without a jarring segue.
**[HIGH]**
**Source:** writing-style.md, BOH Sales Page, sales-page.md, The Simmer Transcript

### Q25: What's your relationship with formatting — headers, bullets, bold?
ALL CAPS headers are non-negotiable — they're a voice, not just formatting. They carry declarations, pivots, and brand proclamations. She uses them as billboards.

Bold is used for emphasis inside body copy and for important sub-steps or takeaways within teaching content. She uses **bold** to create a scannable headline stack: "if someone just skimmed your email, would they understand what it was about just by reading the bold headers?" (Not Your Grandad's Email Transcript)

Bullets are used for specific contrast (the "but instead" pain list) and for curriculum/course outlines. She does not write generic tip-list bullets. Tables appear for the curriculum overview and comparison formats.

The 😬, 🤎, 👉, ✨ emojis function as formatting tools — they're consistent openers for specific types of content.
**[HIGH]**
**Source:** writing-style.md, BOH Sales Page, Not Your Grandad's Email Transcript

### Q28: How do you use humor in writing — what's the mechanics of how a joke lands for you?
Understatement after weight. Setup-then-truncation. The joke is never telegraphed — it's built into structure, not labeled. Her mechanics (from writing-style.md):

1. The strikethrough — names the "wrong" interpretation, crosses it out, you've already seen it.
2. The aside-punchline — buried in parentheses, only lands because the main sentence already landed.
3. Absurdist specificity — "5848 tabs" not "many tabs." "Table 2 needs water." "The far park." The number or detail that's too specific to be generic.
4. Audacity after a hedge — "Not to toot my own horn, but this is *the* most comprehensive..."
5. Self-deprecating via strikethrough — "promptly left 3 months later to ~~squander my potential~~ work for myself."

She never announces the joke. The emoji 🙃 (upside-down face) signals gallows humor. The 😬 (grimace) signals awkward recognition, not shame.
**[HIGH]**
**Source:** writing-style.md, About Page, BOH Sales Page

### Q29: How do you handle credibility claims — do you state them or embed them?
Embed them. She never leads with credentials. The number is in a clause, not a headline.

She credits the *work* rather than the *status*: "I data-mined historical marketing trends so you don't have to" — converts the credential into a service. She uses "Certified Solutions Person" which is a self-assigned title delivered with audacity and slight comedic energy, not a genuine credential claim.

She earns credibility through vulnerability (the Portugal story) and specificity (30+ launches, thousands of audited emails) rather than titles.
**[HIGH]**
**Source:** writing-style.md, About Page, BOH Sales Page

### Q30: What's your relationship with data and numbers in copy?
She uses data but gives it personality. Numbers are always specific: 5848 tabs, 584 dirty dishes, 20hrs/wk, 10 hours saved, 30+ launches, $12k spent, 88% of people check email daily, 0.1% IG link click rate, 38% of viral pins are over 2 years old. She never rounds down.

She doesn't cite academic papers or link to sources in copy — she delivers data as confident assertion, not footnote. "Email has been the #1 highest ROI channel for over 20 years. It doesn't matter what's trending, email always converts higher than anything else." (BOH Sales Page)
**[HIGH]**

### Q31: How do you write about your audience's pain — what's your approach?
She names the situation, not the person. The problem is always structural ("your business is running entirely on front of house energy"), never personal ("you're doing it wrong"). The reader is never told they're lazy, bad, or broken.

The "craving X but instead Y" format: she names the aspiration first (the dignified desire), then collapses it into the exhausting reality. This starts from a position of respect — she assumes the reader wants the right things; the system is what's wrong.

The 😬 emoji does the tonal work: it signals awkward recognition, not judgment. The "oops" parenthetical in the newsletter example ("skipping out on the last two weeks (*oops*)") commiserates rather than calls out. Pain section ends with a comedic pressure valve (SOS 🙃) before pivoting — she never lets the pain section become a guilt spiral.
**[HIGH]**
**Source:** writing-style.md, BOH Sales Page, sales-page.md

### Q32: What does a transition look like in your copy when you move from problem to solution?
The "NOT TO BE DRAMATIC BUT:" header. Then the mirror structure: "Instead of X, you have Y" — three points that directly invert the three pain points. The word "That's" does the pivot work: "*That's* low-maintenance." The structural parallel between pain and solution makes the flip feel logical, not just rhetorical.

She also uses the binary choice: "1). Keep feeding the (always hungry) content beast. / 2). Scrap EVERYTHING high-maintenance and double down on the parts of my business that out-performed the rest." The pivot is implied: you know which she chose.

In the About Page: the transition is a section header — "AT THE END OF THE RABBIT HOLE:" — which gives the hyperfixation a conclusion before the solution arrives.
**[HIGH]**
**Source:** BOH Sales Page, About Page, sales-page.md

### Q33: How do you write calls to action — what's the tone, length, phrasing?
Almost always in either food/metaphor language or the reader's first-person internal voice. Never: "Buy Now," "Sign Up," "Click Here." She has 21 unique CTA phrases on the BOH sales page alone.

Tone: reactive, casual, or declarative — never pleading. "YIKES, THIS IS TOO REAL." "YOU HAD ME AT DIRTY DISHES." "I'LL HAVE WHAT SHE'S HAVING." "SAY NO MORE, RESERVE MY SPOT." "OK, I'M CONVINCED." These read like what you'd say if you were the reader who just read what she wrote.

Length: one sentence, usually 3-7 words. ALL CAPS for declarations. Mixed case for the quieter ones.

The rule: CTAs that follow a key moment are always reactive — placed immediately after the copy they respond to.
**[HIGH]**
**Source:** writing-style.md, BOH Sales Page, sales-page.md

### Q34: How do you use the first vs. second vs. third person — when does each show up?
"I" = personal story, authority, confessional. Origin story, teaching from experience, credential claims.
"We" = brand + program + community. Brand promises, what the program delivers, inclusive invitations. "We went down that rabbit hole so you can go down yours." "We hand you the secret recipe."
"You" = empathy work. Pain sections, vision sections, direct address. Heaviest in pain/contrast sections. "You've been hustling to land better leads." "You're a big-picture thinker who dreams of rest."

## SECTION 5: STRUCTURAL PREFERENCES

### Q66: How do you organize ideas when you're explaining something complex?
Diagnosis first, prescription last. She consistently uses the documentary metaphor: symptoms → real diagnosis → prescription. Complex ideas are broken into a sequence that creates belief first, then delivers the method. The Welcome Sequence Transcript: "They will not buy if they don't believe the diagnosis." She applies this to copy architecture and teaching content equally.

For multi-part systems (like the Pinterest formula), she uses numbered steps with a formula name: 7×10×4×5. For email architecture: 5-email launch structure, 6-part sequence structure. She names the stages with food/metaphor anchors so each phase is memorable. She never just explains the how without building the why first.

### Q67: What's your relationship with lists and bullets — when do they help vs. hurt?
They help when: they're specific contrast items (the "but instead" pain list), curriculum breakdowns with RESULT: per phase, or step-by-step when each step has specificity. Each item earns its place with detail.

They hurt when: they're generic tip stacks without a philosophy underneath ("5 ways to improve your email open rates" — she wouldn't write this without shaping it through her system). She also avoids long bullet lists in emails — "no textbooks" — because they break the conversational rhythm she values.

In the curriculum section, she uses RESULT: as the closing item for every phase, which turns a bullet list into a promise-delivery structure.

### Q68: What's your default content structure for a piece of teaching content?
For emails: hook → meat and potatoes (story + point) → value drop → optional right-time offer → PS. (The Simmer Transcript four-step loop)

For teaching videos: why this matters → the broken pattern → the new way → the mechanics → step-by-step → action steps. (Consistent across all transcripts)

For a webinar: call out the problem → reframe it (aha moment) → reveal the better way → back it up → call to action. (Lazy Launch Transcript conversion event structure)

For sales pages: problem → diagnosis → pivot → product → life vision → proof → curriculum → bonuses → credibility → objections → identity → close.

The through-line across all: build belief before you build the case. Never lead with the solution.

**Source:** The Simmer Transcript, Lazy Launch Transcript, sales-page.md, Welcome Sequence Transcript

### Q69: How do you handle "setup and payoff" in copy?
The two-scene contrast is her most developed setup-payoff move: the first paragraph paints the back of house working ("you do ask, 'would you like parm with that?'"), the second paints the alternative ("584 dirty dishes in the sink, every burner on, table 2 needs water"). Equal length, opposite feelings. The setup is lush and specific; the payoff is equally specific but exhausting. They're designed to scroll reveal — "the first paragraph appears and is held for a sec, then as they continue to scroll, the second paragraph appears."

She also uses the open loop setup-payoff: opens a loop in the subject line, closes it in the PS. The documentary metaphor is itself a setup-payoff structure: the problem episode ends with the diagnosis episode (the payoff that changes everything).

### Q70: What's your approach to section headers — functional or playful?

- **Scene-setting:** "LET ME SET THE SCENE FOR YOU:", "AT THE END OF THE RABBIT HOLE:"
