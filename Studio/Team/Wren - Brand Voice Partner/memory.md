### 2026-06-27 — Pre-seed from June 6 voice memo

**Source:** [[2026-06-06-boh-launch-email-inspo]]

**What this taught us:**

**1. She thinks in sensory universals, not abstractions.**
"Onions and garlic. White wine and butter." Not "fundamental principles" or "core frameworks." Real things. Specific things. When she's reaching for a metaphor, it's physical and domestic — cooking, plants, fabric, things you can touch. Wren: reach for the concrete, not the conceptual.

**2. Swear words are load-bearing, not decorative.**
"Writing fucking good emails is something Claude can't teach you, contrary to popular belief." The f-word there is doing work — it's emphasis and it's intimacy and it's proof she's not performing. Don't soften them. Don't move them. If you're not sure whether to include one, include it.

**3. The run-on thinking-out-loud quality is her voice, not a draft flaw.**
The voice memo is full of comma splices, sentence fragments, and redirections mid-thought ("well I mean okay but like"). When she's most herself, she sounds like she's thinking aloud. Clean sentences that resolve neatly are often LESS her than messy ones that redirect. Write toward the mess, not away from it.

**4. She identifies as a history girl, not a futurist. This is structural.**
"I could always understand the past. I could see the patterns. I wasn't the one making the new path — I was the one who cried when she saw the new path." This is her positioning identity. She is not bullish on what's coming. She is confident about what works. Never write Alyssa as someone predicting trends or excited about what's next. She's the one who says "I told you this always works."

**5. She sells to obsessives, not aspirationals.**
The target audience she describes is the parasites person, the indigenous plants professor, the bilirubin-level person. Not "aspiring entrepreneurs" or "ambitious women." People who already give a shit about something very specific and just need the business system to support them. Wren: when writing for her audience, speak to the obsession, not the ambition.

**6. The 4-Hour Work Week reference is about nostalgia, not endorsement.**
She read it at 21. It made her uncomfortable (hiring people in Bangladesh felt wrong). The reference works because her audience probably had the same uneasy experience. Use the reference to *position against* that version of entrepreneurship, not for it.

**7. She loves wise old men and finds that embarrassing.**
"I love listening to a wise old man. As a feminist it almost makes me cringe to say this out loud." She reads old books. She trusts universal truths. She quotes Stephen Covey and Dale Carnegie. This is not ironic. It's her actual intellectual heritage. Don't make her sound like a girl boss who only quotes other women creators. She quotes the old men AND she's a feminist AND she knows it's complicated AND she says so.

**8. Her strongest lines are contrarian reversals.**
"I hope Stephen Covey got many a BJ." "What's next? Fuck, I'm afraid of what's next." "I'm not here to tell you what's next, I'm here to tell you what works." The flip from expected to unexpected is where her best sentences live. Set up the assumption, demolish it, land somewhere true.

**9. The minimum viable rhythm matters to her.**
She wants to know "the monthly just works" — the minimum she needs to do to keep the machine running, not the maximum. She does not want to out-hustle anyone. This is philosophical. Write her as someone who has made peace with doing less and it working better.

**10. She has a specific "who this is not for" instinct.**
"If you're the only one who understands digital marketing, sees the future, and knows hooks — this is not for you, babe." She excludes deliberately. She's not trying to convert the hustle-culture crowd. Wren: write for the person who is already partly persuaded. The copy isn't trying to convince skeptics; it's giving believers permission.

### 2026-06-27 — Pre-seed from writing-style.md and voice-profile.md analysis

**Source:** [[Archive/Studio-Analysis-voice-retired-2026-07-10/writing-style.md]], [[Archive/Studio-Analysis-voice-retired-2026-07-10/voice-profile.md]]

**What this taught us:**

**11. Five words: Conspiratorial. Grounded. Dry-funny. Permissive. Precise.**
This is the voice fingerprint from the profile. Her top subject line: "re: tmrw's meeting" — 57.4% open rate. Her voice is most itself when it looks accidental but isn't. It works because it's specific, warm, and sounds like it wasn't trying. That's the target — never the result of trying, always the result of knowing exactly what she's doing.

**12. She never does "X, not Y" constructions.**
This is explicitly in the Don'ts. No contrast pairs dressed up as wisdom. "Slow marketing, not hustle" — she would never write that. She'd write "slow marketing books more clients. I can prove it." Show, don't structure.

**13. She never uses em dashes in the performative sense.**
Em dashes for drama or emphasis are an AI tell. Her writing uses them functionally or not at all. Every em dash Wren writes should be interrogated.

**14. She writes to one specific person.**
Not "entrepreneurs" or "people who." One person who she knows exactly. The reader can feel this. When Wren is writing to a general audience, it sounds like Alyssa is performing. When writing to one specific reader she can see, it lands.

**15. She earns the teachable moment through story, not through structure.**
She doesn't say "here are 3 things." She tells you something that happened and the lesson arrives at the end almost accidentally. The framework is the after, not the before.

### 2026-06-27 — Pre-seed from carousel analysis and subject line system

**Source:** [[Studio/Analysis/carousel-analysis.md]], [[Studio/Analysis/Frameworks/subject-line-operating-system.md]]

**What this taught us:**

**16. Her carousel hooks are identity calls, not curiosity gaps.**
Her best carousels open with something that makes a specific person self-select — not "5 ways to grow your list" (curiosity gap) but "if you're still starting from zero every month, this is a system problem" (identity call). The reader should feel seen before they swipe.

**17. She uses the word "slow" as a power word, not a modifier.**
Slow marketing. Slow biz. Slow Sunday. It's not describing pace — it's describing philosophy. Don't use it as an adjective. Use it as an identity marker.

**18. Preview text is part of the email, not an afterthought.**
The subject line + preview text is one thought split across two lines. She treats them as a pair. Wren: always write preview text when writing subject lines, always check that they work together before presenting.
