## Hook Patterns Visible

These repeat enough to matter:

- **Anti-content-machine claims:** "Burn your Content Calendar.", "Your Business Should Be More BORING", "Organic Marketing Is Dying"
- **Seasonal planning permission:** "Is February the new January?", "How I plan May so I can take full advantage of June"
- **Weird coined phrases:** "Procrastavoidance", "Socks & Sandals Marketing", "your Business needs a BBL"
- **Specific resource promise:** "80 Subject Lines", "Wanna Steal My Pinterest Recipe?", "The Pinterest Marketing Plan for Beginners"

- **Real-life input as content source:** "Your Next Newsletter is Already on Your Phone", "One good Thought", "How I Stopped Starting from Scratch Every Week"
- **Slow/soft business identity:** "going at your own pace in a world that's rushing is a super power", "Why Smart Founders Are Going Low Maintenance", "Slowly Viral."

## Working Dos

- Let the cover make one clear promise, confession, or claim.
- Use object-world specificity: toast, garlic, martini glass, shoe, pepper, receipt, plate, phone, toast note.
- Keep the largest words legible in the grid before any microcopy matters.
- Use odd but plain language. The best lines feel like something Alyssa would actually say, not a named marketing mechanic.
- Let the prop argue with the copy. The object is not decoration; it creates the world the hook lives in.
- Keep relationship posts and "For The Girls" posts in the mix so the feed does not become only offers and teaching.

## Likely Don'ts / Cautions

- Do not let aesthetic mood replace the promise when the post is meant to teach or sell.
- Do not make the cover a filing label, even if the visual is pretty.
- Do not overtrust tiny microcopy; most of it disappears at grid size.
- Do not copy the top-9 skeleton into every new post. This grid proves Alyssa's range is broader than the winners.
- Do not treat the red-X covers as confirmed failures until Alyssa says what the Xs mean.
