# Copywriter Role Planning Document

*This is a planning tool, not a job description. You're reading this before you're ready to pull the trigger. The goal is to make sure that when you are ready, the hire is surgical — not hopeful.*

---

## 1. Role Title and Positioning

**Working title:** Brand Voice Partner

Do not call this person a "copywriter." A copywriter brings a framework and adapts it to clients. That is specifically not what you need. What you need is someone who suspends their own instincts and operates inside a documented voice system that isn't theirs — while still bringing enough independent taste to know when something is off.

"Brand Voice Partner" signals:
- They are an extension of your thinking, not a vendor producing deliverables
- They are partnered to a specific voice, not to a brief
- The relationship has an ongoing character — they're not project-in, project-out

**How to describe the relationship to candidates:** "You will write as me. Not like me — as me. There is a Second Brain that documents exactly what that means. Your job is to know it so well that I can't tell your drafts from something I would have written myself. The ceiling here is indistinguishable."

**What this is not:**
- A ghostwriter in the traditional sense (they're not writing a book from transcripts; they're operating an existing, documented voice system across multiple content types on a recurring basis)
- A content manager (they are not scheduling, strategizing the editorial calendar, or managing platforms — just the writing itself)
- A conversion copywriter who brings a "proven process" (the process is already here; they execute inside it)

## 2. Scope of Work

### What they own

**Tier 1 — High volume, recurring (the weekly engine):**
- Weekly Simmer emails: drafting full emails from provided topic prompts or Alyssa's raw ideas
- Subject lines + preview text: for every email type; treated as a first-class deliverable, not an afterthought
- Soft Sunday emails: the most personal register — provided with a real-life prompt or scene; they execute

**Tier 2 — Periodic, moderate stakes:**
- Sales emails (promo and launch sequences): based on Lazy Launch doctrine; real urgency only; drafted from Alyssa's launch notes
- Social captions (IG): based on content themes Alyssa provides; casual register; humor-forward
- Pinterest pin descriptions: keyword-informed but still in voice; short; idiomatic metaphor use only

**Tier 3 — Occasional, high-stakes:**
- Sales page copy (new product launches): full calibration required; Second Brain Level 3 protocol; always reviewed heavily before publish
- Opt-in / landing page copy: compressed sales page format; built from provided offer details

### What Alyssa always owns herself

- The origin story in any new context — she can provide the raw material; the partner does not invent origin moments
- The decision about whether the voice is right — final approval on every piece before it publishes
- Topics, prompts, and directional input for Simmers and Soft Sundays — she provides the seed; the partner grows it
- Launch strategy and offer positioning — what gets said about an offer and in what sequence is her call
- Any copy where the "I" is Alyssa making a specific personal claim she hasn't made before (new vulnerability, new personal story, new admission)

### What is explicitly out of scope for this hire

- Platform management, scheduling, or analytics
- SEO strategy or keyword research
- Email sequence strategy (they write inside the system; they don't design it)
- Anything requiring original strategic thinking about the business direction
- Voice coaching or training other people in the brand voice

### Week-to-week shape of the work

In a steady-state week: one Simmer email draft with subject line + preview text, two to four social captions, a handful of Pinterest descriptions as batches arise. Promo periods add two to six sales emails per launch. Sales pages are quarterly at most — they are heavy lifts that require the full Second Brain protocol and significant Alyssa review time.

## 3. The Non-Negotiable Sensibility Requirements

These cannot be installed. If a candidate doesn't bring these, no amount of Second Brain immersion will fix them.

**They instinctively understand why understatement is funnier than announcement.**
The funniest moments in Alyssa's copy are parenthetical. They're buried in asides. They arrive after a long setup and then land in seven words. A candidate who puts the joke in the headline, uses exclamation marks for emphasis, or telegraphs the punchline will never write in this voice — not because they lack skill, but because they have a different sense of timing. This is not trainable past a certain age.

**They understand permission as a structural argument, not just warmth.**
"Go ahead, waste your time" is not a soft brand moment. It's the entire product promise condensed to five words. A candidate who reads it as a tone choice — rather than as the mechanism of the offer — will produce copy that's warm but empty. They need to understand that every permission statement is an argument: the back of house runs itself so that you are free to do this specific thing. The freedom is the product. The permission is the pitch.

**They do not write in generic urgency.**
Not as a stylistic choice — as a matter of principle. Alyssa's copy is explicit about this: urgency must be real. A candidate who has been trained in standard launch copy, who defaults to "limited spots," "don't miss this," or "this is your sign," is carrying habits that will require constant correction. The issue is not that they use those phrases — it's that they reach for them automatically. That reflex is incompatible with this voice.

**They are comfortable with her specific weirdness and will not iron it flat.**
"Ur (not)NEW march plan inside" as a subject line, "Stoner Math" as a subject line, the deliberate "(lol)" at the end of a confession, "bc i forgot my phone at home" in a list of things you should know about her — all of these will read as errors or insufficient polish to a conventionally trained copywriter. The right person reads them as precise. They will not suggest making them "cleaner." They will make more of them.

**They are readers of good things, not consumers of marketing content.**
The sentence rhythm, the dry humor, the literary specificity ("read poetry by the window" as a life-pleasure, not "pursue creative hobbies") — these come from reading. Not from studying copywriting frameworks. The candidate who talks about their favorite newsletters, their favorite essays, or a novel that surprised them is far more likely to bring the right texture than the candidate who talks about their swipe file and their conversion rate benchmarks.

**They have a natural instinct for the "slightly wrong" hook.**
The raccoon in a cashmere sweater formula — short, casual, slightly emotionally loaded, a tiny bit wrong — requires a specific sensibility that doesn't come from copywriting training. It comes from having a sense of mischief, from finding something funny about the way a phrase scrapes against expectations. A candidate who pitches subject lines that are technically strong but completely correct will never hit this. You want the person who writes "socks & sandal marketing" and thinks it's obvious.

**They are disciplined without being rigid.**
They will read the Second Brain files before every task. They will check the routing map. They will not skip the Don'ts. But they also won't use the Second Brain as a crutch to avoid judgment — they'll internalize it until it becomes invisible and then write from that place. The discipline is the floor; taste is what gets built on top of it.

## 4. Skills That Can Be Trained vs. Must Be Brought

### Must be brought (non-negotiable, cannot be installed)

- Instinct for comic timing and structural humor
- Comfort with casualness that is calibrated, not sloppy
- Nose for when something sounds like it came from a swipe file
- The "slightly wrong" sensibility — knowing when a phrase scrapes interestingly against expectations
- Genuine ease with reading source material before writing (not as a chore but as a natural working habit)
- Taste for specificity over abstraction (writes "crossing something off a to-do list in black pen" not "loves staying organized")
- Comfort with ambiguity — ability to produce a strong draft without having every detail resolved

### Can be taught through the Second Brain

- The food/restaurant metaphor system and where it belongs vs. where it doesn't
- The CTA catalog and the logic behind each register (food metaphor vs. reader's internal voice vs. conversational/direct)
- The email structure for Simmers, sales emails, and Soft Sundays
- The routing discipline — which analysis files to read for which task types
- How the "But Instead" pain structure works and when to use it
- The permission voice pattern and how it connects to the product promise
- The specific vocabulary (lol, bc, ur, kinda, fkn — when and where each lands)
- The difference between ALL CAPS (declaration), italics (personal emphasis), and lowercase (aside/throwaway)
- Strikethrough as a live reframe device
- The origin story arc — how it's edited differently for about-page vs. sales-page contexts
- Real urgency doctrine (Lazy Launch: urgency must be real)
- Preview text as a first-class deliverable with three distinct jobs
- Subject line pattern taxonomy (DM voice, contradiction hook, soft personalization, INSIDE pattern)

### The honest truth about training

The Second Brain is an unusually complete onboarding resource. For the right person, it can get them to 80% voice match relatively fast. But the last 20% — the weird subject line that shouldn't work but does, the permission statement that lands as an argument rather than a vibe, the joke that's buried exactly deep enough — that 20% will not come from reading the files. It comes from having the sensibility before they ever read a word. Hire for the 20% that can't be taught; let the Second Brain do the rest.

## 5. The Second Brain as Onboarding

### Week 1 reading order

The sequence matters. Do not let them read out of order.

1. `me.md` — identity, voice in five words, what she sells, the restaurant metaphor, the permission pattern. This is the orientation. Read it like you're meeting someone for the first time.
2. `analysis/writing-style.md` — all 14 sections, in order. Section 12 (The Don'ts) should be read twice. Once is not enough.
3. `examples/about-page.md` — read the actual copy, not the analysis. This is the highest density of her casual register currently available. Read it as a reader, not as a copywriter.
4. `analysis/copy-task-routing.md` — the routing map. This teaches them how to use the Second Brain before they try to use it.
5. `analysis/subject-line.md` — the raccoon formula. The "slightly wrong" principle. The preview text as first-class deliverable.

Week 2: based on their first assignment type, add the relevant analysis file (sales-page-analysis.md for sales work, about-page-analysis.md for first-person work) plus the corresponding example.

### First test assignment

Before they write a single publishable word, give them this:

**Task 1: Subject line batch**
Write 10 subject lines for a hypothetical Simmer email about the creative block that comes from over-planning. No additional context. Work from the Second Brain.

*What a pass looks like:* At least three of the ten have the raccoon quality — short, casual, slightly wrong. At least two show the DM-voice register. None contain generic urgency language. None explain the topic outright. At least two have natural preview text pairings they've thought through. One or two are "a tiny bit wrong" in an interesting way. The batch as a whole does not read like it came from a copywriting course.

*What a fail looks like:* Polished subject lines that explain the email content. Subject lines that would appear in a swipe file. Any "don't miss this" or "I wanted to share something" energy. Even one line with an exclamation point used for enthusiasm rather than comedic effect.

**Task 2: Simmer email draft**
Draft a Simmer email based on this prompt: "Alyssa is thinking about the Soft Sunday she had last weekend — she made pasta from scratch, forgot to check her phone, and had a realization mid-knead about why slow things compound in a way that fast things don't."

*What a pass looks like:* The draft leads with a real moment, not a concept. It has at least one permission statement that connects to the product promise. The humor is structural and parenthetical, not announced. The ending doesn't pitch anything unless the story genuinely earned it. The subject line they choose for it would fit in the raccoon formula. The preview text is treated as a second line, not a summary.

*What a fail looks like:* Opening with a concept instead of a scene. A CTA that says "sign up for Back of House" without the story earning it. Any generic nurture email patterns ("This week I want to talk to you about..."). Humor that's telegraphed. An ending that wraps up neatly instead of giving permission.

### What "voice-match certified" means before they publish

They do not publish anything without Alyssa's review for the first three months. Full stop.

"Voice-match certified" is not a test they pass once — it's a trust level reached over time. The certification moment is when Alyssa reads a draft and doesn't remember whether she wrote it herself. That is the actual bar.

Before that moment, the working model is: they draft, she edits, she explains the edit, they learn the pattern. The Second Brain gets updated when an edit reveals a pattern that isn't documented yet. Over time, the edits get smaller. The certification is the moment the edits stop being about voice and start being about preference.

Practical indicators of readiness before publishing without heavy review:
- Subject lines consistently hit the raccoon formula without coaching
- CTAs are never "Buy Now," "Sign Up," or "Learn More"
- Permission statements appear naturally and connect to the product logic
- The Don'ts from writing-style.md Section 12 do not appear
- The food metaphor is used at transition moments and CTAs, not in every sentence
- Humor lives in the parenthetical and aside, not in the headline

## 6. The Evaluation and Vetting Process

### Where to find them

This person is probably not on a copywriting job board. Places to look:

- **Independent newsletter writers** — specifically people writing newsletters in a personal, idiosyncratic voice that has developed over time. They have already built the "authentic casual calibration" muscle. Look at who writes newsletters that you yourself find funny.
- **Former or current journalists** (longform, features, culture) who have moved into brand work — they know how to write with a specific voice and they read for a living
- **Referrals from creative service providers in your community** — your audience is the creative service provider world; someone in that world who writes well is the most likely candidate
- **People who write well in communities you're already in** — Slack groups, newsletters, professional communities where you've noticed someone's voice

Do not look at: copy agencies, conversion copywriting courses, marketing content mills, LinkedIn thought leaders who write in bullet points.

### What to look for in their portfolio

**Green flags:**
- Writing that sounds like a specific person with a specific point of view — you could not attribute it to "a good copywriter," only to them
- Humor that is structural, not performed — jokes that land inside sentences, not headlines
- Evidence of reading: references, a specific literary sensibility, vocabulary that suggests they read things beyond marketing content
- Specificity as a default — "the far park" over "a park," "5848 tabs" over "lots of tabs"
- Restraint — knowing when not to push the joke, when to be direct, when to let a sentence breathe
- At least one piece that surprised you in the "slightly wrong" direction

**Red flags in a portfolio:**
- Polished but unmemorable — technically correct, nobody would re-read it
- CTAs that say "click here," "sign up," "buy now," or "don't miss this"
- Testimonial-heavy without voice — good results but the writing itself is generic
- Self-described as a "launch copywriter" or "conversion optimization specialist" as the primary identity
- A swipe file or "frameworks" orientation — they talk about the process more than the work
- Samples that were written for multiple clients and sound exactly the same — high adaptability, low taste

### The test project

After portfolio review, give them both tasks from Section 5 (subject line batch + Simmer email). Give them access to the Second Brain. Give them three days.

The test project is not about seeing if they can write well. You already know they can write — that's why they got to this stage. The test is:
- Do they read the Second Brain before writing, or do they rely on their own instincts?
- Does the output sound like Alyssa or like a skilled writer writing for a brand they just read about?
- Do they have natural instinct for the "slightly wrong" energy or is it imposed and effortful?

### Pass/fail criteria for the test

**Pass:** You read the subject line batch and find at least two lines you'd actually use. You read the Simmer email and there's at least one sentence you couldn't tell if you wrote yourself. The Don'ts from Section 12 are not present. The CTAs are not generic. The permission voice appears at least once.

**Fail:** Any of the following — generic urgency language appears anywhere, subject lines are all "technically correct" but flat, the Simmer email opens with a concept instead of a scene, the food metaphor is overused or forced, humor is announced rather than structural, the preview text is ignored or treated as a summary.

**The conversation after the test:** Ask them what was hard. Ask what they'd do differently. Their answer will tell you whether they understood what they were building toward or whether they were performing competence. The right person will name something specific — "I found it hard to know when to deploy the food metaphor and when to let it rest" or "I kept wanting to explain the subject line and had to force myself not to." The wrong person will say the test was straightforward.

## 7. The Wrong-Person Warning Signs

These are the signals that someone is technically a good copywriter but wrong for this role specifically. Some of these will appear in the portfolio review. Some will surface in the test project. Some will only come out in conversation.

**They lead with their process, not their taste.**
If they describe their work primarily in terms of the frameworks they use ("I follow a problem-agitate-solution structure"), they're telling you their orientation is toward replicable method, not specific voice. Alyssa's copy does not follow PAM or AIDA or any other acronym. The process is read the Second Brain, internalize the voice, write from that place. A candidate who needs a framework will keep looking for one here.

**They offer to make it "more scannable" or "cleaner."**
This surfaces in the test project feedback or in early editorial conversations. The moment someone suggests making a subject line "clearer" when its power is in the ambiguity, or flagging that the preview text is "a bit off" when it's deliberately weird, or cleaning up "bc" into "because" — that's a red flag. They're optimizing for a voice that isn't the goal.

**They talk about their clients in terms of results, not voice.**
"I helped a client achieve 40% open rates" tells you nothing relevant. "I spent three months getting inside a client's head before I could write a single email" tells you everything. The right person talks about the creative problem of matching voice. The wrong person talks about the business outcome of their work.

**They suggest adding social proof "earlier in the funnel."**
Alyssa's about page has zero testimonials by design. The sales page sequences social proof carefully for persuasion architecture reasons. A candidate who reads these pages and suggests repositioning testimonials for conversion optimization is showing you they're mapping her brand to standard conversion principles. That's not what this job is.

**They think "lol" and "bc" are errors.**
If they correct the casual vocabulary in a draft without being asked — or ask if you "meant to write that" — they don't understand that the informality is the precision. It's not sloppy. It's calibrated. The right person reads "bc i forgot my phone at home" and thinks: perfect, that's exactly right.

**They describe themselves as a "conversion copywriter."**
Not a disqualifier on its own, but a yellow flag. Conversion copy training builds specific reflexes (urgency, objection handling, social proof density) that are in direct conflict with several of Alyssa's deliberate choices. A "conversion copywriter" who has actively worked against those reflexes is possible. A "conversion copywriter" who arrived at this application believing their training is the asset is likely a mismatch.

**Their own writing sounds like everyone else's.**
Their newsletter, their LinkedIn posts (if they have them), their test project cover note — if their own voice is generic or polished-but-bland, they don't have the sensibility. The right person cannot turn it off. Their own writing has the texture. You can feel it.

**They don't mention anything about what they read.**
Not a disqualifier — but in a conversation or intro note, the right person will mention something they've been reading that has nothing to do with marketing. A novel, an essay, a newsletter they love, something weird and specific. It won't be forced. It'll just be how they talk about what's interesting to them. That tells you they're a reader, which is the substrate for everything else.

### Gaps that will make onboarding harder

**Gap 1: No real Simmer email examples on file**
`examples/simmers/` is empty. This is the highest-volume weekly task the partner will do, and the onboarding reading order sends them to this folder. Until it's populated, they're calibrating from the about page (the closest available analog) rather than from actual Simmer emails.

*What to do before hiring:* File three to five Simmer emails you feel good about. They don't need analysis — just the real examples. This will reduce calibration drift on the most common task.

**Gap 2: No real Soft Sunday examples on file**
`examples/soft-sundays/` is empty. The copy task routing map explicitly flags this as the highest-risk task in the library for uncalibrated execution. A partner who drafts Soft Sundays without examples will be guessing at register.

*What to do before hiring:* File two or three Soft Sundays you consider strong. Or explicitly flag to the partner that Soft Sundays have no example file and that every draft should be treated as a calibration iteration with heavy Alyssa review.

**Gap 3: Subject line analysis is v1 only (30 emails)**
The directional patterns are confirmed, but the v2 that incorporates top performers from two years of data will be meaningfully better for training. The partner can work from v1 — it's enough to avoid the wrong patterns and reach for the right ones — but the confirmation from v2 would make training sharper.

*What to do before hiring:* Not blocking — but if the two-year performance data is available, rebuilding subject-line-analysis.md as v2 before the hire would give the partner better ground truth to work from.

**Gap 4: No carousel or IG-specific analysis file**
If IG captions and carousels are in scope (they are), the partner currently works from `writing-style.md` Sections 3 and 7 only, with no format-specific examples. This is adequate but creates more calibration uncertainty than the email formats.

*What to do before hiring:* Not blocking. But if you bring this person on, the carousel and IG caption analysis file should be on the development roadmap within the first three months of the partnership. They'll surface what's missing faster than you can when working alone.

**Gap 5: `about_me.md` (XML voice profile) awaiting Alyssa review**
The AI-mined voice profile exists but is marked as a strong draft with ~13 questions at LOW/MED confidence. Resolving those gaps through the gap interview would make the profile more reliable for an external partner.

*What to do before hiring:* Run the gap interview and finalize `about_me.md` before handing the Second Brain to a new partner. This is the most compressed calibration document in the library — a polished version of it shortens their onboarding meaningfully.

### The hire-readiness verdict

**Right now:** Hireable for a limited scope (Simmers, subject lines, social captions, Pinterest). Not ideal for sales pages or Soft Sundays until the example gaps are filled.

**With three to five Simmer examples filed and `about_me.md` finalized:** Hireable for full scope. Every task type has either examples or complete analysis documentation to calibrate from.

**The minimum viable state to start looking:** File the Simmer examples and finish the gap interview. That is the two-item pre-hire checklist. Everything else can be built during the partnership as the work reveals what's missing.

## Addendum: This Is an AI Team Member

The Brand Voice Partner described in this document will be an AI specialist — the same model as Larry, Penn, Pax, and the rest of the team. Same underlying model, different operating hat. That changes the mechanics of the hire substantially, while leaving the substance of the role untouched.

---

### What changes from the human hire plan

**There is no discovery process.** No discovery calls. No test projects. No portfolio review. No three-day trial period. The "hire" is designing and deploying the agent — choosing a name, writing the system prompt, and wiring the Second Brain as the agent's context layer. The calibration problem that takes a human partner months to solve is, for an AI agent, a prompt engineering problem. It either loads the right files and produces calibrated output, or it doesn't. The feedback loop is immediate.

**The taste problem is solved through context loading, not candidate screening.** The entire Section 3 of this document — the non-negotiable sensibility requirements — exists because a human partner either has the instinct or they don't, and you cannot install it. An AI agent doesn't bring instincts. It brings whatever context you give it, applied consistently. The "raccoon in a cashmere sweater" sensibility is not something the agent develops — it's something that gets documented precisely enough that the agent can replicate it reliably. That's what `analysis/subject-line.md` is for. That's what Section 12 of `writing-style.md` is for. The Second Brain was always the onboarding document. For an AI agent, it is also the entire brain.

**Calibration is immediate if context loading is correctly designed.** A human partner calibrating over three months is learning to suppress their own instincts and replace them with Alyssa's. An AI agent has no competing instincts to suppress — it writes from what it's given. If the context is right, the output is calibrated from day one. If the output is wrong, the fix is in the context, not in the relationship.

**The risk profile shifts.** For a human hire, the risk is: wrong person, costs months to discover. For an AI agent, the risk is: wrong prompt, or context not loaded, or Second Brain gaps not filled before deployment. The gaps documented in Section 8 of this document are no longer "pre-hire checklist items" — they are pre-deployment blockers. An agent with no Simmer email examples will produce uncalibrated Simmer drafts on the first task, not the thirtieth. The gap becomes an immediate problem, not a slow-building one.

**Consistency is mechanical.** A human partner has good days and bad days. They build toward the voice over time but can also drift from it. An AI agent starts every session from exactly the same documented voice. Drift happens when the Second Brain isn't updated — not because the agent stopped paying attention. This means the Second Brain is not just an onboarding resource; it is the living source of truth that the agent reads before every task. Keeping it current is now a maintenance requirement, not a one-time build.

**The Second Brain is complete enough to deploy.** Section 8 of this document called it "hireable for limited scope right now." For an AI agent, the threshold is slightly different: the agent can work on any task where there is enough documented context to calibrate. For Simmers and subject lines, that context exists now. For sales pages, it exists now. For Soft Sundays, it doesn't — and the agent should flag that gap rather than guess at it. The hire-readiness verdict from Section 8 stands, but the framing shifts: instead of "limited scope until gaps are filled," it's "full scope with explicit fallback behavior when examples are missing."

---

### What stays the same

**The scope of work is identical.** Tier 1 (Simmers, subject lines, Soft Sundays), Tier 2 (sales emails, IG captions, Pinterest), Tier 3 (sales pages, opt-in pages) — all of it is this agent's domain. The weekly engine, the periodic work, the occasional high-stakes pieces. No change.

**What Alyssa always owns herself is unchanged.** The origin story in any new context, final approval before anything publishes, topics and prompts for Simmers and Soft Sundays, launch strategy and offer positioning, any copy where "I" is Alyssa making a personal claim she hasn't made before. An AI agent writing "as Alyssa" makes the ownership question more important, not less. This agent writes drafts. Alyssa publishes them.

**The Don'ts are non-negotiable.** They go in the system prompt, not just the reference files. "Level up," "take action," "mindset shift," "this is your sign," "do the work," "crush your goals," generic urgency, corporate positivity, passive voice, press-release tone — these are hard-coded exclusions. An AI agent that has access to the Don'ts list and ignores it is a prompt failure, not a calibration gap. They go in the operating contract.

**The read-before-write discipline is unchanged — and is now a hard protocol.** The routing map (`copy-task-routing.md`) described what a disciplined human partner should do before every task. For this agent, it is the operating procedure. The pre-task read sequence is not a suggestion; it is step one of every task, without exception.

**The quality gate still applies.** The question "does this sound like Alyssa, or does it sound like an AI?" is the same question it always was. The agent must self-check before delivering any draft. The self-check is structured and documented in the shim so that it runs every time, not when the agent remembers.

### The pre-task protocol (how this agent starts every copy task)

Before writing a single word, in order:

1. Load `PKM/Second Brain/me.md` — identity anchor, voice in five words, brand promise, personal markers, permission pattern.
2. Load `PKM/Second Brain/analysis/writing-style.md` — all 14 sections if time allows; at minimum Sections 1, 3, 5, 7, and 12 (Section 12 is never skippable).
3. Check `PKM/Second Brain/analysis/copy-task-routing.md` — find the specific task type; follow the routing table exactly (which files to read, which to skim, which to skip).
4. Load the task-specific analysis file(s) named in the routing table.
5. Read at least one real example from `PKM/Second Brain/examples/` — the one most relevant to the task type. If no example exists for this task type, note the gap, flag it to Larry, and default to `examples/about-page.md` as the closest available calibration source.
6. Then — and only then — write.

For Level 1 tasks (quick, low-stakes): steps 1, 2 (Section 12 only), and 6. No exception on Section 12.
For Level 3 tasks (sales pages, about pages, anything publishing publicly): all six steps, nothing skipped.

---

### The output quality gate

Before delivering any copy draft, the agent must self-check:

**Against the Don'ts (Section 12, writing-style.md):**
- No generic urgency language: "limited time," "don't miss this," "this is your sign," "last chance"
- No corporate positivity: "we're excited to share," "thrilled to announce"
- No hustle framing: "level up," "do the work," "crush your goals," "show up authentically"
- No passive voice or press-release structure
- No generic benefit stacking without specificity
- No "value" used as a vague noun (if it appears, it either has specific content or it goes)
- "Strategy" is only used in specific, actionable context — not as a selling promise

**Against the subject line formula (for subject lines and preview text):**
- Does at least one line have the raccoon-in-a-cashmere-sweater quality — short, casual, slightly emotionally loaded, a tiny bit wrong?
- Does any line explain the email content outright? If yes, cut it.
- Does any line contain generic urgency language? If yes, cut it.
- Is preview text treated as a second line with its own job — not a summary of the subject line?

**Against the food metaphor rules (for any copy using the metaphor):**
- Is the metaphor working structurally, or is it decorative?
- Does it appear at a transition point or CTA? Or is it showing up mid-argument where it creates noise?
- If removing the metaphor from a sentence makes the sentence clearer, remove the metaphor.

**The final check:**
Read the draft out loud (silently, as a mental exercise). Ask: does this read like a DM from Alyssa, or does it read like email marketing copy? If the answer is the latter, the calibration is off. Identify the specific sentences that broke the register and rewrite them before delivering.
