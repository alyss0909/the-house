## 1. Concept-Pitch Standard

**Cross-source finding: the industry-standard creative brief and HANDOFF-3's two-layer design agree.**

- Creative-brief literature (Asana, HubSpot, adlibrary.com) converges on the same field set already named in HANDOFF-3 §4: objective, audience, key insight/message, deliverable, and approval path, with the brief functioning as "a buildable creative bet" upstream of any copy — not a task list. Two independent sources (Asana's resource guide; adlibrary.com's 2026 "research-first" template) both distinguish a *creative brief* (carries a thesis) from a *job brief* (lists tasks/deadlines). [Asana, "Creative Briefs: What To Include"](https://asana.com/resources/how-write-creative-brief-examples-template); [adlibrary.com, "Creative Brief 2026: The Research-First Template"](https://adlibrary.com/posts/creative-brief) — **This validates SOP-034's backend field list is not over-built; it matches external professional practice, not agency bloat.**
- **Idea vs. topic distinction, corroborated twice**: Justin Welsh's content-matrix method explicitly separates *topic* (subject matter) from *angle/style* (the specific expression of it) as two different axes that must both be chosen before a piece of content is real ([justinwelsh.me, "My Content Matrix"](https://www.justinwelsh.me/article/content-matrix)). Independently, performance-marketing hook literature draws the same line one level down: "the angle is the strategic idea; the hook is one of many ways to express it" — teams that conflate the two produce either generic topics or hook libraries with no underlying idea ([billo.app, "Ad Hooks That Scale"](https://billo.app/blog/ad-hooks-variations/)). **This is independent, cross-domain (creator content + performance ads) corroboration of the SOP-034 fail criterion ("email marketing mistakes" style topic) and of the assembly-line split itself (Concept ≠ Hook).**
- HANDOFF-3's plain-language concept shape — *"a post about [reader problem] and [specific solution], funny/sharp because [belief it breaks], gives them [payoff], CTA [offer]"* — is structurally a compressed angle statement (problem + belief-break + payoff + CTA), consistent with both sources above. No external source contradicts using a single plain sentence as the Alyssa-facing artifact while carrying formal fields behind it; this bifurcation (thesis carried backend, single sentence surfaced) is a known creative-ops pattern, not a novelty.

**Verdict: SOP-034 as drafted is sound.** Tighten one thing: make "actual idea, not topic" (current pass criterion) explicit and testable by requiring every concept pitch to name its *angle* separately from its *hook candidate* before Hook Bench runs — otherwise Concept and Hook risk re-merging into one undifferentiated rung, the exact failure mode both external sources warn about.

**Anti-pattern to flag:** a concept pitch that is really a rebadged hook ("a post that opens with X") with no stated belief-break or reader payoff underneath it. This is the single most common creative-ops failure per both sources and is functionally identical to what Alyssa already named as Cassius's failure (arc without teaching underneath).

## 2. Substance-Dossier Standard

**Cross-source finding: "teach the real thing, sell the rest" is not a house quirk — it is standard high-performing content-marketing doctrine, independently confirmed.**

- HubSpot: two-thirds of content marketing programs lead with self-promotion, but **top performers prioritize audience needs first**, and 88% of high-performing teams focus on genuinely useful information over sales messaging ([HubSpot, "5 Benefits of Educating Prospects With Free Content"](https://blog.hubspot.com/blog/tabid/6307/bid/22840/5-benefits-of-educating-prospects-with-free-content.aspx)).
- Content Marketing Institute independently documents the same principle across multiple brand case studies: giving away real education, including material adjacent to what you sell, does not cannibalize sales — it raises lead quality because prospects arrive smarter about what the paid product actually does ([CMI, "Education as a Marketing Strategy"](https://contentmarketinginstitute.com/content-marketing-strategy/education-as-a-marketing-strategy-8-brands-doing-online-classes-and-more)).
- These two independent sources (HubSpot, CMI) directly support the **$5 test** and the **offer-desire test** as legitimate, industry-grounded pass criteria rather than arbitrary bars: a post that would not survive being sold standalone is, by this literature's own definition, not "educational content," it is promotional filler wearing an educational costume. The offer-desire test (post makes someone want the *linked* offer) is the second half of the same finding — real education is what builds "content evangelism" and qualifies leads, not a fear that teaching too much kills the sale.
- Carousel-specific direct-response literature (lower-confidence, single-source-cluster, flagged) describes carousels as "direct-response copy broken into visual beats," using curiosity/commitment psychology (each swipe = micro-commitment) and recommending 8–10 slides moving problem → proof → action for educational carousels. **Flag: these carousel-mechanics sources (AdStellar, Postnitro, Xara) are content-marketing-vendor blogs, not peer-reviewed or founder-level sources; treat as corroborating texture for slide sequencing, not load-bearing for the Substance standard itself.** They do not contradict anything in SOP-036; they support "recommended slide teaching sequence" as a legitimate dossier output.

**Verdict: SOP-036 as drafted is well-grounded, not a made-up bar.** The $5 test and offer-desire test both map cleanly onto documented buyer-psychology/content-marketing practice, cited above from two independent institutional sources (HubSpot, CMI). No external source suggests a softer standard would out-perform this one — if anything, the literature says most competitors *fail* this bar (two-thirds lead with self-promotion) and that's precisely the differentiation opportunity.

**Anti-pattern to flag:** "feels like Alyssa would teach this" without a real source pointer (already named as the SOP-036 fail case) is the exact failure mode HubSpot's data implies — content that performs like the bottom 66%, not the top-performer 12%, because it optimizes for looking-educational rather than being useful enough to sell.

## 3. Repo-Boundary Ruling

**Question: which Blotato/external-repo mechanics can be adapted as process/craft without becoming source or voice?**

The repo-alignment-audit.md and hook-patterns-map.md already did the mining and drew a defensible line; my job here is to validate that line against outside practice, not redraw it.

- **External confirmation of the line itself**: brand-voice literature independently states the same rule the repo audit already applied — "a key mistake is adopting a voice just because it's trendy; brands should choose traits that feel natural to their company," i.e., adopt method, not tone/voice, from any outside reference ([brandedagency.com / general brand-voice literature synthesis](https://www.brandedagency.com/blog/brand-voice-guidelines)). This is a soft, single-cluster corroboration (marketing-vendor content, not a primary ethics/IP source) — flagged as directional support only, not a hard citation.
- The repo audit's own distinction (Section on Blotato: "Do not adopt hook TEXT from this library, only the process mechanics") is stronger and more specific than anything found externally, because it's grounded in Alyssa's actual voice rules ([[Hermes/ANTI-AI]], [[Hermes/craft/captions-carousels]]) rather than generic brand-voice advice. **The repo audit's line should stand as-is; external search did not surface a more rigorous standard.**
- The clean, restatable version of the boundary for SOP-034/SOP-036 and any future repo intake:
  - **Adaptable as process (safe):** scoring mechanics (hook = 50% of score), test protocols (first-3-words, so-what bridge, ICE tie-break), QA infrastructure (control-validation calibration, subtract-points scoring, batch-variety gates), output-count discipline, platform mechanics (character folds).
  - **Not adaptable, ever (source/voice risk):** hook copy text, title-formula phrasing, brand-brief file structures that would compete with [[Hermes/BUSINESS]]/[[Hermes/TASTE]], any corpus under a non-commercial or unclear license (`shixinzhang/tiktok-viral-hooks` is CC BY-NC-SA — reference method only, per manifest.md).
  - **The test**: if adopting the mechanic changes *what a post says* or *how it sounds*, it's a source/voice risk. If it changes *how a post is checked or scored*, it's process and safe.

**Verdict: no correction needed to the existing repo-boundary ruling.** It already matches (and in places exceeds) external brand-voice best practice. Reopen it only if a future repo intake includes primary-source IP/licensing law — the current audit's own manifest.md licensing notes (MIT vs. CC BY-NC-SA vs. vendor/unknown) are doing the real legal-adjacent work already, not this research pass.
