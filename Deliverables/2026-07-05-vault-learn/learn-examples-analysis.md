## 3. Analysis Distillations — 3–5 Operational Findings Per File, With Verbatim Key Lines

### writing-style.md
1. Five-word voice ID, verbatim: *"Conspiratorial. Grounded. Dry-funny. Permissive. Precise."*
2. The short-punch-after-long-setup rhythm is her single most reliable sentence-level tell: *"I'd systemized practically everything I could and hired a team to help me get my precious hours back. But, ironically, who do you think ended up having to (micro)manage everything? 👍This girl 👍"*
3. Hard Don'ts, verbatim list: *"level up," "take action," "mindset shift," "this is your sign," "do the work," "crush your goals," "hustle harder," "be consistent," "show up authentically," "find your why," "live your best life."*
4. On "value": *"Please retire the word 'value.' We get it. You're helpful."*
5. Pain is named at the system level, never the person: *"your business is running entirely on front of house energy"* — never "you're doing it wrong."

### sales-page.md
1. Full persuasion arc, verbatim structural logic: *"Problem → Diagnosis → Pivot → Product → Life Vision → Proof → Curriculum → Bonuses → Credibility → Story → Objections → Identity → Proof again → Close."*
2. The pain-list formula: *"[craving/want] but instead [specific reality]"* with 😬 as the identification (not shame) marker.
3. CTA catalog has 21 unique phrases across three registers (food-metaphor, emotional-response-mirror, conversational) — never "Buy Now."
4. `RESULT:` is non-negotiable at the end of every curriculum phase — "closes the content loop with a promise, not a description."
5. The "not another system" objection is handled by redefining the word rather than dismissing the objection: *"A real system is simple: it's just a setup where different pieces work together to get a result… without you having to constantly manage it."*

### about-page.md
1. Core thesis: the about page sells *"the right to be trusted,"* not Back of House.
2. Opens with the reader's experience before her own name: *"Dreaming of late-night creative sessions by candlelight, but instead you're burning the candle at both ends just trying to keep your business running?"*
3. Trust ratio: for every authority "I" statement, roughly two vulnerability/personality "I" statements — about 2:1 toward vulnerability.
4. The solution (email + Pinterest) is buried past the halfway point deliberately: *"if she led with it, you wouldn't trust it."*
5. Testimonials, certifications, client rosters are all deliberately absent from this page — those live on the sales page. *"Testimonials would shift the page from 'you trust her story' to 'you trust her results.'"*

### home-page.md
1. Core thesis: the home page is *"a recognition device,"* not an introduction.
2. Marquee copy does the entire anti-hustle argument in three words: *"HUSTLE CULTURE IS OVERCOOKED."*
3. The strikethrough performs the brand philosophy live: *"~~running your business~~ your life a hell of a lot easier."*
4. Specificity beats aspiration every time — *"Finally making it to the 2nd floor of that antique mall"* over "more time for the things you love."
5. Never lead with credentials, client results, or her name on cold-audience copy.

### subject-line.md
1. North-star voice descriptor: *"Raccoon in a Cashmere Sweater"* — short + casual + slightly emotionally loaded + a tiny bit wrong.
2. Opens vs. clicks are different jobs — *"Your audience opens for Alyssa the person. They click for Alyssa the useful strategist."*
3. The single most important data point: `🙅‍♀️ DON'T "grow your list" do this instead…` — 5.42% CTR, more than double the #2 click rate.
4. `Why I burned down a perfectly good business (on purpose)` is the gold standard — wins both opens (56.08%) and clicks (1.7%).
5. Preview text is the single biggest missed leverage point — many emails ship with blank preview text; it should be treated as a second subject line doing a *different* job, never a summary.

### soft-sunday.md
1. First lines are almost never conceptual — always a scene, confession, or blurt: *"I cried into my fridge this week."*
2. The metaphor pivot is always confessed out loud, never pre-explained: *"I couldn't help but see the parallels"* — this honesty is what makes the metaphor land.
3. The Check In ritual (mood binary → anchor word → "the one thing" fill-in → "Wishing you a cozy, soft Sunday 🤎") is mandatory in every single issue, verbatim, no exceptions.
4. "Value" as a noun never appears — she names the specific thing given instead.
5. The voice-flagged email's tell: generic Label+description construction with zero personality inside the description (*"☁️ Strategy O1: Dust off the Trophies / Repurpose old workshops..."*) — accurate, but "no Alyssa in it."

### email-microstyle-forensics.md
1. Em-dashes in real body prose ≈ **zero**. All 62 raw hits are metadata/headings/templated blocks or the one flagged-AI email.
2. Trailing dots (`..`/`…`/`...`) are her real signature punctuation — combined ~110 events, dwarfing em-dash usage; the flagged AI email inverted this (21 periods, 1 trailing dot).
3. The banned construction, named explicitly: *"the crisp single-line antithesis 'It's not X. It's Y.'"* — the one clean instance in the whole real corpus lives inside the flagged-AI email.
4. ALL-CAPS emphasis is required and high (18–49 words per real email) — the flagged email under-capped at 10, an AI tell.
5. Vowel-stretching ("Buuuut," "Soooo," "juuust") appears multiple times per real email and zero times in the flagged one.

### simmer-opening-forensics.md
1. Pivot-timing budget: real Simmers pivot to business language at 1% (Delta), 29% (Bath), 34% (Closet) of body — hard cap ~35%, extendable to ~50% only if pre-paid by subject/preview/opening-conflict signal.
2. Scene-stakes test: opening scene must pass ≥2 of 4 — conflict, loss/stakes, absurdity, emotional charge. A static object of desire (e.g., "a ladder I want") scores 0/4.
3. Metaphor-nuance test (all 3 required): ≥2 distinct mapped business insights, internal tension/paradox, and a self-aware exit joke ("I swear I'm done with the bath metaphors").
4. The rejected draft failed on compounding grounds: pivoted at ~40% (over budget) with zero pre-payment AND ran on a static, conflict-free wishlist scene.
5. "Ladder = evergreen assets" fails all three metaphor tests: one flat insight, no tension, nothing to wink at.

### carousel.md
1. Archetype→metric map is the operational core: Trend Report → saves/non-follower reach; Manifesto → shares with zero CTA; Visual Joke → max cold shares; Data Argument → new follows; Story/POV → comments; Product Reveal → conversions.
2. The credibility formula recurs near-verbatim across every trend/data post: *"I've reviewed my notes (& industry reports) from last year and this is what I'm seeing in my own business and BTS with clients who make their money online but don't want to live online."*
3. Every carousel has exactly one pattern-interrupt slide, always at ~75–80% through, never the last slide.
4. The single most-used phrase in the whole dataset: *"make their money online but don't want to live online"* — appears in 4 of 9 posts, drives 87–93% non-follower reach on every post that uses it.
5. The manifesto format (Jan 22, "Wasting Time") earned 397 shares with **zero product CTA** — proof that permission-slip content is forwarded, not converted.

### carousel-hook-forensics.md
1. Rejected hook `Your List Isn't Cold. It's Just Full.` fails on 5 simultaneous, provable counts (reframe-shape-on-a-cover, no payoff, no target/2nd-person on cover, labels-don't-hook, filing-slug eyebrow).
2. Alyssa's covers NEVER use "It's not X, it's Y" reframe shapes — confirmed across a full scan of 12+ real covers; that shape only appears on interior implication/closing slides.
3. Cover length sweet spot: 4–9 words, with a hard ceiling near 10 only when a parenthetical payoff earns the overflow.
4. Cover punctuation rule: period or nothing — never a question mark on a claim/paradox cover (questions are reserved for CTA/engagement slides).
5. Every winning cover carries one of five payload types: fix, threat, confession, series-stake, or joke. Zero of these = not a hook.

### carousel-flow-and-weird-hooks.md
1. "Weird hooks" are coined-concept namings that presume the phenomenon already exists ("the tiny list effect," "the potato chip problem," "Stoner Math") rather than describing it.
2. RULE 1 — no reuse of a documented-winner construction with swapped nouns; `(on purpose)` and similar tacked-on parentheticals are now "burned" as detectably-hers formulas.
3. The credibility line on slide 2 IS the open-loop secondary hook — not two separate findings, one slide doing both jobs.
4. Slide-8 "aha" is always a **content** reversal (bait-and-switch, villain-reveal, reframe) — the visual pattern-interrupt is a separate device marking the CTA doorway, not the aha itself.
5. Validated default 9-slide spine for the Education/Trend archetype: Cover → Credibility/open-loop → Agitate → Value×4 → Aha (content) → CTA (visual interrupt here).

### boh-teaching-index-phases-0-2.md / phases-3-5.md
1. Verbatim answer to "what is content for," synthesized from her own words: *"Every pin, every post, every blog is 'funnel-forward' — its job is not views, it's to lead someone... onto the list, into the sequence, and down to the signature dish."*
2. The Double Yes Method: freebie = First Yes (pre-qualifier), tripwire = Second Yes — *"people who buy from you once are 70% more likely to purchase again."*
3. The Symptoms→Diagnosis→Prescription (documentary/doctor) structure governs the whole welcome sequence: *"People will not buy if they don't believe the diagnosis."*
4. The 7×10×4×5 Pinterest batching formula: 7 URLs × 5 templates × 2 overlays × 4 copy combos × 5 boards = 1,400 unique pins, "buy back 6 months of your time."
5. Feed-based vs. index-based visibility is her cleanest conceptual reframe: *"One resets. One compounds."*

### workshop-webinar-analysis.md
1. All three webinars open with an alignment move before any teaching — Identity Reclaim ("You didn't sign up to be an influencer"), Audience Mirror ("sick of never being done"), or Aspiration Confirmation ("if you want to unhitch your wagon...").
2. Same core statistics recur across all three: 0.1% IG link-click rate, 80% reach decline, 19-hour post lifespan, 60% zero-click Google searches.
3. The Portugal origin story is the structural center of all three, repositioned by context — structural center (Out of Office), compressed into the reframe (Slowly Viral), after-the-system-overview (Slow Marketing Plan pitch class).
4. Webinar voice diverges from written voice: almost no strikethrough/ALL-CAPS/parenthetical devices live, more sequential structure, lighter humor, more casual address ("guys").
5. Hard don't: never apply the teaching-webinar permission close ("social media becomes optional") to a pitch-class launch sequence — it undersells the offer; pitch-class closes are logistical inventories, not visions.

## 5. Contradictions and Flags

**1. "Cozy CEO"/anti-hustle brand voice vs. genuinely high output.** Flagged explicitly inside `voice-profile.md` itself as a "productive contradiction," not something I'm inferring: she sells rest and slowness *and* is intensely high-output (30+ launches, built her own app, 1,400-pin batching systems, learned to code). The file's own resolution: "never call the method lazy — it's strategic. The lazy is in the maintenance, not the setup." Not a real contradiction once you apply that rule, but worth flagging because a careless writer will trip on it — e.g., using "lazy" un-ironically anywhere but the officially approved "Lazy Launch" / "Lazy Girls" program names would violate this.

**2. Length rules aren't fully consistent across files.** `email-microstyle-forensics.md` gives a hard mechanical rule ("aim ≤ ~12 sentence-ending periods in a full-length email body"). `voice-archive.md` Q71 explicitly says she "doesn't give a fixed word count rule" and calibrates to medium/skimmability instead ("You're never too long if you're saying something new"). These aren't contradictory in spirit (both ultimately gate on "does every sentence do work"), but one file offers a hard number and the other explicitly disclaims hard numbers — a writer following the wrong one in isolation could over- or under-apply the rule.

**3. Carousel cover word-count rule is stated as absolute in one file, then the same file's own data disproves the absolute framing.** `analysis/carousel-hook-forensics.md` Rule 1 states "4–10 words. Sweet spot 4–9... Ten is the ceiling." But `analysis/carousel-flow-and-weird-hooks.md`'s own word-count table (built from the same corpus) shows several real covers at 9–10 words functioning fine specifically *because* of a parenthetical payoff overflow, and explicitly concludes: "'Under 7 words' is her aspiration and her best performers' pattern, not a universal law." The two files don't disagree on the underlying mechanism (overflow only works with a parenthetical payoff) but one states a stricter absolute ceiling than the other's own data supports. A writer should follow the flow-and-weird-hooks framing (main clause ≤7, parenthetical overflow allowed) as the more precise rule.

**4. Contrast/antithesis rules differ in strictness between the general voice bible and the forensic file.** `writing-style.md` Section 4 lists "And honestly?" and "au contrair" as approved pivot devices without restriction. `email-microstyle-forensics.md` goes further and explicitly *bans* the crisp "It's not X. It's Y." antithesis shape outright, citing the one clean instance of it living inside the flagged-AI email as proof. These aren't contradictory — the forensic file is simply a stricter, later, more mechanically precise pass over the same voice — but `writing-style.md` doesn't itself carry this specific ban, so a writer using only `writing-style.md` (which `copy-task-routing.md` designates as the mandatory Section-12-Don'ts read) could still write a banned antithesis construction without realizing it. **Recommend**: fold the em-dash-zero and antithesis-zero rules from `email-microstyle-forensics.md` directly into `writing-style.md`'s Don'ts section, since routing currently sends every task through `writing-style.md` but not necessarily through the microstyle forensics file.

**5. `me-i-need-ideas-i-already-had.md` is flagged inconsistently across files in terms of how "quarantined" it is.** The Soft Sunday INDEX says "use for content/strategy ideas only, not voice calibration." `analysis/soft-sunday.md` cites it multiple times as a *positive* structural example (the "what's in/what's out" is not used here, but other structural notes are drawn from it) while also using it as the canonical *negative* voice example in the same document. `copy-task-routing.md` doesn't mention this email at all. Not a factual contradiction, but the handling is inconsistent — some files use it only as an anti-pattern, `analysis/soft-sunday.md` uses it as both anti-pattern and (for structure) a legitimate reference in the same breath. A future reader skimming only one file could come away thinking it's either fully banned or fully fine.
