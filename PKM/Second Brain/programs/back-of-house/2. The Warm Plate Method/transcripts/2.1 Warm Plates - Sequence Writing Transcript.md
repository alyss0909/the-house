## Why Most Welcome Sequences Suck

[00:00:21] I could go on my soapbox here for hours about just how powerful this specific welcome sequence is and how many people get it wrong and end up kind of writing off email marketing in general because their welcome sequence doesn't work and so they believe email marketing doesn't work. And the problem with most welcome sequences is that they suck, and most people frame this as something that is really about them and their business and even really about their offers when, as we know from everything we've done thus far, really every part of this model is about your buyer.

[00:01:09] And what I've noticed as I've audited thousands of sequences at this point — at least thousands of emails — is that the way that people talk to me, the way that people share their offer, the way they solve their problem, their philosophy, the way they talk about it with people when they're at events or in person is so starkly different from the way that they write in their emails, the way that they talk on their sales pages. And it creates this really strange disconnect between what they actually believe, who they are, how they speak to people, and what their business is projecting onto their dream buyers.

[00:01:43] I think one of the quickest ways to get yourself in the right head space for writing these email sequences is to think about how you talk to people when you are in the room with them. If a lot of your business right now is based on word of mouth or referrals or being in person and being able to have a conversation with someone, you are already ahead of the game in how you can have these conversations over email. The whole point of what I've created in this sequence is really not to teach you anything other than how to take what you naturally do in normal everyday conversation and apply that to an actual email sequence.

## The Documentary Moment

[00:03:47] That's the goal of this email sequence. We wanna make a huge impact at this point, and this impact sequence is designed to give your audience that aha moment over and over again. And if you get this wrong, people will not end up on your sales page.

## The Symptoms / Diagnosis / Prescription Framework

[00:07:31] So this is the documentary and we break it down into three parts. I do mix my metaphors here because it's one part documentary and one part doctor. The perfect sequence in my eyes has three main parts:

1. **The first part** really digs into the symptoms that they're experiencing right now.
2. **Then** we actually get them to believe their diagnosis.
3. **The last part** is your prescription — here's what I think you should do.

[00:08:00] And I hinted at this so far in the documentary. In the beginning it's: here's what you're experiencing. By the middle you're like: here's the real problem. It's not that you are unhappy because you're depressed or because of the food you're eating or because blah blah blah. The real problem is that you're so overwhelmed that you never wanna spend any time in your house and you're constantly trying to escape. And then part three is the prescription.

[00:08:47] And so if I went to the doctor and they immediately heard my symptoms and said, okay yeah cool. You have a headache, no problem. Here, take this. I would be like, hang on. Maybe it's just that I need to drink more water. I don't know if I really want like a prescription right away.

[00:09:02] But if they start asking me questions about, oh, you have a headache. Is it over here? Is it over here? And do you notice that it gets worse whenever you are working? And does it hurt when I do this? And you're like, oh my God. Yeah, I had no idea. Like that's related. Then I'm really interested. I'm like, this guy gets it. This person — this is not their first rodeo. They've seen this before. And now I'm invested to hear what they think the true problem is. And that's when we can introduce the diagnosis. And when I truly believe the diagnosis and I'm trusting them, then when they say, okay, so here's what we're gonna do. I want you to take this prescription one time a day. I'm like, okay, yeah, I'm writing this down. I'm gonna try it.

[00:10:00] This is what we have to accomplish with your sequence. You're going to break up your emails into these three parts. I recommend choosing at least two symptoms emails, two diagnosis emails, and two prescription emails. But you can play with this. We've had people who have done as few as five emails, and we've seen people who have emails that are like eight or 16 parts long. So you can do whatever you think your person needs, but I always recommend starting with a six email sequence. So two symptoms, two diagnosis and two prescription.

[00:10:40] If I was going to beef it up, I would say have more in the diagnosis section. This is where you can really put a couple extra emails. So even if you just had one or two symptoms and then four diagnosis and two prescription, that would really work.

## The Symptoms Phase

[00:16:00] At this point, your goal is really to paint the picture of their problem. We wanna start to really highlight the symptoms that they are already experiencing, but also introducing some of these other symptoms that they might not have considered yet.

[00:16:16] I like to think of this like when you're Googling your symptoms, when something's going wrong, and then you start to see — do you have headaches? And are you peeing a lot? And you're like, what? I am, I thought it was just because I was getting older. And then they're saying, in that case, you might be pregnant. So we wanna start introducing some of these other symptoms or the odd connections between things that are going on in their life or their business or their relationships, and start to really draw their attention to those things while solving their problems. This is a really great way to get buy-in from them, that you get it, and get them to wanna binge your emails.

[00:18:00] The truth is, if your dream client doesn't see themselves in sentence one, they're not gonna read sentence two. So if you're talking about yourself over and over, it's like when someone starts opening up a photo album and all the photos are of somebody else's family — at a certain point you're like, was I at this party? So you've gotta make sure that they see themselves in these early emails.

## The Diagnosis Phase (Most Important)

[00:19:33] All right, let's get into phase two, which is the diagnosis. This is the most important part. This is the most important section of all of these emails. You have to get this part right, so take your time here. The truth is they will not buy if they don't believe the diagnosis.

[00:20:11] And the way we do that is give them aha moments and get them to see your solution as the only solution. While doing that, we wanna spend a long time here, so you have to make sure that each one of these emails is actually giving them quick wins, because the more quick wins you can give them, the more likely they are to continue to open the next emails.

[00:21:00] Every single email should follow a four part process:

1. **Create a belief shift** for them.
2. **Diagnose** and get them to buy into the diagnosis.
3. **Give them a win** while teasing the solution.
4. **Paint the picture** of what the outcomes are if they follow that solution.

[00:22:04] **Subject line:** "How to stop being so damn busy." — I hint at the fact that they're always busy, but also I'm suggesting that I'm gonna show them how to solve that problem within this email.

[00:22:19] Then I come out right off the bat: "Tell me if this rings a bell. Your business is your baby and there's this nagging feeling that you're spending all of your time at your desk, but you're not actually getting anywhere." — A very specific example of that symptom showing up in their everyday life.

## Final Tips

[00:33:14] This is a stock nurture sequence. This has nothing to do with your freebie. In a dream world, you could have 30 freebies and all of them will lead people to this sequence. So keep your freebie out of it as much as possible.

[00:34:03] Every single email has to have a clear call to action. You should have three ways for them to click on your call to action in every single email. Always add a PS. The PS should reiterate the same point of the email and link to that call to action.

[00:34:38] Make sure you're not writing novelish stuff. Imagine that someone's reading this in an email on their phone. Short paragraphs. Break things up. Bold key areas. Really leverage conversational copy.

[00:35:36] Once you've written the bulk of your emails, do one round of edits — a tone edit. Ask yourself: does this sound like me for real? Or was I trying to create a diagnosis email with a value add and I had all of these pieces that I was trying to put together in one email and I forgot that this is just a conversation between me and somebody at an event or me and somebody on a discovery call. Like, how would I actually talk? What would this actually be like?

[00:36:27] Alright? Get writing. I hope you enjoy getting the gems pulled out of you. And really, truly I want you to lean into the you that shows up as your best self, whether that's at events or on discovery calls, or just chitchatting with your dream clients. And put that into these emails.
