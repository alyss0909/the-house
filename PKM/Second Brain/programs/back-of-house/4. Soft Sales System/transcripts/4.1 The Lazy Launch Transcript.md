## What a Lazy Launch Actually Is

You've done the work, you've been growing qualified leads. They've come through your freebie, they've been through your sequence, and some of them have maybe even raised their hand with your right-time offers but not taken you up on actually joining. This is the perfect time to add in a promo period — I call this a soft stir.

They've been simmering. A certain percentage of them, every once in a while, need a little shakeup, a little nudge, in order to actually take action. Urgency really works for certain types of buyers. That's what this does.

Everybody out here is trying to overcomplicate what an actual launch is. It's not that deep. I've done more than 30 launches in my own business and been behind the scenes in thousands. At its core, a live launch is literally just:
- A couple of emails to a list that's warm
- Some kind of conversion event that gets people in the room with you, creates excitement, and a little bit of urgency that gets people to get off the fence

---

## The Essentials

A lazy launch: the stuff that does the heavy lifting and none of the extra fluff.

If I need a quick and dirty launch, these are the things I'm including every single time:

- **One offer** — your core offer; not changing anything
- **Your core philosophy** — nothing new
- **One conversion event** — a webinar, workshop, or other event
- **Five sales emails** — that's it

And as a bonus: how you recycle and repurpose all of this so that every single launch is more effective and more effortless over time.

If you've created your back of house correctly, you already have most of the resources you need. The same persuasion tactics you used to create your sales page, your freebie, your tripwire, your impact sequence — those are the exact same tactics you use to create any launch event and launch emails. Do the work once, then repurpose, recycle, reshuffle. Say the same things from a different angle to capture your audience at different points based on what kind of buyer they are.

## Prerequisites Before You Launch

Two prerequisites:

**1. About 300–500 hot leads on your list.** I've had clients launch with literally 50 or 200 people and have very successful launches — but those people were super hot because they came from live events or personal networks. The bottom line: you need a hot audience.

Signs your community is hot:
- Replies to your emails
- High open rate (30–50%)
- Good click rate
- DMs, traction on social posts
- People clicking on your click tricks and segmentation moments
- High engagement on your sweet segments

**2. People are actually engaging.** If you've been doing your simmer and your segmenting and your click tricks, you've already built a way to gauge: is now a great time to launch, or should I save my energy?

If you've come this far, you've already set yourself up for the email-first business model — your list is hot, you've been simmering, they've been raising their hand.

## Building Urgency: Coupons, Bonuses, Scarcity

The goal is to nudge the people who are already hot and probably ready. Give them a little urgency.

**Coupons:** Now is a great time to use the buffer you built into your pricing. Offer a coupon code. Extended payment plans. Tiered or early bird pricing. Go back to your re-offer strategy: what's the highest price you'd confidently charge, and what's the lowest price you're ready to offer during this promo?

**Bonuses:** Everybody loves a bonus. Highlight something cool that's already in your course, or add something new. Give it a value, call it a bonus, make it limited. Bonuses don't need to be overcomplicated. They should be fun, cute, nice-to-have things — like a gift with purchase that gets you to actually stand in line at checkout and buy the book because you know it's going to come with cozy reading socks. (Very niche Canadian reference to Indigo. Those who know, know.)

**The real urgency:** The coupon creates urgency. The bonus creates urgency. But you also want some reason for people to purchase *right now*:
- Countdown timer in your emails
- Disappearing bonuses at specific times
- Coupon actually expires
- Prices increase as days go by or as spots fill
- Limited availability or seats (share how many are filled as launch week goes on)

**Your urgency must be real.** If someone can just join next quarter, they'll join next quarter. Get ahead of that: "I'm going to open it again at this time, but this coupon and this bonus are expiring right now."

## The Three-Week Launch Framework

My tried-and-true go-to:

- **Week 1:** Push to Register — invites to your conversion event
- **Week 2:** Event Week — host your conversion event
- **Week 3:** Push to Buy — cart opens Monday, closes Friday

**Email breakdown:**
- ~4 push-to-register emails
- ~5 push-to-show + replay emails
- ~5 push-to-buy (sales) emails

**Hosting:** You can host your webinar/event twice — same content, just gives people more opportunities to see you live. Not required. If it's your first launch, just do one webinar on Wednesday and call it a day.

Everything is flexible — open cart on Wednesday, close on Sunday, do a two-week launch or a four-week launch. But use week 1 = invites, week 2 = event, week 3 = sale as the framework to move around.

## The Conversion Event: Bird's Eye View

The conversion event is the most important part of your launch. You need something to rally your people around and get them excited. Otherwise it's no different than the evergreen offers already built into your back of house.

I'll keep calling this a webinar because that's what I always host — but technically it can be anything that achieves the same belief-building objectives.

**Conversion event options:**
- Webinar / masterclass / live class / workshop (most common; they're all more or less the same thing)
- Paid workshop (be careful with this — with a small list, you want as many people as possible at your event)
- Private podcast
- Popup Zoom open house
- Live hot seat coaching / teardowns
- Challenge, hackathon, or write-a-thon
- Waitlist loot bag drops (send a freebie or gift each week leading up to cart open)
- Video series
- Virtual summit (I don't really recommend this in this instance, but it can work)
- In-person networking event, workshop, or live teaching

*It honestly doesn't matter what it is. What matters is what it does.*

---

## What Every Good Conversion Event Does

All of those event types do the exact same thing: **build a specific belief.**

You have to get them to believe in your philosophy. Get them to buy into your diagnosis.

**Step 1 — Disrupt industry norms:** Say why the old way is BS

**Step 2 — Validate their experience:** What is the nightmare? What is the dream? Validate their craving for a better way or an easier way.

**Step 3 — Reveal the new way:** Your philosophy. The better way they haven't thought of or looked at yet.

**Step 4 — Back it up by teaching your method:** Here's my philosophy, this is the new way, here's how it actually works.

**Step 5 — Give a reason to take action:** Come work with me.

---

Said another way:

1. **Call out the problem** — the thing they're sick of but still sucking at. "Here's what I'm noticing, here's the pattern I'm picking up on, and maybe you're feeling it too."

2. **Reframe it** — show them the actual reason things haven't been working. Give them the aha moment. *"I didn't think of it like that. She's so right."* If you can do that, you have them hooked. This is the key to a good conversion event.

3. **Reveal the better way** — your philosophy backed up by your system, method, framework, mindset. The shift they need to have. Gets them nodding their head.

4. **Call to action** — straight-up ask for the sale or the booking.

**What you're recycling:** The "let me guess" framework = calling out the problem. The "real problem" diagnosis = the reframe. Old way / new way = revealing the better way. You've already written all of this.

## Conversion Event Styles with Examples

**Hot seat coaching:**
- Kick off by calling out a pattern: "This is one thing I keep seeing over and over again, I'm so happy everyone's here because I want to specifically coach someone through this..."
- In the live session, coach one or a few people through it — the whole group learns
- You're reframing and revealing a better way in real time
- Drop hints throughout: "When I'm working with my clients in back of house..." or "Part of module three is this template I always use, let me pull it up and use it with you..."
- End with a proper close: offer to help them do the exact same thing behind the scenes; share the course or offer; give urgency

**Waitlist loot bag drops (4 weeks):**
- Drop 1: A loss calculator — calls out the problem, shows how much they're leaving on the table
- Drop 2: Red flag checklist — shows all the red flags in common freebies, why most people attract the wrong leads
- Drop 3: A Notion planner — reveals your way, gives a sneak peek at your philosophy + method with behind-the-scenes access
- Drop 4: An automation template — giving away the island, selling the boat. "Here's the automation — but let me help you actually use it."

**Paid workshop (everyone's working on something together):**
- Open by sharing why you're doing this thing differently — calling out what wasn't working before, why you hated it, what you actually wanted instead
- Teach the bird's eye view of your new way — the reframe + overarching philosophy (keep this quick)
- Core workshop: guide them through one great piece of your method, implement it live. This alone should be worth the ticket price.
- Close: invite them to build the full system with you. Show them piece one and that the other pieces exist. "Take this — if you want us to help you implement it all and you want our templates, here's where you can get that." → invite into your core offer

## What to Actually Teach at Your Event

We always want events to be worthwhile, interesting, worth coming to — not just a sales pitch. People should be taking notes, taking screenshots, being actually tuned in.

*There should be a turning point in the class where it feels like you've just handed them the secret code.* It should feel exciting and disruptive, but also like an obvious bridge from the nightmare (what's keeping them up at night) to the dream (what they want).

**What I always follow:** I tell them what they should do, when they should do it, and exactly why they need to do it now. All the nitty gritty details of *how* are delivered as soon as they pay.

I'm not really gatekeeping — I just can't go through everything in a quick class. It's much more effective to show them: here's my philosophy, here's why you should be doing this, here's the proof, here's when it makes the most sense, and if you want to know exactly *how* we do it — if you want me to hand it over to you or do it with you — that's where the signature dish comes into play.

**Two-way warning:** Make sure these events are good. You can absolutely burn out your list by having them feel like there's no point in going to your events because they're all just thinly veiled pitches. We have students coming to our webinars knowing I'm going to pitch, knowing how the launch method works — and they come anyway. Because I try to make them useful.

## The Call to Action at the Event

This is where you straight-up ask for the sale. Layout the scarcity and urgency.

- This can be to book a call — you don't have to name the price on the call; you can give away coupons on the call or hint at bonuses
- You can give specific bonuses for people who take action during the live event
- If it's something people come to live: "Anybody who books their call by this date gets this cool thing"
- Make it very obvious during the conversion event that there's urgency — the cart will close, the price will go up, or a bonus will disappear at a specific time

If your business model is a revolving door where people can always join — what makes them want to join *right now*? This is really important. You've probably had people at a launch say, "when's the next time you're opening this? I'll join then." Get ahead of that: "Yeah, I'm going to open it again at this time, but this coupon and bonus are expiring right now."

## The Five Sales Emails

The most important emails in the entire launch sequence. These happen after the conversion event and they're your push to buy.

**The five types:**
1. **The Announcement** — celebrate, explain the promo, share the promotion details, get hype
2. **The Logic Email** — for the spreadsheet girlies; numbers, data, stats, measurable benefits, proof of results
3. **The Emotion Email** — for emotional decision makers; paint the picture of how they'll feel, the be-do-have moment; who will they be, what will they be able to do, what will they have
4. **Last Day (Earlier Send)** — FAQ email; overcome the 3 main objections on the fence; make it clear what's expiring tonight
5. **Last Day (Final Send)** — short, urgent, countdown timer visible in the email; "this is it, clock is ticking, once that timer runs out here's what you'll miss"

You really only need to nail these five emails. All the rest are invitations to the event and reminders. But these sales emails do the heavy lifting.

---

## Breaking Down Each Sales Email

**Email 1 — The Announcement:**
Obvious. Celebrate with your people. Make it clear you're having a promo, share the promotion details (cool things, run out, take action before timer). Get hype.

**Email 2 — The Logic Email:**
For people who need the numbers and figures and data. Give it to them — results, stats, measurable benefits. At this point they cannot argue with the facts.

**Email 3 — The Emotion Email:**
For people who are more emotional decision makers. Get them in their feels. Paint the picture of how they're going to feel after. Give them the be-do-have moment: *Who are they going to be? What will they be able to do? What will they have after they take you up on your signature dish?* Paint the outcome — the dream on the other side of the bridge. Let them know what's going to change when they start following your philosophy.

**Email 4 — Last Day, Earlier Send:**
Generally I send two emails on the last day — one earlier, one just a couple hours before the timer runs out. The earlier one: "Hey, today is the last day." Lay the urgency on thick. But really use this one to get people off the fence.

Think about three major objections someone might have right now:
- Price
- Time
- Not totally bought in on a specific style you use

Use FAQs to overcome objections. If it's a time thing: "I keep getting this question about not having a lot of time to write emails... I'm going to give you these templates, show you how to write it in less than five minutes a day, and show you how to automate this so you get more time back every single day."

Make it obvious what's expiring tonight.

**Email 5 — Final Send:**
Just a couple hours or even minutes before the timer runs out. Short. "This is it." Clock is ticking, countdown timer visible in the email. "Once that timer runs out, here's what you're going to miss out on."

## Repurposing Emails for Social Media

It's totally optional to post on social during a launch. We've done launches with no social at all. But it's easy to repurpose your emails into social posts, and it is worth it.

**Mirror your emails with social posts:** If you're sending four push-to-register emails, have four socials that kind of match. Two push-to-show emails = two push-to-show socials.

**If you haven't posted on social in months:** Don't suddenly post a bunch of stuff on your feed and then ghost again. Leverage stories instead and mirror each email with stories.

**If you've been sprinkling things in:** Put it on your feed, get some Reels out there — sometimes that's enough for someone to think, "oh I also saw an email in my inbox about this event, let me open that."

**How to repurpose (example from a BOH announcement email):**
Original email copy: *"I didn't predict the future of marketing, but I don't have to — if you zoom out, one thing has never changed: the highest converting platforms are always search and email. With that data in hand, we double down on two things. The question shouldn't be what's working right now. The question should be what's always worked."*

Repurposed: Take the hook, build a carousel. Same copy, slightly adjusted hook. "This girl has no original ideas and back of house contains nothing new" → then the same copy runs as the carousel content.

The caption comes directly from the email. The call to action from the last slide of the carousel is pulled straight from the close of the email. People are not reading everything in your carousels and captions — reuse the good work.

## Recycling and Organizing Your Launches

This is the thing that gets missed the most and is the most important. If you have a really good way to take what works and recycle it, you can launch more often with even less work every time.

**My Google Drive folder structure for each launch:**
1. **Launch copy doc** — one big Google Doc with all launch copy
2. **Socials folder** — socials copy doc + all graphics/videos
3. **Webinar slides** — repurposed almost 100% each time
4. **Launch debrief document**
5. (Optional) Guest podcast pitching schedule, ads, affiliate emails

Everything together, easy to find, duplicatable for next time.

**Naming:** Keep all your launches organized by year and quarter (2019 Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4 / 2020 Q1...) so everything is right there and ready to be recycled.

## Launch Cadence: Grow → Nourish → Launch

I personally recommend having some kind of promo once per quarter. Lights a little fire, gives a little stir to your audience. Different people are hot at different times.

**Month-by-month template:**
- **Month 1 — Grow:** Launch ads, do collaborations, amp up your pins. Heavy focus on new leads coming in.
- **Month 2 — Nourish:** Don't skip a simmer email. Add videos to emails. Double down on segmentation and click tricks. Keep your people warm.
- **Month 3 — Launch:** Invite week → event week → buy week

Then the loop continues. Every single quarter: grow, nourish, offer.

This works because it gives you enough time to get new leads in the door and get those leads hot to make your promo periods worthwhile.

**Coming at your launch from 4 different angles (advanced):**
- Q1 angle: email marketing ("emails are the silent killer, you never have to go on social again")
- Q2 angle: Pinterest ("search is the new social, here's how we automate and batch Pinterest")
- Q3 angle: [another pillar of your philosophy]
- Q4 angle: [seasonal or personal]

That way you have four different webinars that each recycle once per year. What are the odds people remember your push-to-register emails from 12 months ago? Very unlikely. You can recycle the majority of that copy and those webinars every single year.

## Launch Debrief

Always do a debrief after every launch. Yes, even when you're sick of it.

The debrief gives you all the important information to not start from scratch next time, and to do more of the stuff that actually worked — making each launch more effective and more profitable.

**After each launch, review:**
- **Sales page** — any tweaks? New screenshots or client testimonials to add?
- **Freebie** — how did it perform? Are those the leads I wanted? Keep or change?
- **Launch copy** — what subject lines performed well? What to keep, what to change, how much can I maintain next time?
- **Socials** — which ones did well? Are there carousel slides to completely repurpose? If a carousel did really well but you don't want to post the exact same thing — take the hook, put it over a video, make it a Reel, take it to Threads or X.
- **Conversion event** — when did energy drop in the webinar? Which loot bag had low downloads? Put a date in your calendar to make edits based on attendance rate, engagement, close rate.

## The Mini Promo: 72-Hour Version

Maybe you want to just take the temperature of your list before doing a full launch. Or you want a quick cash injection without doing all the work. That's where the mini promo comes in.

Anytime I want a little cash in the door, I add a 72-hour promo — the same kind of shakeup, just on a smaller scale.

**How it works:**
1. Go through your right-time offers and choose a feature or a benefit
2. Choose a reason for them to join now
3. Email your list: "I'm not promoting this anywhere else for the next 72 hours. Anybody who joins gets [thing]."
4. Do socials? Maybe a couple sneaky stories. That's it. No big production.
5. Reply word → you send them the details (loom, Google Doc, checkout link)
6. Follow up a few days later: "Have you had a chance to check this out? There's only X amount of hours left."

**Key difference from regular right-time offer:** The whole email IS the offer, not a "by the way" at the end of a story email. You can still tell a story and give value, but the right-time offer should feel like the whole point of the email.

**No logistics in the initial email.** Save those for after they reply.

**72-hour promos in your sequence:** Take your best-performing right-time offers and add them into your sequence at the end. Don't add a countdown timer because it won't make sense, but create urgency with the copy. Keep the magic word very specific so you know which are in the sequence vs. live.

A little trick: we only send sequence add-on emails on Thursdays. Regular simmer emails go out on Sundays, promo emails on Tuesdays. So we know not to send anything live on Thursdays, because people in our sequence might be getting promo emails that day.

These are really easy ways to bring in some extra cash whenever you need it on demand, even when you're not in launch mode or not ready to be in launch mode yet.
