## What the Data Shows

### Archetype → Metric relationship

| If your goal is... | Use this archetype | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Saves + non-follower reach | Trend Report (series format) | Jan 15, Jan 20, Jan 29 |
| Shares (with zero product ask) | Manifesto / Identity mirror | Jan 22 (397 shares, no CTA) |
| Maximum shares (cold discovery) | Provocation + visual joke | Feb 19 (3,038 shares — #1 all year) |
| New followers from cold audience | Data Argument + fear hook | Mar 3 (491 follows — #1 all year) |
| Comments + conversation | Personal story / POV | Feb 24 (251 comments — 2nd highest) |
| Direct comment conversions | Product Reveal or Step Framework | Feb 6, Feb 10 |

### Series momentum is real

Jan 20 hit 132,758 views — the highest in the dataset — partly because Jan 15 (Part 1) had already primed the algorithm and audience. Part 2 rides warm momentum into cold feeds. Jan 29 (Part 3) shows series fatigue in raw views (18,112), but saves go UP to 905 because the remaining audience is the most qualified.

Rule: Start a series in January when strategic intent is highest. Part 1 should be the most accessible entry point.

### The "make money online but don't want to live online" phrase

Appears verbatim in Jan 15, Jan 20, Jan 29, and Feb 10. It's not filler — it's the brand's core audience-mirror phrase, and it's what drives the high non-follower rates (87–93% of reach from non-followers on all trend posts). When someone who's never heard of Alyssa sees those 9 words, they self-identify instantly.

## Pattern Observations

### 1. Every carousel has exactly one pattern interrupt slide

Every carousel has exactly one slide that visually breaks the template. In every case, it:
- Falls at ~75–80% through the deck
- Announces the CTA zone
- Uses a dramatic background shift or brand-logo-fills-frame treatment
- Is never the last slide — at least one slide follows it

This is not accidental design variety. It is a deliberate attention-reset at the highest-stakes moment.

### 2. Two cover variants appear in most carousels

Jan 15, Jan 20, Jan 29, and Feb 10 all include 2–3 cover versions in the same image deck. One is personality-forward (stops the scroll), one is SEO-optimized ("2026 Slow Marketing Trends for Online Service Providers..."). The SEO version is used for Pinterest repins where searchability matters.

### 3. The credibility formula is almost identical across all trend/data posts

> "I've reviewed my notes (& industry reports) from last year and this is what I'm seeing in my own business and BTS with clients who make their money online but don't want to live online."

This sentence appears near-verbatim in Jan 15, Jan 20, Jan 29, and March 3. Three credibility layers in one sentence: personal observation + research basis + client access. Do not paraphrase it — the consistency is part of the brand's trust signal.

### 4. The step framework carousels use a metaphor system, not plain labels

Feb 10 runs a restaurant/food metaphor through all four steps: "The Key Ingredient" / "Serve Your Bread & Butter" / "Take Reservations" / "Fresh Batches Same Recipe." Each headline is a metaphor that names the step without literally describing it. The reader has to swipe to decode — which is the primary engine keeping people reading. The metaphor is NOT decorative; it's the swipe mechanic.

### 5. The manifesto format does something no other type does

Jan 22 got 397 shares with no product ask, no comment trigger, and no data. Pure identity-mirror statements. The "For CEOs Refusing to Rush" footer appeared on every slide. People share permission-slip content with specific friends who need to hear it — which is why shares go up even with zero CTA. If the goal is word-of-mouth brand equity, not conversions, use the Manifesto format.

### 6. The provocation/visual-joke hook is replicable

Feb 19's strikethrough on "Daddy" → handwritten "Mama" correction generated 3,038 shares — the #1 most shared post in the dataset. The visual punchline is screenshot-worthy before anyone reads the copy. The self-correction format (say the taboo thing, cross it out, replace it with the feminist/brand-correct version) is a device, not a one-off. It should be used sparingly (maximum once per content cycle) for maximum-reach moments.

### 7. Fear hook + validation + cliffhanger is the best follow-driver

March 3 used a three-punch cover: (1) "Organic Marketing Is Dying" — fear + validation, (2) "Reach is way down" — observable symptom, (3) "The real reason might piss you off" — cliffhanger. This drove 491 follows — the highest follow rate in the dataset. New audiences find it, feel validated, and follow for the solution. Use this three-punch formula for launch week or any time list growth is the goal.

## Voice Patterns Identified

### The three-layer voice system

All 9 carousels operate on three distinct typographic voice layers. Each layer carries different content:

| Layer | Format | What it carries |
|---|---|---|
| All-caps spaced tracking body text | LOOKS LIKE THIS, WIDE LETTER-SPACING | Authority: data, argument, observations, explanation |
| Large editorial serif headline | **Looks Like This at giant scale** | Statement: the memorable phrase, the named concept, the paradox |
| Handwritten/script annotation | *looks like this* with arrows | Intimacy: personal asides, CTA whispers, insider notes |

**The handwritten layer is load-bearing.** "My handholding →" / "it's easier than you think" / "let her bring the traffic" — all the warmth and personality lives here. When it's absent, the carousel feels cold. When it's overused, it feels cluttered. One handwritten annotation per slide is the ceiling.

### Recurring voice signatures

- **The parenthetical aside** — main text makes bold claims; parenthetical does the intellectual honesty work: "(you've probably answered these questions verbatim already)" / "(it's hurting conversions)"
- **The extended vowel** — "BUUUT..." / "EEEVERYTHING" — signals spoken delivery, not polished copy. Used once per carousel maximum.
- **The self-aware understatement opener** — "Not to be dramatic but..." / "JUST KIDDING." — disarms before a bold claim
- **The personal evidence anchor** — "I'm seeing this with my own clients every single day" — appears exactly once per carousel, early in the teaching body
- **The rhetorical wink** — "How's that for a chill content strategy?" / "(Imagine that?!)" — invites the reader to share the feeling of relief

### The most-used phrase in the dataset

**"make their money online but don't want to live online"** — appears in Jan 15, Jan 20, Jan 29, and Feb 10. It is Alyssa's audience-definition in 9 words. It names an aspiration (make money online) and a rejection (not living there) simultaneously. Non-follower rates spike on every post that uses it.

## Design Patterns Identified

### Color is a content-category signal

The audience learns to read the palette as a signal about what kind of content they're getting:

| Background | Content type | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| Deep wine/burgundy red | Manifesto, brand identity, emotional moments | Warm, bold, stops scroll |
| Warm cream/parchment | Trend Report (series), Step Framework | Editorial, calm, research-backed feel |
| Pale powder blue | Data Argument, evidence-based posts | Analytical, clinical, airy |
| Dark charcoal/moody | CTA slides only | Maximum visual contrast at highest-stakes moment |

### Every carousel has a recurring prop character

One physical object appears on most or all slides, doing metaphorical work. Prop choices from the dataset:

| Prop | Carousel | The metaphor |
|---|---|---|

### Footer watermarks appear on every slide without exception

A consistent footer tagline on every slide:
- Brands every screenshot
- Functions as a content-series label
- Accumulates as a brand promise across the carousel

Active footer taglines in the dataset:
- `BACK — OF — HOUSE`
- `THE MOST — AESTHETIC — MARKETING PLAN`
- `FOR CEOS | REFUSING | TO RUSH`
- `ONLINE BUSINESS PREDICTIONS` + `Part N`
- `PREP YOUR BIZ FOR A NEW MARKETING ERA`
