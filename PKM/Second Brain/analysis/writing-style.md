## 1. Voice Identity

**The five words:** Conspiratorial. Grounded. Dry-funny. Permissive. Precise.

These are not generic adjectives. Here's what they earn:

**Conspiratorial** — She writes like she's letting you in on something the rest of the industry doesn't want you to know. The tone is "between us." Her credibility isn't performed; it's confided. She doesn't say "I'm an expert." She says she went down the rabbit hole *so you don't have to*, then hands over the findings like a friend who did the research.

**Grounded** — She references specific, mundane, real-world details: "the far park," "cutting my bangs (again)," "I forgot my phone at home." These aren't aspirational lifestyle signals — they're genuinely specific things that happen to a specific person. This is what separates her from the "laptop on a beach" influencer-marketer.

**Dry-funny** — Her humor lands because it's underplayed. She's not telegraphing the joke. "Not to toot my own horn, but this is *the* most comprehensive and supportive course on the internet" is funny because of the audacity immediately after the hedge. The joke is in the construction, not in the exclamation mark after it.

**Permissive** — She has a structural habit of giving you explicit permission to do the thing you secretly want to do. "So, go ahead, waste your time." "Let social media be a creative outlet again." This is rare in marketing, where everything is push-energy. Her pull-energy is what makes her brand distinctive.

**Precise** — She uses numbers when she uses them: 5848 tabs, 20hrs/wk, 20 years, 10 hours saved, $12k spent, 30+ launches. She doesn't say "lots of tabs" or "years of research." The specificity is what makes the casual delivery land — she's clearly done the work.

**What makes a line obviously NOT Alyssa:**
- Generic urgency: "Don't miss out!" / "Limited time only!"
- Corporate positivity: "We're so excited to share…"
- Hustle framing: "Working harder to achieve your dreams"
- Vague aspirational lifestyle: "Build the life you've always wanted"
- Exclamation point overuse (she uses them sparingly and usually for comedic effect)
- Passive voice or corporate structure: "It has been our experience that…"
- Empty affirmations: "You've got this!" as a standalone closing line
- Questions she's going to answer immediately anyway with zero dramatic tension

## 2. The Metaphor System

The food/restaurant metaphor is not decorative. It is structural — it organizes the entire brand architecture.

### Load-bearing brand vocabulary (core terms that anchor the brand)

These terms appear across multiple contexts and are non-negotiable in her brand:

| Term | Where it lives | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Back of House** | Course name, business concept | The primary brand anchor. The entire back-end operational metaphor. |
| **Simmer** | Module name, email strategy concept | The ongoing weekly email strategy — "keeping things warm" |
| **Warm Plate Method** | Phase name | Email automation phase |
| **Soft Sales System** | Phase name | Low-pressure promo approach |
| **Mise en Place** | Pre-work module name | The prep work before the course begins |
| **The Signature Dish** | Offer metaphor | The hero offer |
| **Specials** | Tripwires / upsells | Secondary offers |
| **Appetite / Hungry / Crave** | Desire language | Replaces "want" in key moments |
| **Cooked up** | Creation language | "I've cooked up just the thing" |
| **Ingredients** | Course components | Replaces "elements" or "modules" |
| **Recipe** | System / strategy | Replaces "system" in casual contexts |
| **Reservations** | Enrollment | "Now Taking Reservations" |
| **Kitchen** | The program container | "Questions for the Kitchen?" |
| **Prix Fixe Menu** | Course curriculum | The full scope laid out |
| **Nonna** | Alyssa herself (as keeper of the recipe) | "passed down from nonna (a.k.a. me 😉)" |
| **Hot and ready** | Automated / prepped | "hot and ready to serve" |
| **Set it and forget it** | Automation outcome | Meal prep language applied to marketing |
| **Feed the machine** | The content hamster wheel | What she's helping you escape |
| **Burnt out** | Used literally AND as food pun | Layered meaning, never forced |
| **Half-baked** | Incomplete / low-quality | "half-baked content" / "half-finished ideas" |
| **Tastebuds** | Discernment / standards | "isn't ever going to tickle your tastebuds" |
| **Slow and low** | The strategic approach | Cooking method applied to marketing pace |
| **A silver platter** | Ease of delivery | "delivered to you on a silver platter" |
| **Slow cooking** | The business model | Opposite of hustle/fast food |

### How the metaphor shows up across contexts

**Course names:** Every phase has a food-adjacent name. The Warm Plate Method. The Simmer. The Lazy Launch. Mise en Place. These are not theme names bolted onto neutral content — the food language is the pedagogical container.

**CTAs:** "SET UP YOUR BACK OF HOUSE" / "SOUNDS DELISH" / "YIKES, THIS IS TOO REAL" / "YOU HAD ME AT DIRTY DISHES" / "I'LL ORDER THIS, PLEASE" / "LET'S SLASH THE MENU" / "I'LL HAVE WHAT SHE'S HAVING" / "ORDER HERE" — every CTA either uses food language or translates it into action language.

**Section headers:** "NOW TAKING RESERVATIONS" / "NOW SERVING:" / "FRESH FROM THE KITCHEN" / "BEST SERVED TOGETHER:" / "THE PRIXE FIX MENU" / "A FIVE-STAR REVIEW" / "CAREFUL IT'S HOT:" / "THE REVIEWS ARE IN"

**Body copy:** The metaphor reaches into explanation: "Maybe it's because you're about one post away from lighting a match to your content calendar, but something tells me creating half-baked content isn't ever going to tickle your tastebuds." This isn't just one metaphor — it's three (lighting a match = burning down, half-baked = incomplete, tickle your tastebuds = satisfy your standards) interwoven naturally.

**The dirty dishes line (a masterclass in extended metaphor):**
> "when you have your very own Back of House, it's like having an entire team of chefs cooking everything for you, with enough time and creativity to send each plate out with a little *somethin' somethin'* and all you have to do is ask, 'would you like parm with that?' But when you're on the hook for being everywhere all at once, it can kind of feel like making dinner with 584 dirty dishes in the sink, every burner on, table 2 needs water (and you've already burnt the butter)."

This is the metaphor at its most developed — two full scenes, complete with sensory detail, contrast built into the image itself.

### Rules for extending the metaphor

1. **The metaphor must serve the point, not perform it.** She doesn't force food language into every sentence. Long stretches of body copy run without any metaphor at all.
2. **Use the metaphor at transition moments.** Section headers and CTAs carry the most metaphor density. Body copy in the middle of an argument drops it.
3. **Specificity > cuteness.** "584 dirty dishes in the sink, every burner on, table 2 needs water" beats "a messy kitchen." The specificity is what makes it land.
4. **Never use the metaphor where it would create confusion about meaning.** The FAQ section is mostly metaphor-free. When she needs to be clinically clear (pricing, tech, time commitment), the food language recedes.

### Parallel metaphor systems

**Rabbit hole / hyperfixation:** "I go down research rabbit holes for fun" / "AT THE END OF THE RABBIT HOLE: Pinterest and Email" / "We went down that rabbit hole so you can go down yours." This tracks the ADHD/creative-brained characteristic throughout the brand story.

**Tabs / windows:** "5848 tabs open (on my computer and just as many in my head)" — the overwhelmed creative is consistently drawn as a person with too many open windows, both literally and mentally.

**Machine / beast:** "feed the machine," "the content beast," "the machine is never full" — this is the anti-metaphor, the thing she's helping you escape. The contrast with food/nourishment (which implies real satisfaction) is deliberate.

**Compound / investment:** She uses financial and time-investment language to legitimize slowness: "compound over time," "outlast the algorithm," "ages like fine wine." This reframes "doing less" as a smarter long-game, not laziness.

## 3. Sentence Structure & Rhythm

This is the hardest section to generalize because her rhythm is genuinely varied. The patterns below are what make her sound like her.

### The short-punch-after-long-setup reversal

Her most common rhythm move: set up a long, winding observation, then land a 3–7 word sentence.

> "You're a big-picture thinker who dreams of *rest*, not churning out another Story that'll disappear in 24 hours (just to prove that you *haven't* disappeared). **You're craving more leisurely pursuits like:**"

> "I'd systemized practically everything I could and hired a team to help me get my precious hours back. But, ironically, who do you think ended up having to (micro)manage everything? **👍This girl 👍**"

> "Everything was '*working*'. **You think I'd be thrilled, right?**"

The short sentence after the long one does two things: it names the punchline AND earns the pause before the pivot.

### Fragment as punchline

She uses incomplete sentences to land observations that don't need the full construction:

> "Au contrair. I was, well, for a lack of a better term, kinda miserable."

> "…but it *sucked*."

> "*That's* low-maintenance."

> "Not another 'system'..."

> "Blogging? **Got it.** Instagram? **Let's automate it.**"

The fragment often arrives after a longer explanation. The truncation signals: "I don't need to say more. You get it."

### Em-dashes, ellipses, and italics

**Em-dash** is used to insert an aside mid-sentence, adding color without breaking the flow:

> "After almost 10 years running an online business, being behind the scenes in hundreds of service pros, & digital marketing research from the last 20 years—they're the most low-maintenance, sustainable tools for growth I've found."

> "you'll have a lean (but satisfying!) offer suite"

Em-dashes also do parenthetical work she wants to emphasize more than actual parentheses:

> "Leads find you; not the other way around."

**Ellipsis** signals trailing off, or a pregnant pause before a pivot:

> "I've been using them for years… But now, they're the ONLY things I'm focusing on."

> "Although my early 20s had *a series of brief stints* in other industries…"

**Italics** serve three distinct functions:
1. **Genuine emphasis on a key word:** "it was … new information at best, *kind of* concerning at worst" / "Everything was '*working*'"
2. **Self-aware quotation marks around a word she's using ironically:** "*actually* dreaded" / "*a series of brief stints*"
3. **To mark a brand phrase or concept:** "*soft* place for your ideas to land" / "*that's* low-maintenance"

### The "and honestly? [question]" opener

One of her most distinctive openers is using "and honestly?" as a rhetorical bridge that seems like a conversational aside but actually pivots the argument:

> "**And honestly? How could it?**"

This construction (appears twice in the sales page alone) creates the feeling of a friend catching herself mid-thought: "wait, no — actually." It validates the reader's frustration by asking a question they're already answering in their heads.

### Line breaks as rhythm

She uses hard breaks not just at paragraph level but within arguments, creating a staccato effect:

> "And it's not because your offer isn't good enough.
> Or because you're not posting enough."

> "1). Keep feeding the (always hungry) content beast.
> 2). Scrap EVERYTHING high-maintenance and double down on the parts of my business that out-performed the rest."

> "Every day feels like you're starting from zero because… you are."

These breaks force the reader to pause at exactly the moment she wants them to. In web copy, line breaks are punctuation.

## 4. The Pain Point Architecture

She names pain in a way that makes people feel understood rather than exposed. This is rare and specific to her.

### The "craving X but instead you're Y" structure

The sales page deploys this at length, using the emoji 😬 as a consistent opener for each contrast:

> "😬Spending time working ON your business **but instead** **you're deep in the weeds** brainstorming new content to keep up with what's trending"
> "😬Taking a long lunch break **but instead you're eating lunch at your desk (again)** hustling to write this week's newsletter after skipping out on the last two weeks (*oops*)"

The pattern: **[idealized behavior] + "but instead" + [actual exhausting reality]**. The structure does something sophisticated — it names the *aspiration* first. She's not starting with "you're burned out." She's starting with "you want to take a long lunch break" — an inherently dignified desire. The shame, if any, comes from the contrast, not from being called out directly.

The *oops* at the end of the newsletter example is critical. It doesn't judge. It commiserates. It says: "yeah, same."

### How she validates before she redirects

On the about page, before she ever names the solution, she builds a full scene:

> "On paper, everything looked solid. My business was doing well (actually better than it had ever been), I'd just come back from a biz funded maternity leave, saved 10 hours each week and cut my work week down to 3 days. Everything was '*working*'."

She validates that the conventional markers of success were there. Then she pivots. This is important because it prevents the reader from thinking "well she just didn't work hard enough." She proves she worked the system and found it wanting. The reader's skepticism is pre-handled.

### Pivot language: "SOS," "and honestly?", "au contrair"

**"SOS 🙃 (and no, that's not short for 'sauce on the side')"** — Used at the peak of the pain section as a comedic pressure valve. The parenthetical pun is there to keep the tone from getting too heavy. This is self-aware enough to show she knows she's naming something uncomfortable, and playful enough to give the reader permission to laugh at it.

**"And honestly?"** — Used to change direction or name an obvious truth she's been building toward. Not rhetorical filler — always a genuine pivot.

**"Au contrair"** — Used in place of "on the contrary." French, used casually and slightly anglicized. Signals: irreverence toward formal register without losing authority. It's not a joke about French — it's just how she would actually say it. Do not treat this as an error to correct or a gimmick to explain.

### Making people feel seen without amplifying shame

The core technique: **she names the situation, not the person.** She never says "you're burned out because you're doing it wrong." She says "your business is running entirely on front of house energy." The problem is structural, not personal.

The testimonials she chose reinforce this. The Meghan Bonhomme testimonial includes "I have capacity in my jam-packed schedule" and "instead of burning my business down (again!) or reinventing the wheel" — language that mirrors Alyssa's without sounding coached, which is rare.

## 5. Casual Registers & Lowercase Markers

### Exact vocabulary

| Word/phrase | How she uses it |
|---|---|
| **lol** | Soft self-deprecation: "running my business felt, well, *hard* (lol)." Always in parentheses. |
| **ya** | Appears in the house calls section: "What to prioritize when ur stressed and tired lol" |
| **bc** | "bc i *forgot* my phone at home" — lowercase, mid-sentence |
| **ur** | "when ur stressed and tired lol" — only in extremely casual contexts (community notes, not hero copy) |
| **kinda** | "kinda miserable" / "kind of concerning at worst" — both contracted and uncontracted versions |
| **fkn** | "which make you really fkn good at what you do" — saves the strong language for a line she really wants to land |
| **obvi** | "a pinterest inspo board to match (obvi)" — in parentheses, throwaway |
| **dude** | "Dude, we must be soulmates." — opens the about page, immediately collapses the professional distance |
| **whole ass** | "a whole ass strategy I came up with in the shower" / "I literally learned to code to create a whole ass app" — used for genuine size/audacity emphasis |

### Lowercase vs. ALL CAPS pattern

**Lowercase** appears in mid-sentence casual insertions — the aside, the throwaway, the qualifier. She often drops to lowercase *within* an otherwise normal-cased sentence to signal informality without breaking the sentence structure.

**ALL CAPS** is used for:
1. Section headers that function as bold declarations or brand proclamations: "HUSTLE CULTURE IS OVERCOOKED" / "NOT TO BE DRAMATIC BUT:" / "CAREFUL IT'S HOT:"
2. Single-word emphasis inside a sentence where italics aren't enough: "they're the ONLY things I'm focusing on" / "the highest-converting platforms have always been email and search. CROSS 'MARKETING' OFF YOUR LIST ONCE AND FOR ALL."
3. Marquee/scrolling text: "HUSTLE CULTURE IS OVERCOOKED // HUSTLE CULTURE IS OVERCOOKED //"

**The pattern is not random:** ALL CAPS = public declaration or brand statement. Italics = personal emphasis within a thought. Lowercase informality = intimacy, aside, throwaway joke. Mixed-case body = default.

### Strikethrough as a humor device

She uses strikethrough to show the "polished" version being crossed out in favor of the real one, or to name the thing she's replacing with something better:

> "I got my dream job right outta university, and promptly left 3 months later to ~~squander my potential~~ work for myself."

> "those (brilliant) ideas ~~scribbled in your notebook~~ now haunting your good night's sleep"

> "turning emails into ~~customers~~ regulars"

> "Consider Back of House to be the left-brained order to your right-brained ~~chaos~~ genius."

> "making ~~running your business~~ your life a hell of a lot easier"

The strikethrough is doing something very specific: it names the conventional, expected, or self-deprecating framing — and visibly crosses it out. The replacement word is always warmer, more aligned with her worldview, or funnier. It's the written equivalent of "actually, scratch that."

### Parenthetical asides as inner monologue

She uses parentheses for at least four distinct types of aside:

1. **Self-aware correction:** "(Actually, that's not true. I *did* look back, but only to data mine historical marketing trends so you don't have to)"
2. **Comiserating joke:** "(*oops*)" / "(lol)"
3. **Qualifier / important nuance:** "(even when you're off the clock)" / "(even if you're just starting)"
4. **Self-referential brand aside:** "(and no, that's not short for 'sauce on the side')" / "(a.k.a. me 😉)"

The inner-monologue parenthetical is her most consistent device for maintaining two tones at once — the authoritative voice and the "also I'm a real person" voice — without shifting the whole sentence's register.

## 6. The Permission-Giving Voice

This is arguably her most distinctive pattern and the hardest to fake.

### Direct examples of permission-giving language

> "So, go ahead, waste your time."

> "A good idea loses its wings every time a creative is too busy to go down a rabbit hole"

> "We went down that rabbit hole so you can go down yours (without EVER getting behind on sales and marketing.)"

> "Let social media be a creative outlet again."

> "The slow marketing system for creative entrepreneurs that books clients while you rest, recharge, or chase your next big idea."

> "Back of House isn't designed for you to do more."

> "...and all you have to do is ask, 'would you like parm with that?'"

### How it connects to her brand promise

The permission voice is structurally tied to the product promise: the back of house *runs itself* so that *you* can go do the things you've been feeling guilty about doing. Permission isn't just tone — it's the mechanism of the offer. She's not selling a marketing system. She's selling freedom from the guilt of not marketing. Every permission statement is an argument for the product.

### The "go ahead, ___" construction

> "So, go ahead, waste your time."

This is the clearest crystallization of her brand. "Wasting time" is reframed as good creative stewardship. "Go ahead" is not passive — it's an explicit nudge that acknowledges the reader's hesitation and overrides it. The construction implies: "I know you're afraid to do this. I'm telling you it's okay."

### How she frames "doing less" as strategic, not lazy

She never uses the word "lazy" as a genuine aspiration (though "Lazy Girls" appears in an approved-by reference on the sales page). The reframe is always strategic:

> "The quickest way to your next client is slow marketing"

> "slow and low is kind of our speciality"

> "the most low-maintenance, sustainable tools for growth"

> "Built to outlast the algorithm, and only requires you to set things up once (and well)."

> "Back of House is THAT dish: **predictable, proven, and permanent**"

Doing less is framed as doing smarter. The emotional release comes from the permission; the intellectual justification comes from the data (20 years of email ROI, compounding search content, etc.).

## 7. Humor Mechanics

Her humor works because it's specific, not performed. She doesn't announce jokes. She builds them into structure.

### Self-deprecating humor

> "I got my dream job right outta university, and promptly left 3 months later to ~~squander my potential~~ work for myself."

The strikethrough does the work. She's naming the worst-case read on her decision and then crossing it out — but you've already seen it. It's funny because she's saying the thing she's not supposed to say.

> "(& thank my ADHD for regular new additions)"

Self-deprecating but also framed as a feature. The parenthetical is key — it's thrown away, not spotlighted.

> "Not to toot my own horn, but this is *the* most comprehensive and supportive course on the internet..."

The hedge ("not to toot my own horn") immediately followed by a bold claim is funny because of the audacity. She knows it sounds boastful; the hedge is performative, not genuine.

### The aside-as-punchline

Many of her funniest moments are buried in parentheses or asides:

> "*(Small European towns really put things into perspective, don't they?)*"

> "*Approved by Lazy Girls.*"

> "*(& thank my ADHD for regular new additions)*"

> "Very niche Canadian reference to Indigo. Those who know, know."

The aside-punchline only works if the main sentence lands first. She never leads with the joke — it's always a footnote to something real.

### Absurdist specificity

> "I have 5848 tabs open (on my computer and just as many in my head) at all times…"

The "5848" instead of "thousands" is what makes it funny. It's too specific to be generic — it's clearly a real number, which is funnier than a round one.

> "584 dirty dishes in the sink, every burner on, table 2 needs water"

Again: specificity. "Table 2" is there. Someone has to go to table 2.

> "Being able to take my daughter to the 'far park'"

Not "a park" or "the park." The *far* park. This is the kind of specific detail that only comes from real life, which is exactly why it lands.

> "Cutting my bangs (again)"

"Again" is the joke. One word, zero setup needed.

### When she doesn't use humor

The FAQ/objection section of the sales page is the most clinical section of her writing. When she's directly addressing a fear ("I already have so little time. How am I supposed to complete a full course?"), she drops the humor almost entirely and gives a straight answer. She earns the right to be funny everywhere else by being genuinely helpful when it matters.

The testimonial excerpts are also humor-free. She lets her clients speak plainly; she doesn't editorialize their language.

### Strikethrough for comedic effect

See Section 5. The mechanism is the visible revision — you see the "wrong" word before the "right" one. That's the joke structure.

### Rules she seems to follow

1. **Never "Buy Now," "Sign Up," or "Click Here."** These phrases don't appear anywhere in her copy.
2. **CTAs are either in the reader's voice (first-person) or in food/brand metaphor language** — rarely just a command.
3. **CTAs that follow a key moment are always reactive** — they read like what you'd say if you were the reader who just read what you wrote. "YOU HAD ME AT DIRTY DISHES" appears immediately after the dirty dishes paragraph.
4. **Multiple CTAs to the same page use variety** — she doesn't repeat "JOIN BACK OF HOUSE" fourteen times. She cycles through different framings.
5. **ALL CAPS for CTAs that are declarations; mixed case for conversational ones.** "SOUNDS DELISH" is a shout. "I'll order this, please" is a quiet request.

## 9. Data & Credibility

She uses data, but she gives it personality.

### How she introduces data points

Never cold. Data is always embedded in context:

> "after almost 10 years running an online business, being behind the scenes in hundreds of service pros, & digital marketing research from the last 20 years they're the most low-maintenance, sustainable tools for growth I've found."

No bullet. No "FACT:" header. It arrives as part of a sentence that already has her voice in it.

> "Email has been the #1 highest ROI channel for over 20 years."

This *is* a standalone statement — but it's in a list with a 📩 emoji, which keeps it from feeling like a stat dump.

### The specific data she uses

| Data point | Where it appears |
|---|---|
| "almost 10 years running an online business" | About page, sales page intro |
| "behind the scenes in hundreds of service pros" | About page |
| "digital marketing research from the last 20 years" | About page, home page ("last 20 years") |
| "#1 highest ROI channel for over 20 years" | Sales page |
| "saving you 20hrs/wk on marketing" | Sales page bullet |
| "saved 10 hours each week" | About page (personal story) |
| "cut my work week down to 3 days" | About page (personal story) |
| "over 30 launches" | Lazy Launch transcript |
| "$12k and lots of time with multiple programs" | Testimonial (Madalyn) |

### How data is framed with personality

> "I've done more than 30 launches in my own business and been behind the scenes in thousands." (Lazy Launch transcript)

There's no citation. She doesn't need one. The number lands as credential, not proof. The structure implies: "I know what I'm talking about because I've done this an absurd number of times."

The "I data-mined historical marketing trends so you don't have to" line on the about page is particularly good — it converts the research from a credential into a service. She didn't just study the data; she did it *for you*.

### What makes her credibility feel earned vs. boasted

She always credits the *work* rather than the *status.* She doesn't say "I'm a top email strategist." She says "after 10 years running an online business" and lets the implication do its job. The Portugal story earns the credentials by showing the stakes: she had success by the industry's metrics and still found it wanting. That vulnerability converts the data into humanity.

## 10. The Origin Story Arc

She has one origin story told multiple ways. These are not different stories — they're different edits of the same footage.

### The key elements of the Portugal story

1. Business was doing well by external metrics (successful, funded maternity leave, 3-day work week)
2. Portugal trip — small fishing village, peaceful
3. Came back and *dreaded* going back to work — new and alarming
4. Named the real problem: she'd systematized and hired, but was now managing the system instead of the creative work
5. "Burned it all down" — the hyperfixation response
6. Dove into the rabbit hole
7. Came out with: Pinterest + Email
8. Built the system that let her go down rabbit holes without sacrificing marketing
9. Now teaching others to do the same

### How the story is told differently in different contexts

**About page version:** Personal, first-person, chronological. Includes the numbered "things you should know about me" list (the tabs, the antique market, the checkmarks). The story is told as confession, not case study. It includes the self-aware aside: "(Small European towns really put things into perspective, don't they?)" The focus is on *who she is* as much as *what she did*.

**Sales page version:** Edited for emotional resonance, faster pace, higher stakes. Key additions: "As a Certified Solutions Person, I just knew every time a new algorithm update or platform popped up, I'd have to come up with a new solution to 'feed the machine.'" The irony is underlined — the systems person created a system that chained her. The ending is a proposition, not a confession: "What if we just slashed the menu, so to speak?"

**What she always includes:**
- The Portugal location and moment of dread
- The "on paper everything was fine" setup
- The hyperfixation pivot
- The email + Pinterest conclusion

**What she varies:**
- The list of personal quirks (about page only)
- The "Certified Solutions Person" frame (sales page only)
- The depth of the "burned it all down" moment
- How long she spends on the problem before the pivot

### The emotional arc

Thriving (surface level) → Dread (the alarm bell) → Hyperfixation (her native problem-solving mode) → System (the output of the hyperfixation) → Permission (the gift she's now passing forward)

This arc is important because *it mirrors what she promises her audience.* She went from the content hamster wheel to a back of house that runs itself. The story IS the product demonstration.

## 11. Section Header Style

ALL CAPS headers are a signature. They're not just formatting — they're a voice.

### Grammar patterns

Headers are frequently not full sentences. They're:
- **Brand phrases:** "SLOW MARKETING FOR CEOS WITH BETTER THINGS TO DO" / "HUSTLE CULTURE IS OVERCOOKED"
- **Incomplete setup lines:** "NOT TO BE DRAMATIC BUT:" / "CAREFUL IT'S HOT:" / "THAT'S EXACTLY WHY:"
- **Scene-setting declaratives:** "LET ME SET THE SCENE FOR YOU:" / "SO…. SUFFICE TO SAY:" / "AT THE END OF THE RABBIT HOLE:"
- **Restaurant-language headlines:** "NOW TAKING RESERVATIONS" / "NOW SERVING:" / "FRESH FROM THE KITCHEN" / "THE PRIXE FIX MENU" / "MEET YOUR BACK OF HOUSE DEPARTMENTS:"
- **Audience/community markers:** "IF THERE'S ONE THING I KNOW ABOUT YOU IT'S THIS:" / "BUT MAYBE THE SINGLE MOST IMPORTANT THING YOU SHOULD KNOW ABOUT ME IS THIS:"

### Marquee/scrolling text

The "HUSTLE CULTURE IS OVERCOOKED // HUSTLE CULTURE IS OVERCOOKED // HUSTLE CULTURE" and "NOW SERVING: The Back of House Every Soft CEO Needs | NOW SERVING: The Back" and "This isn't fast food baby, it's slow marketing prepared to perfection." lines are explicitly designed to scroll as marquee text. She marks these in the copy itself.

Marquee text is reserved for her most compressed brand statements — lines that could stand alone as billboards.

### How headers set up the copy below

**"NOT TO BE DRAMATIC BUT:"** — the colon and the self-aware hedge preemptively defuses the boldness of what follows. The header says "this is a big claim and I know it." The copy then makes the big claim about everything shifting.

**"CAREFUL IT'S HOT:"** — implies: stop scrolling, this is important. The copy below is about the secret ingredient to a softer business. The metaphor works double duty: hot food = pay attention, and the content IS "hot" (urgent/valuable).

**"LET ME SET THE SCENE FOR YOU:"** — transitions the about page from list-of-facts to narrative mode. The "for you" makes it a service, not a monologue.

**"THE MORAL OF THIS STORY?"** — closes the about page narrative with a deliberate fable structure, which then lands the brand promise: "We went down that rabbit hole so you can go down yours."

## 12. What She Never Does — The Don'ts

### Generic marketing phrases she actively avoids

These phrases do not appear in her copy: "level up," "take action," "mindset shift," "this is your sign," "do the work," "crush your goals," "hustle harder," "be consistent," "show up authentically," "find your why," "live your best life."

The word "strategy" is used but always in specific, actionable contexts — never as a vague promise.

She does not use "value" as a noun without irony. The Lazy Launch transcript explicitly mocks it: *"Please retire the word 'value.' We get it. You're helpful."*

### Corporate tone markers she doesn't use

- Passive voice ("it has been found that")
- Third-person self-reference ("Alyssa Coleman is a strategist who...")
- Press release language ("we are excited to announce")
- Generic benefit stacking without specificity ("save time, make money, reduce stress")
- Motivational poster language ("success is a choice")

### Fake urgency language she avoids

She has real urgency mechanics (countdown timers, expiring bonuses, real scarcity) and the Lazy Launch transcript is explicit: **"Your urgency must be real."** This is doctrine, not just style. You won't find "only 3 spots left!" without actual limited spots, or "this offer expires" without an actual expiry.

### What kinds of lists she doesn't write

She does not write generic "here's how to [X]" tip lists. Her lists are either:
- Structural (course phase breakdowns with food metaphor names)
- Personal/specific (the "a non-exhaustive list of the little things I'm making time for" section — things like "Agatha" and "Becoming a clay artist")
- Contrast-based (the 😬 "you're craving X but instead Y" format)

A bulleted list of "5 tips for better email marketing" would not appear in her copy without being shaped by the system she teaches.

### The HUSTLE CULTURE IS OVERCOOKED moment

The scrolling marquee "HUSTLE CULTURE IS OVERCOOKED" at the top of the home page before the pain point section is her clearest anti-pattern statement. It signals: I am not going to tell you to work harder. This frames everything that follows. Anyone who enters her brand through hustle-culture language (even accidentally) is using the wrong voice.

### The "not another system" objection she handles — and what it tells us

From the BOH sales page:

> "I get it. Everyone and their dog (who currently has more followers than you) has a 'system' to sell you. Some secret $50K blueprint, or 'effortless' automation (that only works if you turn around and sell it to someone else🧐). *Ugh*. The buzzword has been stretched so thin it barely means anything anymore."

She actively mocks the exact language her product might be categorized under. This tells us: she is acutely aware of the crowded market she operates in, and she distances herself from it by naming and roasting the worst versions. The don't is: never use "system" as a vague selling point. If you use it, define it precisely and immediately.

## 13. The "We" vs. "I" Pattern

She shifts intentionally between pronouns. The shift is meaningful, not random.

### When she says "I" (personal, story, authority)

"I" appears consistently in:
- The origin story (first-person narrative is the only way to tell it with authority)
- Personal trait lists ("I go down research rabbit holes for fun," "I send 'k here's the plan' voice notes")
- Authority claims based on personal experience ("I've done more than 30 launches")
- Confessional moments ("I was, well, for a lack of a better term, kinda miserable")
- When describing her proprietary thinking ("What I prefer is to send one more thought-out email")

"I" = the person who did the work and is now teaching it.

### When she says "we" (brand, community, inclusion)

"We" appears when:
- Making brand-level promises: "Lucky for you, slow and low is kind of our speciality"
- Describing what the program does together: "We hand you the secret recipe"
- Building community sense: "We went down that rabbit hole so you can go down yours"
- Naming what the program includes: "we've also scripted, templated, designed, and strategized pretty much EVERYTHING"
- Meta-copy and program descriptions: "Our slow marketing systems help online business owners..."

"We" = Alyssa + the program + the student community. It is inclusive and implies team without being corporate.

### When she uses "you" (direct address)

"You" is used most heavily in pain-point and vision sections — the places where she's directly naming your experience:

> "You've been hustling to land better leads and make more sales."
> "You're a big-picture thinker who dreams of *rest*."
> "You'll have a lean (but satisfying!) offer suite."

"You" does the empathy work. It closes the distance between the writer and the reader. The home page opens with "you" within the first paragraph and stays there through the pain section before shifting to "we" at the solution.

### How it shifts across page types

- **About page:** Starts with "you" (opener question directed at the reader), then shifts almost entirely to "I" for the story, then returns to "we" at the close for the brand promise section.
- **Home page:** "you" in pain section, "we" in solution section, "I" in the personal lifestyle/time connoisseur section.
- **Sales page:** Heavy "you" throughout pain/contrast structure, "we" in course description and what's included, "I" in the founder bio and origin story.
