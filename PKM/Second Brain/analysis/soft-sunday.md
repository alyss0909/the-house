## 1. What Soft Sundays Reveal About Alyssa's General Writing Voice

The Soft Sunday reveals the Alyssa that her sales pages and about page can only gesture toward. The public copy is conspiratorial and grounded; the newsletter is where "slightly unhinged" is a literal self-description she uses in the body of the email and doesn't apologize for.

### How she opens

The first line of a Soft Sunday is almost never a concept. It is almost always a scene, a confession, or a blurt.

- "**I cried into my fridge this week, [FIRST NAME GOES HERE].**" (your-list-called-theyre-hungry) — bold, first-person, physically specific, slightly absurd
- "**I failed statistics in university [FIRST NAME GOES HERE] … twice**" (i-failed-formula-for-growth) — confession, with built-in self-deprecation and the ellipsis doing comedic timing
- "it's Mama Bear version of Alyssa writing today, **because there's something that's got me fired up right now** ok?" (ur-not-new-march-plan) — declares a persona shift, sets emotional tone before any content
- "There's something about September that always kinda feels like standing at a cross road, don't you think [FIRST NAME GOES HERE]?" (if-lorelai-had-an-online-biz) — opens with the reader's feeling, not Alyssa's fact

The common thread: the first sentence drops the reader into something real or emotionally loaded before a single piece of business content appears. The exception is "I FIXED your email list" — that email opens with "Ok, I know growing your email list is on your mind (proud of you for that)" — still personal, still direct address, still relational. It never opens with "Today I want to talk about..."

### How she earns the pivot from personal to business

She doesn't pivot abruptly. She draws the parallel out loud, then signals the turn with a connector phrase.

From your-list-called-theyre-hungry:
> "🍳 As it happens, I was also working on a NEW 3 day email list revival plan for IG and I couldn't help but see the parallels."

She names the pivot explicitly — "I couldn't help but see the parallels" — which is itself a small permission structure. She's not claiming the fridge story was planned as a metaphor. She's confessing she noticed the connection. That's more believable and more intimate.

From i-failed-formula-for-growth:
> "I actually love numbers now, because (just like my TA) they actually make a difference in my daily life"

The stats failure story runs for five paragraphs before she earns the turn to business formulas. She doesn't rush the personal section because the story IS the point — it reframes numbers as human, which is exactly what she needs the reader to feel before she hands them three formulas.

### Warmth without losing the edge

The warmth in Soft Sundays is never saccharine. She uses "babe" once (i-fixed-your-email-list: "You deserve it, babe") and it works because the surrounding copy has already earned intimacy through specificity and the big sister persona. When she says "ily (in a big sister kinda way)" in the same email, the qualifier does the work — it's warm but it's not fawning.

The edge is preserved through:
- Direct instructions with the softening already baked in: "**Lovingly, I need you to cut that sh*t out.**" (ur-not-new-march-plan)
- Calling out behavior patterns without making the reader feel stupid: "every. single. new. month. we wake up, stretch, sip our little coffee… **annnnd promptly forget everything we learned last month?**" — the stretched-out "annnnd" is doing comedic distancing so the call-out doesn't sting
- Setting up the reader's bad habit and then not dwelling on it: she pivots to the fix within one sentence

### Length management

The emails range from tight (your-list-called-theyre-hungry, which is 3 sections total) to explicitly self-described as "sorrrta long & is slightly unhinged" (i-fixed-your-email-list). What drives the choice appears to be the content type:

| Email | Length | What drives it |
|---|---|---|
| your-list-called-theyre-hungry | Short | Single metaphor, single CTA, topic is simple |
| i-failed-formula-for-growth | Medium | One story + three formulas, clean structure |
| ur-not-new-march-plan | Medium | Three steps, each with one product |
| if-lorelai-had-an-online-biz | Long | Four buying seasons, each detailed |
| why-i-burned-down-my-business | Long | Three reasons + personal reflection, origin-story weight |
| i-fixed-your-email-list | Very long | Full plan (3-day process + 7 email types + automation) — she pre-disclaims the length |

The pre-disclaimer on length is specific to Alyssa: "HEADS UP: this email got a little out of control, sorrrta long & is slightly unhinged on account of me sharing everything you can do NOW..." She doesn't apologize — she warns you and makes it sound like a good thing. The parenthetical that follows: "(sorry & your welcome)" — lowercase, no apostrophe in "your," which is either intentional (character) or a live typo she left in because it reads like something she'd actually send.

### Register shifts and when they happen

Across all six emails she operates in at least three distinct registers, often within the same section:

**Personal/intimate:** When she's telling a story, sharing a life moment, or speaking as a friend. Lowercase is heaviest here. Sentences are short or half-finished. Parentheticals appear frequently.

**Strategic/teacher:** When she presents a framework, formula, or plan. Formatting gets cleaner. Headers appear. Bold carries the key concept. But she never goes fully clinical — the humor stays, just the information density increases.

**Hype/energizing:** When she's building to a product or action. Energy goes up, words stretch ("Buuuut," "eeeease," "looooong"). Capitalization increases. The tone is: best friend who just got excited about something and wants you to get excited too.

The register almost never shifts mid-sentence. She finishes a thought in one register, then the section break or the next paragraph shifts the key.

### What makes these feel like a real person vs. an AI that read her once

Four things:

1. **The specific, useless-for-marketing detail.** "I MIGHT have been in my luteal phase... but i swear that had nothing to do with it" (your-list-called-theyre-hungry). A brand manager would cut this. Alyssa leaves it in because it's true and because it's the kind of detail that makes readers feel like they're reading a real person's actual Sunday email, not a content asset.

2. **The nested aside.** The stats email has a "(sidenote within a sidenote)" that runs five lines about writing an essay on wind turbines for a midterm. This is not efficient. It is completely human. AI would not put a nested aside in a body copy paragraph because it breaks the information architecture. Alyssa does it because that's how she actually thinks.

3. **The visible typo left in.** "sorry & your welcome" in i-fixed-your-email-list. It probably is a typo. It reads like she sent it before she proofed it, which is exactly the energy of a human sending you an email.

4. **The pivot that's openly confessed.** She doesn't pretend the fridge story was a planned metaphor. She says "I couldn't help but see the parallels." That honesty is more convincing than a clean constructed metaphor would be.

## 2. What's Unique to Soft Sundays vs. Her Other Copy

### Personal life details that only appear here

| Detail | Email |
|---|---|
| Crying into the fridge (luteal phase mention) | your-list-called-theyre-hungry |
| Helping grandma move into a retirement home, handling china and tablecloths | i-fixed-your-email-list |
| Making nonna tomato sauce from scratch, two hundred jars, kitchen smelling like san marzanos | if-lorelai-had-an-online-biz |
| Building a custom GPT rabbit hole over 6 months, creating 7 assistants | me-i-need-ideas [content ref only] |
| Burning down the business in March 2024, the slow dread starting to sink in | why-i-burned-down-my-business |
| Failing stats twice in university, nearly crying in front row, the TA who was "almost definitely high" | i-failed-formula-for-growth |

These details do not appear on her about page, home page, or sales page. They are newsletter-only. They serve a specific function: they signal to readers that this is a different level of access than public-facing copy.

### Self-deprecating oversharing

Only in Soft Sundays does she:
- Warn readers a specific email is "sorrrta long & is slightly unhinged" (i-fixed-your-email-list)
- Admit she "brought down the professor's vibe by holding back tears in the front row semester after semester" (i-failed-formula-for-growth)
- Confess a pivot is related to her hormones and then immediately walk it back ("I MIGHT have been in my luteal phase… but i swear that had nothing to do with it") (your-list-called-theyre-hungry)
- Name-check that she "haaaad just figured out how to replace myself for a 9-month-self-funded-mat-leave" — the stretched-out "haaad" signaling she's proud of this but also aware she's bragging (why-i-burned-down-my-business)

### The pep talk framing ("big sister voice")

The Pep Talk section is the primary zone for this voice. Its header text is always: "the weekly pep talk you didn't know you needed (from your business big sis)." The big sister voice operates differently from the teacher/strategist voice:

- Big sister addresses the reader's emotional state before the business problem
- Big sister uses "ily" and "babe" (in context)
- Big sister calls the reader on a pattern while keeping the warmth ("Lovingly, I need you to cut that sh*t out.")
- Big sister expresses pride: "Ok, I know growing your email list is on your mind (proud of you for that)." (i-fixed-your-email-list)
- Big sister uses "my business besties (you)" — the parenthetical makes "besties" feel less like a marketing word and more like a term she uses and then catches herself using (i-fixed-your-email-list)

What would make this cringe: removing the parenthetical qualifiers that keep the warmth from becoming performative. "You are my business bestie!" without the self-awareness would read like a brand voice. "My business besties (you)" reads like a person.

### The anchor word ritual and "one thing" prompt

Every single Soft Sunday ends with this exact format in The Check In section:

1. A binary mood question (two options given, reader chooses their vibe)
2. "Give yourself a word or a feeling to anchor into when you're in the thick of the week if that's your thing."
3. "This week I'm going with: [WORD]" — always her word, always bolded, always bookended with the same emoji twice
4. The cloud emoji: "☁️ then, can you answer this? **the one thing:**"
5. The fill-in: *"my week would be a success if the only thing I did was ________________."*
6. Close: "Wishing you a cozy, soft Sunday 🤎"

This ritual does not exist on any other copy asset. The anchor word changes every week; the format never does. Her words across these emails: "magic," "Restore," "Clocked In," "prolific," "grounded," "fired up." None of them are generic productivity words. They are emotional or atmospheric.

### Sections that only exist in Soft Sundays

| Section name | Function |
|---|---|
| **The Pep Talk** | Personal story + big sister energy + emotional framing |
| **The House Report** | Business teaching, "what's in / what's out" format or steps |
| **Nerding Out On** | Tools, tech, automations she's currently into |
| **Bake Break** | Prompts, frameworks, things to marinate on |
| **Time Luxuries** | Life lately — offline time, personal stories, what she's making time for |
| **Just Upgraded** | Products, promos, what's new and on sale |
| **On The Calendar** | Events, sessions, replays with CTAs |
| **Creep My List** | Permission for readers to copy her current focus/to-do |
| **The Check In** | Ritual close: mood question, anchor word, the one thing, sign-off |

Not every email uses every section. Shorter emails (your-list-called-theyre-hungry) use three. Longer emails (i-fixed-your-email-list) use six or seven.

## 3. Emoji Use — Surgical Rules

### Which emojis she uses and where

**Section openers / header area:** She uses emoji to mark the start of a named section and again at the close of the header text. This creates a visual bracket:
- 🫶 _the weekly pep talk you didn't know you needed (from your business big sis)_ 🫶
- ✨ _Life lately: celebrating the little things..._ ✨
- 🍦 _ok, let's take a minute to check in..._ 🍦
- 👯‍♀️ _the inside scoop on current upcoming events..._ 👯‍♀️
- ✍️ _Some Inside Intel on what's working..._ ✍️
- 💅 _What's new, what's hot..._ 💅
- 🤓 _tech, tools, automations..._ 🤓
- 👀 _for the nosey ceos..._ 👀

The bracket pattern (emoji — italic text — emoji) appears in EVERY section header across all six emails. This is structural, not decorative.

**In-body emphasis / reaction:** Single emoji at the start of a bullet or a moment that needs visual weight:
- 🥺 "Is it you?"
- 🥹 "Is it them?"
- 🫠 The overwhelm moment
- 🤡 "I **wasn't getting to work on the fun parts**"
- 🔥 "**So we burned it all down.**"
- 🐣 "after I came back from maternity leave"
- 🧸 Cozy / playful emphasis
- 🎁 Free resource offered
- 💫 Vision / big sister moment

**Punctuation-style emoji:** Used like a period or exclamation at the end of a sentence where the emoji IS the reaction:
- "Maybe that had nothing to do with it 🙃"
- "and I hate feeling like it's a task that will **literally NEVER be off my to do list.**" — no emoji here, interestingly, the caps do the work instead
- "my profit margins were shrinking fast 🫠"

**The 🤎 emoji:** The Soft Sunday signature color. It appears in:
- "Wishing you a cozy, soft Sunday 🤎" — ALWAYS at the close, every single email
- In-body moments of genuine warmth: "🤎 _We prioritized making things feel more human with things like these soft sunday emails_" (why-i-burned-down-my-business)
- Anchor word framing: "🤎 **Restore** 🤎" (i-fixed-your-email-list), "🤎 **One focus** per month" (i-fixed-your-email-list)

### Density rules

In the Pep Talk section: roughly 1 emoji per 2-3 lines of text. They appear at the start of key points, at emotional peaks, or as section-break visual punctuation.

In the House Report teaching sections: density drops. When she's delivering structured content (3-day plan, 4 buying seasons, 3 formulas), emoji appear at the start of each numbered item but not mid-explanation. The teaching mode uses emoji like bullet points, not like reactions.

In The Check In: 2-3 emoji for the mood options, the anchor word is bookended by its own emoji twice, the cloud emoji ☁️ marks "the one thing," and 🤎 closes. This section has the most consistent emoji density because the format never changes.

### What wrong emoji use looks like

- Emoji mid-sentence where it interrupts the reading flow rather than punctuating it
- Using emoji that don't appear in her existing vocabulary (she does not use ✅, 📌 as generic check marks outside the ur-not-new-march-plan structured list context; she does not use 💪 for hype)
- Placing emoji AFTER bold text where the bold already does the emphasis — doubling up dilutes both
- Three or more emoji in a row without section-header context
- Using emoji to signal enthusiasm in a way that feels like a brand account ("🎉 Exciting news!! 🎉") — she would never do this

### Recurring combos

- 🫶 ... 🫶 — the Pep Talk bracket
- ✨ ... ✨ — the Time Luxuries bracket
- 🍦 ... 🍦 — The Check In bracket (ice cream = treat, soft energy)
- 👯‍♀️ ... 👯‍♀️ — On The Calendar bracket
- 💅 ... 💅 — Just Upgraded bracket

These are fixed. They do not rotate.

## 4. Bolding Patterns

### What gets bolded

**The punchline or payoff of a thought.** After a longer setup, the bold lands the landing:
> "And I won't pretend it was a simple journey, it took a lot of **un-learning, a lot of re-remembering** and a series of dominoes falling that led to completely restructuring my business." (why-i-burned-down-my-business)

The bold is on "un-learning, a lot of re-remembering" — the unexpected pairing that IS the insight.

**The thing she most needs skimmers to catch.** If you read only the bold text, you should be able to follow the argument:
> "And since I've never met a good problem i didn't like to solve with automation 🤖... I tried automating this, hiring that person, using this software... **But here's the truth:** It ended up leaving me completely overwhelmed." (why-i-burned-down-my-business)

"But here's the truth:" bolded signals: stop here, this is the turn.

**Key concepts and named frameworks:**
> "automated email sequence," "updated lead gen strategy," "new automated email sequence" — when she names a specific mechanism, it gets bold.

**Direct commands or pivots:**
> "**Lovingly, I need you to cut that sh*t out.**" (ur-not-new-march-plan) — the whole sentence bolded because it IS the thesis of the section
> "**Buuuut, after I came back from maternity leave**" — the "but" turn gets bold to signal the pivot

**Product and offer names when introduced:**
> "**project simplify was born**" — launch moment of a new thing she named
> "**My BABY** (and long-time best seller) **MPQ was closed**" — the name gets bold but the structural phrase does too

**The "one thing" setup and "this week I'm going with" anchor word:** Always bolded, every email, no exceptions.

### What does NOT get bolded

- Parenthetical asides — these are designed to read lighter, not heavier
- The personal story setup paragraphs — the narrative builds into the bold, it doesn't start there
- Platform or tool names mentioned in passing (Kit, Flodesk) unless they're being positioned as a key teaching point
- The sign-off / close of the email
- The section header italic text (it's italic, not bold, by design)

### Bold + other emphasis interaction

Bold rarely doubles with italics in the same word. When both appear in a section, bold does conceptual emphasis (what to remember) and italics do tonal emphasis (how to read the word — irony, stress, self-awareness). They serve different registers.

Bold + ALL CAPS does appear occasionally: "**literally NEVER**" (why-i-burned-down-my-business) — the bold holds the phrase, the CAPS land the specific word with extra weight. This is a highlight-within-a-highlight and appears sparingly.

### Density

In Pep Talk sections: roughly 3-6 bolded phrases per 200 words. Dense enough to create a skim path but not so dense that everything is "important."

In teaching sections: higher density because more concepts need calling out. The 3-day plan in i-fixed-your-email-list bolds each step label AND the key instruction within each step.

**The bolding wrong signal:** When bold appears in every sentence, nothing is bold. In the voice-flagged "me-i-need-ideas" email, the strategy section reads as lighter on bold than the other emails — each strategy is a cloud header with brief unbolded description. This contributes to the flatter, more AI-adjacent feel — there's no clear skim path, so nothing stands out.

## 5. CAPS, Lowercase, and Punctuation Patterns

### ALL CAPS in newsletters vs. sales pages

In sales pages, ALL CAPS is used for section headers and brand declarations ("HUSTLE CULTURE IS OVERCOOKED"). In Soft Sundays, ALL CAPS appears in body copy for emotional peaks and in what feels like a shouted parenthetical.

**Emotional peak CAPS in newsletters:**
- "**🔥 So we burned it all down. (yep, even the profitable parts)**" — the CAPS was NOT used here, notably; she trusted the fire emoji and bold
- "**We're entering march as grown ass women with MEMORIES!**" (ur-not-new-march-plan) — CAPS on MEMORIES because it's the audacity of the concept
- "**EVERYTHING came out**" (your-list-called-theyre-hungry) — CAPS for a physical action, the emphasis is comedic
- "**and I couldn't help but see the parallels. YOU KNOW WHAT TO MAKE!**" — CAPS on the payoff moment
- "**I FIXED your email list**" — subject line CAPS on the verb for punchy energy

The newsletter CAPS rule: use it for the ONE word or phrase in a section that is the apex of the emotional or comedic arc. It almost never appears on a plain noun or a structural label.

### Lowercase starts to sentences and what they signal

Intentional lowercase mid-email (not at sentence start) signals intimacy, stream-of-consciousness, or de-escalation after an intense moment:
- "we made creativity the priority." (after the all-bold, fire-emoji burndown moment) — the lowercase is almost a breath
- "it's Mama Bear version of Alyssa writing today" — lowercase "it's" to open a Pep Talk section; feels like she started typing before she set up the thought
- "bc i *forgot* my phone at home" — referenced in me.md, appears in newsletters as natural text

The pattern: the more personal and confessional the sentence, the more likely the lowercase. The more structural and teaching-mode, the more standard title/sentence case.

### Ellipsis patterns

**Trailing off (genuine hesitation or pause):**
- "I paused, I pulled back, I sat with the uneasiness of not knowing what was next, and **somewhere in that quietness, my next steps started whispering back.**" — no ellipsis here, she completed the thought; the period is the pause

**Building suspense before a pivot:**
- "👀 ... But then **i started to enjoy it**" (why-i-burned-down-my-business) — the emoji + ellipsis creates a visual stutter, slowing the reader down right before the reversal
- "🙈 … And then i started to **dread the thought of coming back**"

**Signaling there's more coming:**
- "sorrrta long & is slightly unhinged on account of me sharing everything you can do NOW to set up your email to be THE source profit next year." — no ellipsis, she actually names the reason; the ellipsis only appears when something is left unsaid

**What she does NOT do:** Use ellipsis as vague punctuation to make things sound mysterious. Every ellipsis she uses earns its place by having something specific before and after it.

### Exclamation points — frequency and function

Her exclamation points are not enthusiasm punctuation. They are either:
1. **Comedic emphasis:** "**Lovingly, I need you to cut that sh*t out!**" — wait, actually she doesn't add an exclamation here; the command is flat, which makes it stronger. The point stands: when she does use one, it punches up.
2. **Group energy/invitation:** "**We're entering march as grown ass women with MEMORIES!**" — the exclamation is the group cheer, pulling the reader in
3. **Warm close:** "Wishing you a cozy, soft Sunday 🤎" — no exclamation, notably, this is always a period + emoji. The warmth doesn't need the exclamation.

She is sparing enough with exclamation points that when one appears, it reads with actual energy. In the voice-flagged "me-i-need-ideas" email, exclamation points are notably absent from the strategy section — it reads flatter as a result.

### Parenthetical patterns specific to Soft Sundays

In newsletters, the parenthetical does more work than in any other copy format. It functions as:

**Inner monologue:** "(and tbh the third time seemed like a pity pass i was really bringing down the professors vibe by holding back tears in the front row semester after semester LOL🥺)" — this is Alyssa thinking out loud WHILE telling a story, not adding a footnote to it.

**The nested aside:** "(actually, sidenote within a sidenote: he let me write an essay instead of take a midterm and i wrote it on the qualitative implications..." — five lines of nested content inside a parenthetical. This would be cut from sales page copy. In the newsletter it's the point.

**Self-aware qualification:** "(sorry & your welcome)" — the grammatical error left in, lowercase, no punctuation inside the parenthetical. It reads like a text.

**Story beat that would break the main sentence:** "(I MIGHT have been in my luteal phase… but i swear that had nothing to do with it 🙃)" — she brackets the hormone aside so it doesn't take over the opening, but she absolutely includes it.

### Subject line and preview text patterns

Subject lines observed across these six emails:

| Subject line | Pattern | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| "Why I burned down a perfectly good business (on purpose)" | Personal story + parenthetical qualifier | The parenthetical "(on purpose)" is the hook — it changes what you think you're reading |
| "I FIXED your email list (the plan inside)" | Audacious claim + parenthetical proof | ALL CAPS verb signals action taken ON BEHALF of the reader |
| "Your list called, they're hungry" | Personification + food pun | Treats the email list as a character; "hungry" does double work |
| "I failed" | Two words. Full stop. | The most nakedly emotional subject line — no context, no qualifier |
| "ur (not)NEW march plan inside" | Lowercase "ur," parenthetical strikes through a word expectation | "ur" signals casual; "(not)NEW" subverts what you think the email will be |
| "If Lorelai had an online biz" | Pop culture reference, no explanation | Works entirely on if you know Gilmore Girls; completely off-topic surface, on-brand for cozy creative energy |

**The raccoon quality** (short, casual, slightly emotionally loaded, a tiny bit wrong) appears strongest in "I failed" and "ur (not)NEW march plan inside." These are the best model subject lines for a new Soft Sunday.

Preview text from "If Lorelai had an online biz": "make more in q4 than you did all year" — this is NOT a summary of the subject line. The subject line is a pop culture reference; the preview text is the actual business value proposition. They are doing completely different jobs.

## 6. How She Frames Herself Within Soft Sundays

### Big sister: when it works, what makes it cringe

The big sister voice appears most explicitly in the Pep Talk section, where it is literally labeled: "the weekly pep talk you didn't know you needed (from your business big sis)."

It works when:
- It's earned by genuine warmth expressed in a specific moment: "I hear this all the time from my business besties (you)" — the parenthetical "(you)" is what makes it work; it's shy about calling them "my besties" but does it anyway
- The call-out is paired with "lovingly" or a qualifier that shows she's on their side: "Lovingly, I need you to cut that sh*t out."
- She admits her own version of the same pattern: "I know the feeling all too well" (why-i-burned-down-my-business) — she's been the person who needed the pep talk

It would cringe if:
- "business big sis" appeared without the parenthetical — as an unqualified claim
- She told readers what to feel or how to think without first naming what THEY are currently feeling
- The warmth came without a specific: "You deserve it, babe" works because it follows "My big sis vision for you in 2026 is to have a list of warm leads, high open rates, real engagement, legit click rates, and consistent sales." The specific vision is what earns the "you deserve it."

### Soft CEO peer positioning

She most clearly positions as alongside the reader (not above) in why-i-burned-down-my-business, where she reveals she was going through the same burnout her readers experience. The language shifts from "here's what you should do" to "here's what we figured out together":

> "🤎 But more than anything, I'm blown away by the connections I've made this year, by this **community of soft ceos** who remind me what's possible when we prioritise intention over busyness"

The "we prioritise" — not "you should prioritize" — is the peer signal. She's inside the community, not presiding over it.

### Teacher/strategist: when she shifts and how she transitions

She shifts into teaching mode via a named connector:
- "**Here are 3 reallyyyy simple formulas to check that out for yourself:**" (i-failed-formula-for-growth) — the "for yourself" signals she's handing something over, not lecturing
- "**K, I know I've convinced you right? Now let me make it even easier for ya with a little 3 day plan ✅**" (i-fixed-your-email-list) — acknowledges the reader's current mental state before going into the plan
- "**So what ARE the four natural buying windows left this year?**" (if-lorelai-had-an-online-biz) — direct question, self-answered, classic transition to teaching mode

The shift is never cold. There is always a connector — a rhetorical question, an acknowledgment, a "here's what we're doing" — before the teaching begins.

### "Business big sis" as earned vs. stated

The label is in the header of every Pep Talk section, but she earns it through action within the email rather than repeating the claim. She earns it by:
- Sharing something vulnerable first (the fridge cry, the failed stats, the burndown)
- Expressing genuine pride in the reader: "proud of you for that"
- Giving something useful without requiring anything in return (the automation template, the Notion doc, the free class)

She does not repeat "as your business big sis" elsewhere in the email body. The claim is made once, in the header, and then demonstrated.

### How she talks about her own life

The Time Luxuries section is the explicit container for personal life content, but personal details bleed into the Pep Talk as setup material. The function is always the same: the personal detail opens a door that the business content walks through.

From if-lorelai-had-an-online-biz:
> "This weekend I picked back up with **my Nonna experiment**... i spent two days making tomato sauce from scratch (like… two hundred jars of it 😅)... **something about the patience required, the old timey process, and the way i know future alyssa will be thanking past me for unlimited low maintenance dinners, that truly felt like Back of House in a jar**"

She earned the product mention through the story. She didn't insert the story to earn the product mention — the sequence matters.

### Positioning her work without selling constantly

She almost never introduces an offer cold. The pattern is:
1. Personal story or relatable situation
2. The business parallel made explicit
3. The content/teaching that stands on its own
4. The offer framed as a logical next step for someone who wants more

Even in shorter emails (your-list-called-theyre-hungry), the fridge story, the metaphor parallel, and the three-step plan ALL appear before the product link. The product is the action you take after you're convinced — not the reason for the email.

## 7. Topics — What Works, How They're Presented, and At What Depth

### Categories of topics covered across the six emails

| Category | Example |
|---|---|
| Business restructuring / burnout recovery | why-i-burned-down-my-business |
| Email list health and strategy | i-fixed-your-email-list, your-list-called-theyre-hungry |
| Business numbers (profit, conversion, lead flow) | i-failed-formula-for-growth |
| Consistency vs. starting-over trap | ur-not-new-march-plan |
| Seasonal buying / promotional strategy | if-lorelai-had-an-online-biz |
| Content repurposing / re-commerce | me-i-need-ideas [content ref only] |

### How she enters a topic

Almost always through a personal story or a relatable physical/emotional moment. The topic name never appears in the first paragraph.

- Email list health → fridge-clearing meltdown
- Business numbers → failing stats twice in university
- Seasonal strategy → the September crossroads feeling
- Not starting over → "Mama Bear Alyssa" getting fired up about the pattern she's noticed
- Business burndown → the slow creative drain that started in March

The personal moment is the on-ramp. The topic is what's waiting at the end of the on-ramp.

### How deep she goes on business content

She does not do full tutorials in the Soft Sunday format. She goes to framework depth, not implementation depth, EXCEPT when she's giving a gift.

- The 3-day action plan in i-fixed-your-email-list is genuinely actionable — but she gives it as a gift ("I made it even easier for ya") and immediately links to a Notion doc for the full implementation. The email contains the structure; the external resource contains the execution.
- The four buying seasons in if-lorelai-had-an-online-biz are at framework depth: each gets a paragraph of psychology + one or two tactical tips + a source note "(Source: The Promo Planner)"
- The three formulas in i-failed-formula-for-growth are named without the actual math ("I wouldn't dare put real math in this email") and she links to a spreadsheet

The pattern: the Soft Sunday teaches the concept and gives the structure; the lead magnet, product, or linked resource contains the implementation.

### How she balances personal vs. business within one email

The ratio shifts by email length and topic weight:

| Email | Personal/Life content | Business content |
|---|---|---|
| your-list-called-theyre-hungry | ~40% (fridge story + close) | ~60% (three-step revival plan) |
| i-failed-formula-for-growth | ~50% (stats failure story) | ~50% (three formulas) |
| if-lorelai-had-an-online-biz | ~25% (nonna sauce) | ~75% (4 buying seasons) |
| why-i-burned-down-my-business | ~50% (Time Luxuries + origin) | ~50% (three reasons + rebuild) |
| i-fixed-your-email-list | ~15% (grandma moment, brief) | ~85% (full plan) |
| ur-not-new-march-plan | ~20% (the Mama Bear framing) | ~80% (three steps + products) |

The shortest, most personal section is always Time Luxuries. It runs 2-4 sentences. Even in the most content-heavy email (i-fixed-your-email-list), she includes: "My grandma's moving into the cutest retirement home (that lowkey feels like a resort), and this month I've been helping her sort through her things..." That's it. Three sentences of real life, then done.

### The through-narrative formula

When she structures narrative in a Soft Sunday:
1. Here's something real that happened to me (the human scene)
2. Here's what it surfaced or reminded me of (the parallel or the feeling)
3. Here's what I noticed about YOU / our community in this same pattern (the bridge to the reader)
4. Here's what to do about it (the framework or plan)
5. Here's where to go next (the soft sell or CTA)

### What makes a topic right for Soft Sunday vs. not

Right: Topics that connect to time, creative energy, business sustainability, anti-hustle, email + Pinterest strategy, the experience of running a small creative service business.

Right: Topics that can be entered through a personal life moment — not every topic can be, and she doesn't force it.

Right: Topics where she has a genuine opinion or a lived experience to draw from. She doesn't cover things neutrally.

Not right: General business advice that doesn't connect to her specific methodology (email + Pinterest + soft sales). She is not a mindset coach, she is not a productivity generalist.

Not right: Topics that require a "hustle harder" framing to deliver. The energy of the topic has to be compatible with soft CEO positioning.

Not right: Advice she hasn't personally tested or experienced. Everything she covers either comes from her own business, her clients inside BOH, or research she has specifically done.

## 8. DOs Within Soft Sundays

**Evidence cited from specific emails throughout.**

**DO: Start with something human and specific before any business content.** Even a one-sentence scene ("I cried into my fridge this week") is enough, as long as it's specific. (your-list-called-theyre-hungry)

**DO: Name the pivot out loud when you move from personal story to business content.** "As it happens, I was also working on... and I couldn't help but see the parallels." (your-list-called-theyre-hungry) The transparency is what makes the metaphor feel discovered rather than constructed.

**DO: Pre-warn readers when an email is unusually long.** Use her language: "this email got a little out of control, sorrrta long & is slightly unhinged on account of..." Frame the length as a gift, not an apology. (i-fixed-your-email-list)

**DO: Use the anchor word ritual every single time without exception.** The format is sacred: mood question → anchor word → "the one thing" prompt → "Wishing you a cozy, soft Sunday 🤎." (every email)

**DO: Include the section header bracket format.** Emoji — italic description — emoji. Every named section uses this. (structural across all emails)

**DO: Let the personal detail breathe.** Don't rush from the fridge story to the email list strategy. The story earns the teaching. Give it at least two paragraphs. (your-list-called-theyre-hungry)

**DO: Use the "what's in / what's out" structure when presenting a contrast.** The headers "WHAT'S OUT" and "WHAT'S IN" are visual anchors that make opposing concepts easy to follow. (i-fixed-your-email-list, if-lorelai-had-an-online-biz)

**DO: Give away something genuinely useful before mentioning a paid product.** The automation template, the Notion list revival doc, the spreadsheet — these come before the product pitch. (i-fixed-your-email-list, i-failed-formula-for-growth)

**DO: Use reply CTAs (Reply STATS, Reply AUTOMATION) when offering a limited, personal next step.** The reply CTA feels one-to-one, not broadcast. (i-fixed-your-email-list: "Reply AUTOMATION," i-failed-formula-for-growth: "Reply STATS")

**DO: Name the reader's emotional state before naming the solution.** "You're wondering wtf you should do about it." State their current feeling, then address it. (i-fixed-your-email-list)

**DO: Include at least one personal life detail in every email, even if it's brief.** The Time Luxuries section exists for this. Two or three sentences about what she's actually doing offline. (all six emails)

**DO: Let your anchor word be atmospheric or emotional, not productivity-task-language.** "magic," "Restore," "grounded," "prolific" — not "productive" or "organized" or "strategic." (Check In sections across all emails)

**DO: Use the stretched vowel for emphasis in conversational moments.** "Buuuut," "Soooo," "Prioriiitize," "eeeease," "brrrrb!" — the visual stretch signals a conversational pause or playful emphasis. (multiple emails)

**DO: Put the most important thing for skimmers in bold.** The bold creates a skim path. If someone reads only the bolded phrases, they should still understand the email's main point. (structural across all emails)

**DO: Include a nested aside when it's genuinely funny or revealing.** The "(sidenote within a sidenote)" about wind turbines and the TA works because it's unexpected and actually funny. It does not exist to pad word count. (i-failed-formula-for-growth)

**DO: Name the community before naming the offer.** "This community of soft CEOs" appears before the BOH mention in why-i-burned-down-my-business. The community is the point; the offer is the vehicle.

**DO: Let the food metaphor appear naturally in the Check In section's mood options.** "Is it a clear the counters, and start from scratch with clean hands kinda week? Or more of a reach for the go-to ingredients, but with a twist vibe?" (ur-not-new-march-plan) The Check In is a natural home for the metaphor because the mood binary often lends itself to kitchen/meal framing.

**DO: Close every product mention with a CTA that reads as action, not obligation.** "Reply STATS and I'll send the link" is casual, optional, one-to-one. "Get it 50% Off >>" is the arrow CTA — straightforward, no fake urgency. (i-failed-formula-for-growth)

**DO: Match your anchor word to the emotional tone of the email.** "Clocked In" appears when the email is about clocking in for Q2. "Restore" appears when the email is about her grandma and rebuilding. The word is not random — it echoes what she just wrote. (i-fixed-your-email-list, i-failed-formula-for-growth)

## 9. DO NOT EVERs Within Soft Sundays

**DO NOT EVER: Open with the topic, the concept, or the strategy.** The first paragraph must be personal, emotional, or scene-based. "Today I want to talk about email list health" would immediately break the format.

**DO NOT EVER: Pre-explain the metaphor.** In your-list-called-theyre-hungry, she does not say "I'm going to use a fridge metaphor to explain email lists." She tells the fridge story and then says "I couldn't help but see the parallels." The metaphor is discovered, not announced.

**DO NOT EVER: Use "value" as a noun.** From writing-style.md and the Lazy Launch transcript: "Please retire the word 'value.'" This is doctrine. "Valuable content" does not appear anywhere in these six emails. When she describes what she's giving, she names the specific thing: "the 3 day action plan," "the automation template," "7 re-engagement emails."

**DO NOT EVER: Use generic pep talk language.** "You've got this!" "Keep going!" "Believe in yourself!" — none of this appears in any Soft Sunday. The pep talk is specific: "My big sis vision for you in 2026 is to have a list of warm leads, high open rates, real engagement, legit click rates, and consistent sales." That's a pep talk. The other stuff is a poster.

**DO NOT EVER: Introduce a product cold.** Every product in every email is introduced after the content that makes it make sense. The Seasonal Promo Planner appears after the four buying seasons are explained. The BOH waitlist appears after three reasons for burning down the business. Nothing is dropped in without setup.

**DO NOT EVER: Let the "Business Big Sis" claim be the whole content.** The label in the header is permission to have that energy; it does not substitute for doing the actual big sister work within the email. Without genuine vulnerability, specific help, and honest acknowledgment of the reader's situation, the label is hollow.

**DO NOT EVER: Use fake urgency.** Not one of these six emails uses "only X spots left" without that being real. The reply CTAs ("Reply STATS," "Reply AUTOMATION") often include genuinely scant availability: "I've got room for 1 more person to get my eyes on their current offer suite before Friday." One person. That is real. (i-failed-formula-for-growth)

**DO NOT EVER: Write a Pep Talk section that doesn't acknowledge what the reader is currently feeling before pivoting to the teaching.** The section always validates before it redirects. "I hear this all the time from my business besties (you) - your email list looks solid on paper. The numbers are good, or getting there. But there's a sneaky chunk of subs..." — she names what they're experiencing before she names what to do. (i-fixed-your-email-list)

**DO NOT EVER: Stretch the food metaphor into allegory in the middle of a teaching section.** In your-list-called-theyre-hungry, she explicitly ends the food metaphor: "they're missing you like you missed the chili paste in the side drawer - lol ok im done with the food thing." The metaphor works at the entry point. Once the teaching begins, she drops it. The self-aware "(lol ok im done with the food thing)" is the signal.

**DO NOT EVER: Use a sign-off that sounds like a brand account.** "Wishing you a cozy, soft Sunday 🤎" — period, single emoji. Not "Have a fabulous week! See you next Sunday! xx, Alyssa 🌸🌸🌸." The close is warm but it's restrained.

**DO NOT EVER: Make the personal life section feel like a brand lifestyle vignette.** The personal details in Time Luxuries are not aspirational. They are specific and slightly mundane: helping grandma sort through china, making tomato sauce for two days, crying into a fridge. These are the opposite of "laptop on a beach."

**DO NOT EVER: Write a Soft Sunday without the Check In section's ritual.** The anchor word + "the one thing" prompt is non-negotiable across all six emails. Even the shortest email (your-list-called-theyre-hungry) has it. It is the close of every Soft Sunday, always.

**DO NOT EVER: Make the teaching content feel like a listicle.** The House Report may use numbered steps (1, 2, 3) or numbered formulas, but the framing is always personal and contextual. "Here are 5 tips for better email marketing" would violate the format. It's always "here are 3 formulas I wish I'd understood earlier, after I failed stats twice" — the personal frame is non-negotiable.

**DO NOT EVER: Use the AI-sounding construction of [Label] + [short generic description].** This is the pattern in the voice-flagged "me-i-need-ideas" email: "☁️ Strategy O1: Dust off the Trophies / Repurpose old workshops, client resources, live event recordings into low ticket offers..." That's a clean, accurate description with no Alyssa in it. Every other email builds in texture, humor, or specificity inside the strategy descriptions themselves. The generic structure is what made it feel AI.

**DO NOT EVER: Open the Pep Talk section without the [FIRST NAME GOES HERE] merge tag or direct address.** Every Pep Talk either addresses the reader by name merge tag or uses "Hey [FIRST NAME GOES HERE]" or "Hey." The opening sentence makes direct contact. It's never a concept statement.

**DO NOT EVER: Stack emoji at the end of a sentence when bold already does the emphasis.** Bold and emoji are emphasis tools that work best separately. Stacking them ("**Important point 🔥**") doubles the signal and dilutes both. The fire emoji appears after a sentence that's NOT already bolded: "🔥 **So we burned it all down. (yep, even the profitable parts)**" — the bold is on the section header content, the fire emoji opens the thought. They're adjacent, not stacked.

**DO NOT EVER: Skip the personal life detail entirely.** Even i-fixed-your-email-list, which is explicitly the most content-heavy, longest email in the set, includes the grandma section. Two sentences. That's the minimum. The detail is non-negotiable because it's the thing that separates the Soft Sunday from a content newsletter.

**DO NOT EVER: Use a CTA that says "Buy Now," "Sign Up," "Click Here," or "Learn More."** This is already in the writing-style.md Don'ts and it applies here with equal force. CTAs in Soft Sundays are either food metaphor language, action-specific ("Reply STATS"), or the reader's internal monologue ("Get it 50% Off >>").

## 10. How Sales and CTAs Appear in Soft Sundays

### When a CTA appears and how it's set up

CTAs in Soft Sundays almost never appear in the first half of the email. They come after:
1. The personal entry point (Pep Talk or opening story)
2. The teaching content (House Report, strategy section, Nerding Out On)
3. A natural landing point where the reader has received something useful

In your-list-called-theyre-hungry, the plan CTA appears after: fridge story → metaphor drawn → three-step structure explained. The CTA is "So, this is your sign to do an email list revival before q1 ends" — notably she uses "this is your sign" here, which appears on the Don'ts list from AGENTS.md. This is a case where evidence from the real email shows a phrase that's been identified as a Don't — acknowledge it and don't repeat it in new copy.

### The soft sell mechanics: how story earns the CTA

From if-lorelai-had-an-online-biz:
> "By the way… I went ahead and built **the ultimate Notion dashboard to take your one signature offer and spin it into four fresh, seasonal promos for the rest of 2025.**"

"By the way…" is the softest possible sales transition. She's spent the entire House Report section teaching the four buying seasons. By the time she introduces the dashboard, the reader already knows why they need it. The "by the way" signals: I'm not changing subjects; this is a natural extension.

From i-failed-formula-for-growth:
> "In Phase one of Back of House we add in the backend system I use to turn a SIMPLE offer suite into something that sells weekly through email... **I've got room for 1 more person to get my eyes on their current offer suite before Friday.**"

The product appears inside the teaching, not after it. She's explaining the system, then mentions it's something she teaches in BOH, then mentions she has one spot. The sequence makes the offer feel like the natural next step for someone who wants to implement what they just learned.

### How "Just Upgraded" and "Nerding Out On" handle products

**Just Upgraded** is the explicit product/promo section. It has its own header and its own energy — slightly louder, slightly more sales-forward. But it still follows the same earn-the-mention logic:

From ur-not-new-march-plan, Just Upgraded contains the Seasonal Promo Planner introduced by:
> "🍝 All you have to do is plug in your signature dish, choose your seasonal angle, and the planner hands you:"

The description is concrete and specific. It names exactly what you get. No vague promises.

**Nerding Out On** is the tools/tech section. Product mentions here are framed as things she's personally into and using — not things she's selling. Even when there's a link, the framing is "I'll let you have one of my automation templates" (gift language), not "Check out this product."

### Reply CTAs: how they work and why

"Reply STATS and I'll send the link" and "Reply AUTOMATION and I'll send the link" both function the same way:

1. They are conversational — the format is a direct reply, like a text
2. They signal limited availability: "I've got room for 1 more person" / "I'm continuing live reviews this week, and I've got room for 2 more people"
3. They create a one-to-one feel even in a broadcast email
4. The keyword is all-caps (STATS, AUTOMATION) — easy to search for in a reply, clean for automation trigger purposes
5. They appear at the END of the House Report or teaching section, after she's already demonstrated her expertise on that exact topic

The reply CTA is never used for a product that's available to everyone at any time. It's used when there's a genuine capacity limit or a genuinely time-limited offer.

### Sales email CTA vs. Soft Sunday CTA: what changes

| Sales email CTA | Soft Sunday CTA |
|---|---|
| Often ALL CAPS with explicit action language | Often lowercase or mixed case, conversational |
| Appears multiple times throughout the email | Appears once or twice, late in the email |
| The whole email builds to the CTA | The CTA is one landing point, not the destination |
| Food metaphor CTAs are common: "YOU HAD ME AT DIRTY DISHES" | Soft Sunday CTAs use reply mechanics or simple link text |
| Can stand alone as a phrase: "SOUNDS DELISH >>" | Soft Sunday CTAs are embedded in sentences: "click here to be the first to see how it works >>" |
| Urgency can be higher (launch windows, real deadlines) | Urgency is real but soft: limited spots, not countdown timers |

The key difference: in a sales email, the CTA is the point of the email. In a Soft Sunday, the CTA is what you do after you've gotten the point of the email (which is the teaching and the relationship).

## Quick Calibration Checklist

Before sending any Soft Sunday draft, answer these 10 questions:

1. **Does the email open with a personal moment, scene, or confession — not a concept or a topic?** (If the first sentence could appear on a sales page, it's wrong.)

2. **Is the pivot from personal story to business content named out loud?** ("I couldn't help but see the parallels" / "As it happens")

3. **Does the Pep Talk section acknowledge what the reader is currently feeling before pivoting to teaching?** (Validate, then redirect.)

4. **Is the anchor word atmospheric or emotional — not a productivity task?** ("magic," "grounded," "restore" — not "productive" or "focused")

5. **Does the Check In section follow the exact ritual format?** (Mood question → anchor word bookended by emoji → ☁️ → "the one thing" → "Wishing you a cozy, soft Sunday 🤎")

6. **Does every named section have the emoji — italic text — emoji bracket format?** (🫶 _text_ 🫶)

7. **Is there at least one specific personal life detail that could not have been invented by an AI reading her brand notes?** (The luteal phase, the grandma's china, the two hundred jars of tomato sauce)

8. **Does every product or offer appear AFTER the teaching that makes it make sense?** (No cold drops, no "by the way here's a thing I'm selling" without the earned context)

9. **Is the food metaphor confined to transition points, the Check In mood question, and the Just Upgraded section — NOT deployed mid-argument as decoration?** (In your-list-called-theyre-hungry, she explicitly says "lol ok im done with the food thing" when it's served its purpose)

10. **If you read only the bolded text, can you follow the main argument?** (Bold is the skim path; if it's incoherent on its own, the bolding is decorative, not structural)
