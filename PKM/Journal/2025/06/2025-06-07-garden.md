# Garden
Okay okay — here’s the gospel of moving your glorious Nikko Deutzia safely:
🗓️ When Should You Move Her?
✨ Best case:
Early Fall (late September-ish for Guelph): Why? The heat stress is gone, but the ground is still warm enough to trigger root growth before winter.
✨ Next Best? Right now (early June), because technically she’s already flowering or just finished — so she’s not putting energy into new flowers, and if you keep her very watered after moving, you’re good.
🔥 But: If you move her now:
Expect a little sulk.
Water deeply every 2–3 days for the first 2 weeks.
Mulch the base to hold moisture and keep roots cool.
🥾 How To Move a Piece Without Hurting Her:
1. Pick your spots:
Target the edges — look for canes that have rooted or spots where the shrub flares out. You’re looking for side-shoots or suckers.
2. Dig wide and deep:
About a 12–15 inch diameter circle around your chosen offshoot. Dig as deep as you can (minimum 12 inches deep if possible) to grab a decent root ball.
3. Be gentle:
It’s more about keeping the root system intact than worrying about taking a huge chunk. If you can get a ball of soil and roots, even if it’s small, Deutzia is hardy and will rebound.
4. Cut clean:
Use clean, sharp pruners to snip the branch connection if it’s tangled with momma plant. Think of it like cutting the umbilical cord — swift and respectful.
5. Plant immediately:
Dig the new hole about twice as wide as the root ball, slightly shallower than the ball’s depth (so the crown sits just at or slightly above soil level).
Water it in well — like a long, slow soak.
6. Mulch:
A nice fluffy 2–3 inch layer of mulch (not touching the stems) will keep moisture in.
7. Shade if it's hot:
You can even rig up a little shade cloth or lean a board against it for a few days if it’s really sunny after transplanting.
🥀 Will She Still Look Good After?
YES. You’re only stealing a side braid, not shaving her head. She’ll bounce back next spring without even noticing you took a piece.
📝 Quick Plan:
If you’re feeling gutsy and it’s not blistering hot today or this week — go for it now.
If you’re patient and want it nearly risk-free — wait until the early fall window.
