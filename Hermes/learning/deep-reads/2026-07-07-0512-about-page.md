## New patterns found

### 1. Caps-colon stage directions are the narrative spine
The page is a *told story*, and she announces every beat out loud in caps ending with a colon — a narrator stepping forward, distinct from sales-page marquee declarations (billboard claims like "HUSTLE CULTURE IS OVERCOOKED"):

> "LET ME SET THE SCENE FOR YOU:"
> "SO…. SUFFICE TO SAY:"
> "AT THE END OF THE RABBIT HOLE:"
> "MEET: THE SYSTEM OF ALL SYSTEMS"
> "THE MORAL OF THIS STORY?"

These are connective tissue, not headers-as-arguments. The reader is being *walked through a campfire story* with the seams showing. "THE MORAL OF THIS STORY?" even flips the device to a question. Rule: in narrative web copy, section transitions are spoken stage directions in caps, one clause, colon (or question mark) terminal.

### 2. Parenthetical prefix-splice is a corpus-wide device, not a caption one-off
Two hits on one page:

> "who do you think ended up having to (micro)manage everything?"
> "surrounded by (multi) passionate creatives"

Parentheses weld an extra morpheme onto a word so one word says two things ("manage" and "micromanage" simultaneously). The caption corpus's "clear(but cozy)" was logged 2026-07-07 as a shipped-unpolished oddity; this page shows it's a *system*. Note the inconsistent spacing — "(micro)manage" welded, "(multi) passionate" spaced — the wobble ships.

### 3. Hedging asymmetry: vulnerability gets triple-hedged, conviction goes flat
Every negative/vulnerable feeling on the page arrives wrapped in stacked hedges — "well," + "kinda/kind of" + a deflating italic or (lol):

> "running my business felt, well, *hard* (lol)."
> "I was, well, for a lack of a better term, kinda miserable."
> "it was … new information at best, kind of concerning at worst."

Meanwhile her claims are stated flat, zero hedge: "**I've always been an entrepreneur at heart.**" / "I love my work, and I'm quite literally obsessed with my clients." The asymmetry IS the move: she whispers the wound and states the conviction. (Extends STYLE §3's "no jokes during vulnerable beats" — the about-page variant is: vulnerable beats get *hedges*, which are softer than jokes, while the hedge stack itself does the self-aware humor.) Also note "at best … at worst" as a two-pole scale device for delivering a bad realization.

### 4. The retraction-that-sells (cliché-literalization)
She uses a stock idiom, retracts it in a parenthesis in real time, takes it literally, and flips the literal reading into a service promise:

> "I doubled down on the parts of my business that could compound over time and never looked back.
> (Actually, that's not true. I *did* look back, but only to data mine historical marketing trends so you don't have to)"

This is TASTE's "visible revision as honesty" with a named mechanic: negate your own cliché → literalize it → the literal version is the value prop. Note it also plants "history girl" credibility without ever leading with credentials.

### 5. Pun-welded contrast (judge calibration)
The headline:

> "Dreaming of late-night creative sessions by candlelight, but instead you're burning the candle at both ends just trying to keep your business running?"

Dream-vs-reality contrast that would smell like a banned reframe — except both halves ride ONE shared literal object (the candle), and it's phrased as a question about the reader's situation, not a frame-swap. What makes it hers: the pun weld. A contrast whose halves share a literal image is a different animal from "This isn't X. This is Y." Grandfathered as source per the 2026-07-05 ruling; the pun-weld distinction goes to ANTI-AI as judge calibration.

### 6. Self-question → self-answer is the page's engine
Three times she asks a question and answers it herself, and the answer always lands as a fragment or a joke:

> "Dreaming of… keep your business running?" → "**Dude, we must be soulmates.**"
> "You think I'd be thrilled, right?" → "Au contrair."
> "who do you think ended up having to (micro)manage everything?" → "👍This girl 👍"

This is the narrative-copy twin of the caption corpus's staged reader Q&A ("What's that? I hear you say") and the transcript's "they're like" roleplay. Same engine, third register.

### 7. Mangled borrowed phrases ship
> "Au contrair." (for *au contraire*)
> "for a lack of a better term" (for *for lack of a better term*)

Her idiom misfires are live on her website and load-bearing (the "Au contrair" line is the STYLE §1 rhythm anchor!). Correcting a borrowed phrase or idiom to dictionary form is a polish-tell — same family as "Siganture DIsh" surviving on the flagship sales page.

### 8. Credentials ride mid-sentence in a because-clause
STYLE §9 bans leading with credentials; this page shows the positive replacement:

> "Because after almost 10 years running an online business, being behind the scenes in hundreds of service pros, & digital marketing research from the last 20 years they're the most low-maintenance, sustainable tools for growth I've found."

Ten years + hundreds of clients + 20 years of research — all buried inside the *reasoning* for a conclusion about tools, in a run-on with a missing comma and an "&", never as a bio flex. Credentials are evidence for a claim, never the claim.

### 9. The about page is reader-first
POV finding: the page opens on the READER's dream (the candlelight question), and "I" doesn't appear until the second section. The bio is framed as service ("there's a lot of things you should probably know about me") and its announced climax is about *them*:

> "BUT MAYBE THE SINGLE MOST IMPORTANT THING YOU SHOULD KNOW ABOUT ME IS THIS:
> I love my work, and I'm quite literally obsessed with my clients."

Then the offer reveal waits ~600 words (already in STYLE §6), and the story's stated moral is reader-directed: "We went down that rabbit hole so you can go down yours." Rule: even the about page keeps the reader at the center — the bio list earns trust, the most-important-thing beat hands the page back to them.

### 10. The aphorism-then-permission couplet
> "A good idea loses its wings every time a creative is too busy to go down a rabbit hole
>
> So, go ahead, waste your time."

The permission line was already doctrine (SOUL §5); the new bit is the couplet structure — an unpunctuated aphorism line states the law, then a short permission line applies it to the reader. Law → license, two lines, no explanation between.

### 11. Bio-list escalation: resume first, absurdity last
The "things you should know about me" list runs 5 items (odd, not 3) and escalates from credibility to absurd texture: (1) dream job quit, (2) entrepreneur at heart, (3) black-pen checkmarks, (4) 5848 tabs, (5) lost coming home from the antique market. The last item is always the weirdest. Also new idiom in item 3: "**(a checkmark could never)**" — the gen-z "could never" personified-negation joke.

### 12. Verse formatting in web copy
Clause stacks break across lines like stanza:

> "When I flat out dreaded going back to work,
> it was … new information at best, kind of concerning at worst."

Plus nonstandard ellipses ship: "SO…." (four dots), "it was … new" (spaced ellipsis mid-sentence). Line breaks are pacing (STYLE §1) extends fully to website copy.

## Rules extracted (DO / DON'T)

**DO**
- Announce narrative transitions as caps-colon stage directions ("LET ME SET THE SCENE FOR YOU:") in story-driven web copy.
- Weld morphemes with parentheses to say two words at once: "(micro)manage."
- Stack hedges ("well," / "kinda" / "(lol)" / italics) on vulnerable confessions; state convictions flat.
- Retract your own cliché in a parenthesis and literalize it into a value prop.
- Ask the reader a question and answer it yourself with a fragment or a joke.
- Bury credentials mid-sentence inside a because-clause, as evidence for a claim about something else.
- Open the about page on the reader; hand the bio's climax back to them.
- End bio lists on the weirdest item; use 5, not 3.
- Let idiom misfires and missing commas ship ("Au contrair," "for a lack of a better term").

**DON'T**
- Don't write headers-as-labels in narrative web copy (stage directions, not "My Story").
- Don't hedge the claims or flatten the confessions — the asymmetry is the voice.
- Don't correct her borrowed phrases, spacing wobbles, or run-on grammar to dictionary form.
- Don't generate new dream-vs-reality contrast headlines; the grandfathered one is pun-welded and hers.
- Don't lead with credentials anywhere, even on the about page — the page proves this is a choice, not an accident.
- Don't joke *at* the vulnerable beat; hedge it instead, joke the line after.
