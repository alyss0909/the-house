## New patterns found

### 1. Refrain regulation — one permission phrase, repeated verbatim, many times
"Don't overthink it" appears ~8 times in one lesson; "short and sweet" ~5 times ("It
should be short and sweet. Please don't overthink this" · "remember, it's short, sweet.
Don't overthink it" · "Short, sweet, cute."). She does not vary the phrasing — the
repetition IS the reassurance. This is the spoken counterpart of the written permission
beat: instead of one permission moment, a spoken lesson runs a permission HEARTBEAT.
Elegant-variation instinct (rewording each recurrence) would erase the device.

### 2. Analogy install → shorthand callback
An analogy gets one full-stretch installation, then returns as a 2-4 word callback with
zero re-explanation:
- Install: "This is the gum at checkout. Okay? People aren't buying books at checkout
  because they haven't read the back... No, they're buying gum. They want gum right now.
  They've got bad breath like, boom, problem solved."
- Callbacks, 20+ minutes later: "It's gum. Okay. We're either getting it or we're not."
  and "so that you don't have bad breath anymore. Get this gum."
Framework names compress the same way — "the Hawaii" becomes a bare noun: "am I really
selling the product or am I selling the actual relief? The thing, the Hawaii, the things
that they're gonna get." Once she's named a thing, she TRUSTS the name.

### 3. Live rewrite pairs (bad version → fragment verdict → good version)
Her core teaching demo: state the bad version with real texture, kill it in a fragment,
then rewrite it in full on the spot.
- "A 32 page ebook on the science of habit building. Not great, really not great, okay?
  ... instead, the Daily Habit Tracker template and five minute morning plan to start
  reprogramming your brain today in under five minutes."
- "a worksheet from my X, Y, Z course client portal... to instead saying my private
  onboarding framework never released outside my 2K program and yours today for only $9."
- "my 10 page workbook on productivity systems for solopreneurs, but nobody cares how
  many pages it is... but finally hitting inbox zero in 15 minutes a day without hiring a
  va. Steal the exact system I use to reclaim six plus hours a week. Now that sounds
  good, right?"
The bad version is never a strawman label — it gets enough texture to be recognizable
("32 page," "10 page workbook"). The verdict is a fragment ("Not great, really not great,
okay?" · "Don't love it." · "not the best. Okay.").

### 4. Buyer roleplay in first person, entered through "like"
She slips into the buyer's head constantly, unannounced, gated by "they're like" /
"you're like" / "I'd be like":
- "damn. This was 10 bucks. I literally can't imagine what would happen if I paid her
  2000. My mind is already blown by spending $20 with this person."
- "wait, didn't I just say I wanted that, but it was free a second ago and now I have to
  pay."
- "that's it, I'm done. I'm, I can't do this for another second."
- "I'd be like, what? I don't know. What is that?" (as the doctor's patient)
This is the spoken form of the italic inner-monologue channel from the sales-email read —
same device, voiced instead of italicized.

### 5. "Blah, blah, blah" — the anti-completeness wave
~7 occurrences. She compresses any obvious continuation instead of enumerating it: "this
is what's in it? This is what. This is how often you take it" gets cut elsewhere to "and
blah, blah, blah." Also the double-strength "or a seven day challenge or blah, blah,
blah, blah, blah." An LLM's instinct is to finish every list; she waves the rest away
once the point lands. Cousin of "whatever your thing is, right?" and "or something like
that" — deliberate vagueness at the EDGES of examples, precision at the center.

### 6. Spoken decision trees + assigning the decision on the spot
Choices are taught as if/then branches, out loud, then the student is put on the clock:
"Do you already have... a product that solves a very specific version of the same pain...
If yes, choose the trip wire." · "If you think somebody would be willing to book a call
and take a sales call with you right now, I would choose the book a call option." · "So
at this point, I want you to think, which [one am] I gonna do." She also names the
branching structure itself: "this becomes a choose your own adventure." And she releases
non-chosen paths explicitly: "If you've just chosen the thank you page, you can basically
skip through the rest of this."

### 7. Borrowed frameworks get credited, with an origin memory
Her own IP gets coined names (Double Yes Method); borrowed tools get attribution plus a
first-contact story: "I like to use the five whys method" · "that's the Hair on Fire
moment. And I remember the first time I read about this it's a common technique that a
lot of like tech companies use." She never claims a borrowed framework — the crediting is
itself a credibility move (history girl energy: this is tested, not invented).

### 8. The ugly-converts doctrine (deliberate imperfection at design scale)
Data-backed, repeated, gleeful: "we've seen the best conversions on all types of pages
when the button is somewhat ugly" · "Orange is not even one of my colors, but this is the
button color that has performed the best" · "the price, like regularly, 300 crossed out,
really ugly. And then only 37... the uglier the better." · "I've seen the ugliest
templates like Google Docs, like literal screenshots from people's phone... I've seen
these convert so well." This extends the TASTE "deliberate imperfection" love from typos
to a full design belief: polish is suspect, ugly is evidence of realness.

### 9. Spoken-register lexicon extensions
- "sick" as top praise: "you know that it's actually sick" · "Get yourself with a really
  sick tripwire delivery email."
- "I'm definitely on one" (self-aware rant flag, right after the preaching frame-break).
- "whole ass" as intensifier: "Give them a whole ass appetizer."
- "fricking" — the softened swear in teaching register: "make this so fricking good that
  they feel almost guilty."
- "you guys" for the cohort at open/close: "And that's it, you guys." · "Can't wait to
  see what you guys make." (Body of the lesson stays second-person singular — plural
  address is bookend-only.)
- Absurd invented placeholder + instant self-mockery: "let's say it's fuzzy pink cat.
  What am I talking about?"

### 10. Real-urgency mechanics, from her own mouth (evidence for the open timer flag)
"We always include a seven minute countdown timer. Just trust me that time works the
best. We've tested so many different times. I've tested an hour or 14 minutes, nine
minutes." followed immediately by the doctrine: "and make these real don't fake it. Make
this real. After seven minutes the page expires. If you only do five calls per month
figure out a way to update it when one gets booked so that it's four." And on the oops
email: "your urgency and your scarcity is real because after this you're never gonna
mention the trip wire again. Be real about that." → Countdown timers are not merely
grandfathered on the BOH page; they are TAUGHT DOCTRINE when the expiry is real. Logged
to [[inbox]] as supporting evidence on the open 2026-07-06 ORDERUP flag (her call still).

### 11. Command imperatives are legal in teaching register
"Go for it." · "Get to work." · "go change it." · "Fill this in." · "Give yourself like
an hour to write the copy, an hour to design it." The STYLE §6 "CTAs are never commands"
rule is COPY-register law; spoken teaching runs on warm imperatives constantly. Register
scoping matters — a teaching script with zero imperatives would read as evasive.

## Contradictions / corrections

1. **Countdown timers**: not a contradiction after all — this transcript shows timers are
   her taught doctrine WHEN REAL ("make these real don't fake it"). Supporting evidence
   appended to the existing inbox flag; Hermes still writes no timers until she rules.
2. **Banned shapes are dense in spoken teaching** (all grandfathered verbatim source per
   the 2026-07-05 ruling, generation ban unchanged): "This is not a time for more
   information. This is time for a quick win." · "People don't wanna learn. They want to
   win." · "the buyer feels like they win immediately and not learn how to win
   eventually." · "selling the product rather than the relief" · "It's not just get
   revenue up. It's not just get leads." · "more is not necessarily more." Judge
   calibration: transcript register hits banned shapes at sales-page-marquee density.
3. **Banned vocabulary appears in speech**: "they're using it really effortlessly" ·
   "why that's going to actually transform my life" (inside the doctor roleplay). Spoken
   register predates/escapes the written bans; transcripts are not vocabulary license.
   Noted in [[inbox]] as calibration data, not proposed rule changes.
4. **"Address one person" (STYLE §8) needs a bookend nuance**: module videos open and
   close plural ("you guys") while the teaching body stays singular. Not a contradiction
   — a refinement, folded into the craft file.
5. **A new banned-adjacent validation shape, positive-first**: STYLE §6 documents
   negation-first validation ("And it's not because X. Or Y."); here she runs the
   inverse — claim first, then trailing negations: "Now, this is a real reason to
   purchase something. It's not just get revenue up. It's not just get leads." Worth
   knowing as HER shape (claim → trailing negations), distinct from the banned
   reframe (negation → punch).

## Rules extracted (DO / DON'T)

DO (spoken/teaching register):
- Repeat ONE permission refrain verbatim throughout ("Don't overthink it") — heartbeat,
  not variation.
- Install an analogy once at full stretch, then call it back in 2-4 words ("It's gum.").
- Teach by live rewrite pairs: textured bad version → fragment verdict → full good
  version.
- Voice the buyer in first person, gated by "they're like / you're like."
- Wave away obvious continuations with "blah, blah, blah" / "or whatever your thing is" —
  vague at the edges, precise at the center ("$9," "seven minutes," "70%").
- Speak decision trees as if/then branches, name the structure ("choose your own
  adventure"), release the non-chosen path, assign the decision now.
- Credit borrowed frameworks with an origin memory; coin only your own.
- Preach the ugly-converts doctrine with her data (orange button, crossed-out $300).
- Use warm imperatives freely ("Go for it." "Get to work.") — teaching register only.
- Plural "you guys" at open/close, singular "you" in the teaching body.

DON'T:
- Learn punctuation from transcripts (transcriber artifacts).
- Vary the permission refrain (elegant variation kills the device).
- Re-explain an installed analogy on callback.
- Enumerate example lists to completion.
- Generate banned shapes or banned vocab because transcripts contain them — verbatim-hers
  carve-out only, per the 2026-07-05 ruling.
- Strip imperatives from teaching scripts because copy CTAs ban commands.
